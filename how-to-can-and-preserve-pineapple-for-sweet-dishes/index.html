How to Can and Preserve Pineapple for Sweet Dishes


Pineapple is a tropical fruit known for its sweet and tangy flavor, making it a perfect addition to a variety of sweet dishes. Whether you enjoy it fresh, in pies, cakes, or as part of fruit salads, canned pineapple can offer a convenient way to enjoy this fruit year-round. The process of canning and preserving pineapple ensures that you can keep the fruit fresh and ready for use even when pineapples are out of season. In this guide, we’ll explore how to can and preserve pineapple for sweet dishes, including the necessary steps, tips, and best practices to ensure your canned pineapple stays delicious.

Why Can Pineapple?

Canning pineapple helps preserve its vibrant taste, nutrients, and texture. If you’ve ever wanted to capture the sweetness of ripe pineapples when they’re in season or have a stockpile of the fruit, canning is the solution. When canned properly, pineapple can last for months and be used in a variety of desserts like pineapple upside-down cake, fruit tarts, or smoothies. Canning also makes it easy to have a ready supply of pineapple for everyday use, so you can quickly add it to your favorite sweet dishes.

Benefits of Canning Pineapple

  • Longer Shelf Life: Properly canned pineapple can last for a year or longer when stored in a cool, dark place.
  • Seasonal Availability: Pineapples are typically in season only in certain months, but canning allows you to enjoy their flavor throughout the year.
  • Convenience: Having jars of preserved pineapple on hand means you can quickly incorporate them into cakes, jams, or pies.
  • Preserving Freshness: Canning helps lock in the pineapple’s flavor and nutrients, ensuring it retains its bright and refreshing taste.

Selecting Pineapple for Canning

The key to successfully preserving pineapple is to choose the right fruit. Here are some tips for selecting the best pineapples for canning:

Choose Ripe Pineapples

Ripe pineapples are essential to ensuring that the canned fruit is sweet and flavorful. A ripe pineapple should have a vibrant yellow color, with a slight softness when you press on the skin. The crown of the pineapple should be easy to pull off, and the fruit should emit a sweet aroma.

Avoid Overripe Pineapples

Overripe pineapples can result in canned fruit that is mushy and overly sweet. While they might still be usable for canning, they won’t yield the best texture or flavor.

Size Matters

Consider the size of the pineapple when canning. Larger pineapples will yield more fruit, making it easier to can in larger batches. Smaller pineapples may be more convenient for small-scale canning, but they may require more effort for peeling and cutting.

Tools and Equipment for Canning Pineapple

Before you begin the canning process, it’s essential to gather the necessary tools and equipment. Here’s a list of items you’ll need:

  • Canning Jars: Use clean, sterilized canning jars. Pint or quart-sized jars work well for preserving pineapple.
  • Lids and Bands: Ensure the lids are new and free of any dents. The bands should fit securely around the jars.
  • Large Pot: A large pot is needed to make the syrup for preserving the pineapple.
  • Tongs: Tongs will help you handle the hot jars and fruit during the canning process.
  • Funnel: A canning funnel helps you fill the jars without making a mess.
  • Peeler and Knife: A sharp knife and a peeler will help you easily peel and cut the pineapple.
  • Jar Lifter: This tool allows you to safely lift the hot jars out of the water bath after processing.

Preparing Pineapple for Canning

Once you’ve gathered the necessary tools and selected your pineapples, it’s time to prepare them for canning. The steps involved are fairly simple and straightforward.

Step 1: Peel the Pineapple

Using a sharp knife, carefully cut off the top and bottom of the pineapple. Stand the pineapple upright and slice off the tough outer skin in strips, following the natural curvature of the fruit. Be sure to remove any eyes (small brown spots) from the flesh using the tip of your knife.

Step 2: Core the Pineapple

Once the pineapple is peeled, cut the fruit in half lengthwise, then remove the core. The core is tough and fibrous, so it’s best to discard it. After removing the core, cut the pineapple into rings, chunks, or pieces, depending on your preference. The size and shape of the pieces will affect how they fit in the jars, so aim for uniform pieces to ensure consistent processing.

Step 3: Prepare the Syrup

Canning pineapple typically requires the use of syrup to help preserve its flavor, texture, and color. The syrup also enhances the sweetness of the pineapple, making it perfect for sweet dishes. There are several syrup options to choose from:

Light Syrup

A light syrup consists of a simple mixture of sugar and water, typically using 1 cup of sugar to 4 cups of water. This syrup allows the pineapple’s natural sweetness to shine while still providing some additional sweetness.

Medium Syrup

For a medium syrup, use 1 cup of sugar to 3 cups of water. This syrup will create a slightly sweeter and thicker solution for preserving your pineapple.

Heavy Syrup

A heavy syrup is made with 1 cup of sugar to 2 cups of water. This syrup is the sweetest and will preserve the pineapple with a stronger sugary flavor, ideal for desserts or dishes that require a lot of sweetness.

To prepare the syrup, simply combine the sugar and water in a large pot and bring it to a boil, stirring to dissolve the sugar. Let the syrup simmer for a few minutes before using it to pack the pineapple.

Step 4: Pack the Jars

Start by sterilizing the jars to ensure that they are clean and free of bacteria. You can sterilize jars by placing them in a hot water bath or running them through a dishwasher. Be sure to sterilize your canning lids as well by boiling them briefly.

Place the prepared pineapple pieces into the sterilized jars, leaving about half an inch of headspace at the top. This extra space is important for expansion during the canning process. Once the jars are filled with pineapple, carefully pour the syrup over the fruit until the jars are full, making sure the pineapple is completely submerged.

Use a clean utensil to remove any air bubbles by gently tapping the jar on the counter or running a spatula along the sides. After this, wipe the rims of the jars with a clean cloth to remove any syrup or residue, ensuring a good seal.

Step 5: Seal the Jars

Place the sterilized lids onto the jars and screw the bands on tightly, but not too tightly. The bands should be secure but not overly tightened, as the air needs to escape during the canning process.

Canning Pineapple: The Process

Once the jars are sealed, it’s time to process the pineapple in a water bath to complete the canning process. The water bath method is effective for canning pineapple and other fruits, as it helps to kill bacteria and prevent spoilage.

Step 1: Boil the Water Bath

Fill a large pot or canner with enough water to cover the jars by at least 1 to 2 inches. Bring the water to a boil, making sure the water is hot but not yet boiling when you add the jars.

Step 2: Process the Jars

Carefully lower the jars into the boiling water bath using a jar lifter or tongs. Be sure the jars are completely submerged and that they are not touching each other. Cover the pot and allow the jars to process for about 15 to 20 minutes for pint-sized jars, or 20 to 25 minutes for quart-sized jars. This ensures the pineapple is properly heated and sealed.

Step 3: Check the Seals

After processing, remove the jars from the water bath and set them on a clean towel or cooling rack. Allow the jars to cool completely for several hours or overnight. Once the jars are cool, check the seals by pressing on the center of the lid. If the lid doesn’t pop back, it’s sealed. If the lid pops up and down, the jar hasn’t sealed properly, and you should refrigerate it or reprocess the jar.

Step 4: Store the Jars

Once the jars are sealed, label them with the date and store them in a cool, dark place. Properly canned pineapple can last for up to a year. Avoid placing the jars in direct sunlight or near heat sources, as this can degrade the quality of the fruit over time.

Using Canned Pineapple in Sweet Dishes

Now that your pineapple is properly canned and preserved, it’s ready for use in a variety of sweet dishes. Here are some ideas to incorporate canned pineapple into your cooking:

Pineapple Upside-Down Cake

Canned pineapple is a classic ingredient for making pineapple upside-down cake. Simply layer the fruit in the bottom of a cake pan, pour your cake batter over the top, and bake. The result is a moist, sweet, and tangy cake with caramelized pineapple.

Pineapple Tarts

Canned pineapple can also be used as a filling for pineapple tarts, a delicious and flaky pastry often enjoyed as a treat during festive seasons. The sweet, soft pineapple pairs perfectly with buttery tart dough.

Fruit Salads

Add canned pineapple to your fruit salad mix for a juicy and flavorful element. Pineapple complements other fruits like strawberries, mangoes, and grapes, adding both sweetness and a refreshing tang.

Smoothies and Beverages

Use canned pineapple in smoothies or tropical beverages. Combine it with other fruits like banana, mango, or coconut for a refreshing and nutritious drink.

Final Thoughts

Canning pineapple is an excellent way to preserve this tropical fruit for future use in your sweet dishes. By following the steps outlined in this guide, you can enjoy the bright, sweet taste of pineapple all year long. Whether you’re adding it to cakes, fruit salads, or tarts, preserved pineapple adds a delicious touch to your desserts. Happy canning!
