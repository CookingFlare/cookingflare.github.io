The Tale of the Dumpling: A Journey Through Chinese Culinary History

Dumplings, known as “jiaozi” in Chinese, are a fundamental component of Chinese cuisine, with their history dating back to ancient times. Legend has it that these delectable foodstuffs were first invented by the hospitals of the Song Dynasty (960-1279 AD) when they needed to serve a cheap and nutritious food for the common people. These portable and filling treats are not only a testament to the rich past and cultural heritage of Chinese gastronomy but can also be prepared effortlessly in your very own kitchen.

Easy Pan-Fried Pork Dumplings

Ingredients:

  • 1 cup all-purpose flour
  • 1/4 cup warm water
  • 1/2 pound ground pork
  • 1 tablespoon finely chopped ginger
  • 1/2 cup finely chopped Napa cabbage
  • 1/4 cup finely chopped green onion
  • 1/4 cup finely chopped wood ears (mushrooms)
  • 1/4 cup finely chopped morning glory
  • 3 tablespoons soy sauce
  • 1 tablespoon sesame oil
  • 1 tablespoon Shaoxing wine (or dry sherry)
  • Salt & pepper, to taste
  • Dumpling wrappers

Instructions:

  1. Make the dumpling wrappers: Combine flour and warm water, and knead until the dough becomes smooth. Roll into a log, then cut into smaller portions. Shape each portion into a flat, round disc, and use a rolling pin to create round wrappers of about 3-4 inches in diameter.
  2. Mix ground pork, ginger, Napa cabbage, green onion, and wood ears in a large bowl. Add soy sauce, sesame oil, Shaoxing wine, salt, and pepper, then stir to combine thoroughly. Set aside.
  3. Lay a dumpling wrapper flat on a clean surface. Place a spoonful of filling in the center. Wet the edges of the wrapper, fold the dough in half, and crimp the edges to seal.
  4. Heat a non-stick pan over medium heat, then coat it with a thin layer of oil. Place dumplings in the pan with the folded side up, working in batches if necessary. Let the dumplings cook for a few minutes, until the bottoms are golden brown.
  5. Pour in a small amount of water to create steam, around half an inch deep. Cover the pan and let the dumplings cook for about 8-10 minutes or until the water has evaporated and the dumplings are cooked through.
  6. Enjoy your pan-fried pork dumplings with a side of ginger sauce or hoisin sauce.

Unraveling the Delicate Threads of Dan Dan Noodles

Originating in China’s Sichuan Province, dan dan noodles are a delectable dish that represents the region’s love for fiery flavors and bold textures. While the dish traditionally involves a cumbersome ladder of serving steps, mastering it at home is surprisingly straightforward.

Dan Dan Noodles

Ingredients:

  • 2 1/2 cups fresh rice noodles or flat spaghetti
  • 3 tablespoons soybean paste (Chiu Chow or tantanmen)
  • 1/3 cup crushed peanuts
  • 1 tablespoon sesame paste (tahini)
  • 1 teaspoon chili oil
  • 1/4 cup black vinegar
  • 2 minced cloves of garlic
  • 1/4 cup minced green onions
  • Crispy wonton strips

Instructions:

  1. Cook the rice noodles according to package instructions. Drain and rinse under cold water.
  2. In a mixing bowl, whisk together the soybean paste, sesame paste, and chili oil. Gradually add black vinegar, then whisk until well blended.
  3. Heat a small quantity of oil in a wok or large pan over medium heat. Toss in crushed peanuts and slightly roast until fragrant, then transfer to the bowl of sauce.
  4. Combine the noodles, garlic, and green onions, then pour over the sauce. Toss until the noodles are evenly coated. Sprinkle with crispy wonton strips and serve immediately.
  5. Savor the taste by adding cilantro, crushed red pepper, or extra chili oil as required!

Unveiling the Silken Layers of Mapo Tofu: Imitation, Not Temporary

Mapo tofu, originated from the Sichuan Province, is an iconic dish that exemplifies the region’s flavorful and fiery taste buds. It comprises a soft and silky tofu blanketing a rich and spicy sauce, with tender chunks of ground pork. The key to its irresistible taste lies in its fusion of smooth textures and fiery spices.

Mapo Tofu

Ingredients:

  • 1/2 pound firm tofu
  • 1/2 pound ground pork
  • 2 tablespoons finely chopped ginger
  • 2 minced cloves of garlic
  • 2 tablespoons fermented black beans, rinsed and mashed
  • 1 tablespoon chili oil, plus more for serving
  • 3 tablespoons Shaoxing wine (or dry sherry)
  • 1 tablespoon soy sauce
  • 1/4 teaspoon Sichuan pepper, toasted
  • 1/4 teaspoon sugar

Instructions:

  1. Cut tofu into small cubes and set aside.
  2. In a pan, heat a tablespoon of oil over medium heat, then add ginger, garlic, and black beans, stirring until aromatic.
  3. Add ground pork and cook until it becomes evenly browned, breaking it up with a wooden spoon.
  4. Mix in the Shaoxing wine, soy sauce, Sichuan pepper, sugar, and chili oil. Continue cooking for an additional two minutes.
  5. Gently fold the tofu cubes into the sauce until they are coated and heated through. Adjust the seasoning as needed and serve immediately, garnishing with additional chili oil if desired.
