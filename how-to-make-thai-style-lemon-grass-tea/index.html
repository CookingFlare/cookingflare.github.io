How to Make Thai Style Lemon Grass Tea


Few beverages capture the unique charm of Southeast Asia quite like Thai style lemongrass tea. This fragrant and soothing drink combines the bright, citrusy notes of fresh lemongrass with subtle herbal undertones, offering a comforting and revitalizing experience. Whether you want a refreshing iced beverage to cool down on a hot day or a warm soothing cup to relax in the evening, this tea can be tailored to fit your mood perfectly.

In this comprehensive guide, we’ll explore the origins and benefits of Thai lemongrass tea, how to prepare it with fresh and dried ingredients, the best ways to serve it, and some creative variations for you to experiment with. By the end of this post, you’ll be equipped to craft this delightful herbal tea right in your kitchen, impress your guests, and perhaps even find a new daily ritual.

Origins and Cultural Significance

Lemongrass (Cymbopogon citratus) is a staple herb in Thai cooking and traditional medicine. Its refreshing aroma and pleasant taste have made it a go-to ingredient not only for soups and curries but also for teas that help cool the body and aid digestion. Thai style lemongrass tea is a testament to the simplicity and elegance of Southeast Asian herbal drinks, where the emphasis lies on natural flavors and health benefits.

Beyond its culinary uses, lemongrass tea is often served in Thai households to calm the mind and is appreciated for its anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties. The tradition of brewing lemongrass into tea likely originated from a blend of folk medicine and everyday culinary practice. Today, lemongrass tea enjoys global popularity as a caffeine-free alternative packed with refreshing zest.

Health Benefits of Lemongrass Tea

The popularity of lemongrass tea extends far beyond its flavor. Herbalists and health enthusiasts alike value it for several reasons:

  • Rich in antioxidants: Lemongrass contains compounds that help neutralize free radicals, which can damage cells.
  • Supports digestion: Drinking lemongrass tea after meals can soothe indigestion, bloating, and stomach discomfort.
  • Anti-inflammatory properties: The natural oils in lemongrass may reduce inflammation and joint pain.
  • Calming effect: Sipping warm lemongrass tea helps reduce anxiety and promote restful sleep, making it an excellent nighttime drink.
  • Detoxifying: Traditionally, lemongrass is believed to flush harmful toxins from the body.

Ingredients and Tools Needed

Before diving into the brewing process, let’s gather the ingredients and tools you’ll need:

Ingredients:

  • Fresh lemongrass stalks: 3 to 4 medium stalks per liter of tea
  • Water: 4 cups (1 liter)
  • Optional natural sweetener: honey, rock sugar, or palm sugar
  • Optional aromatics: fresh ginger slices, lime leaves, or a small cinnamon stick
  • Optional citrus slices: lime or lemon wedges for garnish

Tools:

  • A sharp knife and cutting board
  • A medium saucepan or pot
  • Fine mesh strainer or tea infuser
  • Teapot or serving jug
  • Cups or glasses for serving

Step-by-Step Guide to Making Authentic Thai Lemongrass Tea

Step 1: Preparing the Lemongrass

The first step is to prep the lemongrass properly to maximize its flavor:

  • Rinse the stalks thoroughly under running water to remove dirt.
  • Trim off the root ends and tough upper leaves.
  • Peel away any dry outer layers to reveal the fresh pale core.
  • Using the back of your knife or a rolling pin, gently bruise or smash the stalks to release their aromatic oils.
  • Cut them into 2-inch pieces to expose more surface area for infusion.

Step 2: Boiling the Lemongrass

Add 4 cups of water to your pot and bring it to a gentle boil.

  • Toss in the prepared lemongrass pieces.
  • If using additional aromatics like ginger, lime leaves, or cinnamon, add them now.
  • Allow the mixture to boil for 3-5 minutes.
  • Reduce heat and simmer for an additional 10-15 minutes to fully extract the flavors.

Simmering is key to balancing the citrus and herbal notes without releasing any bitterness.

Step 3: Straining the Tea

After simmering, remove the pot from heat.

  • Use a fine mesh strainer or cheesecloth to strain the liquid into a teapot or large jug.
  • Press gently on the lemongrass pieces to squeeze out maximum flavor but avoid forcing bitterness through.

You should have a golden-yellow, clear liquid with a vibrant lemony aroma.

Step 4: Sweetening and Serving

Thai lemongrass tea is traditionally enjoyed with a bit of sweetness.

  • Stir in your sweetener of choice while the tea is still warm. Thai recipes typically use rock sugar or palm sugar, but honey or white sugar work well too.
  • Taste and adjust the sweetness to your preference.

You can drink this tea hot or chilled:

  • To serve hot: Pour into cups and garnish with a slice of lime or lemon.
  • To serve iced: Let the tea cool first, then refrigerate for at least an hour. Serve over ice with fresh lime wedges.

Tips for Best Results

  • Fresh lemongrass yields the most vibrant flavor, but dried lemongrass can be substituted in a pinch. Use about 2 tablespoons of dried lemongrass per liter of water.
  • Avoid boiling the tea too long, as over-extraction can lead to bitterness.
  • For a creamier twist, add a splash of coconut milk or evaporated milk before serving.
  • Keep leftover tea refrigerated and consume within 2 days to maintain freshness.

Creative Variations to Enhance Your Lemongrass Tea Experience

The beauty of this tea is its versatility. Below are a few ideas to elevate the basic recipe:

1. Lemongrass and Ginger Glow

Add freshly sliced ginger to the simmering pot for a warming spicy kick that also boosts digestion.

2. Citrus Lemongrass Refresher

Stir in fresh lime or lemon juice and a sprig of mint after straining. Serve chilled over ice for a summer thirst-quencher.

3. Floral Blossom Blend

Add dried jasmine or chrysanthemum flowers to the brew for a fragrant floral note that pairs beautifully with lemongrass.

4. Lemongrass Chai Twist

Combine lemongrass with traditional chai spices like cardamom, cloves, and cinnamon for a unique fusion tea.

5. Coconut Lemongrass Cooler

Mix brewed lemongrass tea with coconut water and a dash of lime juice for a hydrating and tropical flavored beverage.

Pairing Thai Lemongrass Tea With Food

Lemongrass tea’s bright and refreshing character complements a wide range of dishes:

  • Spicy Thai curries: The tea cools the palate after rich, spicy bites.
  • Grilled seafood or chicken: Its citrus notes contrast deliciously with charred flavors.
  • Fresh spring rolls: The herbal qualities match well with light, fresh ingredients.
  • Light desserts: Pair with coconut pudding or mango sticky rice to round out a meal.

Storing and Reheating Lemongrass Tea

If you make a larger batch, store the tea in an airtight container in the fridge for up to two days. To reheat, warm gently on the stove—avoid boiling again to keep the flavor delicate.

Final Thoughts

Making Thai style lemongrass tea is an easy and rewarding process that invites you to slow down and appreciate the subtle complexity of simple ingredients. Its refreshing aroma and layered flavor make it endlessly versatile, while the myriad health benefits add to its appeal. By following the steps and experimenting with variations, you can bring a piece of Thai herbal tradition into your home and enjoy this soothing beverage anytime you crave a taste of sunshine and calm in a cup.

Feel free to share your own lemongrass tea adventures or any special twists you’ve discovered. After all, good tea and good conversation are the perfect companions.

Enjoy your journey into the world of Thai lemongrass tea—may every sip bring refreshment and warmth!
