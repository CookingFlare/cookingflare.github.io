The Secret to Making Perfect Falafel at Home


Falafel is a beloved dish that has earned its place in the hearts of food lovers worldwide. Whether enjoyed as a sandwich, a salad topping, or as part of a mezze spread, falafel offers a delightful combination of crispy exterior and a flavorful, tender interior. But achieving the perfect falafel at home can be a challenge. If you’ve ever tried to make it and ended up with falafel that falls apart, is too dense, or lacks flavor, you’re not alone. The key to making falafel just right lies in understanding the ingredients, the technique, and the little tips and tricks that elevate this dish from good to extraordinary. In this post, we’ll explore everything you need to know to make falafel that is crisp on the outside, soft on the inside, and bursting with flavor.

What is Falafel?

Falafel is a deep-fried ball or patty made from ground chickpeas (or fava beans) mixed with herbs and spices. It’s commonly served in a pita or flatbread with fresh vegetables and tahini sauce. The origins of falafel are debated, with some attributing it to Egypt, where it was traditionally made with fava beans, while others trace it back to the Levant region, where chickpeas are often used. Regardless of its origins, falafel has become an international favorite, beloved for its flavor, versatility, and satisfying texture.

The Secret to Perfect Falafel: Ingredients Matter

To make falafel at home, you need a few basic ingredients. But it’s not just about gathering the right items—it’s about knowing how to use them properly. Here’s a breakdown of the key ingredients you’ll need:

1. Chickpeas (Garbanzo Beans)

The star of the show is the chickpea. It provides the base for the falafel mixture and contributes to its characteristic flavor and texture. The most important tip when making falafel is to use dried chickpeas, not canned ones. Canned chickpeas are already too soft and will result in falafel that’s mushy and prone to falling apart.

Soak the dried chickpeas overnight in plenty of water. This step helps them soften and become the perfect texture for blending into the falafel dough. You should aim to soak your chickpeas for at least 12 hours.

2. Fresh Herbs

Fresh herbs are a key ingredient for a vibrant, aromatic falafel. The classic herbs used in falafel are parsley and cilantro (coriander). These herbs give the falafel its fresh, grassy flavor that balances the earthiness of the chickpeas. You can use a mixture of both or go with your personal preference. Some variations also include dill or mint.

3. Onions and Garlic

Onions and garlic provide additional flavor depth to the falafel. The onions add sweetness, while the garlic infuses the mixture with its signature punch. Use raw onions, finely chopped, as they will blend better into the falafel dough.

4. Spices and Seasonings

The seasoning is what brings your falafel to life. Common spices used in falafel include cumin, coriander, and paprika. Cumin adds a warm, earthy note, while coriander gives a slightly citrusy flavor. A pinch of cayenne pepper or chili powder can add a bit of heat if you like your falafel on the spicier side. Salt and black pepper are essential to balance all the flavors.

5. Baking Soda

Baking soda is a small but important ingredient in falafel. When mixed into the falafel dough, it helps create a light and airy texture by giving the mixture a slight rise as it fries. This is particularly important to ensure that your falafel has the right crunch on the outside without being too dense inside.

6. Flour or Breadcrumbs

To bind the mixture together and prevent it from falling apart when frying, you will need a little flour. Chickpea flour, all-purpose flour, or even breadcrumbs work well. Use just enough to hold the dough together, but don’t overdo it—too much flour can make the falafel heavy.

The Importance of Proper Technique

Once you’ve gathered your ingredients, it’s time to turn them into perfect falafel. Technique plays a huge role in the final product. Here’s how you can master the falafel-making process:

1. Soaking the Chickpeas

As mentioned earlier, soak your dried chickpeas overnight. This is a crucial step to achieve the right texture. If you’re short on time, avoid using canned chickpeas, as they won’t provide the same consistency. The dried chickpeas should absorb enough water to soften and plump up, allowing them to blend into a smooth mixture.

2. Blending the Mixture

The next step is blending the chickpeas with the other ingredients. This is where a food processor comes in handy. Place the soaked chickpeas, herbs, onions, garlic, spices, and a little flour or breadcrumbs in the food processor. Pulse the ingredients until the mixture becomes coarse and crumbly, with small pieces of chickpea visible. Be careful not to over-process the mixture—if it turns into a paste, the falafel will be dense and mushy. The goal is to achieve a consistency that holds together when shaped into balls or patties but is not too wet.

If the mixture feels too loose or sticky, add a little more flour. Conversely, if it feels too dry or crumbly, add a splash of water to bring it together.

3. Resting the Mixture

Once you’ve blended the ingredients, it’s important to let the falafel dough rest for at least 30 minutes in the fridge. This resting period allows the flavors to meld together and helps the dough firm up, making it easier to shape. It also helps the baking soda activate, ensuring that your falafel will fry up light and crispy.

4. Shaping the Falafel

When it’s time to shape the falafel, use your hands to form small balls or patties, about the size of a walnut. The key to perfect falafel is not to pack the mixture too tightly. If you compact the dough too much, your falafel will be dense instead of light. A gentle touch will ensure a crispy exterior with a fluffy interior.

Some people use a falafel scoop or spoon to make uniform balls, but you can also shape them with your hands. Aim for a consistent size to ensure even frying.

5. Frying the Falafel

Falafel is traditionally deep-fried in hot oil, but if you prefer a lighter option, you can shallow fry them in a pan. Heat the oil in a deep pot or skillet to around 350°F (175°C). To test if the oil is hot enough, drop a small piece of the falafel mixture into the oil—if it sizzles and rises to the surface, the oil is ready.

Carefully lower the falafel into the hot oil in batches. Don’t overcrowd the pan, as this can lower the temperature of the oil and result in soggy falafel. Fry the falafel for about 3-4 minutes per side, or until they are golden brown and crispy. Use a slotted spoon to remove the falafel from the oil and place them on a paper towel-lined plate to drain any excess oil.

6. Serving and Enjoying

Now that your falafel is ready, it’s time to serve! The best way to enjoy falafel is in a pita or flatbread with fresh vegetables such as tomatoes, cucumbers, and lettuce. Drizzle with tahini sauce or garlic sauce for an extra burst of flavor. You can also serve them as part of a mezze platter with hummus, baba ganoush, and tabbouleh.

Falafel can also be enjoyed on its own as a snack or appetizer. Its crispy exterior and flavorful interior make it a perfect treat for sharing with friends and family.

Tips for Perfect Falafel Every Time

  • Use dried chickpeas: Canned chickpeas make falafel too soft and likely to fall apart during frying. Always use dried chickpeas and soak them overnight.
  • Don’t over-process the mixture: The texture should be coarse and chunky, not smooth and paste-like.
  • Rest the dough: Allow the falafel dough to chill in the fridge for at least 30 minutes to firm up and allow the flavors to develop.
  • Fry at the right temperature: Make sure the oil is hot enough before frying. If the oil is too cool, the falafel will absorb too much oil and become greasy.
  • Keep the falafel light: Avoid pressing the dough too firmly when shaping. A light touch ensures a crispy crust and fluffy interior.

Variations on Traditional Falafel

While the classic falafel made with chickpeas is delicious, you can also experiment with different variations to suit your tastes. Some people add fava beans, which give the falafel a slightly different texture and flavor. You can also add a variety of herbs or spices, like mint, dill, or sumac, to put your own twist on the recipe.

Baked Falafel

For a healthier version of falafel, you can bake them instead of frying. Preheat your oven to 400°F (200°C) and place the shaped falafel on a baking sheet lined with parchment paper. Lightly brush or spray the falafel with olive oil and bake for 20-25 minutes, flipping halfway through. While baked falafel won’t have the same crispy exterior as fried ones, they can still be tasty and satisfying.

Air-Fried Falafel

If you own an air fryer, it’s a great option for making falafel with less oil. Preheat the air fryer to 375°F (190°C). Arrange the falafel in a single layer in the air fryer basket, spray lightly with olive oil, and cook for 12-15 minutes, shaking the basket halfway through.

Conclusion

Making perfect falafel at home requires a bit of patience and technique, but the results are worth it. By using high-quality ingredients, paying attention to the texture of your falafel mixture, and following a few simple tips, you can create falafel that’s crisp, flavorful, and satisfying. Whether you serve them in pita with tahini sauce or enjoy them as part of a mezze platter, homemade falafel is a dish that will impress your family and friends. So roll up your sleeves, gather your ingredients, and get ready to enjoy the delicious world of falafel from the comfort of your own kitchen!
