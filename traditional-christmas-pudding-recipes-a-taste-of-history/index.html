Traditional Christmas Pudding Recipes: A Taste of History


The rich, spicy aroma of a Christmas pudding, steaming hot from the kitchen, is a festive tradition that has been passed down for centuries. The history of Christmas pudding is deeply intertwined with the celebration of Christmas itself, and the dish has evolved in numerous ways to become the iconic dessert it is today. In this blog post, we will take a deep dive into the origins of Christmas pudding, explore its traditional recipes, and offer tips for preparing this festive treat for your holiday celebrations.

A Brief History of Christmas Pudding

Christmas pudding, also known as plum pudding or simply Christmas dessert, has a long and storied history that dates back to medieval England. While the exact origins are debated, it is widely believed that the pudding began as a savory dish in the early Middle Ages, consisting of a mixture of meat, grains, and spices. Over time, it transformed into a sweet dessert that we recognize today, with the addition of fruits, suet, sugar, and treacle.

The earliest known reference to Christmas pudding appears in the 14th century, in a manuscript called The Forme of Cury, which contained recipes for various medieval dishes. However, it wasn’t until the 17th century that the dish began to take on its modern form. During this period, the recipe evolved into a sweet, spiced dish that was steamed rather than boiled.

The transformation of the Christmas pudding into the iconic dessert we know today was solidified in the 18th and 19th centuries. The dish became deeply associated with Christmas celebrations, with traditions such as stirring the pudding with a wooden spoon while making a wish, placing a coin inside for good luck, and serving the pudding with a sprig of holly on top. These customs continue to be enjoyed by many families today.

The Ingredients of Traditional Christmas Pudding

One of the reasons Christmas pudding is so beloved is its rich, complex flavor. The key ingredients in a traditional Christmas pudding are simple, but when combined, they create a deeply satisfying and indulgent dessert. Letâ€™s take a closer look at the typical ingredients:

  1. Dried Fruit: Currants, sultanas, raisins, and sometimes prunes or figs are used in the pudding. The fruit adds natural sweetness and moisture to the mixture, contributing to the pudding’s characteristic texture.
  2. Suet: Traditionally, suet (the hard fat from around the kidneys of cows or sheep) was used in the pudding to create a rich, dense texture. It gives the pudding its characteristic crumbly, moist texture and is essential for a traditional recipe.
  3. Flour: Plain flour or self-raising flour is used to bind the pudding ingredients together, creating structure.
  4. Sugar: Brown sugar is often used to add depth of flavor, along with treacle or golden syrup to enhance sweetness.
  5. Spices: Cinnamon, nutmeg, and allspice are the typical spices used to season the pudding, giving it its warming, festive flavor. Cloves may also be added for an extra kick.
  6. Eggs: Eggs help to bind the ingredients and provide richness to the texture.
  7. Milk or Beer: Some recipes call for milk, while others use dark beer or stout. The use of stout enhances the depth of flavor and adds richness to the pudding.
  8. Zest and Juice of Citrus Fruits: Lemon or orange zest is often added to provide a fresh, citrusy note that balances the richness of the suet and dried fruit.
  9. Nuts and Alcohol: Some versions of Christmas pudding contain chopped nuts, such as almonds or hazelnuts. A generous splash of brandy or rum is often added to enhance the flavor and make the pudding extra indulgent.

How to Make Traditional Christmas Pudding

Making Christmas pudding is a labor of love, but the result is a spectacular dessert that will become a centerpiece of your holiday celebration. The process is relatively straightforward but requires some time and patience, as the pudding needs to be steamed for several hours to achieve its signature texture.

Ingredients for Traditional Christmas Pudding

  • 1 cup currants
  • 1 cup raisins
  • 1/2 cup sultanas
  • 1/2 cup chopped dried figs (optional)
  • 1/4 cup mixed candied peel
  • 1/2 cup suet (vegetarian suet can be used if preferred)
  • 1 cup plain flour
  • 1 teaspoon baking powder
  • 1 teaspoon ground cinnamon
  • 1/2 teaspoon ground nutmeg
  • 1/4 teaspoon ground allspice
  • 1/4 teaspoon ground cloves (optional)
  • 1/2 cup brown sugar
  • 2 large eggs
  • 1/2 cup stout or dark beer
  • 1/2 cup milk
  • 2 tablespoons dark rum or brandy (optional)
  • Zest and juice of one lemon
  • Zest and juice of one orange
  • A pinch of salt

Instructions

  1. Prepare the Dry Ingredients: In a large mixing bowl, sift the flour, baking powder, and spices (cinnamon, nutmeg, allspice, cloves) together. Stir in the sugar, suet, dried fruit, candied peel, and any nuts you’re using.

  2. Add the Wet Ingredients: In a separate bowl, whisk the eggs, milk, stout (or dark beer), rum (or brandy), and citrus zest and juice. Add this wet mixture to the dry ingredients and stir well until everything is fully combined.

  3. Mix the Batter: The batter will be thick, but should hold together. Use a spoon or your hands to ensure that all the fruit and dry ingredients are well-coated with the liquid.

  4. Steam the Pudding: Grease a 2-liter (4-pint) pudding basin and spoon the batter into the basin, pressing it down gently. Cover the top of the pudding with a piece of parchment paper, followed by a piece of aluminum foil. Secure the foil tightly around the top of the basin with string, ensuring that the pudding is sealed properly.

  5. Steam for 6 Hours: Place the pudding in a large pot of simmering water. The water should come halfway up the side of the pudding basin. Cover the pot with a lid and steam the pudding for 6 hours, topping up the water as necessary. Itâ€™s essential to maintain a gentle simmer throughout the steaming process.

  6. Rest and Store: Once the pudding is fully cooked, remove it from the pot and allow it to cool. If you are not planning to serve it immediately, wrap the pudding in fresh parchment paper and foil and store it in a cool, dark place until Christmas Day. Traditional Christmas pudding is often made several weeks in advance to allow the flavors to mature.

  7. Reheat Before Serving: On Christmas Day, you can reheat the pudding by steaming it for an additional 2-3 hours, or by microwaving it if you’re in a hurry. If you choose to reheat it in the microwave, slice the pudding before reheating to ensure it heats evenly.

  8. Serving: To serve, turn the pudding out onto a warm serving plate. For extra festive flair, drizzle over some brandy or rum and carefully light it with a match just before serving, creating a spectacular flaming effect. Accompany the pudding with a dollop of brandy butter, fresh cream, or custard.

Tips for Making the Perfect Christmas Pudding

  1. Make it in Advance: The longer the pudding sits, the more the flavors have a chance to develop. Traditionally, Christmas pudding is made weeks or even months ahead of time. If you want to make your pudding ahead, simply store it in a cool, dry place, tightly wrapped.

  2. Choosing the Right Fruit: If youâ€™re new to making Christmas pudding, feel free to adjust the types of dried fruit according to your preferences. The combination of currants, raisins, and sultanas is classic, but figs, apricots, or dates can add a unique twist to the flavor.

  3. Adjusting the Sweetness: Some people prefer their Christmas pudding on the sweeter side, while others enjoy a more savory taste. You can adjust the amount of sugar according to your taste, but remember that the dried fruits will also contribute natural sweetness to the pudding.

  4. Steaming vs. Boiling: While steaming is the traditional method of cooking Christmas pudding, some modern recipes may suggest boiling or baking the pudding. However, steaming is the best way to ensure a moist and rich texture thatâ€™s perfect for the holidays.

  5. Make it a Family Tradition: Making Christmas pudding can be a fun family activity. Allow everyone to take turns stirring the mixture while making a wish, and donâ€™t forget to hide a coin (or other trinkets) inside the pudding for extra luck and fun!

Whether you follow the classic recipe to the letter or put your own twist on the ingredients, this festive dish is sure to delight everyone at the table.
