The Art of Dim Sum: Steamed Dumpling Recipes to Try


Dim sum, a traditional Cantonese culinary experience, is much more than just a meal; it’s a celebration of flavors, textures, and social interaction. The heart of dim sum lies in its diverse array of small dishes, with steamed dumplings taking center stage. In this blog post, we’ll delve into the art of crafting the perfect steamed dumplings, exploring various recipes that will tantalize your taste buds and impress your guests.

Understanding Dim Sum

Dim sum dates back to the Song Dynasty (960-1279 AD), where it originated as a roadside snack for travelers. Over the centuries, it evolved into a full dining experience, often enjoyed during brunch and accompanied by Chinese tea. The term “dim sum” translates to “touch the heart,” which reflects the small, bite-sized portions designed to satisfy without overwhelming.

Types of Steamed Dumplings

Before we dive into the recipes, it’s essential to understand the different types of steamed dumplings you can explore:

  1. Jiaozi: These are Chinese potstickers filled with meat and vegetables, typically pan-fried and then steamed.
  2. Shumai: Open-topped dumplings usually filled with pork and shrimp, often garnished with a pea on top.
  3. Har Gow: Translucent dumplings filled with shrimp and wrapped in a delicate rice flour skin.
  4. Xiao Long Bao: Soup dumplings filled with broth and meat, known for their delicate skin and rich flavor.
  5. Baozi: Steamed buns filled with a variety of ingredients, from meats to sweet pastes.

Recipe 1: Classic Pork and Shrimp Shumai

Ingredients

  • For the Filling:

    • 200g ground pork
    • 100g shrimp, peeled and chopped
    • 2 spring onions, finely chopped
    • 1 tablespoon soy sauce
    • 1 teaspoon sesame oil
    • 1 teaspoon cornstarch
    • Salt and pepper to taste

  • For the Wrapper:

    • 20 round dumpling wrappers
    • Extra flour for dusting

Instructions

  1. Prepare the Filling: In a bowl, combine the ground pork, chopped shrimp, spring onions, soy sauce, sesame oil, cornstarch, salt, and pepper. Mix until well combined.

  2. Assemble the Shumai: Take a dumpling wrapper and place about a tablespoon of filling in the center. Gently gather the edges and pinch them together, leaving the top open. Repeat until all the filling is used.

  3. Steam the Dumplings: Line a bamboo steamer with parchment paper. Place the shumai in the steamer, leaving space between each one. Steam over boiling water for about 8-10 minutes or until fully cooked.

  4. Serve: Enjoy the shumai hot, with soy sauce or chili oil for dipping.

Recipe 2: Har Gow (Shrimp Dumplings)

Ingredients

  • For the Dough:

    • 200g tapioca flour
    • 100g wheat starch
    • 1 tablespoon vegetable oil
    • 200ml hot water

  • For the Filling:

    • 250g shrimp, peeled and chopped
    • 1 teaspoon ginger, minced
    • 1 tablespoon bamboo shoots, finely chopped
    • 1 tablespoon soy sauce
    • 1 teaspoon sesame oil
    • Salt and pepper to taste

Instructions

  1. Make the Dough: In a mixing bowl, combine tapioca flour and wheat starch. Add vegetable oil and hot water gradually, mixing until a smooth dough forms. Knead for a few minutes until elastic. Let it rest for 30 minutes.

  2. Prepare the Filling: In another bowl, mix chopped shrimp, ginger, bamboo shoots, soy sauce, sesame oil, salt, and pepper.

  3. Form the Dumplings: Divide the dough into small balls. Roll each ball into a thin circle. Place a teaspoon of filling in the center and fold the dough over, pinching the edges to seal.

  4. Steam: Arrange the dumplings in a steamer lined with parchment paper. Steam for about 6-8 minutes.

  5. Serve: Serve the har gow with soy sauce or your favorite dipping sauce.

Recipe 3: Xiao Long Bao (Soup Dumplings)

Ingredients

  • For the Dough:

    • 300g all-purpose flour
    • 150ml water
    • A pinch of salt

  • For the Filling:

    • 300g ground pork
    • 2 tablespoons soy sauce
    • 1 tablespoon ginger, minced
    • 1 tablespoon scallions, finely chopped
    • 1 tablespoon sugar
    • 200ml chicken stock (gelatinized)

Instructions

  1. Prepare the Dough: In a bowl, mix flour and salt. Gradually add water until a dough forms. Knead until smooth, then cover and let it rest for at least 30 minutes.

  2. Make the Filling: In a bowl, combine ground pork, soy sauce, ginger, scallions, sugar, and gelled chicken stock. Mix well.

  3. Form the Dumplings: Divide the dough into small pieces. Roll each piece into a thin circle. Place a tablespoon of filling in the center and carefully pleat the edges, sealing the dumpling at the top.

  4. Steam: Place the dumplings in a steamer lined with parchment paper, making sure they are not touching. Steam for 10-12 minutes.

  5. Serve: Xiao Long Bao is best enjoyed hot. Serve with a dipping sauce made from soy sauce and rice vinegar.

Recipe 4: Vegetable Dumplings

Ingredients

  • For the Filling:

    • 100g shiitake mushrooms, minced
    • 100g cabbage, finely chopped
    • 50g carrots, grated
    • 2 cloves garlic, minced
    • 1 tablespoon soy sauce
    • 1 teaspoon sesame oil
    • Salt and pepper to taste

  • For the Wrapper:

    • 20 round dumpling wrappers
    • Extra flour for dusting

Instructions

  1. Prepare the Filling: In a skillet, heat a bit of oil and sautÃ© the garlic until fragrant. Add mushrooms, cabbage, and carrots. Cook until softened. Stir in soy sauce, sesame oil, salt, and pepper. Let it cool.

  2. Assemble the Dumplings: Take a dumpling wrapper and place about a tablespoon of filling in the center. Fold and seal the edges.

  3. Steam: Place the dumplings in a steamer lined with parchment paper and steam for about 10 minutes.

  4. Serve: Serve with a spicy dipping sauce or vinegar.

Tips for Perfect Dumplings

  • Dough Consistency: The key to a great dumpling is the dough. It should be smooth and elastic. If it feels sticky, add a little more flour.

  • Steaming: Ensure your steamer is well-prepared with enough water to create steam without boiling over. A bamboo steamer gives an authentic touch.

  • Filling Variations: Donâ€™t hesitate to experiment with fillings. You can use different meats, seafood, or vegetables based on your preference.

  • Serving Suggestions: Dim sum is all about sharing. Serve your dumplings with a variety of dipping sauces, such as soy sauce, chili oil, or vinegar, for a complete experience.

Conclusion

The art of making steamed dumplings is a beautiful blend of tradition, flavor, and creativity. By trying out these recipes, you can bring a piece of Cantonese culture into your kitchen. Whether it’s for a special occasion or a casual weekend meal, these dumplings are sure to impress and delight. So gather your ingredients, invite some friends over, and enjoy the delightful experience of dim sum at home!
