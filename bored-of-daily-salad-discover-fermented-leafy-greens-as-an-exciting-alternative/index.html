Bored of Daily Salad? Discover Fermented Leafy Greens as an Exciting Alternative


Recent years have seen plenty of us becoming salads aficionados. With the promising benefits of consuming an abundance of leafy greens, adding unique dressings and ingredients to keep it exciting has come natural. However, there’s one notion most of us haven’t dared to explore yet: fermenting our greens. Let’s embark on a new culinary adventure together and find out why fermented leafy greens might just be our new favorite culinary escape from the daily salad boredom.

It’s time for a small introduction to a world beyond the classic pickles. While fermented vegetables have been hot in culinary trends, tahini (!), the potential for fermenting leaves hasn’t quite made its way to the mainstream yet. A unique concept, right? Before we dive in, let’s first understand what fermentation is. It’s an ancient process of preserving food through intentional cultivation of beneficial microorganisms. In simpler terms, it’s innocently encouraging microbiomes to alter and preserve your food.

Why ferment leafy greens instead of eating them fresh? Some people argue that fermented greens are easier to digest, making essential nutrients such as vitamins and minerals a more accessible option for the human body. Furthermore, they have a distinguishably pleasant, earthy flavor. Fermenting also prolongs the shelf life of greens, which is great for some who crave a reliable source of fresh food without worrying about spoilage.

To explore the potential for fermented leafy greens, below are some entries essential to a successful first experience:

Korean Style Kimchi

Kimchi is Korea’s famous spicy fermented cabbage. It’s incredibly easy to whip up and adds a vibrant punch of taste to any meal. Here’s a simple recipe for you.

  • 1 head of napa cabbage
  • 2 tablespoons of salt
  • 2 cloves of garlic
  • 1 tablespoon of ginger
  • 1/4 cup of gochujang (a spicy chili paste)
  • 2 tablespoons of apple cider vinegar
  • 1/4 cup of fish sauce
  • 1 scallion

Directions:

  1. Slice the cabbage and soak in cold water mixed with salt for several hours.
  2. Sauté garlic and ginger, then mix with remaining ingredients (gochujang, apple cider vinegar, fish sauce, and chopped scallion).
  3. Once the cabbage is well soaked, drain and add the sauce. Make sure every slice is equally coated.
  4. Store the mixture in a jar and leave it for several days at room temperature. Once fermented to your taste, transfer it to the fridge to slow the fermentation.

Sauerkraut

A well-loved European addition to your table, sauerkraut, literally means ‘sour cabbage’ in German. Below is a tested recipe:

  • 2-3 heads of green cabbage
  • 4 tablespoons of sea salt
  • 1 teaspoon of caraway seeds
  • Optional: juniper berries, grated carrot

Directions:

  1. Shred the cabbage finely and place it in a large bowl. The finer the cabbage, the better the craut will be.
  2. Sprinkle the salt and caraway seeds (and any other additions) over the cabbage.
  3. Massage everything together to get the salt evenly distributed.
  4. Place the cabbage mixture into a jar, ensuring not packed too tightly.
  5. Weight it down using an object to keep the cabbage submerged (e.g., glass jar filled with water).

Fermenting sauerkraut takes around 3-5 days. It offers an incredible flavor profile and a prolonged shelf life.

Simple Dandelion Greens

Not an indoor gardener? Fear not! Forage for free from your greener neighborhood spots - dandelion greens, especially when fermented, offer a surprising and delicious addition to the usual salad suspects.

Ingredients:

  • A bunch of dandelion greens
  • 2 tablespoons of salt
  • 1 tablespoon of honey
  • 2 tablespoons of apple cider vinegar
  • 1 garlic clove
  • Optional: any saisonal herbs - fresh parsley, chive, etc.

Directions:

  1. Pluck the greens from the roots and make sure to give them a good rinse.
  2. Boil the greens for about two-minutes, strain them, and gently press them dry.
  3. Combine the greens, salt, honey, apple cider vinegar, minced garlic, and any herbs you like in a jar.
  4. Stir the mixture until everything is evenly distributed and submerged.
  5. Let it ferment for a few days, such as on your windowsill or area that showcases the jars outside.

Now that we’ve taken a deep dive into the wonderful world of fermented leafy greens, how excited are you to try new exciting twists on your daily greens consumption? It’s time to ditch the boredom and explore fermenting your leafy greens. Nevermind the salad singularity, the future lies in the world of fermented greens!
