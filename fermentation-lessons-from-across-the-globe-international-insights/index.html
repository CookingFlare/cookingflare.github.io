Fermentation Lessons from Across the Globe: International Insights


Fermentation, the process of transforming food through microorganisms, has been happening worldwide for centuries. It has been a staple of numerous cuisines, enriching the flavors and textures of a variety of dishes. Below, we dive into the rich history and international roots of fermentation and explore some delicious recipes that illustrate its beauty.

The Roots of Fermentation

Fermentation has been crucial in human history since ancient times. The root of this process can be traced back to years of trial and error, with people experimenting with different methods to preserve their food before the invention of refrigeration. Throughout history, different civilizations have utilized fermentation in their cuisine to enhance flavors, increase nutrient content, and create new food options.

The Chinese have been fermenting ingredients for over 2,000 years, as evidenced by the discovery of 2,000-year-old pickled vegetables from the Han dynasty. As a result, China’s fermented delicacies range from familiar options like pickled rice vinegar to unique foods like ‘douchi,’ fermented soybeans, and ‘Fermented Mustard Greens’ (‘zha cai’), a type of pickled Mustard Greens.

Japan’s ferments take on various forms like miso, umami-rich soybean paste, and ‘moto-ori,’ pickled green tea leaves that are typically used in sushi rolls.

Korea has an extensive range of ferments, with some popular options being kimchi, a spiced anchovy sauce (‘cheongjang’), and fermentation in clay pots (’nori-jjim’).

The Power of Fermentation

Fermentation is more than just the key to countless flavorful dishes. It packs a nutritional punch. As microorganisms break down food, they release various vitamins and enzymes, enhancing the food’s nutritious value. Plus, fermentation lowers phytic acid levels, improving the absorption of minerals like zinc and iron.

Moreover, fermented foods aid gut health by introducing beneficial bacteria into the gut microbiome, improving digestion and enhancing immunity. Fermentation also accounts for a lowered risk of obesity, diabetes, and heart disease thanks to the decrease in inflammatory markers.

International Ferment Recipes

South African ‘Umbuzi’

Hailing from South Africa, Umbuzi (om-bu-zi) is a fermented banana and plantain dish that’s truly unique. After peeling and slicing the banana/plantain, let the mixture ferment for a day and a half.

Ingredients:

  • 2 medium-sized overripe plantains or bananas
  • 2 tablespoons of vinegar
  • Salt to taste
  • 2-3 cloves of garlic, sliced
  • 1/2 teaspoon ground paprika
  • 1/2 teaspoon ground coriander
  • 1/2 teaspoon ground cumin
  • 2 tablespoons vegetable oil
  • 1 tablespoon finely chopped fresh cilantro

Instructions:

  1. Peel and slice your plantain or banana in a circular motion or into spiralized noodles.
  2. Place your slices into a large pot that can either be covered or be easily sealed.
  3. Add vinegar, garlic, paprika, coriander, cumin, salt, and oil to the pot. Mix well to combine.
  4. Time to ferment: Cover the pot and let it sit at room temperature for 24–36 hours.
  5. If desired, heat your pot over medium heat until your Umbuzi is cooked and piping hot.
  6. Garnish your Umbuzi with fresh cilantro and serve!

Ethiopian Injera

Injera is a creatively flat, spongy Ethiopian bread made from teff flour. It can be used as a utensil to scoop up stews, curries, and vegetables—a definite plus in those who love multifunctional food.

Ingredients:

  • 1 cup teff flour
  • 2 cups warm water
  • 1 teaspoon baking soda
  • 1/4 cup chickpea flour (Optional)
  • 1/2 teaspoon salt
  • 1/2 teaspoon sugar
  • 2 teaspoons fresh ginger, minced
  • 2 teaspoons fresh garlic, minced

Instructions:

  1. Mix teff flour, chickpea flour, salt, sugar, ginger, and garlic in a large mixing bowl.
  2. Gradually, add warm water while whisking to create a smooth batter.
  3. Heat your injera pan or skillet over a medium flame.
  4. Pour about 1/2 cup of batter into the pan and cook for about two minutes, until the bottom of the injera is golden brown.
  5. Flip and cook the other side for one minute.
  6. Serve your injera warm as a fabulous addition to your Ethiopian meal.

Fermentation isn’t limited to specific cultures or mindsets. It’s a universal process, a matter of tradition and adaptation, a beautiful concoction of flavor and nutrition, a worldwide tale of triumph over scarcity, bringing forth layers of taste, richness, and, of course, history.

So, whether you are interested in safeguarding your food for a longer time, seeking a new source of nutrition, or desiring to break into the world of fermentation, try out these tempting recipes and let your taste buds roam across the globe. Happy cooking!
