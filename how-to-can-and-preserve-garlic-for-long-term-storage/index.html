How to Can and Preserve Garlic for Long-Term Storage


Garlic is one of the most versatile and flavorful ingredients found in kitchens around the world. Its pungent aroma and bold flavor can elevate dishes from simple to extraordinary, making it a staple in many homes. However, fresh garlic can sometimes be hard to use up before it starts to sprout or spoil. Thankfully, there are several methods to preserve garlic for long-term storage, allowing you to enjoy its benefits all year round.

One of the best ways to preserve garlic is through canning. Canning allows you to store garlic in a way that maintains its flavor and texture for months or even years. In this post, we will dive into the various methods of canning and preserving garlic, along with tips and tricks to ensure its longevity.

Why Preserve Garlic?

Preserving garlic offers several benefits beyond extending its shelf life. Here are just a few reasons why you might consider preserving garlic at home:

  1. Maximize Freshness: By canning or preserving garlic, you can lock in its fresh taste, ensuring you always have ready-to-use garlic on hand, even during the off-season.
  2. Easy Meal Prep: Having preserved garlic available eliminates the need for peeling and chopping fresh cloves every time you cook, making meal prep faster and more convenient.
  3. Budget-Friendly: Buying garlic in bulk and preserving it can save you money in the long run, as fresh garlic tends to have a short shelf life.

Whether you’re a home gardener, a garlic enthusiast, or just someone looking for a practical way to preserve food, canning garlic is a great solution.

How to Can Garlic for Long-Term Storage

Before you begin canning garlic, it’s important to follow safe and proper techniques to ensure your garlic remains safe to eat. Garlic, like other low-acid foods, has the potential to harbor harmful bacteria such as Clostridium botulinum, which causes botulism. When garlic is preserved improperly, it can create an environment where these bacteria can thrive, so it’s essential to follow food safety guidelines closely.

There are two main methods for canning garlic: water bath canning and pressure canning. Pressure canning is the safer and more effective method for garlic, as it reaches a higher temperature that can destroy harmful bacteria.

What You’ll Need for Canning Garlic

To get started with canning garlic, you’ll need the following equipment:

  • Fresh garlic bulbs (the number depends on how much you want to preserve)
  • A large pot of water (for blanching)
  • A sharp knife or garlic press
  • A cutting board
  • Jars (quart or pint-sized canning jars)
  • Lids and rings (ensure they are in good condition)
  • A pressure canner
  • A jar lifter
  • A clean cloth
  • Vinegar (optional for flavoring)
  • Salt (optional for seasoning)

Step-by-Step Guide to Canning Garlic

1. Select and Prepare Your Garlic

Begin by selecting fresh garlic bulbs. You’ll want to use firm, mature garlic heads with no signs of mold, sprouting, or damage. The fresher the garlic, the better the final product will be.

Peel the garlic cloves, removing the outer papery skin. You can do this by gently pressing down on the cloves with the side of a knife to loosen the skin, or by simply using your fingers. Once the cloves are peeled, rinse them thoroughly in cool water.

2. Blanch the Garlic

Blanching garlic helps to soften the flavor and removes some of the strong bitterness that can sometimes develop in preserved garlic. Bring a pot of water to a boil, and add your peeled garlic cloves. Boil them for 2-3 minutes, then immediately transfer them to a bowl of ice water to stop the cooking process.

This step will also help with peeling the cloves more easily if you haven’t done so yet. After blanching, drain the garlic and set it aside.

3. Prepare the Jars

Before filling your jars, you must sterilize them to ensure that no bacteria will contaminate the garlic during the canning process. You can sterilize jars by boiling them in a large pot of water for 10-15 minutes or by running them through the dishwasher.

Once sterilized, place the jars on a clean towel or cloth to dry.

4. Pack the Jars

Once your garlic cloves are prepared and your jars are ready, it’s time to pack the jars. For the best results, pack the garlic tightly into the jars, but don’t overfill them. You want to ensure there’s some headspace (about 1 inch) at the top of each jar.

Optionally, you can add salt to the jars for extra flavor. A teaspoon of salt per quart jar is sufficient. Some people also like to add vinegar or herbs (such as rosemary or thyme) to infuse the garlic with additional flavors.

5. Pressure Can the Garlic

Fill your pressure canner with the appropriate amount of water, according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Once the water is in the canner, place the filled jars inside, ensuring they are not touching each other. Secure the lid and lock it in place.

Bring the pressure canner to the proper pressure, which is usually around 10-15 pounds of pressure. The time required to process garlic in the pressure canner is typically 40-45 minutes for pint jars and 50-55 minutes for quart jars. This extended time at high pressure ensures that all harmful bacteria are eliminated.

6. Let the Jars Cool

After processing, turn off the heat and allow the pressure canner to cool naturally. Once the pressure has been fully released, carefully remove the jars using a jar lifter and place them on a clean towel to cool.

You should hear a satisfying “pop” as the jars seal. The lids should be concave, indicating a proper seal. Let the jars cool completely before storing them.

7. Store the Canned Garlic

Once your garlic is canned, it’s time to store it. Keep the jars in a cool, dark place, such as a pantry or basement. Properly canned garlic should last for up to a year or longer, depending on the quality of the garlic and the canning process.

After opening a jar, garlic should be refrigerated and used within a few weeks for the best quality.

Additional Tips for Preserving Garlic

1. Garlic in Oil Caution

It’s important to note that garlic preserved in oil should never be processed through water bath canning due to the risk of botulism. Garlic in oil should always be stored in the refrigerator and used within a short period (usually a week or two).

If you wish to preserve garlic in oil, it’s recommended to freeze it rather than canning it. Simply blend the garlic cloves with oil and freeze the mixture in small portions.

2. Freezing Garlic

Freezing is another excellent way to preserve garlic. You can freeze whole cloves, chopped garlic, or garlic paste. To freeze garlic cloves, peel them and place them on a baking sheet. Once frozen solid, transfer the cloves to an airtight container or freezer bag.

Freezing garlic does not harm its flavor or texture significantly, and it’s a quick and convenient method for long-term storage.

3. Drying Garlic

Garlic can also be preserved by drying. You can use a dehydrator or an oven set to a low temperature (around 140°F or 60°C) to dry garlic cloves. Once dried, store them in an airtight container. Dried garlic can be rehydrated in water or used in powdered form for cooking.

4. Pickling Garlic

If you enjoy tangy flavors, pickling garlic is another option. After peeling your garlic cloves, place them in sterilized jars and cover them with a pickling brine (vinegar, water, salt, and optional spices). Process the jars using a water bath canning method for about 10 minutes. Pickled garlic can be enjoyed as a snack or used in recipes.

Conclusion

Canning and preserving garlic is a practical and rewarding way to keep this essential ingredient available throughout the year. Whether you choose to can it, freeze it, or dehydrate it, there are plenty of methods to ensure your garlic stays flavorful and usable for months to come. Remember to always follow proper food safety guidelines to preserve the garlic correctly and avoid any potential risks.

With these tips in mind, you can enjoy fresh garlic whenever you need it, and create meals that burst with flavor, even in the depths of winter. Happy canning!
