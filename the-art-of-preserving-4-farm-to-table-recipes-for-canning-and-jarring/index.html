The Art of Preserving: 4 Farm to Table Recipes for Canning and Jarring


Canning and jarring are not just practical methods of preserving food—they are an art. Through these age-old techniques, we can lock in the vibrant flavors of the harvest season, ensuring that the tastes of summer can be enjoyed year-round. Preserving food also empowers us to eat sustainably, reduce food waste, and enjoy farm-to-table goodness long after the harvest is over.

In this post, we explore four delightful recipes for canning and jarring, ranging from fruits to vegetables and everything in between. These farm-to-table creations will not only fill your pantry with the essence of the season but will also serve as the perfect way to elevate any dish with the bright, fresh flavors of nature.

1. Spicy Tomato Jam

This spicy tomato jam is a perfect blend of sweetness and heat, making it a versatile addition to your pantry. It’s great as a condiment for burgers, sandwiches, or even served with cheese and crackers. It’s also the ideal way to preserve your summer tomatoes, especially when you have a bumper crop of ripe, juicy tomatoes.

Ingredients:

  • 4 pounds of ripe tomatoes, peeled and chopped
  • 2 cups of sugar
  • 1 cup of red onion, finely chopped
  • 1 cup of apple cider vinegar
  • 2 cloves of garlic, minced
  • 2 tablespoons of finely grated ginger
  • 1 teaspoon of chili flakes (adjust based on heat preference)
  • 1 teaspoon of salt
  • 1/2 teaspoon of black pepper
  • 1/4 teaspoon of cinnamon
  • 1/4 teaspoon of ground cloves

Instructions:

  1. Prepare the tomatoes: Start by peeling the tomatoes. To do this, score an “X” on the bottom of each tomato, blanch them in boiling water for 30 seconds, and then transfer them to an ice bath. The skins will peel away easily. Chop the tomatoes into small pieces, discarding the cores.

  2. Combine the ingredients: In a large pot, combine the tomatoes, sugar, apple cider vinegar, red onion, garlic, ginger, chili flakes, salt, pepper, cinnamon, and cloves. Stir everything together, and bring it to a simmer over medium heat.

  3. Cook the jam: Allow the mixture to simmer gently for about 1 to 1.5 hours, stirring occasionally. The jam will thicken as the liquid evaporates, so be patient. You’ll know it’s done when it has a jam-like consistency.

  4. Jar the jam: While the jam is simmering, sterilize your jars by boiling them for 10 minutes. Once the jam is ready, ladle it into the hot, sterilized jars, leaving about 1/4 inch of headspace. Wipe the rims of the jars clean with a damp cloth, and seal with sterilized lids.

  5. Process the jars: To ensure a good seal, process the jars in a boiling water bath for 10 minutes. After processing, let the jars cool and check the seals. Properly sealed jars can be stored in a cool, dark place for up to a year.

This spicy tomato jam is an incredible way to preserve summer tomatoes and turn them into something truly special. Whether you spread it on your morning toast or pair it with roasted meats, this jam will bring a zesty burst of flavor to any meal.

2. Pickled Beets with Mustard Seeds

Pickling is an excellent way to preserve beets, one of the most nutritious vegetables available. This recipe combines the earthy sweetness of beets with the tangy, aromatic pickling brine, complemented by mustard seeds for an extra burst of flavor. These pickled beets make a perfect side dish, salad topping, or addition to charcuterie boards.

Ingredients:

  • 10 medium-sized beets
  • 1 1/2 cups of apple cider vinegar
  • 1/2 cup of water
  • 1/4 cup of sugar
  • 2 teaspoons of mustard seeds
  • 1/2 teaspoon of salt
  • 1/2 teaspoon of black peppercorns
  • 3 cloves of garlic, peeled
  • 2 sprigs of fresh thyme

Instructions:

  1. Cook the beets: Start by trimming the beet tops, leaving about 2 inches of the stems intact. Place the beets in a large pot, cover with water, and bring to a boil. Reduce the heat and simmer for about 45 minutes or until the beets are tender. Once cooked, remove the beets from the water and let them cool slightly. Peel the skins off using your fingers or a vegetable peeler.

  2. Prepare the pickling brine: In a medium saucepan, combine the apple cider vinegar, water, sugar, mustard seeds, salt, peppercorns, and garlic. Bring the mixture to a boil and then reduce the heat to a simmer. Allow the brine to cook for about 5 minutes to infuse the flavors.

  3. Slice the beets: Once the beets are cool enough to handle, slice them into 1/4-inch thick rounds or wedges, depending on your preference.

  4. Pack the jars: Sterilize your jars and pack the sliced beets tightly into each jar. Add a sprig of fresh thyme to each jar for a subtle herbal note.

  5. Pour the brine: Pour the hot brine over the beets, ensuring that they are completely covered. Leave about 1/2 inch of headspace at the top of the jar. Wipe the rims of the jars clean, seal with sterilized lids, and process in a boiling water bath for 10 minutes.

  6. Cool and store: Let the jars cool completely and check the seals. If sealed properly, store the jars in a cool, dark place for at least two weeks to allow the flavors to meld before enjoying.

Pickled beets with mustard seeds are a wonderful way to preserve the earthy taste of beets while adding layers of complexity with the mustard seed and brine. Whether enjoyed as a snack or incorporated into salads, these pickled beets will make your taste buds dance with delight.

3. Peach and Lavender Jam

Peach season is a time of abundance, and nothing quite captures the essence of summer like a jar of homemade peach jam. Infused with the delicate fragrance of lavender, this peach jam will not only brighten your mornings but will also make an extraordinary gift. It’s perfect on toast, in yogurt, or even swirled into desserts.

Ingredients:

  • 5 cups of peeled, pitted, and chopped fresh peaches (about 6-8 peaches)
  • 4 cups of sugar
  • 1/4 cup of fresh lemon juice
  • 2 tablespoons of dried lavender buds
  • 1/4 teaspoon of ground cinnamon
  • 1 packet of fruit pectin (follow the instructions on the package for measurements)

Instructions:

  1. Prepare the peaches: Begin by peeling and pitting the peaches. To peel them easily, blanch the peaches in boiling water for 1 minute, then transfer them to an ice bath. The skins will slip off effortlessly. Chop the peaches into small pieces and set them aside.

  2. Infuse the lavender: In a small saucepan, combine 1/4 cup of water and the dried lavender buds. Bring the water to a simmer and cook for about 5 minutes to infuse the lavender. Strain out the lavender and discard it.

  3. Cook the jam: In a large pot, combine the chopped peaches, lavender infusion, sugar, lemon juice, and cinnamon. Stir to combine and bring the mixture to a boil. Once boiling, add the fruit pectin and cook for 5-10 minutes until the jam reaches the desired consistency. You can test the jam by placing a spoonful on a chilled plate—if it wrinkles when you push it, it’s ready.

  4. Jar the jam: Sterilize your jars and ladle the hot jam into them, leaving about 1/4 inch of headspace. Wipe the rims clean and seal with sterilized lids.

  5. Process the jars: Process the jam jars in a boiling water bath for 10 minutes to ensure they are sealed properly. Allow the jars to cool, and check that the seals are intact.

Peach and lavender jam is a refined treat that pairs beautifully with both savory and sweet dishes. It’s a great way to preserve the fleeting flavor of peaches and add a touch of elegance to your pantry.

4. Carrot and Dill Pickles

Carrots are a surprisingly delightful addition to the world of pickling. The natural sweetness of carrots pairs wonderfully with the brine, and the dill brings a fresh, herbal note that enhances their flavor. These carrot and dill pickles are perfect as a crunchy snack or a refreshing side dish.

Ingredients:

  • 8 medium-sized carrots, peeled and cut into sticks
  • 1 cup of white vinegar
  • 1/2 cup of water
  • 1 tablespoon of sugar
  • 1 tablespoon of salt
  • 1 tablespoon of mustard seeds
  • 2 cloves of garlic, smashed
  • 4-5 sprigs of fresh dill
  • 1/2 teaspoon of red pepper flakes (optional)

Instructions:

  1. Prepare the carrots: Peel and cut the carrots into sticks or rounds. Ensure they are cut to fit your jars.

  2. Make the brine: In a saucepan, combine the vinegar, water, sugar, salt, mustard seeds, garlic, and red pepper flakes. Bring the mixture to a boil, stirring to dissolve the sugar and salt. Once it boils, remove it from the heat.

  3. Pack the jars: Sterilize your jars and pack the carrot sticks tightly into each jar, placing a sprig of fresh dill in each jar for added flavor.

  4. Pour the brine: Pour the hot brine over the carrots, making sure they are fully submerged. Leave about 1/2 inch of headspace at the top of the jar. Wipe the rims of the jars clean, seal with sterilized lids, and process in a boiling water bath for 10 minutes.

  5. Cool and store: Allow the jars to cool completely. Check the seals, and store the pickles in a cool, dark place. Let them sit for at least 2 weeks before enjoying so the flavors can develop.

Carrot and dill pickles bring a fresh, crunchy contrast to any meal. They’re the perfect accompaniment to sandwiches, charcuterie boards, or even as a stand-alone snack.

Canning and jarring are wonderful ways to preserve the bounty of the seasons and ensure that your pantry is stocked with vibrant, homemade goodies. These four recipes—from spicy tomato jam to carrot and dill pickles—offer a perfect introduction to the world of preserving. With a little patience and creativity, you can enjoy the farm-to-table experience all year long. Happy preserving!
