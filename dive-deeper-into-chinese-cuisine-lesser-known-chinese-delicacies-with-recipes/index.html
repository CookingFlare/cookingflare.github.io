Dive Deeper into Chinese Cuisine: Lesser-Known Chinese Delicacies with Recipes


Chinese cuisine is a vast and flavorful culinary world that often finds its highlight in famous dishes such as dumplings, Kung Pao chicken, and Peking duck. However, beneath the surface of the more renowned options lie lesser-known delicacies that boast unique flavors and histories. In this blog post, we invite you on a gastronomic adventure to uncover five dishes you may have overlooked. Each of these dishes comes with a simple and authentic recipe that will enable you to create mouthwatering meals in your own kitchen.

1. Yangzhou Fried Rice

The charm of Yangzhou fried rice is in its simplicity yet delightful taste of fresh ingredients. The dish originates from Yangzhou, a city in China’s Jiangsu Province, where many famous chefs have contributed to its refinement.

Ingredients:

  • 3 cups of cooked, cooled rice
  • 2 tablespoons of vegetable oil (divided)
  • 2 eggs, beaten
  • 4 ounces of Chinese sausage (Lap Cheung), sliced
  • 4 ounces of shrimp, peeled and deveined
  • 1/2 cup of frozen peas, thawed
  • 1/4 cup of diced onions
  • 2 tablespoons of soy sauce
  • 1 teaspoon of sesame oil
  • 1/4 teaspoon of pepper
  • 3 green onions, thinly sliced

Instructions:

  1. Heat 1 tablespoon of vegetable oil in a wok or a large skillet over medium heat. Add the beaten eggs and scramble until cooked. Remove from the wok and set aside.
  2. In the same wok, add the remaining 1 tablespoon of vegetable oil. Sauté the Chinese sausage until golden brown, and then add the shrimp to cook until pink.
  3. Add onions, peas, and cooked rice to the wok. Mix well and break up any clumps of rice.
  4. Add the scrambled eggs back into the wok, followed by soy sauce, sesame oil, pepper, and green onions. Mix thoroughly and cook for another 2-3 minutes.
  5. Serve hot, and enjoy the delightful harmony of flavors.

2. Beggar’s Chicken

Beggar’s chicken, or mangmu cai in Chinese, is a popular dish in China’s Guangdong province, particularly in the city of Foshan. It is a baked chicken stuffed with a medley of vegetables and spices.

Ingredients:

  • 1 whole chicken (3-4 pounds)
  • 2 tablespoons of soy sauce
  • 1 teaspoon of salt
  • 1/4 cup of hoisin sauce
  • 1/4 cup of rice wine
  • 1/4 cup of oyster sauce
  • 1 tablespoon of sugar
  • 1 tablespoon of five-spice powder
  • 1/2 cup of unsalted butter
  • 1/2 cup of chopped green onions
  • 1/4 cup of sliced ginger
  • 3 cloves of garlic, minced
  • 1/2 cup of chopped mushrooms
  • 1/2 cup of chopped bell peppers

Instructions:

  1. Mix hoisin sauce, soy sauce, rice wine, oyster sauce, and sugar in a bowl. Set aside.
  2. Combine five-spice powder, butter, green onions, ginger, garlic, mushrooms, and bell peppers in another bowl.
  3. Stir the stuffing mixture into the sauce mixture, ensuring a well-blended marinade.
  4. Clean the chicken inside and out, pat it dry, and rub it with salt inside and out.
  5. Stuff the chicken with the marinade mixture, ensuring it is evenly distributed.
  6. Wrap the chicken tightly in aluminum foil, and place it on a baking tray.
  7. Preheat the oven to 375°F (190°C) and bake the chicken for 1 hour.
  8. After 1 hour, remove the aluminum foil, increase the heat to 425°F (220°C), and bake for another 15 minutes or until the skin turns crispy and golden brown.

3. Spicy Fish Head

A dish originating from Sichuan cuisine, the spicy fish head features a bold, spicy flavor that is sure to invigorate the taste buds.

Ingredients:

  • 1 whole fish head, cleaned (1.5-2 pounds)
  • 1/2 cup of soybean oil
  • 2 tablespoons of Sichuan peppercorns
  • 4 cloves of garlic, minced
  • 1-inch piece of ginger, thinly sliced
  • 1/4 cup of fermented black beans, rinsed and drained
  • 2 tablespoons of chili bean paste (Doubanjiang)
  • 2 green onions, chopped
  • 1 lemon, cut into wedges
  • Salt, to taste

Instructions:

  1. In a wok or deep skillet, heat the soybean oil over medium-high heat. Add Sichuan peppercorns, garlic, ginger, black beans, and chili bean paste. Stir and cook until fragrant.
  2. Add the fish head to the wok, and season with salt and stir to coat the fish with the sauce.
  3. Cook the fish for 10 minutes or until the paste turns dark red and the fish is cooked through.
  4. Garnish with green onions, and serve with a lemon wedge on the side.

4. Sizzling Chuan Chuan Xiang (Mouth-Watering Skewers)

This exciting and interactive dish is originally from Shaanxi province. Diners cook their skewers using a portable stove, making this a fun communal experience.

Ingredients:

  • Various meats (pork belly, chicken, beef), cut into bite-sized pieces
  • Various vegetables (cabbage, mushrooms, bell peppers, onions)
  • 1 tablespoon of rice wine
  • 1 tablespoon of black vinegar
  • 1 tablespoon of sugar
  • 1/2 teaspoon of five-spice powder
  • 1 teaspoon of sesame oil
  • 2 green onions, chopped

Instructions:

  1. Mix rice wine, black vinegar, sugar, five-spice powder, and sesame oil in a bowl, creating a marinade.
  2. Marinate the meats in the mixture for at least an hour, preferably overnight.
  3. Skewer the marinated meats and vegetables, alternating between meat and vegetable.
  4. Heat the skewers over a stove or grill until the meat is cooked.
  5. Serve the skewers with a small bowl of remaining marinade for dipping.

5. Qingdao drunken chicken

Hailing from the coastal city of Qingdao, this dish tells a story of combining well-brewed beer with succulent chicken, creating a unique flavor profile.

Ingredients:

  • 1 whole chicken (3-4 pounds)
  • 1 cup of beer
  • 2 green onions, chopped
  • 1 tablespoon of salt
  • 1/2 cup of soy sauce
  • 2 tablespoons of sake
  • 2 bay leaves
  • 1 cinnamon stick
  • 1/2 teaspoon of white pepper
  • 2 cloves of garlic, minced
  • 4 star anise

Instructions:

  1. Mix beer, soy sauce, sake, green onions, salt, bay leaves, cinnamon stick, white pepper, garlic, and star anise in a large pot.
  2. Place the chicken in the pot and ensure it is submerged in the marinade. Refrigerate for 24 hours.
  3. Preheat the oven to 350°(F) (175°C). Remove the chicken from the marinade and pat it dry with paper towels.
  4. Season the chicken with a little white pepper and salt, and place it in a roasting pan.
  5. Bake the chicken for 1 hour or until the juices run clear and the skin is golden brown.

Now that you are equipped with the key ingredients and secret recipes, why not invite some friends over and embark on this culinary journey? Regardless of whether you’re a beginner or a seasoned chef, these lesser-known dishes will unlock a new realm of flavors in Chinese cuisine. Enjoy, and remember, the secrets of the kitchen await those who dare to explore.
