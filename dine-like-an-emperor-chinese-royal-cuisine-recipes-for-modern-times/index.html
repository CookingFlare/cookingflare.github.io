Dine Like an Emperor: Chinese Royal Cuisine Recipes for Modern Times


Chinese royal cuisine, also known as Guang-Dong cuisine, was once an essential part of the imperial dynasties, worn like a gem in the emperor’s crown. This sophisticated and opulent cuisine is a testament to the rich history and sophisticated palates that once thrived in China’s royal courts. Dine like an emperor with these magnificent recipes adapted for modern times:

  1. Empress’s Delicacies Dumplings A delicate yet sumptuous dish, this dumpling recipe is steeped in the essence of the Chinese royal lineage. It represents the elegance and poise of Empress Dowager Cixi, whose love for fine food was legendary.

Ingredients:

  • 250g ground pork
  • 1 minced scallion
  • 100g finely chopped dried shrimp
  • 1 tablespoon finely chopped ginger
  • 2 tablespoons soy sauce
  • 1 tablespoon sesame oil
  • Salt and pepper to taste
  • 250g dumpling wrappers
  • Chopped green onion for garnish

Instructions:

  1. In a large bowl, combine ground pork, scallion, dried shrimp, ginger, soy sauce, sesame oil, salt, and pepper. Mix well and set aside for 15 minutes to marinate.

  2. Lay out the dumpling wrappers on a flat surface. Place a small portion of the filling in the center of each wrapper.

  3. To fold the dumplings, bring the edges together, pinching well to seal. Repeat for the remaining wrappers and filling.

  4. Heat a large non-stick frying pan over medium heat, add 2 tablespoons of oil.

  5. Place the dumplings in the pan and cook for 2-3 minutes or until the bottoms turn golden brown. Add 2 tablespoons of water, then cover with a lid and steam for 5-6 minutes.

  6. Uncover the pan, allowing the remaining water to evaporate. Drizzle with sesame oil and garnish with green onion before serving.

  7. Lotus Seed Paste Chicken This dish, named after the royal symbol of the lotus, was enjoyed by the emperors as a symbol of abundance and prosperity. The lotus seeds paste adds a luxurious touch to this delightful chicken dish.

Ingredients:

  • 1 whole chicken, cut into pieces
  • 1 cup lotus seed paste
  • 2 tablespoons rice wine
  • 2 tablespoons soy sauce
  • 1 tablespoon sugar
  • 4 cloves garlic, minced
  • 2 tablespoons vegetable oil
  • 1 teaspoon Sichuan peppercorns (optional)

Instructions:

  1. Rinse the chicken pieces under cold water, then pat them dry with paper towels.

  2. Combine rice wine, soy sauce, sugar, and garlic in a bowl to create the marinade.

  3. Coat the chicken pieces with the marinade, and set aside for 30 minutes to marinate.

  4. Heat vegetable oil in a large wok or frying pan over medium-high heat. Add the chicken pieces and cook until golden brown.

  5. Reduce heat to low and add lotus seed paste, mixing well with the chicken to ensure the paste sticks to each piece. Cook over low heat for 10-12 minutes, or until the chicken is cooked through and the paste is heated through.

  6. For a touch of royal enhancement, sprinkle Sichuan peppercorns over the chicken just before serving.

  7. Roast Sucking Pig Tiger Style Steeped in tradition, the roaring concept of the “tiger style” evokes the ferocity and majesty the ancient emperors sought in their feasts. The roast sucking pig, traditionally served in the imperial courts, is now a symbol of wealth and social status.

Ingredients:

  • 1 whole sucking pig (about 6-8 kg)
  • 2 cup rice wine
  • 2 cup soy sauce
  • 1 cup honey
  • 1 cup hoisin sauce
  • 1 cup sesame oil
  • 1 cup Chinese five-spice powder
  • 5 star anise
  • 10 cloves garlic, minced
  • 2 bay leaves

Instructions:

  1. Preheat the oven to 200°C (390°F).
  2. Combine rice wine, soy sauce, honey, hoisin sauce, sesame oil, five-spice powder, star anise, garlic, and bay leaves in a large bowl to create the marinade.
  3. Clean the sucking pig and place it in a deep roasting pan. Pour the marinade over the pig, making sure every part of the pig is coated.
  4. Cover the pig loosely with aluminum foil and roast for 3 hours in the oven. Remove the foil for the last 30 minutes of cooking, allowing the pig to crisp up.
  5. Let the pig rest for 15 minutes before carving and serving. The exquisite marriage of flavors, both aromatic and succulent, is fit for an emperor.

Embrace the grandiosity, the opulence, and the meticulous finesse of Chinese royal cuisine by reviving these lost dynastic dishes, ensuring the memory of a thousand years of imperial culinary prowess smiled upon us lives on. These meals are a passage to the past, an elegant reminder of a time when gastronomy transcended the realm of sustenance, becoming an art form that propelled the Chinese emperors to the refinement only known to an emperor.

Adapt these royal recipes to your palates, enhance them with your creativity, and dine like an emperor in your very own domain. Bon appétit!
