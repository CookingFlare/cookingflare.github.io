The Sichuan Cuisine: Exploring the Spicy World of Chinese Recipes


Ah, Sichuan cuisine – the heart and soul of China’s gastronomic landscape. A vibrant mix of colors, textures, and flavors, known for its use of tongue-tingling Sichuan peppercorns and chili oils. In our quest for culinary adventure, let’s immerse ourselves in this delectable world by exploring some of the most celebrated dishes originating from the Sichuan province.

  1. Mapo Tofu (MAPAZHONG) For a hearty Yet healthy appetizer, what better start than Mapo Tofu, or ‘Pockmarked Mother Tofu’? This dish, as the name suggests, is from Mapo Village in Sichuan and boasts a tantalizing hot and numbing sensation.

Ingredients:

  • 400g firm tofu
  • 250g ground pork
  • 2 green onions
  • 2 garlic cloves
  • 1 piece of ginger
  • 2 tablespoons of Sichuan peppercorns
  • 1 tablespoon of fermented black beans
  • 1 tablespoon of chili bean paste
  • 2 tablespoons of soy sauce
  • 2 tablespoons of potato starch
  • 2 cups of water

Instructions:

  1. Dice the tofu into bite-sized cubes and set aside.

  2. In a wok, heat oil and add black beans,chi landlord, pepper and ginger, and sauté until fragrant.

  3. Add the pork and garlic, breaking down the meat and cooking until it’s no longer pink.

  4. Incorporate the tofu into the wok, and pour in the water. Bring to a boil.

  5. Mix everything together and add the soy sauce, adjust seasoning according to taste.

  6. Thicken the sauce with potato starch and serve with green onions on top, ready to be devoured.

  7. Kung Pao Chicken (Gongbao chicken) Sharing the name, not the history, with the famous Chinese General Zuo Zongtang, Kung Pao chicken is a burst of flavors and textures. This classic Sichuan dish showcases the delicate balance between the heat and the sweetness.

Ingredients:

  • 500g boneless chicken, cut into chunks
  • 1 cup of peanuts
  • 10 dried red chilies
  • 50g Sichuan preserved vegetable
  • 3 green onions
  • 3 cloves of garlic
  • 2 tablespoons of rice wine
  • 1 tablespoon of sugar
  • 2 tablespoons of soy sauce
  • 1 tablespoon of chili bean paste
  • 1 tablespoon of sesame oil
  • 1 cup of cornstarch
  • 3 cups of water

Instructions:

  1. Dry-fry the peanuts until golden brown, reserve for later use.

  2. Rinse the red chilies and soak in hot water for 30 minutes until softened, then drain and chop.

  3. In a bowl, gently toss the chicken pieces with rice wine and a pinch of salt, marinate for about 15 minutes.

  4. In a large bowl, mix together the sauce ingredients, soy sauce, sugar, and chili bean pastes.

  5. Heat a wok over high heat, then add oil, deep fry the chicken until golden brown, coat in cornstarch to crisp up, and set aside.

  6. In the same wok, add another splash of oil, sauté garlic, green onion, pepper, and chili until fragrant. Add the soaked dried chilies, Sichuan preserved vegetables and chili bean paste, stir fry for a bit.

  7. Add fried chicken into the wok, pour over the sauce, and stir until sauce is thickened.

  8. Serve hot, topped with peanuts, garnished with green onions, and drizzled with sesame oil.

  9. Dan Dan Noodles (Dandan Mian) A street food staple that’s both filling and robust, Dan Dan noodles are a perfect example of how the nomadic history of Sichuan cuisine influenced its development. This dish is packed with fragrant spices and textures.

Ingredients:

  • 400g noodles
  • 200g ground pork
  • 100g pickled mustard greens
  • 2 green onions
  • 1 piece of ginger
  • 2 tablespoons of Sichuan peppercorns
  • 1 tablespoon of fermented black beans
  • 1 tablespoon of chili bean paste
  • 2 tablespoons of soy sauce
  • 2 tablespoons of vinegar
  • 3 tablespoons of chili oil
  • 2 tablespoons of sesame paste
  • 1 tablespoon of sugar
  • 1 cup of boiling water

Instructions:

  1. Blanch the noodles until al dente, drain, and set aside.
  2. In a wok, heat oil and add Sichuan peppercorns, black beans, ginger, and sauté until aromatic.
  3. Add the pork and cook until fully browned, breaking down the meat.
  4. Add pickled mustard greens, green onions, and sauce ingredients: soy sauce, vinegar, sugar, sesame paste, mix well.
  5. Drop in boiling water, and stir until everything is smooth and creamy.
  6. To serve, place the noodles into a bamboo steamer lined with oiled parchment paper, and pour the sauce over the noodles, then give it a final mix.

Grab your chopsticks and prepare for an extraordinary culinary experience. As you enjoy these iconic Sichuan delicacies, don’t forget to pay attention to the interplay of flavors and textures. Embrace the richness and heat to understand the essence of what makes Sichuan cuisine, as complex as life itself, yet simple and accessible enough to be cherished by generations.
