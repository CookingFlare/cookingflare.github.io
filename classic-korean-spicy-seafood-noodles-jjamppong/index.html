Classic Korean Spicy Seafood Noodles (Jjamppong)


Few dishes capture the essence of Korean cuisine as vibrantly as Jjamppong, a hearty bowl of spicy seafood noodles. With its luscious broth bursting with complex flavors, fresh vegetables, and an array of seafood, Jjamppong is a feast for both the eyes and the palate. Whether you’re familiar with the fiery charm of Korean cooking or just beginning your culinary journey, this dish offers a delightful experience that encapsulates the soul of Korean comfort food.

What Makes Jjamppong Special?

Jjamppong isn’t just about heat; it’s a symphony of flavors balanced perfectly in a tantalizing broth. The key ingredient is gochugaru, or Korean chili flakes, which deliver the perfect level of spiciness and a smoky depth that lingers on the palate. Beyond the spice, the broth teems with umami-rich seafood, sweetness from fresh vegetables, and the aromatic depth of garlic and ginger. It’s bold, soulful, and endlessly satisfying.

While it is a staple of Korean restaurants, making Jjamppong at home can be a rewarding endeavor. With simple instructions and thoughtful preparation, you can recreate this iconic dish and customize it to suit your taste preferences.

Ingredients You’ll Need

The beauty of Jjamppong lies in its adaptability. A variety of fresh vegetables, seafood, and aromatics combine into a harmonious dish. Here’s a comprehensive list of ingredients to guide you:

For the Broth

  • Water or Broth: 6 cups of water, chicken stock, or seafood stock. A flavorful base is essential.
  • Anchovy Stock (Optional): For an authentic Korean flavor, use anchovy broth as your base.
  • Soy Sauce: 2 tablespoons of soy sauce for added umami depth.
  • Dried Kelp or Kombu (Optional): A slice for extra richness.

Aromatics

  • Garlic: 4-5 cloves, minced.
  • Ginger: 1 tablespoon, grated or finely minced.
  • Green Onions: Chopped, to build flavor and garnish the finished dish.

Vegetables

  • Carrots: Thinly julienned, for sweetness and texture.
  • Cabbage: 2 cups, chopped. Napa cabbage works best.
  • Onions: 1 medium onion, sliced.
  • Zucchini: 1 medium, julienned.
  • Fresh Spinach or Bok Choy: A handful for added freshness.

Seafood

  • Shrimp: 8-10, peeled and deveined.
  • Squid: Fresh or frozen, sliced into bite-sized pieces.
  • Clams or Mussels: 1 cup, cleaned and scrubbed.
  • Optional: Scallops or pieces of white fish like cod for added variety.

Noodles

  • Fresh Wheat Noodles or Ramen: You’ll need about 1 pound. Alternatively, udon noodles or spaghetti can work in a pinch.

Seasonings & Heat

  • Gochugaru: Korean Chili Flakes (2 tablespoons, adjust to taste).
  • Gochujang: Korean Chili Paste (1 tablespoon, for a subtle sweetness).
  • Sesame Oil: 1 teaspoon to enhance the aroma.
  • Salt and Pepper: To taste.

Step-by-Step Cooking Instructions

1. Prepare the Base Broth

Begin by preparing your broth. If using anchovy stock, boil a handful of dried anchovies and kelp in a pot with six cups of water for about 20 minutes. Strain and set aside for later use. Alternatively, start with chicken or seafood stock. This step lays the foundation for a rich and flavorful soup.

2. Sauté the Aromatics

In a large pot or wok, heat a tablespoon of vegetable oil over medium heat. Add minced garlic, ginger, and the white parts of the green onions. Cook for a minute until fragrant, ensuring the aroma fills your kitchen.

3. Bloom the Gochugaru

Add the Korean chili flakes (gochugaru) into the pot with the aromatics. Stir constantly for about 30 seconds to release the oils and smoky richness from the chili flakes. Be cautious not to burn the spices, as this may lead to bitterness.

4. Build the Soup

Pour the prepared broth into the pot, followed by soy sauce and gochujang. Stir well to fully dissolve the chili paste. Bring the liquid to a simmer and allow the flavors to meld for 5-10 minutes. Taste and adjust seasonings as needed.

5. Add the Vegetables

Toss in the cabbage, carrots, onions, zucchini, and greens like spinach or bok choy. Let the vegetables cook down for about 5 minutes, softening slightly while retaining their vibrant colors. This step is crucial for building texture and sweetness in the soup.

6. Add the Seafood

Carefully add the shrimp, squid, clams, and any optional seafood to the simmering soup. Cook until the clams open, the shrimp turn pink, and the squid becomes tender—usually just a few minutes. Be mindful not to overcook the seafood, as it can become rubbery.

7. Cook the Noodles

Meanwhile, boil the noodles according to package instructions and drain thoroughly. To prevent clumping, toss the noodles lightly with a drizzle of sesame oil or neutral oil.

8. Combine and Serve

Divide the cooked noodles between serving bowls and ladle the spicy seafood broth directly over the noodles, ensuring each bowl gets a generous helping of vegetables and fresh seafood. Garnish with chopped green onions and serve immediately.

Tips for Perfect Jjamppong

  • Adjust Spice Level: If you prefer a milder version, reduce the amount of gochugaru or omit gochujang altogether. Alternatively, enhance the spice with an extra dash of chili flakes.
  • Seafood Alternatives: Not a fan of clams? Substitute with scallops, fish fillets, or even tofu for a vegetarian twist.
  • Fresh Is Best: Fresh seafood brings out the best in this dish. If unavailable, high-quality frozen seafood can work as a substitute.

Why Jjamppong Belongs in Your Cooking Repertoire

From its bold flavors to its nourishing warmth, Jjamppong is more than just a meal—it’s an experience. The spicy kick invigorates your senses, the combination of ingredients tells a story of harmony, and the versatility allows for endless customization. Learning to make Jjamppong is embracing the adventurous and communal spirit of Korean cuisine.

Whether you’re searching for a new dish to impress your guests, craving a comforting bowl of noodles after a long day, or aspiring to explore cultural culinary traditions, this dish is a must-try. The process is as enjoyable as the result, inviting you to savor the artistry and the flavors each step of the way.

Storing and Reheating Leftovers

Though best enjoyed fresh, Jjamppong also makes fantastic leftovers. Store the soup and noodles separately to prevent the noodles from becoming soggy. The soup can be refrigerated in an airtight container for up to three days. Simply reheat on the stove, bringing the broth to a gentle boil, and add freshly boiled noodles before eating.

Final Thoughts

Classic Korean Spicy Seafood Noodles (Jjamppong) is a dish that embodies the vibrant and dynamic flavors of Korea. With fresh vegetables, succulent seafood, and a broth that’s both spicy and savory, it’s a bowl of comfort that rejuvenates the spirit. This recipe welcomes creativity, so experiment with your ingredients and make it your own. Gather your ingredients, follow the steps, and savor the magic of Jjamppong in your own kitchen.
