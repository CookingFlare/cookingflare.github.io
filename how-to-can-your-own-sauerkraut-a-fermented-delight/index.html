How to Can Your Own Sauerkraut: A Fermented Delight


When it comes to preserving the harvest and creating bold, tangy flavors, few things hold as much allure as sauerkraut. This time-honored method of fermenting cabbage has been cherished across cultures for centuries, and the results are as nutritious as they are delicious. If you’ve ever considered taking your pantry game to the next level, canning your own sauerkraut is an excellent way to ensure you have this probiotic-rich condiment on hand year-round.

In this guide, we’ll walk you through the entire sauerkraut canning process, from selecting the best cabbage to safely fermenting and processing jars for long-term storage. By the end, you’ll not only have a newfound respect for this old-world technique but also jars upon jars of crisp, perfectly tart sauerkraut to enjoy.

Why Make Sauerkraut at Home?

Canning your own sauerkraut isn’t just about saving money or customizing flavor. It’s a way to connect with the simple joys of creating something from scratch, using minimal, natural ingredients. Here are a few perks of homemade sauerkraut:

  • Health Benefits: Packed with probiotics, sauerkraut supports your gut microbiome, aids digestion, and boosts your immune system. Store-bought versions often lose these benefits due to pasteurization.
  • No Unnecessary Additives: Homemade sauerkraut is free from artificial preservatives found in many commercial versions.
  • Flavor Tailoring: You control the salt level, fermentation duration, and optional aromatics.
  • Sustainability: Homemade sauerkraut reduces food waste by preserving surplus cabbage.

Tools and Ingredients You’ll Need

Before you dive in, it’s essential to gather the necessary supplies. Preparing in advance will streamline the canning process and ensure your sauerkraut ferments safely.

Ingredients:

  • Fresh cabbage (green, red, or a mix, but green is most traditional)
  • Fine sea salt or non-iodized salt (iodized salt can interfere with fermentation)

Tools:

  • A sharp knife or mandoline for shredding cabbage
  • A large mixing bowl
  • A fermentation crock or wide-mouth glass jars
  • A clean kitchen scale for accurate salt measurement
  • A tamper or wooden spoon for packing the cabbage tightly
  • Cheesecloth or a clean, breathable cloth
  • Weights or a small plate to keep the cabbage submerged
  • A water bath canner and canning jars for final preservation

Step 1: Selecting and Preparing the Cabbage

Start with the freshest, firmest cabbages you can find. Heads that feel heavy for their size indicate well-packed leaves, a crucial detail for optimal fermentation. Choose organic varieties if possible, as they’re free from pesticide residues.

  1. Clean and Prep: Remove the tough outer leaves of the cabbage. These are usually damaged or dirty. Set one or two whole outer leaves aside; you’ll need them later.
  2. Shred the Cabbage: Using a sharp knife or mandoline, finely shred the cabbage into thin strips. The smaller the shreds, the more evenly they ferment.
  3. Weigh the Cabbage: For every pound of cabbage, use about 1–3 tablespoons of non-iodized salt. The salt-to-cabbage ratio is crucial for fermentation, so measure carefully.

Step 2: Salting and Massaging the Cabbage

Salt is the magic ingredient in sauerkraut. It not only draws out the cabbage’s natural juices but also creates the ideal environment for good bacteria to thrive while deterring unwanted microbes.

  1. Combine and Massage: Place the shredded cabbage in a large mixing bowl and sprinkle it with salt. With clean hands, massage the cabbage thoroughly for 5–10 minutes until it begins to release its liquid. The cabbage should eventually feel wilted with a briny puddle forming at the bottom.
  2. Taste Test: Taste the cabbage to ensure it’s salty but not overwhelmingly so. Adjust with a pinch of extra salt if needed.

Step 3: Packing the Fermentation Vessel

Once the cabbage is properly salted and massaged, it’s time to shift everything into its fermentation home.

  1. Pack Tightly: Transfer the cabbage, in small batches, into a clean crock or wide-mouth glass jars. As you add each handful, use a tamper or wooden spoon to press the cabbage down firmly. You’re aiming to eliminate air pockets.
  2. Add the Brine: Pour any liquid from the mixing bowl into the jar to cover the cabbage completely. If necessary, top it off with additional brine (1 tablespoon salt dissolved in 1 cup of water).
  3. Use the Outer Leaves: Take the reserved outer leaves and press them over the top of the shredded cabbage. This keeps the smaller shreds submerged.

Step 4: Fermentation

Proper fermentation is where the magic happens. Lactobacillus bacteria will go to work, breaking down the cabbage and creating that signature tangy flavor.

  1. Cover and Weight: Place a clean cloth over the jar to keep dust and pests out while allowing air circulation. Place a fermenting weight or small dish on top to ensure the cabbage stays submerged.
  2. Monitor: Over the next few days, bubbles and a tangy aroma should appear. This is a good sign the fermentation is proceeding correctly. Check daily to ensure the cabbage remains submerged.
  3. Adjust Time: Fermentation typically takes 1–4 weeks, depending on the temperature. Taste the sauerkraut periodically, starting around day five, to determine when it reaches your desired tartness.

Step 5: Canning Your Sauerkraut

Once you’ve achieved the perfect level of fermentation, it’s time to move on to the final step: canning for long-term preservation. A hot water bath preserves the sauerkraut safely without sacrificing its vibrant flavor.

  1. Prepare Jars: Wash and sterilize your canning jars, lids, and rings. Keep them warm to prevent cracking during processing.
  2. Fill the Jars: Pack the sauerkraut into the jars, leaving about half an inch of headspace. Pour the brine over the cabbage to cover it completely.
  3. Seal the Jars: Wipe the rim of each jar, place the lid on top, and screw the ring on until fingertip-tight. Avoid overtightening.
  4. Process in Water Bath: Place the jars in a water bath canner and process for 10 minutes (adjust for altitude if necessary).
  5. Cool and Store: Allow the jars to cool on a towel for 12–24 hours. Check the seals before storing in a cool, dark place.

Tips for Success

  • Temperature Control: Fermentation thrives between 65–72°F. Cooler temperatures will slow the process, while higher temperatures may result in spoilage.
  • Cleanliness is Key: Always use clean tools and hands to prevent contamination.
  • Experiment with Flavors: Add caraway seeds, garlic, or juniper berries to diversify your sauerkraut.

Serving and Enjoying Your Sauerkraut

Your homemade sauerkraut is both versatile and delicious. Use it as a topping for hot dogs or bratwursts, layer it into sandwiches, or pair it with roasted meats. It even works as a simple, healthful side dish on its own.

However you choose to enjoy it, there’s nothing quite like knowing you’ve made it from scratch. The combination of vibrant flavor, rich probiotics, and the satisfaction of preserving a food tradition will give you all the more reason to keep canning.

So why not give it a try? Roll up your sleeves, grab some cabbage, and let the fermentation adventure begin!
