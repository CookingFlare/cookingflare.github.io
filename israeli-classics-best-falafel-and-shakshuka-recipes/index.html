Israeli Classics: Best Falafel and Shakshuka Recipes


Israeli cuisine is a delightful fusion of flavors, textures, and ingredients that reflect the diverse cultural influences of the region. Among the most iconic dishes are falafel and shakshuka. These traditional foods are not only enjoyed by locals but have gained popularity worldwide for their unique taste and wholesome ingredients. In this post, we’ll explore the origins of falafel and shakshuka, dive into the best recipes for each, and offer tips for making them at home. Whether you’re new to Israeli food or an experienced home cook, these recipes will transport you straight to the heart of the Middle East.

Falafel: The Heart of Israeli Street Food

Falafel is one of the most beloved street foods in Israel, and for good reason. These crispy, deep-fried balls are made from ground chickpeas or fava beans, blended with herbs and spices, and then fried to perfection. Traditionally served in pita bread with an array of fresh toppings and sauces, falafel offers a satisfying combination of crunchy, spicy, and savory flavors. Here’s how to make the perfect falafel at home.

Ingredients:

  • 2 cups dried chickpeas (garbanzo beans) – soaked overnight
  • 1 large onion, roughly chopped
  • 4 cloves garlic, minced
  • 1/2 cup fresh parsley, chopped
  • 1/2 cup fresh cilantro, chopped
  • 1-2 green chilies, deseeded and chopped (optional)
  • 1 teaspoon cumin
  • 1 teaspoon coriander
  • 1 teaspoon ground turmeric
  • 1 teaspoon baking powder
  • Salt and pepper to taste
  • 1/4 cup flour (more if needed)
  • Vegetable oil for frying

Instructions:

1. Prepare the Chickpeas:

Start by soaking the chickpeas in plenty of water overnight. They should more than double in size. Once they are soaked, drain the chickpeas and place them in a food processor.

2. Blend the Falafel Mixture:

Add the onion, garlic, parsley, cilantro, green chilies (if using), cumin, coriander, turmeric, baking powder, salt, and pepper to the food processor. Pulse the mixture until everything is finely ground but not pureed. You should have a coarse dough-like consistency, not too smooth. If the mixture feels too wet or sticky, add a little flour to help it bind together.

3. Let the Mixture Rest:

Transfer the falafel mixture to a bowl, cover it, and refrigerate for at least an hour. This step allows the flavors to meld and makes it easier to shape the falafel.

4. Shape the Falafel:

Once the mixture is chilled, form it into small balls or patties, about 1-1.5 inches in diameter. If the mixture is too sticky to shape, lightly moisten your hands or dust them with flour.

5. Fry the Falafel:

Heat the vegetable oil in a deep frying pan or pot over medium-high heat. You need enough oil to submerge the falafel balls. When the oil is hot, gently drop the falafel in batches, being careful not to overcrowd the pan. Fry the falafel for 3-4 minutes on each side until golden brown and crispy.

6. Drain and Serve:

Remove the falafel from the oil and let them drain on paper towels. Serve immediately in warm pita bread with your favorite toppings such as hummus, tahini, pickled vegetables, tomatoes, cucumbers, and lettuce. For a refreshing twist, drizzle some lemon juice over the falafel before eating.

Shakshuka: A Flavorful Israeli Breakfast

Shakshuka is another iconic dish that has made its way to breakfast tables, brunch spots, and dinner menus around the world. This rich, flavorful dish consists of poached eggs in a spiced tomato sauce, often with a base of onions, garlic, and peppers. While shakshuka is traditionally enjoyed in Israel, it has roots in North Africa and the Middle East, and each country adds its own variations.

Shakshuka is perfect for breakfast, lunch, or dinner, and it’s as versatile as it is delicious. You can adjust the spices to your taste, add more vegetables, or even sprinkle some feta or olives for an added layer of flavor.

Ingredients:

  • 2 tablespoons olive oil
  • 1 onion, sliced thinly
  • 1 bell pepper, sliced (red or green)
  • 3 cloves garlic, minced
  • 1 teaspoon cumin
  • 1 teaspoon paprika (preferably smoked)
  • 1/2 teaspoon chili flakes (optional, adjust to heat preference)
  • 1 can (14 oz) crushed tomatoes (or 5-6 fresh tomatoes, diced)
  • Salt and pepper to taste
  • 4-6 large eggs
  • Fresh parsley or cilantro, chopped (for garnish)
  • Crumbled feta cheese (optional)
  • Pita bread or crusty bread (for serving)

Instructions:

1. Prepare the Sauce:

In a large skillet or pan, heat the olive oil over medium heat. Add the onion and bell pepper and sauté for about 5-7 minutes, until they become soft and slightly caramelized. Add the garlic, cumin, paprika, and chili flakes (if using) and cook for another minute until fragrant.

2. Add the Tomatoes:

Stir in the crushed tomatoes, salt, and pepper, and let the mixture simmer on low heat for about 15 minutes. If the sauce becomes too thick, add a splash of water or vegetable broth to reach your desired consistency. Taste and adjust the seasoning if necessary.

3. Poach the Eggs:

Once the tomato sauce has thickened and the flavors have developed, make small wells in the sauce using a spoon. Crack the eggs carefully into each well. Cover the pan with a lid and cook for 6-8 minutes, depending on how runny or firm you prefer your eggs. For runny yolks, keep the cooking time on the shorter side.

4. Garnish and Serve:

Once the eggs are cooked to your liking, remove the pan from the heat. Garnish with fresh parsley or cilantro and crumbled feta cheese (if desired). Serve with warm pita bread or crusty bread to dip into the rich tomato sauce and runny eggs.

Serving Suggestions for Falafel and Shakshuka

Both falafel and shakshuka can be served in various ways, depending on your personal preferences. Here are a few suggestions to elevate these dishes:

Falafel Toppings and Sides:

  • Tahini Sauce: A creamy sesame sauce made with tahini, lemon juice, garlic, and water. It complements the crunch of the falafel and adds a rich flavor.
  • Hummus: Smooth, creamy hummus is a great addition to a falafel wrap or platter. It pairs perfectly with the spiced falafel.
  • Pickled Vegetables: Serve falafel with a side of pickled cucumbers, turnips, or carrots for a tangy crunch.
  • Israeli Salad: A fresh salad of tomatoes, cucumbers, onions, and parsley, dressed with olive oil and lemon juice, adds a refreshing balance to the richness of falafel.

Shakshuka Variations:

  • Add Cheese: Top your shakshuka with crumbled feta or goat cheese for an added layer of creaminess and tang.
  • Spicy Shakshuka: Add sliced jalapeños or chili peppers to the tomato sauce for an extra kick of heat.
  • Veggie Shakshuka: Incorporate more vegetables like spinach, zucchini, or eggplant to make it a heartier dish.
  • Herbs and Yogurt: Garnish shakshuka with a dollop of yogurt and a sprinkle of fresh herbs like dill or mint for added freshness.

The Magic of Israeli Cuisine

Falafel and shakshuka are just two examples of the vibrant and flavorful dishes that make Israeli cuisine so unique. The balance of spices, the freshness of the ingredients, and the emphasis on communal dining make Israeli food truly special. Whether you’re enjoying falafel on a busy street in Tel Aviv or sharing shakshuka with friends around a table, these dishes are a reminder of the rich cultural heritage and culinary traditions of Israel.

By following these recipes, you can recreate the flavors of Israel in your own kitchen, sharing the joy of these delicious meals with family and friends. So, gather your ingredients, get cooking, and experience the magic of Israeli food in every bite.
