Exploring the Flavors of Korean Street Food: Top Recipes


Korean street food is a vibrant, delicious, and integral part of the country’s culinary culture. From savory snacks to sweet treats, the streets of Korea are lined with vendors offering a wide range of flavorful dishes that are quick to prepare and packed with unique tastes. Whether you’re visiting Korea or simply exploring Korean cuisine from home, there’s a dish for everyone.

In this blog post, we will take a deep dive into some of the most popular and iconic street foods in Korea. We’ll explore their origins, flavors, and how to prepare them in your own kitchen. These dishes are not only delicious but are a perfect way to experience the essence of Korean culture through food. Let’s get started!

1. Tteokbokki (떡볶이) – Spicy Rice Cakes

What is Tteokbokki?

Tteokbokki is one of the most beloved street foods in Korea. This dish consists of chewy rice cakes simmered in a spicy and slightly sweet sauce made from gochujang (Korean red chili paste), sugar, and soy sauce. It’s often served with fish cakes, boiled eggs, and vegetables, making it a hearty and satisfying snack.

How to Make Tteokbokki at Home

Ingredients:

  • 1 pound of cylindrical rice cakes (tteok)
  • 1 tablespoon of vegetable oil
  • 1/2 onion, thinly sliced
  • 1/2 cup of fish cakes (optional), cut into bite-sized pieces
  • 3 tablespoons of gochujang
  • 1 tablespoon of gochugaru (Korean chili flakes)
  • 2 tablespoons of soy sauce
  • 1 tablespoon of sugar
  • 1 teaspoon of minced garlic
  • 2 cups of water or anchovy broth
  • 1 boiled egg (optional)
  • Green onions (for garnish)

Instructions:

  1. Prepare the Rice Cakes: If using refrigerated or frozen rice cakes, soak them in warm water for about 20 minutes to soften them.

  2. Cook the Sauce: In a large pan, heat vegetable oil over medium heat. Add the onion and cook until softened. Stir in the minced garlic and cook for another minute. Add gochujang, gochugaru, soy sauce, and sugar. Mix everything together, allowing the paste to slightly caramelize for about 2 minutes.

  3. Simmer the Rice Cakes: Add the water or anchovy broth to the pan, bringing it to a simmer. Once it’s boiling, add the rice cakes and fish cakes. Stir occasionally to ensure the rice cakes are evenly coated with the sauce.

  4. Let It Cook: Continue to cook the mixture for about 10-15 minutes, until the sauce thickens and the rice cakes become tender and chewy.

  5. Add the Egg: If using a boiled egg, peel it and add it to the pan, letting it warm through in the sauce.

  6. Serve: Garnish with chopped green onions and serve hot.

Tteokbokki is perfect for any occasion, whether as a snack or a comforting meal.

2. Hotteok (호떡) – Korean Sweet Pancakes

What is Hotteok?

Hotteok is a popular winter snack that is often sold by street vendors during the colder months in Korea. These delicious pancakes are stuffed with a sweet mixture of brown sugar, cinnamon, and crushed nuts, then fried to golden perfection. The combination of crispy outside and gooey, warm inside makes hotteok irresistible.

How to Make Hotteok at Home

Ingredients:

  • 2 cups of all-purpose flour
  • 2 tablespoons of sugar
  • 1/2 teaspoon of salt
  • 1 tablespoon of active dry yeast
  • 1/2 cup of warm water
  • 1 tablespoon of vegetable oil
  • 1/2 cup of brown sugar (for the filling)
  • 1/4 teaspoon of ground cinnamon
  • 1/4 cup of crushed walnuts or peanuts (optional)
  • Vegetable oil (for frying)

Instructions:

  1. Prepare the Dough: In a large mixing bowl, combine the flour, sugar, salt, and yeast. Add the warm water and oil, mixing until the dough comes together. Knead the dough for about 5 minutes until it’s smooth and elastic. Cover the bowl with a damp cloth and let the dough rise for about an hour, or until it has doubled in size.

  2. Make the Filling: In a small bowl, combine the brown sugar, cinnamon, and crushed walnuts or peanuts (if using). This mixture will be used to fill the pancakes.

  3. Form the Pancakes: Once the dough has risen, punch it down and divide it into small portions (about 8-10 balls). Flatten each dough ball into a small disc and place a teaspoon of the sugar-nut mixture in the center. Fold the edges over and pinch them together to seal the filling inside.

  4. Fry the Pancakes: Heat a tablespoon of vegetable oil in a pan over medium heat. Place the stuffed dough balls seam-side down in the pan. Flatten them gently with a spatula to form thick pancakes. Fry for about 3-4 minutes on each side, or until golden brown and crispy. You may need to add a bit more oil as you cook the pancakes.

  5. Serve: Serve the hotteok warm, straight from the pan. Enjoy the crispy exterior and the warm, sugary filling inside.

Hotteok is a comforting and sweet treat that is perfect for cold weather, but it can be enjoyed year-round!

3. Gimbap (김밥) – Korean Sushi Rolls

What is Gimbap?

Gimbap is often referred to as Korean sushi, but it’s actually quite different. While sushi features raw fish and vinegared rice, gimbap uses cooked ingredients and is wrapped in seaweed with seasoned rice. Gimbap is a versatile dish, making it a popular snack or picnic food. It’s packed with a variety of fillings such as pickled radish, spinach, egg, and even beef or fish cakes.

How to Make Gimbap at Home

Ingredients:

  • 2 cups of short-grain rice
  • 2 tablespoons of rice vinegar
  • 1 tablespoon of sugar
  • 1/2 teaspoon of salt
  • 5 sheets of nori (seaweed)
  • 1/2 cup of spinach, blanched
  • 2 large eggs, scrambled
  • 1/2 cucumber, julienned
  • 1/4 cup of pickled radish (danmuji), sliced thinly
  • 1/2 pound of cooked beef (or imitation crab, if preferred), thinly sliced
  • Sesame oil (for brushing)
  • Salt (for seasoning)

Instructions:

  1. Prepare the Rice: Cook the rice according to package instructions. Once cooked, transfer the rice to a large bowl and allow it to cool slightly. In a small bowl, mix the rice vinegar, sugar, and salt until dissolved. Stir the vinegar mixture into the rice and let it cool to room temperature.

  2. Prepare the Fillings: Blanch the spinach in boiling water for a minute, then squeeze out the excess water and season it lightly with a pinch of salt and sesame oil. Scramble the eggs and slice them into thin strips. Julienne the cucumber and slice the pickled radish into thin strips.

  3. Assemble the Gimbap: Place a sheet of nori shiny-side down on a bamboo mat or flat surface. Spread a thin layer of rice over the nori, leaving about an inch at the top free of rice. Layer your fillings (spinach, egg, cucumber, radish, and beef) in the center of the rice.

  4. Roll the Gimbap: Using the bamboo mat, carefully roll the gimbap tightly from the bottom, pressing gently as you go. Once the roll is formed, brush the outside with a little sesame oil to give it a glossy finish.

  5. Slice and Serve: Use a sharp knife to slice the gimbap into bite-sized pieces. Serve immediately or wrap it in plastic for later.

Gimbap is a great dish for lunch, picnics, or as a snack. It’s easy to customize with your favorite fillings, making it an incredibly versatile dish.

4. Odeng (오뎅) – Korean Fish Cake Skewers

What is Odeng?

Odeng, or eomuk (fish cake), is another popular street food in Korea. It consists of fish cakes that are skewered and served in a warm broth. Odeng is often enjoyed with a cup of hot broth, especially during chilly days. The fish cakes are made with fish paste and flavored with various seasonings and spices.

How to Make Odeng at Home

Ingredients:

  • 1/2 pound of fish cake (eomuk), sliced into thin pieces
  • 2 cups of water or broth (anchovy or vegetable)
  • 1 tablespoon of soy sauce
  • 1 tablespoon of mirin (optional)
  • 1 teaspoon of sesame oil
  • 1/2 teaspoon of sugar
  • Skewers (wooden or metal)

Instructions:

  1. Prepare the Broth: In a pot, combine the water or broth, soy sauce, mirin, sesame oil, and sugar. Bring it to a boil, then reduce the heat and let it simmer for 5-10 minutes.

  2. Skewer the Fish Cakes: Thread the fish cakes onto skewers, arranging them neatly.

  3. Cook the Odeng: Add the fish cake skewers to the simmering broth and cook for 3-5 minutes, or until heated through.

  4. Serve: Serve the odeng in the broth, either as a snack or paired with rice for a light meal.

Odeng is simple yet comforting, and the broth adds a delightful depth of flavor to the fish cakes.

Conclusion

Korean street food offers a diverse and exciting array of flavors, textures, and ingredients that reflect the rich culinary traditions of the country. From the spicy and savory tteokbokki to the sweet and crispy hotteok, these dishes provide a wonderful taste of Korea’s food culture. Whether you’re trying these recipes at home or enjoying them on the bustling streets of Korea, there’s no doubt that these iconic snacks will leave a lasting impression on your palate.

By trying your hand at making these dishes at home, you can bring the flavors of Korean street food into your own kitchen, creating a fun and flavorful experience for yourself and your loved ones.
