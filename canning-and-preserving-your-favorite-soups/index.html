Canning and Preserving Your Favorite Soups


Opening a jar of homemade soup months after you’ve made it can transport you back to the moment you first stirred together those wholesome ingredients. Whether it’s your grandmother’s chicken noodle soup or a hearty lentil stew, canning and preserving soups offer a way to enjoy your culinary creations all year long. This process not only saves time and money but also ensures that you’re eating wholesome, additive-free food when life gets busy.

Below, we’ll explore the techniques, tools, and tips to safely and successfully can and preserve your favorite soups. With a bit of preparation, you’ll soon have a pantry stocked with jars brimming with goodness, ready to be enjoyed any time.

Benefits of Canning Soup

Canning isn’t just a throwback to simpler times; it’s a practical method of food preservation that guarantees nutrition and flavor. Here are some benefits of preserving soup at home:

  • Convenience: Homemade canned soup can be a lifesaver on busy days. Simply heat and serve for a quick, nourishing meal.
  • Cost-Efficiency: Making large batches reduces waste and saves money by buying ingredients in bulk.
  • Customization: You control the recipe, ensuring flavors, spices, and ingredients match your preferences.
  • Shelf Stability: Properly canned soups can last for months or even a year without refrigeration.
  • Sustainability: Reduce packaging waste by reusing jars instead of buying store-bought alternatives.

Safety First: Preparing for Canning

Canning soup is easy and approachable, but it’s essential to follow proper safety guidelines. Foodborne illnesses, like botulism, can result from improper canning methods. Here’s what you need to know before starting:

Choose the Right Equipment

Canning soups requires a reliable pressure canner since water bath canning is not safe for low-acid foods like meats, beans, or vegetables. Invest in the following tools:

  • Pressure Canner: Necessary for low-acid foods to reach a high enough temperature to eliminate harmful bacteria.
  • Canning Jars: Mason jars with airtight lids are perfect for preserving soups.
  • Canning Accessories: Jar lifters, funnels, and bubble remover tools ensure safe handling and accurate filling.

Prepare Your Workspace

Your kitchen should be impeccably clean to minimize contamination. Wash all tools, jars, and lids in hot, soapy water prior to use. Sterilize jars by boiling them or running them through the dishwasher on a high heat cycle.

The Step-by-Step Canning Process

Step 1: Select Your Recipe

When choosing soups to can, consider recipes with minimal dairy, thickeners, or oils. These components can interfere with the preservation process. Here are some examples of excellent soups for canning:

  • Chicken and vegetable soup (omit noodles for better shelf life).
  • Split pea soup.
  • Tomato-based soups.
  • Minestrone or vegetable soup with beans.
  • Beef stew (without potatoes).

Step 2: Cook Your Soup

Prepare your soup but slightly undercook it. For example, if your recipe includes vegetables, leave them slightly firm as they’ll continue to cook during the pressure canning process. Never add dairy, cream, flour, or other thickeners at this stage; these should be incorporated later when reheating the soup.

Step 3: Fill Jars

Using a funnel, ladle your hot soup into sterilized mason jars. Fill to the recommended level, typically leaving 1 inch of headspace at the top. This space allows for expansion during the canning process.

Step 4: Remove Air Bubbles

Use a bubble remover tool or a knife to gently stir the soup in the jar, removing any trapped air bubbles. Air bubbles can prevent proper sealing and compromise the safety of your preserved soup.

Step 5: Seal the Jars

Wipe the rims of the jars with a damp cloth to ensure they’re clean, then place sterilized lids on top. Secure the lid tightly with a metal band, but avoid overtightening as slight movement is necessary for sealing.

Step 6: Pressure Can the Soup

Follow your pressure canner’s instructions for operation. The processing time will depend on your soup recipe but typically ranges from 60 to 90 minutes. During this time, the canner will reach a temperature of 240°F, killing any bacteria or contaminants.

Step 7: Cool and Store

Allow the jars to cool undisturbed for at least 12 hours. Once cooled, check each lid for a proper seal by pressing down gently in the center—if the lid doesn’t pop back, it’s sealed correctly. Store sealed jars in a cool, dark pantry. Any jars that failed to seal should be refrigerated and consumed within a few days.

Popular Soup Recipes for Canning

Here are a few ideas to inspire your next big batch of soup:

Classic Chicken and Vegetable Soup

Combine bone broth, chunks of chicken, carrots, celery, and onions. Season with thyme and bay leaves. Avoid adding noodles during canning; instead, cook them fresh and add them when reheating.

Tomato Basil Soup

Blend fresh tomatoes, onions, garlic, and a handful of basil for a velvety soup. Skip cream during the canning process to ensure proper preservation and add a touch when serving.

Hearty Lentil Soup

Packed with lentils, carrots, and celery, this soup is deliciously spiced with cumin and smoked paprika. Lentils hold their texture well during canning, making them an ideal choice.

Beef and Bean Chili

Brown ground beef and mix it with kidney beans, diced tomatoes, onions, and spices. Adjust heat levels to your preference with chili powder. This one is perfect when you’re craving something warming and filling.

Tips for Successful Soup Canning

  • Label Your Jars: Always include the soup name and canning date. This helps you keep track of freshness and ensures you consume jars within their optimal shelf life.
  • Rotate Your Stock: Practice the “first in, first out” method, consuming older jars first to prevent spoilage.
  • Test Seals Regularly: Periodically check that seals remain intact. If a jar loses its seal, discard the contents immediately.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Skipping the Pressure Canner: Never use a water bath canner for soups, as it does not reach the temperature needed to eliminate bacteria.
  2. Overpacking Jars: Leaving insufficient headspace can result in leakage or prevent proper sealing.
  3. Adding Dairy: Ingredients like milk, cream, and cheese can break down during the preservation process, compromising texture and safety.
  4. Using Improper Recipes: Ensure every ingredient is suitable for canning. Avoid flour, rice, pasta, or potatoes, as these don’t preserve well.

Reheating Your Preserved Soups

When you’re ready to enjoy your soup, simply pop open the jar and pour it into a saucepan. Heat on medium until warmed through, and feel free to doctor it up for added freshness. This is the ideal time to add ingredients like cream, fresh herbs, rice, or noodles.

Conclusion

Canning and preserving your favorite soups is a rewarding way to stock your pantry with comforting, homemade meals. With the proper tools, techniques, and attention to detail, you’ll create a stunning array of soups to enjoy throughout the year. The process may take a bit of effort upfront, but the payoff comes every time you grab a jar off the shelf and recall the care and thought that went into every spoonful. So grab your pressure canner, pick your favorite seasonal soup, and embark on this satisfying journey of preserving food at home!
