Mastering the Art of Chinese Cooking: Basic but Essential Techniques for Exquisite Results


Chinese cuisine is a world-renowned culinary art, and learning its intricate techniques can elevate your cooking to new heights. In this blog post, we’ll delve into the basic but essential Chinese cooking techniques that will allow you to create delicious, authentic dishes. Be prepared to embark on a flavorful journey that will challenge and expand your palate. Let’s get started!

  1. Wok Stir-Frying

The wok is an essential tool in Chinese cuisine, and stir-frying is arguably the most popular cooking method. This technique requires high heat and minimal oil, ensuring that your food cooks quickly and remains moist while developing a mouthwatering, slightly crispy texture.

To accomplish this, start with a preheated wok and pour a little oil into the center. Once it’s hot, spread the oil around the wok using a spatula or ladle. Before adding the ingredients, you’ll want to season the wok with a little salt to create a layer of seasoning on the bottom, which helps prevent sticking. Then, add your ingredients, keeping like items together to keep their respective tastes separate. Ensure efficient circulation of food by tossing it occasionally, and avoid overcrowding the wok to prevent steaming.

  1. Steaming

Steaming is another popular cooking method that uses a steamer or a wok set up with a steaming rack. Steam maintains the natural flavors and nutrients of food, whereas boiling can sometimes leach these essential elements. A simple, yet delicious dish to try steaming, is steamed fish with ginger and scallions.

Prepare the ingredients by seasoning the fish with a little salt, pepper, and optionally some soy sauce. Lay a piece of ginger and several scallions on the fish, and place it on the steaming rack. Add water to a depth of about one or two inches in your steamer or wok, and bring it to a boil. Set the fish in the rack above the water, cover with a lid, and steam for around 10 minutes. Once finished, serve immediately for the best flavors and textures.

  1. Braising

Braising is a combination of cooking methods that involves first searing the ingredients on high heat before allowing them to simmer slowly in a liquid. This technique tenderizes tougher cuts of meat and can also be used to cook vegetables and tofu.

To start, thoroughly dry your ingredients with paper towels before searing; this prevents the liquid inside from diluting the SOUGHT-OUT tastes. Heat 2-3 tablespoons of oil in your wok on high heat, placing the ingredients in batches so as not to overcrowd the pan. Once they develop a golden-brown crust, remove them from the wok and set them aside. In the same pan, add a bit more oil and cook the aromatics like garlic, ginger, or shallots until fragrant. Then, deglaze the pan with some broth or water, scraping the bottom of the pan to release all those delicious bits stuck to the surface. Return the browned ingredients to the wok and simmer on low heat until perfectly tender.

  1. Pan-frying

Pan-frying can be likened to stir-frying, except that the ingredients are not moved around in the pan as often. This technique requires a bit more fat as some ingredients may release their natural oils to help in achieving a satisfyingly crunchy exterior.

Begin by heating enough oil in your wok or pan at a medium-high setting to cover about halfway up the ingredient’s height. The ideal oil temperature should allow the ingredient to sizzle gently without frantically bubbling. Once properly heated, carefully place your ingredient in the oil and allow it to cook until you achieve the desired level of crispiness. Always remember to regulate the heat to prevent burning.

  1. Sauce-making

A sauce ties all the flavors of a Chinese dish together. Mastering the art of sauce-making is essential to create a well-rounded and flavorful meal. In general, you’ll want to reduce the mixture to create a syrupy consistency while making the flavors more concentrated.

For a basic sauce, start by heating oil in your wok, followed by minced ginger, garlic, or onions. Cook until fragrant and then add the seasonings, such as soy sauce, oyster sauce, or hoisin sauce, depending on your preference. Stir gently to incorporate flavors while allowing the liquid to reduce. Finally, add water, or chicken stock, syrup, or beer to achieve the desired consistency.

Conclusion

Developing these basic techniques will open doors to an expansive world of Chinese cuisine. The best part is that once you’re comfortable with these methods, you can create your own variations as you encounter more recipes and ingredients. We hope this post has inspired you to step into the delightful realm of Chinese cooking, and we can’t wait to see (and taste!) what you create. Happy cooking!
