A Beginner's Guide to Mexican Cooking Techniques


Mexican cuisine is a vibrant tapestry of flavors, colors, and textures. For beginners, diving into Mexican cooking can feel overwhelming, but with the right techniques and a bit of guidance, you can create delicious, authentic dishes right from your kitchen. This guide will introduce you to essential Mexican cooking techniques, helping you to master the art of this beloved cuisine.

Understanding the Essentials of Mexican Cooking

Before we delve into specific techniques, it’s important to understand what sets Mexican cooking apart. At its core, Mexican cuisine combines indigenous ingredients with influences from Spanish and other cultures, resulting in a rich and diverse culinary landscape. Key ingredients include:

  • Corn: The foundation of many dishes; used in tortillas, tamales, and more.
  • Chilies: Providing heat and depth of flavor, available in various forms (fresh, dried, powdered).
  • Beans: A staple protein source, often served alongside meals.
  • Herbs and Spices: Fresh cilantro, oregano, and cumin are common.
  • Tomatoes and Onions: Base ingredients for salsas and sauces.

With these essentials in mind, let’s explore the techniques that will help you bring authentic Mexican flavors to your kitchen.

1. The Art of Tortilla Making

Ingredients:

  • 2 cups masa harina (corn flour)
  • 1½ cups warm water
  • A pinch of salt

Technique:

  1. Mixing: In a bowl, combine masa harina and salt. Gradually add warm water until the mixture forms a soft dough.
  2. Kneading: Knead the dough for about 5 minutes until it is smooth and pliable.
  3. Shaping: Divide the dough into small balls (about the size of a golf ball).
  4. Pressing: Use a tortilla press or a heavy skillet to flatten each ball into thin discs.
  5. Cooking: Heat a comal or skillet over medium-high heat. Cook each tortilla for about 30 seconds on each side, until lightly toasted.

Freshly made tortillas can elevate any dish, whether it’s tacos, enchiladas, or a simple quesadilla.

2. Roasting and Toasting Ingredients

Roasting and toasting are fundamental methods in Mexican cooking that enhance the flavors of various ingredients.

Chilies:

  • Technique: Toast dry chilies on a hot skillet until they become fragrant and slightly darkened. This process intensifies their flavor, making them perfect for salsas or sauces.

Vegetables:

  • Technique: Roasting tomatoes, onions, and garlic adds a depth of flavor. Simply toss them with a bit of oil, season with salt, and roast in the oven at 400°F (200°C) until caramelized.

Example:

Roasted tomato salsa can be made by blending roasted tomatoes, garlic, onions, cilantro, lime juice, and salt for a fresh condiment that pairs beautifully with many dishes.

3. Sautéing and Stir-Frying

Sautéing allows for quick cooking while preserving the freshness of ingredients. It’s commonly used for preparing the base of many Mexican dishes.

Technique:

  1. Heat Oil: In a skillet, heat a couple of tablespoons of vegetable oil over medium heat.
  2. Add Aromatics: Sauté finely chopped onions and garlic until translucent.
  3. Incorporate Vegetables or Proteins: Add chopped vegetables (like bell peppers, zucchini, or mushrooms) or proteins (like chicken or beef), cooking until tender.

Example:

This technique is essential for making fajitas. Sauté sliced bell peppers, onions, and marinated chicken, then serve with warm tortillas and toppings.

4. Making Salsas and Sauces

Salsas are a cornerstone of Mexican cuisine, serving as condiments, toppings, or even main ingredients. The key to a great salsa lies in the balance of flavors and freshness.

Common Salsas:

  • Pico de Gallo: A fresh salsa made with diced tomatoes, onions, cilantro, lime juice, and jalapeños.
  • Salsa Verde: Made with tomatillos, cilantro, onions, and jalapeños, this green salsa is great for tacos and grilled meats.

Technique:

  1. Choose Your Base: Decide between fresh or roasted ingredients based on the type of salsa you want.
  2. Blend: For a smoother texture, blend ingredients in a food processor; for a chunkier salsa, chop ingredients by hand.
  3. Season: Don’t forget to taste and adjust seasoning with salt and lime juice.

5. Braising and Slow Cooking

Braising is an essential technique for creating flavorful, tender meat dishes in Mexican cuisine, such as barbacoa or birria.

Technique:

  1. Sear the Meat: Start by browning your meat (like beef chuck or pork shoulder) in a hot pot with oil for flavor.
  2. Add Aromatics: Include onions, garlic, and spices, sautéing until fragrant.
  3. Liquid Addition: Pour in broth, tomatoes, or a mixture of both, ensuring the meat is partially submerged.
  4. Cover and Cook: Simmer on low heat or transfer to a slow cooker for several hours until the meat is fork-tender.

Example:

Barbacoa is often made with beef chuck braised with chilies, spices, and vinegar, resulting in tender, flavorful meat perfect for tacos or burritos.

6. Preparing Common Dishes

Tacos

The simplicity of tacos allows for creativity. Use any protein (like carne asada, carnitas, or grilled veggies), top with fresh salsa, onions, and cilantro, and serve in warm tortillas.

Enchiladas

Fill tortillas with shredded chicken or cheese, roll them up, and place in a baking dish. Top with red or green sauce and cheese, then bake until bubbly.

Tamales

A delightful treat made by wrapping masa dough around a filling (like pulled pork or cheese) in corn husks and steaming them until cooked through.

7. Mastering the Use of Spices and Herbs

Mexican cuisine relies heavily on spices and herbs to create depth and complexity in dishes.

Technique:

  • Toast Spices: Briefly toast spices like cumin and coriander in a dry skillet to release their essential oils before using them in your dishes.
  • Fresh Herbs: Always add fresh herbs like cilantro towards the end of cooking to maintain their bright flavors.

Example:

A classic mole sauce combines ingredients like chocolate, chilies, nuts, and various spices, showcasing the importance of balancing flavors and textures.

8. Achieving Perfect Rice and Beans

Rice and beans are staples in Mexican cuisine, serving as sides or bases for many dishes.

Mexican Rice:

  1. Sauté: In a saucepan, heat oil and sauté chopped onions until translucent. Add rice and toast for a few minutes.
  2. Add Liquid: Pour in chicken or vegetable broth, along with tomatoes and spices. Cover and simmer until the rice is cooked.

Refried Beans:

  1. Cook Beans: Start with soaked pinto beans, boil until tender, then drain.
  2. Mash and Fry: In a skillet, sauté onions in oil, add the beans, and mash them, frying until creamy.

9. Embracing Fermentation and Pickling

Fermentation and pickling are integral to Mexican cuisine, adding tang and depth to dishes.

Technique:

  1. Pickling: For quick pickled onions, combine sliced onions with vinegar, water, salt, and sugar. Let sit for at least 30 minutes.
  2. Fermentation: Try fermenting salsa or vegetables like jalapeños for a unique flavor profile.

10. Presentation and Garnishing

Presentation is key in Mexican cooking. Use colorful ingredients and unique serving dishes to create an enticing display.

Techniques:

  • Garnishing: Top dishes with fresh herbs, crumbled cheese, or avocado slices for added color and flavor.
  • Plating: Use bowls for salsas and sauces, and arrange tacos or enchiladas neatly on a plate with accompaniments on the side.

Conclusion

Embarking on a journey into Mexican cooking can be a rewarding and delicious experience. By mastering these techniques, you’ll not only gain the skills to create authentic dishes but also a deeper appreciation for this rich culinary tradition. Remember, cooking is about experimentation and enjoyment—so don’t be afraid to infuse your unique touch into each recipe you try. Happy cooking!
