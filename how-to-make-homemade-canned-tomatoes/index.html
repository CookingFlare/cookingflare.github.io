How to Make Homemade Canned Tomatoes


Preserving the flavors of ripe, juicy, garden-fresh tomatoes is one of the most fulfilling ways to enjoy their taste throughout the year. Not only does home-canning allow you to savor the reward of your gardening efforts during winter, but it also gives you full control over the ingredients and quality of your pantry staples. Whether you’re planning to use the canned tomatoes for comforting soups, irresistible pasta sauces, or hearty stews, this guide provides you with everything you need to know to craft the perfect batch of homemade canned tomatoes. So, roll up your sleeves, grab your apron, and let’s dive into the art of preserving!

Selecting the Perfect Tomatoes

The foundation of great canned tomatoes lies in selecting the right variety and quality of tomatoes. Opt for meaty and flavorful types such as Roma, San Marzano, or plum tomatoes. These varieties are less watery and have a rich, concentrated flavor ideal for canning. When choosing, look for tomatoes that are firm, ripe, and free from blemishes, bruises, or cracks. Overripe or underripe tomatoes can compromise the texture and taste of the canned product, so take the time to pick the best ones.

Preparing the Ingredients and Tools

Ingredients:

  • Ripe, fresh tomatoes (approximately 20 pounds will yield 7 quarts of canned tomatoes)
  • Fresh lemon juice or citric acid (for acidity balance)
  • Salt (optional, for flavor enhancement)

Tools:

  • Large stockpot for blanching
  • Canning jars with lids and rings
  • Jar lifter
  • Canning funnel
  • Large bowl for ice water
  • Clean towels
  • Ladle or large spoon
  • Canning rack (essential for boiling water canner setup)
  • Boiling water canner or large pot with a lid
  • Paring knife

Proper preparation is key to both the success and safety of the canning process. Make sure your jars, lids, and tools are thoroughly cleaned and sterilized before beginning.

Preparing Your Tomatoes

Step 1: Wash and Core

Start by washing your tomatoes under cool running water to remove any dirt or residue. If there are any stems left, remove them, and then use a paring knife to core each tomato, taking out the hard area near the stem.

Step 2: Blanch and Peel

Blanching is a simple way to loosen the skins, making peeling a breeze. Bring a large pot of water to boil and carefully lower the tomatoes into the water using a slotted spoon. Let them boil for about 45 to 60 seconds or until the skin begins to curl or crack. Immediately transfer the tomatoes into a large bowl of ice water to stop the cooking process. After cooling for a minute, peel the skin off each tomato. It should come off easily; use your fingers or a knife to assist if necessary.

Step 3: Optional Preparation

Depending on your preference, you can either leave your tomatoes whole, cut them into halves or quarters, or crush them slightly for easier packing in jars. If you like your tomatoes without seeds, this is the time to scoop them out with a spoon.

Sterilizing Jars and Equipment

Clean jars and lids are essential for preserving your canned tomatoes safely and effectively. Wash the jars, lids, and rings in hot soapy water, rinse thoroughly, and sterilize by boiling them in a pot of water for 10 minutes. Let them dry on a clean towel while you prepare the tomatoes.

Packing Tomatoes into Jars

Step 1: Add Acid

Before packing the jars, add 2 tablespoons of bottled lemon juice or ½ teaspoon of citric acid to every quart jar. This ensures that the canned tomatoes reach the necessary acidity level to prevent the growth of harmful bacteria like botulism.

Step 2: Pack the Tomatoes

Using a canning funnel, pack the prepared tomatoes tightly into the sterilized jars. If using crushed tomatoes, scoop them in and press gently to minimize gaps. Leave about ½ inch of headspace between the tomatoes and the rim of the jar. If desired, sprinkle a pinch of salt for extra flavor.

Step 3: Add Liquid

Fill the remaining space in each jar with boiling water or hot tomato juice until the tomatoes are fully covered, maintaining the ½ inch of headspace. Use a spoon or knife to gently stir and release any trapped air bubbles.

Sealing the Jars

Wipe the rims of the jars with a damp towel to ensure there is no residue or food particles that could prevent an airtight seal. Place the sterilized lids on top and screw on the rings until finger-tight. Do not overtighten, as air needs to escape during processing.

Processing the Jars

Step 1: Prepare the Boiling Water Canner

Fill your boiling water canner or large pot about halfway with water and heat it until it reaches a gentle boil. Place the jars onto the rack inside the canner so that they do not touch each other. Make sure the jars are fully submerged, with at least 1 inch of water covering them.

Step 2: Process the Jars

Process the jars in boiling water for 40 minutes for quart-sized jars or 35 minutes for pint-sized jars. Adjust processing time based on your altitude—consult a reliable canning guide for specific adjustments.

Step 3: Cool and Test the Seal

Once processed, use the jar lifter to carefully remove the jars and place them on a clean towel or cooling rack. Let them cool undisturbed for 12 to 24 hours. You’ll hear the satisfying “pop” sound that indicates a secure seal. Check each jar by pressing the center of the lid—if it doesn’t spring back, the seal is successful. Any jars that didn’t seal properly should be refrigerated and used within a few days.

Storing Your Homemade Canned Tomatoes

Label your jars with the date you canned them and store them in a cool, dark, and dry place. Properly sealed jars can last up to a year, allowing you to enjoy that garden-fresh taste long after the growing season is over.

Cooking Ideas for Canned Tomatoes

One of the joys of homemade canned tomatoes is their versatility in the kitchen. Use your canned tomatoes to make a savory marinara sauce, enrich a creamy tomato bisque, or elevate a rustic ratatouille. They’re also fantastic for casseroles, chili, and slow-cooked roasts.

Troubleshooting Tips for Beginners

  • Cloudy Liquid: A cloudy appearance may occur due to starch content in certain tomato varieties. This does not affect the safety of the canned tomatoes.
  • Separation: Sometimes, the solids and liquids in the jar will separate after processing—this is natural and nothing to worry about.
  • Non-Sealing Jars: If a jar doesn’t seal, try transferring the tomatoes to a new jar and repeating the processing step.

Why Homemade Canned Tomatoes?

Homemade canned tomatoes aren’t just a way to save money or preserve your harvest—they’re an opportunity to create a pantry staple that reflects your taste and style. You can adjust the acidity, salt, and texture to suit your dishes perfectly, making every meal memorable.

Take pride in knowing exactly what goes into your food, fearlessly crafting homemade goods that add depth and richness to your culinary creations. So embrace this timeless tradition and relish the satisfaction of popping open a jar of your expertly preserved tomatoes when you need them most. Happy canning!
