Korean Braised Chicken with Potatoes (Dakdori) Recipe


Korean cuisine is known for its complex flavors, vibrant colors, and the harmonious balance of salty, sweet, spicy, and savory notes. One of the most beloved comfort food dishes in Korea is Dakdori, a mouthwatering braised chicken dish that incorporates tender chicken, hearty potatoes, and a flavorful sauce. This dish is perfect for anyone looking to experience the rich and satisfying flavors of Korean cooking at home. In this post, we’ll walk you through the process of making Dakdori (Korean Braised Chicken with Potatoes) from scratch, providing tips and tricks to ensure your meal is nothing short of spectacular.

What is Dakdori?

Dakdori (닭도리) is a traditional Korean braised chicken dish that is both comforting and packed with flavor. “Dak” means chicken in Korean, and “dori” is a colloquial term for chicken legs, referring to the drumsticks or thighs commonly used in this dish. The chicken is braised with potatoes, vegetables, and a savory, slightly spicy sauce made from gochujang (Korean chili paste), soy sauce, garlic, and sugar. The result is tender, juicy chicken paired with perfectly cooked potatoes, making it an irresistible meal for any occasion.

Key Ingredients for Dakdori

To make Dakdori at home, you’ll need a handful of ingredients that are easy to find at your local Asian grocery store. Here’s what you’ll need:

For the Braised Chicken:

  • Chicken – Bone-in, skin-on chicken thighs or drumsticks are ideal for this recipe. They are juicy, tender, and absorb the flavors of the sauce beautifully.
  • Potatoes – Use starchy potatoes, such as russets, which will become soft and creamy once braised.
  • Onion – Adds sweetness and depth to the sauce.
  • Garlic – Fresh garlic cloves, minced, to infuse the dish with aromatic flavor.
  • Ginger – A small knob of ginger, grated or finely chopped, provides a nice hint of warmth to the dish.
  • Green onions – For garnish and added freshness.

For the Sauce:

  • Gochujang (Korean chili paste) – This ingredient is essential for creating the spicy and umami-rich flavor that characterizes Korean braised chicken. Gochujang is a fermented chili paste made from red chili peppers, fermented soybeans, rice, and salt.
  • Soy sauce – A key ingredient for the base of the braising liquid, adding saltiness and richness to the sauce.
  • Sugar – Brown sugar or regular granulated sugar works best to balance the spiciness and saltiness of the dish.
  • Sesame oil – Adds a toasted, nutty flavor to the sauce.
  • Rice wine – Optional, but helps to tenderize the chicken and deepen the flavors.
  • Water or chicken broth – To create the braising liquid that will simmer the chicken and potatoes to perfection.

How to Make Dakdori (Korean Braised Chicken with Potatoes)

Step 1: Preparing the Ingredients

Before you begin cooking, it’s important to prep all of your ingredients. Start by peeling and cutting the potatoes into bite-sized chunks. Be sure to rinse them in cold water to remove any excess starch, which will help them cook evenly.

Next, slice the onion and mince the garlic and ginger. If you haven’t done so already, cut the chicken into pieces, or leave it whole, depending on your preference. Chicken thighs or drumsticks are best, as they will remain tender during the braising process.

Step 2: Making the Braising Sauce

To make the flavorful braising sauce, combine the following ingredients in a bowl:

  • 2 tablespoons of gochujang
  • 3 tablespoons of soy sauce
  • 1 tablespoon of sugar (brown or white)
  • 1 tablespoon of sesame oil
  • 1 tablespoon of rice wine (optional)
  • 1 tablespoon of minced garlic
  • 1 tablespoon of minced ginger
  • 1/2 cup of water or chicken broth

Stir the mixture until the gochujang and sugar dissolve completely, creating a thick, flavorful sauce. The balance of spicy, sweet, and savory in the sauce will infuse the chicken and potatoes as they braise.

Step 3: Searing the Chicken

In a large, heavy-bottomed pot or Dutch oven, heat a tablespoon of vegetable oil over medium-high heat. Once the oil is hot, add the chicken pieces skin-side down. Sear the chicken for 3-4 minutes on each side until the skin turns golden brown and crispy. This step is essential for locking in flavor and texture before the braising process.

Once the chicken is seared, remove it from the pot and set it aside.

Step 4: Sautéing the Onions and Garlic

In the same pot, add a bit more oil if necessary and sauté the chopped onion, garlic, and ginger. Stir-fry them for about 2-3 minutes until the onion becomes translucent and fragrant. This step will bring out the natural sweetness of the onion and help deepen the flavors of the dish.

Step 5: Braising the Chicken and Potatoes

Return the chicken to the pot, and add the potatoes around the chicken pieces. Pour the prepared sauce over the chicken and potatoes, making sure everything is evenly coated.

Next, add 1-2 cups of water or chicken broth to the pot. The liquid should cover about half of the chicken and potatoes, as the braising process will allow the liquid to reduce and concentrate in flavor.

Bring the mixture to a simmer over medium heat, then reduce the heat to low. Cover the pot with a lid and let it simmer for about 40-50 minutes, stirring occasionally. During this time, the chicken will become tender and juicy, while the potatoes will absorb all the flavors of the sauce.

Step 6: Adjusting the Sauce and Serving

Once the chicken and potatoes are fully cooked and tender, taste the sauce. If you find that it needs more sweetness, add a bit more sugar. If you’d like more heat, add extra gochujang. You can also adjust the saltiness with more soy sauce, depending on your preference.

After the sauce has reached your desired flavor, turn off the heat. Serve the Dakdori hot, garnished with freshly chopped green onions for a burst of freshness and color.

Tips for the Perfect Dakdori

  • Choose the right cut of chicken: Bone-in, skin-on chicken thighs or drumsticks are best for this recipe. These cuts stay moist and tender during the braising process, and the skin adds flavor and texture.
  • Don’t skip the searing step: Searing the chicken before braising adds depth of flavor and helps to create a crispy, golden skin.
  • Use starchy potatoes: Russet potatoes work best in this dish as they break down slightly while braising, absorbing the flavors of the sauce and becoming tender and creamy.
  • Adjust the heat level: If you prefer a spicier dish, increase the amount of gochujang. If you’re sensitive to heat, start with less and taste as you go.
  • Garnish generously: The green onions not only add a pop of color but also a fresh, zesty contrast to the rich, braised chicken.

Serving Suggestions

Dakdori is typically served with steamed white rice, which helps balance the intense flavors of the braised chicken. The rice soaks up the delicious sauce, making every bite even more satisfying. You can also pair Dakdori with a side of kimchi for an extra burst of tangy, spicy flavor.

If you’re looking for a more substantial meal, you could serve it alongside some stir-fried vegetables or a Korean-style salad with a sesame dressing to complement the richness of the chicken.

Storing and Reheating Dakdori

If you have leftovers, Dakdori keeps well in the refrigerator for up to 3-4 days. Simply store it in an airtight container. To reheat, place the chicken and potatoes in a pot with a little water or chicken broth to prevent the sauce from drying out. Heat it over low heat until warmed through.

Dakdori can also be frozen for up to a month. Make sure to let the dish cool completely before freezing, and store it in a freezer-safe container. When you’re ready to eat it, thaw it overnight in the refrigerator and reheat it as usual.

Final Thoughts

Dakdori is a flavorful, comforting, and hearty dish that brings the essence of Korean home cooking right into your kitchen. With tender chicken, perfectly braised potatoes, and a savory-sweet sauce, it’s a dish that’s sure to please your family and friends. Whether you’re looking for a weeknight dinner or a dish to impress at a dinner party, Dakdori is the perfect choice.

Give this recipe a try, and enjoy the bold, complex flavors of Korean cuisine in your own home.
