The Best Middle Eastern Sauces and Condiments: Recipes Included


Middle Eastern cuisine is known for its vibrant flavors, aromatic spices, and, notably, its array of exquisite sauces and condiments. These staple accompaniments not only enhance the main dishes but also add a touch of authenticity to every bite. From creamy tahini to tangy labneh, these condiments can elevate even the simplest meals to gourmet levels. Below, we’ll explore some of the most beloved Middle Eastern sauces and provide you with easy-to-make recipes so you can bring these traditional flavors into your own kitchen.

Tahini Sauce: A Versatile Classic

Tahini sauce is a cornerstone of Middle Eastern cooking. Made from ground sesame seeds, it’s creamy, nutty, and slightly tangy. This sauce pairs beautifully with falafel, grilled meats, roasted vegetables, and even as a base for salad dressings. Here’s how you can make it at home:

Ingredients:

  • 1/2 cup tahini paste
  • 1/4 cup freshly squeezed lemon juice
  • 1 clove garlic, minced
  • 1/4 cup cold water (more as needed)
  • 1/4 tsp salt

Recipe:

  1. In a mixing bowl, combine the tahini paste, lemon juice, garlic, and salt.
  2. Gradually add cold water while stirring continuously. The mixture may seem to seize up, but keep stirring until it transforms into a smooth, creamy sauce.
  3. Adjust salt to taste and add more water if needed to reach your desired consistency.

Tahini sauce can be stored in an airtight container in the refrigerator for up to one week. Whether drizzled over roasted eggplant or used as a dip for pita bread, it’s an indispensable addition to a Middle Eastern platter.

Toum: Lebanese Garlic Sauce

Toum, meaning “garlic” in Arabic, is a bold and fluffy emulsion of garlic, oil, and lemon juice. This garlic sauce is a flavor bomb and a must-try for anyone who loves robust flavors. Traditionally served with shawarma, grilled chicken, or kebabs, toum is easy to fall in love with.

Ingredients:

  • 1 cup garlic cloves, peeled
  • 2 cups neutral oil (vegetable or sunflower)
  • 1/4 cup lemon juice
  • 1/4 cup cold water
  • 1/2 tsp salt

Recipe:

  1. Place the garlic cloves and salt in a food processor. Blend until the garlic is finely minced, scraping down the sides as needed.
  2. With the processor running, slowly drizzle in the oil in a very thin stream.
  3. Alternate between adding oil and a tablespoon of lemon juice at a time until a thick, creamy consistency forms.
  4. If the mixture becomes too thick, add a little cold water to loosen it during blending.

Toum can be stored in the refrigerator for up to 3 weeks. A little goes a long way, making it the perfect complement to grilled dishes or as a bold sandwich spread.

Harissa: A Spicy Tunisian Condiment

For those who crave a hint of heat in their meals, harissa is the answer. This North African chili paste, with its smoky, spicy complexity, works wonders as a marinade, a dip, or a flavor booster for soups and stews.

Ingredients:

  • 6-8 dried red chilies
  • 3 garlic cloves
  • 2 tsp ground cumin
  • 2 tsp ground coriander
  • 1 tsp smoked paprika
  • 4 tbsp olive oil
  • 2 tbsp tomato paste
  • Juice of 1 lemon

Recipe:

  1. Soak the dried chilies in warm water for about 20 minutes, then drain and deseed them.
  2. In a food processor, blend the chilies, garlic, cumin, coriander, paprika, and lemon juice into a rough paste.
  3. Slowly add the olive oil while blending until a smooth consistency is achieved.
  4. Stir in the tomato paste for added depth of flavor.

Store harissa in a clean jar topped with a thin layer of olive oil to preserve its freshness. It will keep in the refrigerator for up to one month. Harissa’s spicy kick can transform your couscous, grilled veggies, or even scrambled eggs.

Labneh: Tangy Yogurt Cheese

Labneh, a strained yogurt cheese, is as simple as it is delectable. Its creamy texture and tangy flavor make it a versatile spread, dip, or even a base for other sauces.

Ingredients:

  • 2 cups full-fat plain yogurt
  • 1/2 tsp salt
  • Olive oil (for drizzling)

Recipe:

  1. Mix the yogurt and salt in a bowl.
  2. Pour the mixture into a cheesecloth-lined strainer set over a bowl. Tie the cheesecloth into a bundle, and let the yogurt drain in the refrigerator for 18-24 hours.
  3. Once strained, transfer the thickened labneh to a container and drizzle with olive oil.

Labneh can be topped with za’atar, chili flakes, or fresh herbs before serving. Spread it on flatbreads, serve it with fresh vegetables, or use it as a base for mezze platters.

Amba: Pickled Mango Sauce

Amba, a pickled mango sauce, is a tangy and slightly spicy condiment popular in Iraqi and Israeli cuisines. Often paired with shawarma, falafel, or sabich sandwiches, its fruity sharpness makes it utterly unique.

Ingredients:

  • 2 unripe mangoes, peeled and sliced
  • 1/4 cup white vinegar
  • 1 tsp turmeric
  • 1 tsp ground fenugreek
  • 1 tsp chili powder
  • 1 tbsp mustard seeds
  • 2 tbsp olive oil
  • 1/2 cup water
  • 1/2 tsp salt

Recipe:

  1. Heat the olive oil in a pan and toast the mustard seeds until they pop.
  2. Add turmeric, fenugreek, and chili powder, stirring for 30 seconds.
  3. Add mango slices, vinegar, water, and salt. Simmer until the mangoes soften and the liquid thickens.
  4. Blend the mixture into a smooth sauce or leave it chunky, depending on your preference.

Amba can be stored in the refrigerator for up to two weeks. Its tangy-spicy profile is the perfect foil for rich, savory dishes.

Zhoug: A Cilantro-Based Herb Sauce

Zhoug, often referred to as Yemenite hot sauce, is an herbal and spicy paste made with fresh green herbs. Its fresh, citrusy heat complements practically anything, from grilled meats to fluffy pita bread.

Ingredients:

  • 1 large bunch fresh cilantro
  • 2-3 green chilies, seeds removed (adjust for spice level)
  • 2 cloves garlic
  • 1/2 tsp ground cumin
  • 1/2 tsp ground cardamom
  • 2 tbsp olive oil
  • 1-2 tbsp lemon juice
  • Salt to taste

Recipe:

  1. Combine cilantro, chilies, garlic, cumin, cardamom, and salt in a blender or food processor.
  2. Blend until finely chopped. Gradually add olive oil and lemon juice while continuing to blend until a slightly chunky paste forms.
  3. Taste and adjust seasoning or acidity as needed.

Zhoug is best served fresh and can be stored for up to one week in the refrigerator. Use it as a dip, a marinade, or a topping for hummus and roasted vegetables.

Middle Eastern sauces and condiments are at the heart of what makes this cuisine so expressive and bold. With these approachable recipes, you’ll be able to explore a world of flavors from the comfort of your own kitchen. Whether you’re preparing an elaborate feast or simply looking to elevate your weeknight meals, these sauces will deliver unrivaled taste and authenticity.

So grab your ingredients, get cooking, and transform your meals with these iconic Middle Eastern creations.
