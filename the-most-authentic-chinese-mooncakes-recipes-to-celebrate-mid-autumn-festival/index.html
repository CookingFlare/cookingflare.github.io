The Most Authentic Chinese Mooncakes Recipes to Celebrate Mid-Autumn Festival


The Mid-Autumn Festival, also known as the Moon Festival, is one of the most significant celebrations in Chinese culture, signaling the end of the harvest season. It is traditionally observed on the 15th day of the 8th month according to the lunar calendar, during which families and friends gather to enjoy mooncakes, moon-viewing, and other delightful activities under a full moon. If you’re fascinated by the rich history and flavors of this festive season, here are some authentic and delicious Chinese mooncakes recipes for you to craft your own Mid-Autumn celebration.

The Art of Chinese Mooncakes

Mooncakes have been a part of this joyous festival since the Song Dynasty (960-1279 AD). The round shape of the mooncakes symbolizes reunion and completeness, while intricate patterns in the mooncake crust known as “dongpo cake” represent the glowing moon and its shimmering reflection on the water. These symbolic meanings only further amplify the magical charm and deep cultural roots of mooncakes.

There are two major types of Chinese mooncakes - the skin or crust (馅皮) and the filling (馅料). The skin of mooncakes can vary from soft and chewy to slightly crisp, and the fillings consist of sweet and savory elements. The most popular mooncake fillings include:

  • Red bean paste
  • Lotus seed paste
  • Jujube and date paste
  • Sweet taro paste
  • Salted duck egg yolk

For those seeking more savory options, meat-based fillings like pork belly or char siu (barbecue pork) may also be fancied. Each region in China celebrates Mid-Autumn Festival with its unique flavor profiles and spins on traditional recipes.

Authentic Chinese Mooncakes Recipes

1. Traditional Lotus Seed Paste Mooncakes

For the lotus seed paste:

  • 200 grams of lye water (2½ tbsp)
  • 500 grams of lotus seeds
  • 1 cup of water
  • 50 grams of sugar
  • A few drops of vanilla essence

For the crust:

  • 250 grams of low-protein flour
  • 75 grams of unsalted butter
  • 75 grams of lye water (1 large egg’s worth)

Preparation:

  1. Soak the lotus seeds in cold water overnight (at least 8 hours or up to one full day). Once fully hydrated, discard the soaking water and rinse well. Combine the soaked lotus seeds and fresh water in a pot and bring to a boil. Reduce heat and gently simmer for about 2 hours until the seeds are fully cooked and tender. Do not drain; let the seeds cool within the water.
  2. Blend the cooked lotus seeds in the water, pushing them through a sieve to extract as much paste as possible. In a large pot, combine the lye water and sugar, heating gently until the sugar dissolves. Stir in the lotus seed paste and cook over low heat for 15-20 minutes, stirring frequently. Allow the paste to cool to room temperature and mix in the vanilla essence. Store the paste in an airtight container in the refrigerator until ready to use.
  3. To make the crust, cut the unsalted butter into small cubes and let it come to room temperature. In a large mixing bowl, sift together the low-protein flour and salt. Make a well in the center of the dry mix and add the butter cubes, followed by the lye water. Use your hands or a pastry cutter to work the butter and flour together until a crumbly dough forms.
  4. Gently knead the dough until smooth and pliable. Cover and let the dough rest in a cool place for about 30 minutes. If the dough becomes too soft while working, place it in the refrigerator for 10-15 minutes until firm enough to handle.
  5. Divide the lotus seed paste into equal portions (weighing about 50-60 grams each for the average mooncake size). Remove a portion of the dough for the crust, flatten it gently, and wrap it around the filling ball. Seal any exposed edges by pinching and folding the crust together, creating a neat and smooth appearance. Repeat the process for each mooncake. If you have a mooncake mold, apply a thin layer of egg wash to the mold and press the shaped mooncake gently against the surface for imprinting. Use a separate mold for each type of filling if you plan to enjoy a variety of flavors for your Mid-Autumn celebration.

2. Yolk-Savory Char Siu Mooncakes

For the char siu filling:

  • 350 grams of marinated pork belly (or store-bought char siu)
  • 4 salted duck eggs
  • 50 grams of unsalted butter

For the crust:

  • Refer to the crust recipe from the traditional lotus seed paste mooncakes

Preparation:

  1. Cut the marinated pork and salted duck eggs into small, bite-sized pieces, and set aside.
  2. Follow the crust recipe from the previous section and roll it into a cylindrical shape. Cut equal-sized portions for both the crust and lotus seed paste mooncakes (about 50-60 grams each). Flatten each piece of dough gently, wrap a portion of the savory filling inside, and seal by pinching and folding the crust edges.
  3. If you wish to use a mooncake mold, apply a thin layer of egg wash to the mold’s surface and gently press the shaped mooncake to create the intricate designs. Use separate molds for each type of filling for an assortment in your Mid-Autumn celebration.

Unlocking the Secrets of Chinese Mooncakes

Though the recipes above offer a path to creating traditional Chinese mooncakes at home, mastering this delightful dessert takes practice and patience. From achieving the delicate balance of sweetness, savory combinations, and crisp or chewy textures, there’s a lot more to mastering the fine art of mooncakes than meets the eye.

For those who can’t find or choose not to use lye water or low-protein flour, alternative suggestions may include all-purpose flour and rice syrup as substitutes. However, please note that the substitutions may impact the final texture and flavor.

Keep experimenting, sharing with friends and family, and best of all – enjoy the time-honored tradition of crafting mooncakes while creating brand-new memories of the Mid-Autumn Festival with those you love. Happy baking, and here’s wishing you a wonderful and scrumptious celebration.
