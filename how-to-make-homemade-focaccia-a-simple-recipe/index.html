How to Make Homemade Focaccia: A Simple Recipe


Focaccia is the epitome of rustic Italian bread—crispy on the outside, soft and chewy on the inside, and infused with fragrant herbs and olive oil. This versatile bread is perfect as a snack, a side for soup or salad, or even turned into a sandwich. Making focaccia at home is not only satisfying but surprisingly simple. Whether you’re a beginner or an experienced baker, this step-by-step guide will walk you through crafting a classic homemade focaccia that fills your kitchen with irresistible aromas and delights your taste buds.

Why Homemade Focaccia?

Commercial focaccia can often lack the depth of flavor and texture found in a freshly baked loaf. When you make focaccia from scratch, you control every ingredient—from the quality of the flour and oil to the choice of herbs and toppings. Homemade focaccia has a tender crumb with irresistibly crispy edges, a perfect balance achieved through a good dough recipe and a bit of patience.

Plus, baking focaccia is a wonderful way to engage with a time-honored culinary tradition and brings a touch of Italy into your home.

Ingredients You’ll Need

For the dough:

  • 4 cups (500g) all-purpose flour or bread flour
  • 1 packet (7g) active dry yeast
  • 1 ¾ cup (420ml) warm water (about 110°F or 43°C)
  • 2 teaspoons sugar
  • 1 teaspoon salt
  • ¼ cup (60ml) extra virgin olive oil, plus extra for drizzling and pan greasing

For the topping:

  • Coarse sea salt or flaked salt
  • Fresh rosemary sprigs (or other herbs like thyme or oregano)
  • Optional: sliced olives, cherry tomatoes, caramelized onions, or garlic cloves

Tools Needed

  • Large mixing bowl
  • Baking sheet (preferably rimmed)
  • Kitchen towel or plastic wrap
  • Your hands (for kneading and dimpling)
  • Measuring cups and spoons

Step-by-Step Instructions

1. Activate the Yeast

Start by dissolving the sugar in the warm water. Sprinkle the yeast over the surface and allow it to sit for 5-10 minutes. You’ll know it’s ready when the mixture becomes frothy and bubbly—a sign the yeast is alive and active. This step kick-starts fermentation and ensures your focaccia will rise beautifully.

2. Mix the Dough

In a large bowl, combine the flour and salt. Make a well in the center and pour in the frothy yeast mixture and olive oil. Using your hands or a spoon, slowly mix the ingredients until the flour is fully incorporated and a shaggy dough begins to form.

3. Knead the Dough

Flour a clean surface lightly and transfer the dough onto it. Knead vigorously for about 10 minutes until the dough becomes elastic, smooth, and slightly tacky but not sticky. Proper kneading develops the gluten structure, which is essential for that characteristic focaccia chewiness.

If you have a stand mixer with a dough hook, you can knead on medium speed for about 7 minutes instead.

4. First Rise

Oil a large bowl generously with olive oil to prevent sticking. Place the dough in the bowl, turning it to coat all sides with oil. Cover loosely with a damp kitchen towel or plastic wrap, and let it rise in a warm, draft-free location for about 1 to 1 ½ hours. The dough should double in size.

5. Prepare the Pan

Once risen, grease a rimmed baking sheet with olive oil. This generous coating helps develop the focaccia’s famous crisp bottom and keeps the bread moist. Transfer your risen dough onto the pan and spread it out evenly with your fingers to fill the entire surface. Don’t worry if it tears or resists stretching at first; let it rest for 5 minutes and try again.

6. Second Rise

Cover the dough again and let it rise for another 20-30 minutes. This additional proofing ensures a light, airy crumb once baked.

7. Create Dimples and Add Toppings

Here’s where focaccia gets its signature look. Using your fingertips, press deep dimples all over the surface of the dough without breaking through. This creates little wells that trap olive oil and toppings, infusing every bite with flavor.

Drizzle more olive oil generously over the top. Sprinkle coarse sea salt evenly, and scatter fresh rosemary sprigs or your choice of herbs. If you’re adding toppings like olives, cherry tomatoes, or garlic cloves, arrange them now.

8. Bake the Focaccia

Preheat your oven to 450°F (230°C). Bake the focaccia in the middle rack for about 20-25 minutes or until the top is golden brown and crispy.

You’ll notice the delightful aroma of olive oil and rosemary filling your kitchen as the bread bakes—an irresistible signal it’s nearly done.

9. Cool and Serve

Once baked, remove the focaccia from the oven and let it cool slightly on a wire rack. This resting period allows steam to escape and the crust to set firmly. Serve warm or at room temperature, sliced into squares or strips.

Focaccia is delightful on its own, paired with olive oil for dipping, or alongside your favorite soups and salads.

Tips and Variations

  • Flour Choice: Use bread flour for a chewier texture or all-purpose for a lighter crumb. Some bakers mix in whole wheat flour for nuttiness.
  • Olive Oil: Quality olive oil makes a significant difference in taste. Use extra virgin olive oil for a rich, fruity flavor.
  • Herbs: Fresh rosemary is classic, but feel free to experiment with thyme, oregano, or sage.
  • Toppings: Get creative! Add caramelized onions, sun-dried tomatoes, sautéed mushrooms, or even thin slices of potato for variety.
  • Cold Fermentation: For deeper flavor, after the first rise, refrigerate the dough overnight. Bring it back to room temperature before shaping and the second rise.
  • Salt: Coarse sea salt or flaky Maldon salt adds a pleasant crunch and burst of flavor post-bake.

Troubleshooting Common Issues

  • Dense or Heavy Bread: If your focaccia is dense, it may be due to under-kneading or insufficient rising time. Ensure the yeast is active and the dough has doubled in size before baking.
  • Dry Texture: Using too much flour or baking at too high a heat can cause dryness. Stick to recommended measurements and oven temperatures.
  • Lack of Flavor: Don’t skimp on olive oil or salt. These two ingredients add richness and amplify the overall taste.
  • Focaccia is Too Flat: Try adding a little extra yeast or double the rising time. Also, handle the dough gently when stretching to retain the air bubbles.

The Joy of Homemade Bread

Making focaccia at home is more than just a cooking task—it’s a sensory experience. From the tactile pleasure of kneading dough through the warm glow of the oven to the moment you tear into a fresh slice, each step connects you with traditions that have nourished families for centuries.

With this simple recipe, no fancy equipment or obscure ingredients are needed. Just a bit of time, patience, and a love of good food. Once you’ve mastered this base recipe, you’ll find endless variations to make focaccia your own.

Why not invite friends over for a focaccia-tasting party? Pair different herb and topping combinations, or experiment with dipping oils infused with garlic, chili, or balsamic vinegar. Homemade focaccia adds not just flavor but warmth and conviviality to any meal.

Enjoy baking and happy eating!
