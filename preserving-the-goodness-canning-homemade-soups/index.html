Preserving the Goodness: Canning Homemade Soups


There’s something truly satisfying about making a big batch of homemade soup. Whether it’s a hearty vegetable soup, a rich and creamy chowder, or a spicy chili, the comforting aroma and flavors of a homemade soup can bring warmth to even the chilliest of days. But what if you could capture that homemade goodness and enjoy it months down the line? That’s where canning homemade soups comes in.

Canning your own soups allows you to preserve the flavors of the season and enjoy them throughout the year. It’s a fantastic way to stock your pantry with wholesome, homemade meals that are ready to heat and eat whenever you need them. Plus, it’s a great way to reduce food waste by utilizing excess produce from your garden or local farmers’ market.

Getting Started

Before you begin canning homemade soups, it’s essential to gather the necessary equipment. You’ll need canning jars, lids, and bands, as well as a large stockpot, a ladle, a funnel, and a jar lifter. It’s crucial to use canning-specific jars to ensure a proper seal and prevent spoilage.

Selecting Recipes

When it comes to canning homemade soups, not all recipes are suitable for preserving. It’s important to choose recipes that have a high acidity level, as acidity inhibits the growth of bacteria that can cause foodborne illness. This makes tomato-based soups, vegetable soups with added acidity (such as lemon juice or vinegar), and meat-based soups with added acidity ideal candidates for canning.

The Canning Process

Preparation

  1. Prep Your Ingredients: Start by preparing your soup according to your chosen recipe. Whether you’re chopping vegetables, browning meat, or simmering a flavorful broth, ensure that your soup is thoroughly cooked before canning.
  2. Sanitize Your Equipment: Wash your canning jars, lids, and bands in hot, soapy water, and rinse them well. Place the jars in a large stockpot, cover them with water, and bring to a simmer. Keep the jars hot while you prepare the soup.

Filling the Jars

  1. Ladle the Soup: Using a funnel, carefully ladle the hot soup into the prepared jars, leaving a 1-inch headspace at the top to allow for expansion during processing.
  2. Remove Air Bubbles: Run a non-metallic utensil, such as a plastic spatula or chopstick, around the inside of the jar to release any trapped air bubbles.
  3. Wipe the Jar Rims: Using a clean, damp cloth, wipe the rims of the jars to ensure a clean seal.

Processing

  1. Apply the Lids and Bands: Place the lids on the jars and screw the bands on until they are fingertip tight – tight enough to hold the lid in place, but not too tight.
  2. Process the Jars: Using a jar lifter, carefully place the filled jars into the large stockpot of simmering water, ensuring that they are fully submerged with at least 1 inch of water above the jars. Process the jars according to the recipe’s recommended time, adjusting for altitude if necessary.
  3. Cool and Test: Once the processing time is complete, carefully remove the jars from the water and place them on a towel-lined countertop to cool. As the jars cool, you should hear a satisfying “pop” as the lids seal. After the jars have cooled, test the seals by pressing down on the center of the lids. If the lids do not flex up and down, the jars are sealed.

Storing and Enjoying

Properly sealed jars of homemade soup can be stored in a cool, dark place for up to a year. Label each jar with the contents and the date it was canned to ensure you use the oldest jars first. When you’re ready to enjoy your canned soup, simply heat it on the stovetop or in the microwave until it’s piping hot, and savor the flavors of your homemade creation.

Canning homemade soups is a wonderful way to extend the joy of cooking and the flavors of the season. With a little time and effort, you can fill your pantry with a variety of delicious, ready-to-eat meals that will bring warmth and comfort to your table throughout the year.

So, next time you’re whipping up a batch of your favorite soup, consider making a little extra to can and savor in the months to come. It’s a simple and rewarding way to preserve the goodness of homemade soups.
