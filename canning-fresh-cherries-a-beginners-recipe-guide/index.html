Canning Fresh Cherries: A Beginner's Recipe Guide


Canning fresh cherries is a rewarding process that allows you to enjoy the sweet, tangy taste of summer all year round. Whether you’re a seasoned canner or a beginner, this guide will walk you through the essentials of preserving cherries in jars, offering tips, step-by-step instructions, and the knowledge you need to create a beautiful pantry stocked with homemade cherry preserves.

Why Can Cherries?

Canning cherries allows you to capture the flavors of summer in a jar. The process of preserving cherries gives you the freedom to enjoy them well after the harvest season is over. Cherries are versatile fruits that can be used in various recipes—whether you’re baking a cherry pie, preparing a sweet topping for ice cream, or adding them to savory dishes like roasted meats. Additionally, home-canned cherries are free from the preservatives often found in store-bought products, making them a healthier option.

This guide will cover the steps for canning fresh cherries using both a water bath canning method and a pressure canning method, so you can choose what works best for your preferences.

Essential Equipment for Canning Fresh Cherries

Before we dive into the canning process, it’s important to gather all the equipment you’ll need. The tools listed below are essential for canning fresh cherries safely and effectively:

  • Canning Jars: Make sure you have enough clean, sterilized jars. Standard pint jars are perfect for cherries, but you can also use quart jars depending on your preference.
  • Lids and Rings: Use new lids to ensure a proper seal. The rings can be reused, but the lids must be replaced each time you can.
  • Canning Pot: A large pot for water bath canning, or a pressure canner if you’re going that route.
  • Jar Lifter: A jar lifter helps safely remove hot jars from the canner.
  • Canning Funnel: A wide-mouth funnel ensures that your cherries don’t spill when filling the jars.
  • Bubble Remover: A plastic bubble remover tool or a spatula can help remove air bubbles that form inside the jar during the canning process.
  • Tongs or Ladle: To handle the cherries and syrup without making a mess.

Choosing the Best Cherries for Canning

When it comes to canning cherries, you’ll want to pick the best quality fruit available. Look for cherries that are ripe but firm, without any blemishes or bruises. The most common varieties for canning include Bing, Rainier, and Montmorency cherries. Sweet cherries (like Bing) are commonly canned in syrup, while tart cherries (like Montmorency) are great for pies, jams, and other preserves.

If possible, pick cherries that are at their peak of ripeness, as they will have the best flavor and texture once canned. Always handle your cherries gently to avoid bruising, as bruised fruit will not preserve as well.

Preparing the Cherries for Canning

Once you’ve selected your cherries, it’s time to prepare them for canning. Follow these simple steps:

  1. Wash the Cherries: Thoroughly rinse the cherries under cold water to remove any dirt or pesticides. You can gently rub them with your fingers to ensure they’re completely clean.

  2. Pit the Cherries: Use a cherry pitter to remove the pits. If you don’t have a pitter, you can carefully slice the cherries in half with a sharp knife and remove the pit by hand. Pitting the cherries helps to preserve the texture and makes them easier to use later in recipes.

  3. Blanch (Optional): Some canners recommend blanching cherries briefly in boiling water to remove the skins. However, this step is not necessary for all varieties of cherries, particularly sweet cherries. Blanching is more commonly used for preserving tart cherries.

  4. Prepare Syrup or Juice: You have two options when it comes to packing your cherries into jars: syrup or juice. For syrup, prepare a simple syrup by dissolving sugar in water. The ratio depends on your sweetness preference, but a common syrup strength is 1 cup of sugar to 2 cups of water for a light syrup. Alternatively, you can use fruit juice such as apple juice or a combination of water and lemon juice for a more tart flavor.

Canning Methods: Water Bath vs. Pressure Canning

There are two primary methods for canning cherries: water bath canning and pressure canning. Each method has its advantages, and the choice depends on your personal preference and the type of cherries you’re canning.

Water Bath Canning

Water bath canning is the most common and straightforward method for canning cherries. It’s ideal for high-acid fruits like cherries, which are naturally safe to preserve with this method. Here’s how to do it:

  1. Sterilize Your Jars: Place your canning jars in a large pot of boiling water for 10 minutes to sterilize them. Remove the jars and set them aside to dry.

  2. Prepare the Canning Syrup: As mentioned earlier, you’ll need a syrup to pack your cherries in. Once the syrup is ready, let it cool slightly.

  3. Pack the Cherries into Jars: Using your funnel, pack the pitted cherries into the sterilized jars, leaving about half an inch of headspace at the top.

  4. Fill the Jars with Syrup: Pour the prepared syrup or fruit juice over the cherries, ensuring that the cherries are completely covered. Leave at least half an inch of headspace to allow for expansion during processing.

  5. Remove Air Bubbles: Gently slide a plastic spatula or bubble remover tool around the inside of the jar to remove any air bubbles. This ensures a proper seal and prevents spoilage.

  6. Wipe the Jar Rims: Using a clean cloth, wipe the rims of the jars to remove any syrup or juice that might interfere with the sealing process.

  7. Apply the Lids and Rings: Place the sterilized lids on top of the jars and screw on the rings until they’re fingertip-tight. Do not over-tighten.

  8. Process the Jars: Place the jars into the boiling water bath, ensuring that they are fully submerged. The water should cover the jars by at least 1-2 inches. Process the jars for 20-30 minutes, depending on the size of the jars and your altitude. Refer to your specific recipe for precise times.

  9. Check for Seals: Once the processing time is complete, carefully remove the jars from the water bath using a jar lifter and let them cool. After 24 hours, check the seals by pressing down in the center of each lid. If the lid doesn’t pop back, the jar is sealed correctly.

Pressure Canning

Pressure canning is another method that is typically used for low-acid foods or when you need to can large quantities of fruit. While cherries are high in acid and usually fine for water bath canning, some canners prefer pressure canning for larger batches or to maintain more flavor and texture in the fruit. Here’s how to use a pressure canner:

  1. Sterilize Your Jars: As with the water bath method, sterilize your jars before use.

  2. Prepare the Syrup: Prepare the syrup or fruit juice as described earlier.

  3. Pack the Jars: Fill the sterilized jars with pitted cherries, leaving half an inch of headspace. Pour the syrup over the cherries, ensuring they are fully covered.

  4. Remove Air Bubbles and Wipe the Rims: Use a bubble remover tool to release air bubbles and wipe the rims of the jars to ensure a tight seal.

  5. Seal the Jars: Apply the lids and rings to the jars, tightening them just to fingertip-tightness.

  6. Pressure Can the Jars: Place the jars into your pressure canner, following the manufacturer’s instructions for your specific model. Generally, you’ll need to process the jars at 10-15 pounds of pressure for about 25-30 minutes. The exact time may vary based on your altitude and jar size.

  7. Cool and Check Seals: After processing, allow the pressure canner to cool completely before removing the jars. Check the seals after 24 hours.

Storing Canned Cherries

After your jars are sealed and the cherries have cooled, store them in a cool, dark, and dry place. A pantry or basement works perfectly. Canned cherries typically last up to a year if stored properly. Once you open a jar, refrigerate any unused cherries and consume them within a week.

Tips for Success

  • Always use fresh, high-quality fruit for the best results.
  • Be sure to sterilize your canning jars, lids, and equipment before each use to prevent contamination.
  • For better texture and flavor, avoid overcooking the cherries during processing.
  • Label each jar with the date it was canned so you can keep track of when to use it.
  • Keep your processing times and temperatures consistent for food safety.

Conclusion

Canning fresh cherries is an enjoyable and satisfying way to preserve the taste of summer for the colder months. With the right equipment, preparation, and techniques, you can create jars of sweet and tangy cherries to enhance your recipes and treat your family to a delicious taste of the season all year long.

Whether you opt for water bath canning or pressure canning, follow the steps carefully, and you’ll be able to store homemade cherries that retain their flavor and texture. So, gather your cherries, prepare your jars, and start canning today—you’ll be grateful to have these homemade treasures when the snow starts to fall.
