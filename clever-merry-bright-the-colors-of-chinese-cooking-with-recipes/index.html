Clever, Merry & Bright: The Colors of Chinese Cooking with Recipes


Chinese cooking is a vibrant world of colors, flavors, and textures. Much like a palace adorned with red lanterns and golden dragons, this cuisine can set your taste buds ablaze with a plethora of emotions. In this post, let’s dive into the marvelous world of colors that make Chinese cooking a feast for the senses.

Whether you are an amateur cook or an experienced chef, the colorful and festive nature of Chinese cooking will provide you with a wealth of knowledge and inspiration. Ignite your culinary creativity by paying close attention to the colors of the dish, as each hue plays a distinct role in enhancing the taste and presentation of the food.

Chinese cuisine is a symphony of ingredients, bringing together diverse tastes and textures through the subtle play of colors. You’ll also learn how the color of food impacts our perception of taste.

Let’s commence our culinary journey with an examination of the significance of colors in Chinese cooking, followed by mouthwatering recipes.

Red Foods: Symbolizing Good Fortune and Happiness

Red is incredibly important in Chinese culture, as it symbolizes good fortune, happiness, and prosperity. Incorporating red foods in your dishes can attract joy, love, and abundance into your life.

Fruits: Cherries, pomegranates, and raspberries add a burst of color and flavor to your dishes. Meals: Red-cooked pork (hongshao rou), a dish showcasing the rich flavors of slow-cooked pork in soy sauce, is a perfect example of the power of red in Chinese cuisine.

Orange Foods: Invigorating Health and Wealth

Orange foods are believed to bring about good fortune and generously impact our physical and spiritual well-being. No wonder, it symbolizes vibrancy and prosperity.

Vegetables: Carrots, sweet potatoes, and red bell peppers are common orange vegetables found in Chinese recipes. Dishes: Ma Po Tofu (Mápó Dòufu) features soft silken tofu in a mouthwatering, fiery-orange sauce that harmonizes spices, bold chilies, and the rich flavors of Sichuan cuisine.

Yellow Foods: Signifying Prosperity and Summer

Yellow betokens prosperity, summer, and the soles of dragons – the symbol of power and good fortune in China. Conveying richness, it is an ideal color to employ during Chinese New Year and other festivals.

Grains: Egg noodles and sticky rice are widespread in Chinese households. Dishes: To highlight the power of yellow, prepare stir-fried eggs with sweet onion and peas, suggesting tender golden flakes of prosperity adorning the plate.

Green Foods: Representing Harmony and Freshness

The color green signifies growth, fertility, and balance in life. Chinese dishes infrequently lack green veggies packed with freshness and serenity.

Vegetables: Leafy greens like spinach, mustard greens, and bok choy are frequently found in salads, stir-fries, and soups. Dishes: Steamed fish with ginger and scallions, a dish simple to prepare, is packed with the lively hues of green scallions that promise prosperity and good health for the year to come.

White Foods: Portending Purity and Freshness

White food symbolizes purity, cleanliness, and fresh beginnings. They provide a sharp contrast with bold colors of other ingredients and freshen up the platter.

Grains: White rice, the staple of Chinese cuisine, is an everyday accompaniment to various dishes. Dishes: Enjoy bian duan, or white sugar pastry twists, which represents the allusions and incantations to attract dazzling victories and accomplishments throughout the year.

Black Foods: Symbolizing Beauty and Wisdom

Celestial black signifies elegance, mystery, and depth. Black foods provide a pleasing and elegant appearance to any Chinese culinary extravaganza.

Pastries: Black sesame seed balls, dim sum delicacies full of heavenly, gooey centers. Dishes: Black bean paste noodles: a recipe showcasing the charm of healthy, hearty noodles enriched with black beans.

Chinese cooking, with its vast palate of colors, is a captivating and artistic world. The colors of Chinese cuisine can do much more than make your table look visually exciting. They play a fundamental role in establishing the emotional and spiritual ties that connect food with the cultural experiences of those who consume it.

Now, let’s dive into a selection of recipes that celebrate the glistening colors of Chinese cuisine.

Colorful Rainbow Vegetable Noodles

Ingredients:

  • 8 oz. Fresh udon noodles (or any Asian noodle of your choice)
  • 1 Medium-sized carrot, julienned
  • 1 Yellow zucchini, julienned
  • 1 Purple cabbage, thinly sliced
  • 1 Medium-sized beet, julienned
  • 1 Red bell pepper, thinly sliced
  • 2 C. Snow peas
  • 2 Tbsp. Sesame oil
  • 1 Tbsp. Soy sauce
  • 1 Tbsp. Rice vinegar
  • 1 Tsp. Sesame seeds
  • Salt to taste

Instructions:

  1. Boil a large pot of water and add udon noodles to cook for about 2-3 minutes, or until al dente.
  2. Finely prepare all the vegetables keeping their color and vibrancy.
  3. Heat sesame oil in a large wok or frying pan over medium heat, and stir-fry the vegetables for 3-5 minutes until they are cooked yet crisp.
  4. Add cooked noodles to the pan and mix well with the vegetables. Stir in soy sauce, rice vinegar, and season with salt to taste.
  5. Sprinkle sesame seeds and mix well. Serve immediately with extra soy sauce or chili oil on the side, if desired.

Easy Cooked Pork with Vegetables (Kau Yuk)

Ingredients:

  • 14 oz. Pork belly or pork shoulder, cut into ½-inch thick pieces
  • 2 Tbsp. Vegetable oil
  • 3 C. Sliced vegetables of your choice (e.g., green beans, bell peppers, carrots, mushrooms)
  • 2 C. Chicken broth
  • 1 Tbsp. Soy sauce
  • 1 Tbsp. Oyster sauce
  • 1 Tbsp. Cornstarch
  • 1 Tsp. Sugar
  • Salt and peper, to taste

Instructions:

  1. In a bowl, mix chicken broth, soy sauce, oyster sauce, cornstarch, and sugar to make a marinade for the pork.
  2. Heat vegetable oil in a large skillet over medium heat, and fry the pork in batches until golden brown, about 5 minutes each side. Set aside.
  3. Reheat the skillet and add the sliced vegetables, cooking for a few minutes until they are tender-crisp.
  4. Return the pork to the skillet, pour the marinade over the pork and vegetables, and cook for an additional 5 minutes, allowing the sauce to thicken.
  5. Season the dish with salt and pepper, garnish, and serve hot with steamed rice.

Set out on a culinary adventure by exploring the vibrant colors of Chinese cooking and let these dishes provide an abundance of the rich, diverse flavors and beautiful aesthetics that embody the world’s oldest cuisine. Ignite your taste buds with the bold colors and zest of Chinese dishes, and maybe, along the way, uncover some stories and traditions that add warmth to your heart and a spring to your step.
