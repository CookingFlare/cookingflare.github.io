Preserving Herbs: How to Can and Store Fresh Herbs


Fresh herbs are an essential component in many kitchens. Their bright flavors can elevate a dish, enhance aroma, and provide an earthy depth to cooking. However, herbs are often seasonal and can lose their flavor and quality quickly if not stored properly. Fortunately, with a few techniques, you can preserve the essence of fresh herbs throughout the year. Whether you’re looking to extend the life of your homegrown bounty or store herbs bought at the market, canning and preserving methods can help keep your herbs fresh and flavorful for months.

In this post, we’ll cover several methods for preserving herbs, focusing on canning, drying, and freezing. We’ll also provide practical tips on how to store herbs properly, ensuring they stay vibrant and full of flavor.

Why Preserve Herbs?

Before diving into the methods, it’s important to understand why preserving herbs is worth the effort. Fresh herbs tend to have a limited shelf life, and their flavor can degrade quickly once they’ve been harvested. Even when stored in the fridge, they can wilt and lose their potency after just a few days.

By preserving herbs, you can:

  • Enjoy fresh flavors year-round: Herbs like basil, oregano, rosemary, and thyme can be used in various dishes, and preserving them allows you to use these ingredients in your cooking no matter the season.
  • Save money: Buying fresh herbs regularly can get expensive, especially when they’re out of season. Preserving them yourself can cut down on your grocery bill.
  • Reduce food waste: By canning or freezing herbs, you can make the most of your harvest, reducing the amount of fresh herbs that go unused and spoil.
  • Have access to homemade flavorings: Homemade herb preserves like herb-infused oils, vinegars, and butters can enhance your meals in a way store-bought products can’t.

Let’s dive into the different methods of preserving herbs so that you can enjoy the best flavors year-round!

Preparing Fresh Herbs for Preservation

Regardless of which preservation method you choose, it’s important to start with high-quality herbs. Select fresh herbs that are free from blemishes, wilting, or yellowing leaves. Ideally, herbs should be harvested in the morning, after the dew has dried but before the sun gets too hot. This ensures the herbs retain the most flavor and essential oils.

Before preserving, gently wash the herbs in cool water to remove any dirt or pests. Dry them thoroughly with a clean towel or let them air dry. You can use a salad spinner for leafy herbs like basil and mint, but ensure that the herbs are completely dry before proceeding to prevent mold growth during storage.

Method 1: Canning Fresh Herbs

Canning is a fantastic way to preserve herbs for long-term storage. By canning, you create a sealed environment that keeps herbs fresh and safe from air and moisture, which can lead to spoilage. There are several ways to can herbs, with the most common method being canning herb-infused oils or vinegars.

Herb-Infused Oils

Herb-infused oils are a great way to preserve fresh herbs while creating flavorful oils that can be used for cooking, dressings, or dipping sauces. The process involves combining herbs with oil and then gently heating it to extract the flavors. The resulting oil is then stored in jars and sealed.

Ingredients:

  • Fresh herbs (rosemary, thyme, oregano, basil, etc.)
  • Olive oil (or other oils like grapeseed, sunflower, or canola)

Instructions:

  1. Prepare the jars: Sterilize your canning jars and lids by washing them in hot soapy water and placing them in a hot water bath for 10 minutes. Set them aside to dry.
  2. Prepare the herbs: Strip the leaves from the stems of your chosen herbs, discarding any tough stems. You can also crush or bruise the herbs slightly to release their oils.
  3. Heat the oil: In a saucepan, heat your oil on medium heat for a few minutes. Be careful not to heat it too much, as you don’t want to burn the oil. The oil should be warm but not hot.
  4. Combine oil and herbs: Place your herbs into the prepared jars. Pour the warm oil over the herbs, making sure the herbs are fully submerged in the oil. Leave about 1/2 inch of space at the top of the jar.
  5. Seal and store: Wipe the rims of the jars with a clean cloth and place the sterilized lids on top. Tighten the lids and process the jars in a water bath canner for about 10-15 minutes. This will help create a vacuum seal. After canning, let the jars cool completely.
  6. Infuse and use: Allow the oil to infuse for at least a week before using, as this will help the flavors deepen. You can store the herb-infused oils in a cool, dark place for several months.

Pro Tip: Herb-infused oils are wonderful for drizzling over salads, grilled vegetables, or roasted meats. However, for safety reasons, never store oils infused with fresh herbs at room temperature for long periods, as this could lead to bacterial growth. For extended shelf life, keep them in the fridge.

Herb-Infused Vinegar

Herb-infused vinegar is another easy way to preserve fresh herbs and can be used as a salad dressing base, in marinades, or for pickling vegetables.

Ingredients:

  • Fresh herbs (basil, rosemary, thyme, tarragon, etc.)
  • White vinegar, apple cider vinegar, or red wine vinegar

Instructions:

  1. Sterilize the jars: Clean your canning jars and lids by sterilizing them in hot water. Dry the jars thoroughly.
  2. Prepare the herbs: Gently bruise the herbs to release their flavor, then place them in the jars.
  3. Add the vinegar: Heat the vinegar in a saucepan just until it begins to steam (do not boil). Pour the hot vinegar over the herbs in the jars, leaving about 1/2 inch of space at the top.
  4. Seal the jars: Secure the lids on the jars and process them in a water bath canner for 5-10 minutes to seal.
  5. Cool and store: After the jars have cooled, store them in a dark, cool place. Let the vinegar infuse for 1-2 weeks before using to allow the flavors to meld together.

Pro Tip: If you prefer, you can use a variety of vinegars to create unique flavor profiles, such as balsamic vinegar for a sweeter taste or red wine vinegar for a tangy kick.

Method 2: Drying Fresh Herbs

Drying is one of the oldest methods of preserving herbs. When done properly, it locks in the natural flavors of herbs and makes them easy to store for extended periods. Drying herbs also removes moisture, which helps prevent spoilage.

Air Drying

This method is simple, requiring minimal equipment and is best for herbs with low moisture content like rosemary, thyme, oregano, and sage.

Instructions:

  1. Prepare the herbs: Tie the herbs in small bundles, using a rubber band or string. Ensure the herbs are dry and free from moisture before beginning.
  2. Hang the bundles: Hang the bundles upside down in a dry, warm, and well-ventilated area. A closet or pantry works well as long as it’s free from direct sunlight.
  3. Wait for drying: Let the herbs hang for 1-3 weeks, checking occasionally for dryness. Once the leaves crumble easily between your fingers, the herbs are ready.
  4. Store the dried herbs: Gently strip the dried leaves from the stems and store them in airtight jars. Label the jars with the herb name and the date of drying.

Oven Drying

If you don’t have the time or space for air drying, using an oven is a faster method.

Instructions:

  1. Prepare the herbs: Strip the leaves from the stems and spread them in a single layer on a baking sheet.
  2. Dry in the oven: Set your oven to the lowest setting (around 180°F or 82°C). Place the baking sheet inside and leave the oven door slightly ajar to allow airflow. Check the herbs every 30 minutes to prevent burning. They should be fully dried within 1-2 hours.
  3. Store the dried herbs: Once dry, remove the herbs from the oven, let them cool, and store them in airtight containers.

Method 3: Freezing Fresh Herbs

Freezing herbs preserves their flavor and aroma, and it’s ideal for tender herbs like basil, parsley, and cilantro, which may not dry well. Freezing also keeps the texture of some herbs intact when used in soups, stews, or sauces.

Freezing Herbs in Ice Cubes

One of the easiest ways to freeze herbs is by making herb ice cubes. This method is especially great for herbs that you use in cooking often.

Instructions:

  1. Prepare the herbs: Wash and chop the herbs into small pieces.
  2. Fill the ice cube tray: Place a small amount of herbs into each compartment of an ice cube tray.
  3. Add water or oil: Fill the tray with water or olive oil, covering the herbs completely.
  4. Freeze: Place the tray in the freezer and let the cubes freeze completely.
  5. Store the cubes: Once frozen, remove the herb cubes and store them in freezer bags or airtight containers.

Freezing Whole Herbs

You can also freeze herbs directly, especially when you plan to use them in cooked dishes. Simply wash and chop the herbs, then lay them flat on a baking sheet and freeze them for 1-2 hours. Once frozen, transfer them to a freezer bag.

Conclusion

Preserving herbs is a fantastic way to ensure you have access to fresh flavors year-round, no matter what the season may bring. Whether you choose to can, dry, or freeze your herbs, each method has its benefits and can help you make the most of your harvest. From herb-infused oils to dried bundles and frozen cubes, there are numerous options for storing herbs and keeping them flavorful and aromatic.

By following these preservation techniques, you can enjoy your favorite herbs throughout the year, adding depth and richness to your culinary creations.
