Sichuan-style Twice-Cooked Pork: A Spicy and Savory Delight


Twice-cooked pork, or “Hui Guo Rou” (回锅肉) in Chinese, is a classic dish from Sichuan cuisine that perfectly embodies the region’s love for bold flavors and fiery spices. This dish features tender slices of pork belly that are first simmered until tender, then stir-fried with a medley of vibrant vegetables and aromatic seasonings. The result is a mouthwatering combination of textures and tastes that will leave you craving more. In this blog post, we’ll delve into the history of twice-cooked pork, explore its key ingredients, and provide a step-by-step guide to making this iconic dish at home.

History and Cultural Significance

Sichuan cuisine, known for its bold and spicy flavors, has a rich culinary history that spans centuries. Twice-cooked pork is one of the hallmark dishes of this region, celebrated for its unique preparation method and robust taste. The name “twice-cooked” refers to the two-stage cooking process: the pork is first simmered to render out excess fat and infuse it with flavor, then stir-fried to develop a crispy exterior and enhance its savory profile.

In traditional Sichuan households, twice-cooked pork is often prepared for special occasions and family gatherings. It symbolizes prosperity and abundance, as the fatty pork belly represents richness. Over time, this dish has gained popularity not only throughout China but also around the world, becoming a beloved staple in Chinese restaurants internationally.

Key Ingredients

Pork Belly

The star of twice-cooked pork is undoubtedly the pork belly. This cut of meat is prized for its balance of lean meat and fat, which provides a succulent texture when properly cooked. Look for pork belly with an even distribution of fat and meat, as this ensures the best results.

Doubanjiang (Fermented Broad Bean Paste)

Doubanjiang, also known as fermented broad bean paste or spicy bean paste, is a critical ingredient in Sichuan cuisine. Made from fermented broad beans, soybeans, salt, rice, and spices, this paste imparts a rich, umami flavor and a potent kick of heat to the dish. Authentic doubanjiang is essential for achieving the signature taste of twice-cooked pork.

Sichuan Peppercorns

Sichuan peppercorns are another defining element of this dish. These small, reddish-brown berries have a unique citrusy aroma and a numbing effect on the palate, which balances the spiciness of the chilies. Toasting the peppercorns before use enhances their flavor and releases their fragrant oils.

Vegetables

Traditional twice-cooked pork includes a variety of vegetables that add color, texture, and freshness to the dish. Common choices are green bell peppers, leeks, and cabbage. The vegetables should be sliced thinly to ensure even cooking and a harmonious blend with the pork.

Aromatics and Seasonings

Garlic, ginger, and scallions are essential aromatics that provide depth and complexity to the dish. Soy sauce, Shaoxing wine, and sugar are used to season the stir-fry, creating a perfect balance of salty, sweet, and umami notes.

Step-by-Step Recipe: Sichuan-style Twice-Cooked Pork

Ingredients

  • Pork belly: 1 pound, skin-on
  • Doubanjiang (fermented broad bean paste): 2 tablespoons
  • Sichuan peppercorns: 1 teaspoon
  • Dried red chilies: 3-5, depending on heat preference
  • Garlic: 4 cloves, minced
  • Ginger: 1 tablespoon, minced
  • Green bell pepper: 1, sliced
  • Leeks: 2, white and light green parts, sliced
  • Cabbage: 1 cup, shredded
  • Soy sauce: 2 tablespoons
  • Shaoxing wine: 1 tablespoon
  • Sugar: 1 teaspoon
  • Vegetable oil: 2 tablespoons
  • Water: 4 cups

Instructions

Step 1: Prepare the Pork Belly

  1. Simmer the Pork Belly: Place the pork belly in a pot and cover it with water. Bring to a boil over medium-high heat, then reduce the heat and simmer for about 20 minutes, or until the pork is cooked through but not falling apart. Skim off any foam that rises to the surface during simmering.

  2. Cool and Slice: Remove the pork belly from the pot and let it cool slightly. Once cooled, slice the pork belly into thin, bite-sized pieces, about 1/8 inch thick. Set aside.

Step 2: Prep the Ingredients

  1. Toast Sichuan Peppercorns: In a dry skillet over low heat, toast the Sichuan peppercorns until fragrant, taking care not to burn them. Let them cool, then grind them into a coarse powder using a mortar and pestle or spice grinder.

  2. Prepare Vegetables and Aromatics: Slice the green bell pepper, leeks, and cabbage. Mince the garlic and ginger. Set all prepped ingredients aside for easy access during stir-frying.

Step 3: Stir-Fry the Pork

  1. Heat the Oil: In a wok or large frying pan, heat the vegetable oil over medium-high heat until shimmering.

  2. Stir-Fry the Pork: Add the sliced pork belly to the wok and stir-fry until the edges start to crisp up and turn golden brown. This should take about 5-7 minutes. Remove the pork from the wok and set aside, leaving any rendered fat in the wok.

Step 4: Build the Flavor Base

  1. Aromatics: Add the minced garlic, ginger, and dried red chilies to the wok. Stir-fry for about 30 seconds until fragrant.

  2. Doubanjiang: Add the doubanjiang to the wok and stir-fry for another minute, allowing the paste to caramelize slightly and release its rich aroma.

Step 5: Cook the Vegetables

  1. Add Vegetables: Add the sliced green bell pepper, leeks, and cabbage to the wok. Stir-fry for 3-4 minutes until the vegetables are slightly tender but still crisp.

  2. Season: Return the pork to the wok along with the soy sauce, Shaoxing wine, sugar, and ground Sichuan peppercorns. Stir everything together to coat the pork and vegetables evenly with the sauce.

  3. Combine and Finish: Continue to stir-fry for another 2-3 minutes until all ingredients are well combined and heated through. Taste and adjust seasoning if needed.

Step 6: Serve

Transfer the twice-cooked pork to a serving platter and enjoy immediately with steamed rice. The dish should be savory, spicy, and slightly numbing, with a wonderful balance of flavors and textures.

Tips for Perfect Twice-Cooked Pork

Choosing the Right Pork Belly

Selecting high-quality pork belly is crucial for the success of this dish. Look for pork belly with a good ratio of fat to meat. The fat should be firm and white, while the meat should be pink and fresh-looking. Avoid pork belly that has an overly strong odor or looks discolored.

Proper Simmering

Simmering the pork belly before stir-frying serves two purposes: it renders out some of the fat and infuses the meat with flavor. Be careful not to overcook the pork during this step, as it will continue to cook during the stir-frying process. Aim for tender but firm slices that hold their shape.

Balancing the Heat

Sichuan cuisine is known for its spiciness, but the level of heat can be adjusted to suit your preference. If you’re new to spicy foods, start with fewer dried chilies and less doubanjiang. You can always add more heat later if desired. Conversely, if you love spice, feel free to increase the amount of chilies and doubanjiang for an extra kick.

Achieving the Right Texture

The key to great twice-cooked pork is achieving the perfect texture balance between the tender pork and crisp vegetables. Stir-fry the vegetables just long enough to retain their crunch, and ensure the pork slices are thin and evenly cooked. This creates a delightful contrast in each bite.

Experimenting with Vegetables

While green bell peppers, leeks, and cabbage are traditional choices, don’t be afraid to experiment with other vegetables based on what you have available. Red bell peppers, onions, and even celery can be great additions. The goal is to maintain a harmonious blend of flavors and textures that complement the pork.

Conclusion

Sichuan-style twice-cooked pork is a testament to the bold and vibrant flavors of Sichuan cuisine. With its tender pork belly, crisp vegetables, and aromatic spices, this dish offers a tantalizing culinary experience that’s sure to impress. By following this step-by-step guide, you can recreate this iconic dish in your own kitchen and discover why it’s a beloved favorite in Chinese gastronomy. Whether you’re a seasoned cook or a beginner, twice-cooked pork is a rewarding and delicious dish that brings the essence of Sichuan right to your dining table. Enjoy!
