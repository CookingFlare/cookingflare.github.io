Greek Custard Cake with Syrup (Portokalopita) Made Easy


Greek cuisine is famous for its bold flavors, rich textures, and timeless recipes. Among its cherished desserts is Portokalopita, an irresistible orange-infused custard cake drenched in syrup. With its fragrant aroma, creamy center, and golden, flaky crust, this dessert holds a special place in many Greek households. While traditional Portokalopita often takes time to prepare, this simplified recipe ensures you can enjoy the same delightful results with less effort.

What is Portokalopita?

Portokalopita is a traditional Greek dessert that perfectly balances sweetness, tanginess, and texture. The name stems from “portokali,” meaning orange, and “pita,” meaning pie or cake. This dish stands out for its unique composition: phyllo (filo) pastry is incorporated into a custard-like batter, then baked to golden perfection and soaked in a fragrant orange syrup. The combination of crispy pastry layers and creamy interiors creates an unforgettable culinary experience.

This simplified version sticks to the authentic flavors and charm of Portokalopita while cutting out some of the more labor-intensive steps. Whether you’re hosting a dinner party or simply craving a taste of Greece, this recipe delivers a satisfying dessert worthy of celebration.

Ingredients You’ll Need

For the Cake:

  • 8 sheets of phyllo pastry (preferably thawed if frozen)
  • 4 large eggs
  • ¾ cup granulated sugar
  • 1 cup Greek yogurt (full-fat works best for creaminess)
  • 1 cup vegetable oil (preferably light olive oil or sunflower oil)
  • Zest of 2 oranges
  • 1 teaspoon pure vanilla extract
  • 1 teaspoon baking powder

For the Syrup:

  • 1 ½ cups granulated sugar
  • 1 ½ cups water
  • Juice of 1 large orange
  • 1 cinnamon stick

Step-by-Step Recipe

1. Prepare the Syrup

Start by preparing the syrup so it can cool while you make the cake. In a saucepan, combine the sugar, water, orange juice, and the cinnamon stick. Heat over medium-high heat until the sugar dissolves, stirring occasionally. Once it begins to boil, reduce the heat and allow the syrup to simmer for about 8–10 minutes, or until slightly thickened. Remove the cinnamon stick and let the syrup cool completely.

2. Dry the Phyllo Pastry

Phyllo pastry is the key to achieving the distinct texture of Portokalopita. To prepare the phyllo, separate the sheets and tear them into medium-sized strips with your hands. Spread the torn pieces out on a clean baking tray, allowing them to air dry for 30–45 minutes. This step ensures the phyllo becomes slightly crisp, helping it absorb the custard and syrup properly during baking.

3. Mix the Custard

In a large mixing bowl, whisk the eggs and sugar together until the mixture is pale and fluffy. Add the Greek yogurt, vegetable oil, orange zest, vanilla extract, and baking powder. Stir well to combine, ensuring you have a smooth and creamy batter.

4. Incorporate the Phyllo

Gradually add the dried phyllo into the custard mixture. Toss gently to coat the pastry pieces evenly. The goal is to have the phyllo pieces thoroughly embedded in the batter without losing their texture.

5. Bake the Cake

Preheat your oven to 350°F (175°C). Grease a medium-sized baking dish with oil or butter. Pour the custard mixture into the dish and spread it evenly. Bake the Portokalopita for about 35–40 minutes or until the top turns a rich golden brown and a toothpick inserted in the center comes out clean.

6. Soak with Syrup

Once the cake has finished baking and is still warm, use a skewer or fork to poke small holes across the surface. Gradually pour the cooled syrup over the cake, ensuring it seeps evenly into every corner. Let the cake rest for at least two hours to absorb the syrup completely—ideally overnight for best results.

Tips for Perfect Portokalopita

Creating a memorable Greek custard cake requires attention to detail. Follow these tips to ensure success every time:

  1. Use Fresh Ingredients: Fresh oranges for zest and juice will infuse the cake with authentic and vibrant flavor.
  2. Avoid Overmixing: When stirring in the phyllo, be gentle to maintain its delicate texture.
  3. Cool Appropriately: Syrup applications work best when one element (cake or syrup) is warm and the other is completely cool.
  4. Let the Cake Rest: Allowing the cake to soak in the syrup overnight enhances flavor and texture.

Variations

Portokalopita is a versatile dessert that can be adapted based on personal preferences or dietary needs:

  • Citrus Twist: Add lemon zest or juice alongside the orange for a brighter flavor profile.
  • Nutty Texture: Sprinkle chopped almonds or walnuts over the cake before baking for a crunch.
  • Gentler Sweetness: Use honey in place of some sugar for a milder sweetness in the syrup.

Serving Suggestions

Portokalopita shines as a standalone dessert, but pairing it can elevate your dining experience even more. Serve it with a dollop of whipped cream or Greek yogurt for added creaminess. Alternatively, enjoy it alongside a cup of traditional Greek coffee or tea for a perfectly balanced finish to your meal.

Storing Portokalopita

If you have leftovers (unlikely, but possible!), store the cake in an airtight container in the refrigerator. It stays fresh for up to four days, with the syrup continuing to enhance its richness over time. Avoid freezing Portokalopita, as thawing it may alter its texture.

Why You’ll Fall in Love With This Recipe

Portokalopita exemplifies everything wonderful about Mediterranean desserts—vibrant flavors, simple ingredients, and a touch of indulgence. Its combination of custard-like interiors, crispy layers, and syrupy sweetness makes for an unforgettable treat. This simplified recipe captures the heart of the traditional dish while making it accessible for home cooks of any skill level.

Whether you’re reminiscing about a trip to Greece or exploring new culinary horizons, Greek custard cake with syrup is a delightful way to bring Mediterranean magic into your kitchen. Try it for yourself, and you’ll soon find that Portokalopita isn’t just a dessert—it’s a celebration of flavor and tradition.
