Korean Soups & Stews Guide: 8 Comfort Classics


Korean cuisine is known for its bold flavors, colorful ingredients, and deep-rooted traditions. Among the array of dishes, soups and stews play a central role in daily meals, especially when the weather turns cold or when families gather around the dinner table. These hearty dishes are not only nourishing but also comforting, offering a taste of Korea’s culinary heritage. In this guide, we will explore 8 classic Korean soups and stews that have become staples in both Korean homes and international kitchens.

1. Kimchi Jjigae (김치찌개) – Spicy Kimchi Stew

One of the most beloved Korean dishes, Kimchi Jjigae is a savory stew made with ripe, fermented kimchi, tofu, and often pork or beef. This dish is often enjoyed as a staple in Korean households and is particularly popular during the colder months. The robust flavors of the fermented kimchi provide a tangy and spicy base, while the addition of tofu offers a soft texture that contrasts with the heat of the broth.

Key Ingredients:

  • Mature kimchi (preferably well-fermented)
  • Pork belly or beef (some variations use tuna or other proteins)
  • Tofu
  • Onion, garlic, and ginger
  • Gochujang (Korean chili paste)
  • Sesame oil

Preparation:

  1. Sauté garlic and onions in sesame oil until fragrant.
  2. Add pork belly or beef, browning it slightly.
  3. Stir in the kimchi and gochujang to enhance the flavor profile.
  4. Pour in water or stock, and bring the stew to a boil.
  5. Let it simmer for 30-40 minutes, allowing the flavors to meld together.
  6. Add tofu near the end of cooking and let it soak in the broth before serving.

Kimchi Jjigae is often served with steamed rice and is a go-to comfort dish that pairs well with a variety of banchan (side dishes).

2. Doenjang Jjigae (된장찌개) – Soybean Paste Stew

Doenjang Jjigae is a savory, umami-packed stew made with Korean soybean paste (doenjang), similar to miso but with a heartier and more earthy flavor. It’s one of the most iconic dishes in Korean cuisine and is often made with vegetables, tofu, and sometimes seafood or meat. The deep flavors of the soybean paste create a rich, comforting broth that is both nourishing and satisfying.

Key Ingredients:

  • Doenjang (Korean fermented soybean paste)
  • Tofu
  • Zucchini, mushrooms, onions, and garlic
  • Kimchi (optional for extra flavor)
  • Anchovy stock (or water)

Preparation:

  1. Begin by preparing an anchovy stock (optional but adds depth).
  2. Sauté garlic and onions in a pot, then add the doenjang and cook for a minute to bring out its flavors.
  3. Add water or the prepared anchovy stock and bring it to a simmer.
  4. Add vegetables such as zucchini, mushrooms, and other seasonal vegetables.
  5. Add tofu and let the stew simmer for 20-30 minutes, allowing the flavors to meld.

Doenjang Jjigae is often served alongside rice, and the combination of the hearty stew and warm rice creates a well-balanced meal that is both filling and full of flavor.

3. Sundubu Jjigae (순두부찌개) – Soft Tofu Stew

Sundubu Jjigae is a flavorful stew made with uncurdled, soft tofu that is typically served bubbling hot in a stone pot. This dish is often made with seafood or pork, along with a spicy broth that brings out the tofu’s delicate texture. It’s commonly enjoyed with rice and is known for its intense, spicy flavor that is balanced by the smoothness of the tofu.

Key Ingredients:

  • Sundubu (soft tofu)
  • Seafood (commonly oysters, clams, or shrimp)
  • Pork (optional)
  • Gochugaru (Korean chili flakes)
  • Garlic, onion, and sesame oil
  • Gochujang (Korean chili paste)

Preparation:

  1. Heat sesame oil in a pot and sauté garlic and onion.
  2. Add the pork or seafood and cook until they begin to release their juices.
  3. Stir in gochugaru, gochujang, and water, creating a spicy broth.
  4. Bring to a boil and add the sundubu (soft tofu), breaking it into chunks.
  5. Let it simmer for a few minutes, allowing the tofu to absorb the flavors.

Serve with steamed rice and enjoy the contrast of spicy broth with silky tofu.

4. Galbitang (갈비탕) – Short Rib Soup

Galbitang is a rich and clear soup made with beef short ribs, garlic, and a variety of vegetables. The long simmering time allows the meat to become tender and the broth to absorb the flavors of the beef. This soup is perfect for colder weather, offering warmth and comfort with its savory and light broth.

Key Ingredients:

  • Beef short ribs
  • Garlic, onion, and green onions
  • Soy sauce
  • Korean radish (mu)
  • Sesame oil

Preparation:

  1. Start by blanching the short ribs to remove impurities, then set them aside.
  2. In a large pot, add the short ribs, water, garlic, and onion. Bring it to a boil, then reduce to a simmer for about 2 hours.
  3. Add Korean radish (mu) and cook for another 30-40 minutes until the meat is tender.
  4. Season with soy sauce and sesame oil.
  5. Serve with a sprinkle of green onions and a bowl of rice.

Galbitang is often enjoyed as a restorative meal, especially for those recovering from illness.

5. Banchan-Style Miyeok Guk (미역국) – Seaweed Soup

Miyeok Guk is a traditional Korean seaweed soup made with miyeok (edible seaweed), garlic, and either beef or seafood. This soup holds special significance in Korean culture, as it is traditionally eaten by women after childbirth to promote recovery, but it’s also enjoyed regularly as a nourishing and healthy dish.

Key Ingredients:

  • Miyeok (seaweed)
  • Beef (often brisket) or seafood
  • Soy sauce, sesame oil, garlic

Preparation:

  1. Soak miyeok in water for about 30 minutes, then drain and cut into bite-sized pieces.
  2. Sauté garlic in sesame oil, then add the beef or seafood and cook until browned.
  3. Add water to the pot, followed by the soaked seaweed. Let it boil for about 30 minutes.
  4. Season with soy sauce and salt to taste.

Miyeok Guk is light yet packed with nutrients, offering a refreshing yet hearty flavor.

6. Jjigae with Banchan (찜닭) – Braised Chicken Stew

Jjigae with Banchan, also known as braised chicken stew, is a beloved dish often served with multiple side dishes. The chicken is braised in a savory broth with vegetables, soy sauce, and aromatic herbs. The stew is typically rich and comforting, with tender chicken falling off the bone.

Key Ingredients:

  • Chicken (whole or parts)
  • Soy sauce
  • Garlic, ginger, and sesame oil
  • Vegetables such as carrots, potatoes, onions
  • Gochugaru (chili flakes) for spice

Preparation:

  1. Brown the chicken in sesame oil, then add garlic, ginger, and vegetables.
  2. Pour in soy sauce, water, and gochugaru. Bring to a boil, then simmer for 1-1.5 hours.
  3. Serve the braised chicken stew with rice and a variety of side dishes (banchan).

This dish is perfect for family meals or gatherings, providing both flavor and nourishment.

7. Tteokguk (떡국) – Rice Cake Soup

Tteokguk is a clear soup made with thinly sliced rice cakes, usually eaten during the Lunar New Year in Korea. The chewy texture of the rice cakes combined with the savory broth makes for a comforting dish, and it is often believed to bring good luck in the new year.

Key Ingredients:

  • Rice cakes (tteok)
  • Beef or chicken broth
  • Garlic and sesame oil
  • Eggs (for garnish)

Preparation:

  1. Boil the broth (beef or chicken) with garlic and sesame oil for flavor.
  2. Add the rice cakes and cook until they are soft and tender.
  3. Serve the soup with a garnish of thinly sliced egg, and optionally, green onions or kimchi for added flavor.

Tteokguk represents a fresh start and a new year, enjoyed by families on special occasions.

8. Kimchi Bokkkeumbap (김치볶음밥) – Kimchi Fried Rice with Soup

While this isn’t technically a soup or stew, Kimchi Bokkkeumbap is often enjoyed alongside soups and stews as a comforting side dish. It’s a simple fried rice made with leftover kimchi, rice, and sometimes vegetables or pork. The spicy kimchi adds a depth of flavor that complements the lighter soups and stews in a Korean meal.

Key Ingredients:

  • Leftover rice
  • Kimchi
  • Pork (optional)
  • Soy sauce, sesame oil
  • Green onions

Preparation:

  1. Sauté kimchi in sesame oil until fragrant, then add pork or other vegetables.
  2. Stir in leftover rice and fry until crispy.
  3. Season with soy sauce and garnish with green onions.

Kimchi Bokkkeumbap pairs wonderfully with any of the stews or soups mentioned above.

Conclusion

Korean soups and stews are a vital part of the cuisine, offering comfort and nourishment with each dish. From the spicy tang of Kimchi Jjigae to the hearty warmth of Galbitang, these 8 comfort classics are perfect for any occasion. Whether you’re seeking something light and healthy or rich and hearty, the variety of flavors, textures, and ingredients in Korean soups and stews ensures that there is something for everyone. The love and tradition poured into these dishes have made them timeless staples in Korean kitchens and beyond.
