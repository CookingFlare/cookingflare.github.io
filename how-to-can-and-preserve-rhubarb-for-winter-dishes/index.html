How to Can and Preserve Rhubarb for Winter Dishes


Rhubarb, with its tart flavor and vibrant red stalks, is a favorite ingredient in many spring and summer dishes. However, its short growing season often leaves us wishing we could enjoy it year-round. Fortunately, canning and preserving rhubarb for winter dishes is an easy and effective way to extend the enjoyment of this unique vegetable (yes, it’s technically a vegetable!) throughout the colder months. In this comprehensive guide, we’ll explore the best methods for preserving rhubarb, the tools you’ll need, and creative ways to use your preserved rhubarb when winter rolls around.

Why Preserve Rhubarb?

Canning and preserving rhubarb allows you to enjoy its refreshing tartness even when it’s out of season. Whether you’re making pies, sauces, or chutneys, preserved rhubarb brings a burst of flavor to winter dishes. Additionally, preserving rhubarb is a fantastic way to reduce food waste, especially if you’ve had a bountiful harvest in your garden. By learning the right techniques, you can store rhubarb in jars and have it available whenever you need it, even when fresh rhubarb is hard to come by.

Tools and Equipment Needed for Canning Rhubarb

Before you get started, it’s essential to have the proper tools and equipment to ensure a safe and effective canning process. Here’s what you’ll need:

1. Canning Jars and Lids

  • Choose mason jars or any glass canning jars with secure lids. Be sure to check the condition of the jars before use to ensure there are no cracks.
  • Lids should be new to ensure a proper seal, but the rings (the metal bands that hold the lid in place) can be reused as long as they’re in good condition.

2. Large Pot or Canner

  • A water bath canner is ideal for canning rhubarb since it allows for even heat distribution during the sealing process. If you don’t have a canner, a large pot with a rack will work just fine.

3. Canning Tools

  • You’ll need a jar lifter to safely handle hot jars, a funnel for easy pouring, and a bubble remover tool to ensure there are no air pockets in the jars.

4. Rhubarb

  • Fresh, firm rhubarb is best for canning. Make sure the stalks are not too thin or overripe, as these will affect the quality of the final product.

5. Sugar, Lemon Juice, and Other Ingredients

  • Depending on your recipe, you may need sugar, lemon juice, or other flavorings. Sugar helps preserve the rhubarb, while lemon juice boosts the acidity, which is important for safe canning.

How to Prepare Rhubarb for Canning

Before canning rhubarb, you’ll need to prepare it properly to ensure the best results. Here’s a step-by-step guide on how to get your rhubarb ready:

1. Wash and Trim the Rhubarb

  • Start by washing the rhubarb thoroughly to remove any dirt or pesticides. Rhubarb stalks are typically cleaned before canning, so make sure to cut off any leaves, as they are toxic when consumed.
  • Trim off the ends of each stalk, cutting them into 1- to 2-inch pieces. The size of the pieces will depend on how you plan to use the rhubarb later, but smaller pieces work well for jams and sauces.

2. Blanch the Rhubarb

  • While some canning recipes call for raw packing (where the rhubarb is placed directly into jars without cooking), blanching the rhubarb first helps to maintain its color, texture, and nutrients.
  • To blanch rhubarb, bring a large pot of water to a boil and prepare an ice bath. Submerge the rhubarb pieces in the boiling water for 2-3 minutes, then quickly transfer them to the ice bath to halt the cooking process. This method helps to preserve the flavor and texture.

3. Prepare Canning Syrup (Optional)

  • Some canning recipes use a syrup to pack the rhubarb, especially for preserving rhubarb in jars. You can make a simple syrup by dissolving sugar in water (typically a ratio of 1 part sugar to 1 part water). For a more tart flavor, you can adjust the sugar levels, or omit it altogether.
  • Alternatively, you can use lemon juice or even ginger for added flavor, depending on your recipe.

How to Can Rhubarb

Once your rhubarb is properly prepared, it’s time to can it. Here are the steps to follow for safely preserving rhubarb through canning:

1. Sterilize the Jars

  • Before filling the jars, you need to sterilize them. Place the jars in a large pot of boiling water for 10 minutes to kill any bacteria. You can also run them through a hot cycle in the dishwasher.

2. Pack the Jars

  • Once the jars are sterilized, it’s time to pack the rhubarb into them. If you’re using syrup, pour the syrup into the jar, leaving about half an inch of space at the top. If you’re canning rhubarb in its own juice, simply pack the jar tightly with the rhubarb pieces.
  • Make sure to leave enough headspace (about ½ inch) to allow for expansion during the canning process.

3. Seal the Jars

  • Use a clean cloth or paper towel to wipe the rims of the jars to ensure a clean seal. Place the lids on top and secure them with the metal rings. Be sure not to overtighten the rings—just tighten them enough to hold the lid in place.

4. Process the Jars

  • Lower the jars into the boiling water bath. Make sure the water covers the jars by at least an inch. Boil for about 10-15 minutes, depending on your altitude and the size of the jars. The water should be at a rolling boil for the entire processing time.
  • Once the jars have been processed, use a jar lifter to remove them from the water. Set the jars on a clean towel or cooling rack to cool.

5. Check the Seals

  • After the jars have cooled, check the seals by pressing down on the center of the lid. If it pops back, the jar has not sealed properly and should be refrigerated and used within a few weeks.
  • Properly sealed jars will have a concave lid and no movement when pressed.

Storing Your Preserved Rhubarb

Once your rhubarb is safely preserved, it’s important to store it in a cool, dark place. A pantry or cellar is ideal, but any area that stays consistently cool and dry will work. Avoid storing your jars in areas with temperature fluctuations, as this can compromise the seals.

Rhubarb preserves typically last up to a year when stored properly, although they may start to lose flavor after six months. Always inspect the jars before using them; discard any that show signs of spoilage, such as rust on the lids or off smells.

Creative Ways to Use Preserved Rhubarb in Winter

Now that you have preserved your rhubarb, you may be wondering how to use it during the winter months. Fortunately, there are plenty of creative ways to incorporate this tart vegetable into your winter meals. Here are a few ideas:

1. Rhubarb Compote

  • Rhubarb compote is an easy and delicious way to use preserved rhubarb. Simply simmer the rhubarb with sugar and a splash of lemon juice to create a thick, sweet-tart sauce. It’s perfect for serving over yogurt, oatmeal, or pancakes.

2. Rhubarb Pie

  • A classic rhubarb pie is a wonderful treat in the winter months when fresh rhubarb is unavailable. Simply thaw your preserved rhubarb and use it in your favorite pie recipe. The tart flavor balances beautifully with a buttery, flaky crust.

3. Rhubarb Jam

  • If you canned your rhubarb with sugar, it can be turned into a delicious jam. Add a bit of pectin, sugar, and lemon juice, then process it into jars for a sweet, tangy spread.

4. Rhubarb Sauce

  • Rhubarb sauce is a versatile topping for ice cream, cakes, and even savory dishes. Simply simmer the preserved rhubarb with a little sugar and a pinch of cinnamon to create a delicious sauce that can be stored in the refrigerator for a week or two.

5. Rhubarb Chutney

  • Rhubarb chutney is a flavorful condiment that pairs well with meats like pork or chicken. Combine your preserved rhubarb with vinegar, sugar, and spices like ginger, cinnamon, and cloves for a tangy-sweet relish.

6. Rhubarb Smoothie

  • For a refreshing winter smoothie, blend preserved rhubarb with yogurt, banana, and a bit of honey. It’s a great way to add some vibrant flavor to your winter mornings.

Final Thoughts

Canning and preserving rhubarb is a great way to extend its availability beyond the short growing season. With a little preparation and the right techniques, you can enjoy the fresh, tart flavor of rhubarb all year long. Whether you choose to make jam, pies, or sauces, preserved rhubarb adds a burst of flavor to any dish. So, gather your rhubarb and start preserving it today—your winter dishes will thank you!
