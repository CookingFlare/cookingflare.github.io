How to Make and Can Homemade Vegetable Broth


A well-made vegetable broth can serve as the flavorful cornerstone of countless dishes, from hearty soups to creamy risottos. But there’s something truly special about making your own vegetable broth from scratch—not just for the rich taste it delivers, but also because it helps you reduce food waste and stay in control of the ingredients. If you’re ready to elevate your cooking with homemade broth and ensure you always have some on hand, this guide will take you through the entire process, including canning it for long-term storage.

Why Make Your Own Vegetable Broth?

Store-bought broths are convenient, but they often contain preservatives, added sodium, and artificial flavors. Crafting your own vegetable broth at home allows you to tailor it to your preferences, using fresh ingredients and avoiding unnecessary additives. Beyond that, making broth can serve as a sustainable kitchen practice—you’ll be amazed at how vegetable scraps you might have otherwise thrown away can be transformed into liquid gold.

Homemade broth is also versatile. Want a sweeter, lighter broth for a delicate vegetable soup? Add carrots and parsnips. Need something more robust for stews or gravies? Throw in mushrooms or some roasted vegetables. The possibilities are endless.

Essential Ingredients for Vegetable Broth

This is where homemade broth shines: you don’t need a strict list of ingredients. The real key lies in using high-quality vegetables. Below are common vegetables and aromatics that pair well together to create a balanced flavor profile:

Base Vegetables

  • Onions: Yellow, white, or red—all bring a slightly different flavor, but all are essential for depth.
  • Celery: Adds subtle saltiness and depth.
  • Carrots: Balances the broth with sweetness.

Complimentary Add-Ins

  • Leeks: Excellent for a mild oniony flavor.
  • Garlic: Use sparingly for a hint of complexity without overpowering.
  • Mushrooms: Add earthiness, especially if you want a darker broth.
  • Parsnips: Sweetens the broth while adding texture.

Herbs and Spices

  • Bay Leaves: One or two will suffice to add herbal depth.
  • Thyme: Fresh or dried sprigs make for a robust flavor.
  • Parsley Stems: While you might discard parsley stems normally, they contain loads of flavor.
  • Peppercorns: For a touch of spice without overwhelming the broth.
  • Salt: (Optional) Consider leaving it out to allow for flexibility when cooking.

Avoid Starchy Vegetables: While potatoes and sweet potatoes are delicious roasted or mashed, their starches can make your broth cloudy, so it’s best to leave them out.

Step-by-Step Guide to Making Homemade Vegetable Broth

1. Gather Your Vegetables

Start by collecting fresh vegetables, but don’t overlook your veggie scraps! Carrot peels, onion tops, celery ends, and herb stems are all ideal for broth. Wash everything thoroughly to remove any dirt or grit.

2. Prep the Ingredients

Chop all your vegetables into chunks. They don’t need to be pretty or uniform—just small enough to extract maximum flavor. If you’re planning to use mushroom stems, leeks, or garlic, be sure to clean them well.

3. Sauté for Extra Flavor (Optional)

For a richer broth, sauté your vegetables in olive oil or butter first. Heat a large stockpot over medium heat, add a drizzle of oil, and cook your vegetables for about 10 minutes until they’re beginning to brown. This step is optional but enhances the depth of flavor.

4. Add Water

Pour enough cold water over the vegetables to cover them by at least an inch or two. Exact quantities will vary based on how many vegetables you’re using, but generally, 8 cups (2 quarts) of water for every 4–5 cups of vegetables works well.

5. Add Herbs and Spices

Toss in bay leaves, parsley stems, thyme, and a handful of whole peppercorns. If you’re adding salt, start with just a small amount—you can always add more later when cooking with the broth.

6. Simmer the Broth

Bring your pot to a gentle boil over medium-high heat, then reduce it to a low simmer. Cover loosely to let steam escape, and cook for 45 minutes to an hour. This timing allows the flavors to meld without overcooking the vegetables.

7. Strain the Broth

Once your broth is aromatic and flavorful, remove the pot from the heat. Use a fine-mesh strainer or cheesecloth to strain the liquid into another pot or large bowl. Discard the vegetable solids—they’ve done their job and won’t taste great anymore. If you want a completely clear broth, strain it again through a second layer of cheesecloth.

8. Taste and Adjust

Take a sip of your broth and adjust the seasoning. Add salt, if necessary, or more water if the broth is too concentrated.

How to Can Homemade Vegetable Broth for Long-Term Storage

Once you’ve made your homemade vegetable broth, the next step is preserving it for future use. Canning is a safe and effective method to ensure your broth stays fresh for months without refrigeration. Here’s how:

1. Sterilize Your Jars

Start by washing your mason jars, lids, and seals with hot, soapy water. Rinse thoroughly, then sterilize them by boiling them in water for 10 minutes. Keep them warm until they’re ready for filling.

2. Heat the Broth

Bring your strained broth back to a boil. It’s essential that the liquid is hot when you pour it into the jars.

3. Fill the Jars

Carefully ladle the hot broth into your sterilized jars, leaving about 1 inch of headspace at the top. Use a funnel for precision and to avoid spills.

4. Remove Air Bubbles

Run a clean utensil (such as a butter knife or chopstick) along the inside edge of the jar to release any trapped air bubbles. Wipe the rim of each jar with a damp cloth to ensure a good seal.

5. Apply Lids and Seal

Place the sterilized lids on the jars, then tightly screw on the bands. Don’t overtighten—you’ll want the lids to form a vacuum seal during the canning process.

6. Pressure Can the Broth

Vegetable broth must be processed in a pressure canner for safe storage. Follow these steps:

  • Fill your pressure canner with the recommended amount of water (consult your manufacturer’s instructions).
  • Place the jars inside the canner and secure the lid.
  • Bring the canner to the appropriate pressure (10–11 psi is common for vegetable broth at lower altitudes).
  • Process pints for 30 minutes and quarts for 35 minutes, adjusting time for your altitude.

7. Cool and Store

Once the processing time is complete, turn off the heat and let the canner depressurize naturally. Remove the jars carefully and place them on a towel or heat-safe surface to cool. Check the seals after 12–24 hours—lids should be firm and slightly indented. Store your canned broth in a cool, dark place.

Tips for Success

  • Label Your Jars: Include the date and contents on the lid or jar. Canned vegetable broth is best used within 12 months for optimal quality.
  • Use Ice Cube Trays for Small Portions: If you don’t want to can the broth, freeze it in ice cube trays for quick, easy-to-use portions.

The Payoff of Homemade Vegetable Broth

Making and canning your own vegetable broth is a small effort that pays off in big ways. Not only will your cooking elevate to new heights with fresh, flavorful broth, but you’ll also feel the satisfaction of creating a pantry staple yourself. Plus, with reduced food waste and the ability to customize every batch, it’s a win for both your kitchen and the planet. So dive in, experiment with flavors, and enjoy the rewards of homemade vegetable broth—your taste buds (and your recipes) will thank you.
