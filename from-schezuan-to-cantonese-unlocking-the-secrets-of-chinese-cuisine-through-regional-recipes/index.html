From Schezuan to Cantonese: Unlocking the Secrets of Chinese Cuisine through Regional Recipes


Chinese cuisine is an intricate and diverse culinary landscape, with each region boasting its unique flavors, textures, and traditional dishes. Two of the most iconic regional cuisines in China are Sichuan and Cantonese, each with a deep history, rich flavors, and distinct techniques that set them apart. This article will take you on a culinary journey through the diverse world of Sichuan and Cantonese cuisine, with a collection of popular recipes from these two fascinating regions.

The Land of Spice: Sichuan Cuisine

Sichuan cuisine is renowned for its bold, spicy, and aromatic flavors, heavily relying on the use of various spices, seasonings, and chili peppers. Sichuan dishes are often characterized by their well-balanced taste, consisting of the “five flavors”: sweet, spicy, bitter, sour, and salty. To truly understand the spice-fueled world of Sichuan cuisine, let’s explore two iconic regional dishes.

Spicy Sichuan Dan Dan Noodles

Dan Dan Noodles, or Dan Dan Mien, are a quintessential Sichuan dish. These noodles are served in a zesty and spicy sauce, making them a beloved snack or lunch in China.

Ingredients:

  • 200g rice noodles (preferably fresh)
  • 100g minced pork
  • 2 tablespoons chili broad bean paste
  • 1 tablespoon soybean paste
  • 2 tablespoons Sichuan peppercorns, ground
  • 1 tablespoon black vinegar
  • 1 tablespoon sesame paste
  • 1 tablespoon sesame oil
  • 2 tablespoons chili oil
  • 1 egg, beaten
  • 3 green onions, chopped
  • 2 tablespoons Sichuan pickled vegetables, chopped

Instructions:

  1. Boil water and cook the rice noodles for 3-4 minutes until al dente. Rinse in cold water and set aside.
  2. In a pan, heat oil and sauté the minced pork until browned. Add the chili broad bean paste, soybean paste, and ground Sichuan peppercorns, and cook for a minute.
  3. Add the black vinegar, sesame paste, and chili oil. Stir well and remove from heat.
  4. In separate bowls, place the noodles, pork sauce, beaten egg, green onions, and pickled vegetables.
  5. Combine everything together and enjoy the explosion of flavors in the Spicy Sichuan Dan Dan Noodles!

Sichuan Double Cooked Pork

Another favorite in Sichuan cuisine! Double cooked pork (Hui Guo Rou) is a slow-cooked tender pork belly served with a delicious and spicy sauce.

Ingredients:

  • 750g pork belly
  • 1 tablespoon soy sauce
  • 1 tablespoon rice wine
  • 1 tablespoon sugar
  • 2 tablespoons cornstarch
  • 2 tablespoons vegetable oil
  • 1 tablespoon Sichuan peppercorns, ground
  • 2 tablespoons chili oil
  • 1 tablespoon garlic, minced
  • 1 tablespoon ginger, minced

Instructions:

  1. Cut the pork belly into thin slices, about half-centimeter thick, and mix with soy sauce, rice wine, sugar, and cornstarch.
  2. Heat oil in a wok or large frying pan and sear the pork until golden brown on both sides. Remove and set aside.
  3. Add the ground Sichuan peppercorns, chili oil, garlic, and ginger into the remaining oil in the pan. Cook for a minute to release the flavors.
  4. Add a small amount of water (about 1/4 cup) and bring to a simmer. Then place the browned pork slices into the sauce and cook gently for 1-2 hours, basting with the sauce periodically until the pork is tender and cooked through.
  5. Serve hot, garnished with chopped green onions.

The Delicacies of Cantonese Cuisine

Cantonese cuisine is known for its emphasis on freshness and simplicity, relying on natural flavors, skillful cooking techniques, and high-quality ingredients. It is considered one of the eight great traditional Chinese cuisines, focusing on " Mong " consumerism, which means “delicacies.”

Sweet and Sour Pork

A popular Cantonese dish, Honey-roasted Pork, or Guanggang Yu Rousi is a mouthwatering harmony of flavors and textures.

Ingredients:

  • 500g pork shoulder, cut into bite-size cubes
  • 1 cup all-purpose flour
  • 1 cup ice-cold water
  • 2 cups rice bran oil
  • 2 cups pineapple juice
  • 1/2 cup sugar
  • 1/2 cup rice vinegar
  • 2 eggs, beaten
  • 2 tablespoons cornstarch
  • 3 green onions, chopped
  • 1 bell pepper, cut into bite-size pieces
  • 1 cup fritters (breaded and fried Chinese doughnuts)

Instructions:

  1. Mix flour and cold water to form a thick batter. Make sure there are no lumps.
  2. Coat the pork cubes with the batter and deep-fry until golden brown. Drain on paper towels and set aside.
  3. In a separate wok or large pan, heat oil and sauté the bell peppers. Set aside.
  4. In a saucepan, bring pineapple juice, sugar, and rice vinegar to a boil, stirring until the sugar dissolves. Lower the heat and thicken the sauce using cornstarch and beaten egg. Season as needed.
  5. In a large serving platter, place the fried pork, bell peppers, and fritters. Drizzle the sweet and sour sauce generously. Garnish with chopped green onions.

Steamed Fish in Cantonese Style

Cantonese stir-frying technique is famous worldwide, but the region’s cuisine also excels in steamed dishes such as the classic Steamed Fish with Soy Sauce.

Ingredients:

  • 500g white fish (such as sea bass, snapper, or grouper)
  • 2 cups water
  • 2 tablespoons rice wine
  • 1 tablespoon ginger, sliced
  • 1 tablespoon soy sauce
  • 2 tablespoons green onions, chopped
  • 1 tablespoon cilantro, chopped
  • Salt to taste

Instructions:

  1. Clean your fish and make a few shallow diagonal cuts from head to tail to help the marinade absorb into the flesh.
  2. Combine water, rice wine, ginger slices, soy sauce, salt, green onions, and cilantro in a bowl. Mix well.
  3. Place the fish in a heatproof dish and pour the marinade over the fish. Alternatively, if using a fish basket, place the fish inside, and pour the marinade over the fish.
  4. Wrap the dish or fish basket with a piece of greased foil or betel leaves if possible.
  5. Steam the fish for 10-15 minutes, depending on the thickness of fish, over medium heat. Take care not to overcook the fish; it should remain tender and moist.
  6. Serve immediately and enjoy this Cantonese classic!

In conclusion, exploring Chinese regional cuisines offers a delightful and fulfilling journey into the heart of China’s diverse culinary landscape. Sichuan and Cantonese dishes prove that the myriad of flavors and techniques found across the country are truly worth exploring. Try these four recipes as starting points to indulge in the richness of taste the Land of the Dragon has to offer. Happy cooking!
