From Soup to Nuts: Navigate the Depths of Chinese Cooking with these Recipes


If you’re someone who’s been wondering how to dive into the world of Chinese cooking, you’ve landed on the perfect article. Today, we’ll explore the magical realms of Chinese cuisine by taking a delectable journey from soup to nuts, bringing you a deliciously-thoughtful line-up of recipes crafted by expert chefs, some of which you may not be as familiar with.

Let’s get cooking!

Soup: Tender Spare Rib Congee

Congee is a Chinese rice porridge that is traditionally eaten for breakfast, but it’s incredibly versatile and can be enjoyed at any time of the day. With its delicate flavors and luxurious tenderness, the spare rib congee is the perfect dish to kick off our culinary adventure.

Ingredients:

  • 200g pork spare ribs
  • 100g short-grain rice
  • 2 cups water
  • 1 piece daikon radish
  • 4 cups chicken broth
  • 2 green onions
  • 2 tablespoons ginger, finely chopped
  • 2 tablespoons soy sauce

Instructions:

  1. Submerge the spare ribs in cool water and let them soak for a few minutes to remove excess fat.
  2. Drain the ribs and cut them into smaller, bite-sized pieces.
  3. In a large pot, bring the chicken broth to a boil and add the ribs. Let them simmer for 20 minutes.
  4. Next, add the rice, daikon radish, and a pinch of salt. Lower the heat and let it cook for 1 hour more.
  5. Halfway through the cooking time, chop the green onions and ginger together. Then set them aside for now.
  6. After letting the congee cook for a full hour, incorporate the chopped green onions and ginger into the pot. Add the soy sauce to taste, and your tender spare rib congee is ready to devour.

Appetizer: Smoked Tofu and Mushroom Dumplings

Dumplings are often considered a staple in Chinese cuisine, and these smoked tofu and mushroom dumplings offer an exciting twist on the classic. Perfectly steamed and filled with smoky flavors, these dumplings will surely delight your senses.

Ingredients:

  • 200g smoked tofu
  • 100g generalized mushrooms
  • 1 piece daikon radish
  • 4 wonton wrappers

Instructions:

  1. Begin by finely dicing the daikon radish, then sauté it in a pan with a splash of vegetable oil over medium heat until tender.
  2. While the radish cooks, slice the mushrooms and chop the smoked tofu into small, bite-sized pieces.
  3. Add the mushrooms to the pan with the radish, sautéing for a few more minutes until fully cooked.
  4. In a mixing bowl, combine the sautéed ingredients with the smoked tofu and season with a pinch of salt and pepper to taste. Allow the mixture to cool before assembling the dumplings.
  5. Once the filling has cooled, work with one wonton wrapper at a time, keeping the others covered to avoid drying. Place a heaping tablespoon of the tofu-mushroom mixture in the center of the wrapper.
  6. Create a small pleated dumpling by following the traditional Chinese method and fold the wrapper over the filling. Set your dumpling aside on a tray and repeat for the remaining wonton wrappers and filling.
  7. Steam the dumplings in a steamer basket over boiling water for 10-12 minutes until they are cooked through and the wrappers are soft.

Main Course: Mapo Tofu

Mapo Tofu is an iconic Sichuan dish, well-known for its spicy and piquant flavors. With a tender, silken tofu backdrop and the fiery, earthy pungency of minced pork, this dish strikes a harmonious culinary balance.

Ingredients:

  • 200g silken tofu
  • 100g ground pork
  • 2 pieces Sichuan peppercorns
  • 1 tablespoon fermented soybean paste
  • 1 teaspoon Sichuan broad bean paste
  • 1 tablespoon ginger, finely chopped
  • 1 clove garlic, minced
  • 2 green onions
  • 1 tablespoon cornstarch
  • 1 cup water
  • Lipstick red food coloring

Instructions:

  1. Begin by coarsely grinding the Sichuan peppercorns in a mortar and pestle. Set aside for later.
  2. In a large pan, cook the ground pork over medium heat, breaking it up into smaller pieces as it cooks. Once fully cooked, remove the pork from the pan and set it aside.
  3. In the same pan, add the fermented soybean paste, Sichuan broad bean paste, chopped ginger, and minced garlic. Cook for 3-4 minutes to meld the flavors.
  4. Next, add the ground pork, the ground Sichuan peppercorns, a dash of lipstick red food coloring, and 1 cup of water. Stir everything together until it’s combined and let it simmer for 5 minutes.
  5. To create a velvety, rich consistency, mix the cornstarch with 4 tablespoons of water in a small bowl and stir it into the sauce. Let it simmer for another few minutes until the sauce has thickened.
  6. Gently fold in the silken tofu cubes and mix well to cover each piece in the aromatic sauce. Serve the Mapo Tofu in your favorite dish, garnishing with thinly sliced green onions.

Dessert: Sweet Corn Soup

A warm and satisfying way to end your Chinese cooking journey, Sweet Corn Soup is a delightfully creamy dish that embodies childlike nostalgia. This dessert will leave you craving more.

Ingredients:

  • 200ml evaporated milk
  • 4 cups water
  • 2 cups corn kernels
  • 1 cup sugar
  • 1 tablespoon cornstarch
  • 1 cinnamon stick

Instructions:

  1. First, let’s make a simple corn stock by boiling the corn kernels in a pot with 3 cups of water for about 20 minutes. Once complete, strain the liquid and set it aside.
  2. Dissolve the cornstarch in 1/4 cup of cold water and set it aside.
  3. In another pot, heat up the corn stock and add the sugar, stirring until the sugar has fully dissolved.
  4. Strain the corn stock into a clean pot, discard the corn, and add the dissolved cornstarch to the mixture. Whisk well to combine.
  5. Bring the mixture to a gentle simmer and slowly pour in the evaporated milk while stirring continuously.
  6. Simmer for an additional 3-5 minutes until the soup thickens, then remove the cinnamon stick before serving.

And there you have it - a journey from soup to nuts in the magical realm of Chinese cooking! Try these recipes at home, experiment with incorporating your own unique flavors, and don’t be afraid to tweak the traditional methods to suit your taste. Most importantly, enjoy the rich tapestry of flavors and let yourself be whisked away in the depth of Chinese cuisine.
