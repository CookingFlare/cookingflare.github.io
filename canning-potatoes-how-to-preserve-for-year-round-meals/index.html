Canning Potatoes: How to Preserve for Year-Round Meals


When it comes to preserving food for long-term use, few methods are as effective and reliable as canning. Canning allows you to store vegetables, fruits, and even meats for months or years, ensuring that you always have access to your favorite ingredients. One of the most versatile and popular vegetables to can is the potato. Canning potatoes is an excellent way to extend their shelf life, save money, and ensure that you always have a convenient, ready-to-use ingredient for your meals.

In this guide, we will walk you through the process of canning potatoes, offering tips, tricks, and essential information to ensure your potatoes are preserved correctly. Whether you’re canning your harvest, taking advantage of sales at the grocery store, or simply looking to stock up for convenience, this method will help you keep potatoes on hand all year round.

Why Canning Potatoes Is a Great Idea

Potatoes are a staple in many households because they’re versatile, nutritious, and easy to cook. However, fresh potatoes can go bad quickly if not stored properly. Canning them helps preserve their freshness, allowing you to enjoy them throughout the year. Here are some key benefits of canning potatoes:

  1. Long Shelf Life: Canning potatoes allows you to store them for months or even years. When stored in a cool, dark place, your canned potatoes can last up to a year or more.

  2. Convenience: Canned potatoes are ready to use right out of the jar. Whether you need them for mashed potatoes, stews, or potato salads, the preparation time is greatly reduced.

  3. No Waste: Canning potatoes helps prevent waste. If you have a large potato harvest or can’t use all the potatoes you bought, canning ensures they don’t spoil before you get a chance to use them.

  4. Cost-Effective: Canning potatoes can be a cost-effective way to buy in bulk and preserve your purchase. Potatoes tend to be inexpensive, especially during harvest season, so stocking up through canning can help you save money in the long run.

  5. Storage Flexibility: Once canned, potatoes take up very little space and can be stored on a pantry shelf, making them easy to stockpile without requiring freezer space.

Types of Potatoes Suitable for Canning

While nearly all potatoes can be canned, some varieties are better suited for preservation than others. When selecting potatoes for canning, consider the following:

  • Waxy Potatoes: Varieties like red potatoes and fingerlings are ideal for canning because of their low starch content and firm texture. They hold their shape well during the canning process and are excellent for dishes like salads, stews, and hash browns.

  • All-Purpose Potatoes: Yukon Gold and other all-purpose potatoes are versatile and can be used in a variety of recipes. They have a moderate starch content, making them suitable for both canning and cooking.

  • Starchy Potatoes: Russet potatoes, while great for baking and mashed potatoes, are more prone to breaking down during the canning process due to their high starch content. If you prefer to can starchy potatoes, consider using them for mashed potatoes after canning rather than in their whole form.

The Best Method for Canning Potatoes

Canning potatoes is a straightforward process that requires some basic equipment and preparation. The most common method of canning potatoes is pressure canning. This is necessary because potatoes are a low-acid food, and using a water bath canner would not reach the high temperatures required to ensure food safety. Pressure canning is the only safe method for canning potatoes and other low-acid vegetables like carrots, green beans, and corn.

Equipment Needed for Canning Potatoes

Before you start canning potatoes, make sure you have the following equipment:

  • Pressure Canner: A pressure canner is essential for safely canning low-acid foods. It ensures that your potatoes are preserved at the correct temperature and pressure.
  • Canning Jars: Mason jars with two-piece lids are the most commonly used containers for canning. Ensure your jars are free from cracks or chips.
  • Jar Lifter: A jar lifter is used to handle hot jars after they’ve been processed.
  • Lid Seals: Make sure you have fresh lid seals for a secure closure.
  • Canning Funnel: This helps fill jars cleanly and efficiently.
  • Bubble Remover Tool: A bubble remover ensures that there are no air bubbles trapped in your jars during the canning process.
  • Large Pot for Boiling: You’ll need a large pot to blanch your potatoes before canning them.
  • Timer: Precision is important when canning, and a timer will help you keep track of processing times.

Preparing Potatoes for Canning

The first step in canning potatoes is to thoroughly clean and prepare them. Here’s how you can do it:

  1. Select Potatoes: Choose fresh, firm potatoes without any signs of spoilage, such as soft spots or sprouting. Wash them thoroughly to remove dirt and debris.

  2. Peel (Optional): You can peel your potatoes if desired, but it’s not strictly necessary. Some people prefer to leave the skins on, especially if they are canning waxy potatoes.

  3. Cut into Uniform Pieces: To ensure even cooking and consistent texture, cut the potatoes into uniform pieces. You can slice them, dice them, or leave them whole, depending on your intended use.

  4. Blanch the Potatoes: Blanching is an essential step in preparing potatoes for canning. It helps preserve their color, texture, and nutrients. To blanch potatoes:

    • Bring a large pot of water to a boil.
    • Add the potato pieces and cook for about 2 to 3 minutes, just enough to start softening them.
    • Immediately transfer the potatoes into a bowl of ice water to stop the cooking process.

  5. Drain and Pack: Once the potatoes have cooled, drain them thoroughly. Pack the potatoes into your prepared canning jars, leaving about an inch of headspace at the top.

Canning Potatoes in a Pressure Canner

Now that your potatoes are prepared, it’s time to can them. Follow these steps to pressure can your potatoes:

  1. Add Water to the Jars: For each jar of potatoes, add water to cover the potatoes. You can use plain water or a saltwater solution (about 1 teaspoon of salt per quart of water) if desired.

  2. Remove Air Bubbles: Use a bubble remover tool to gently press down along the sides of the jar and release any air bubbles trapped around the potatoes.

  3. Clean Jar Rims: Before sealing the jars, use a clean, damp cloth to wipe the rims of the jars. This will ensure a proper seal.

  4. Seal the Jars: Place the lids on the jars, ensuring that they are centered and seated properly. Screw the rings onto the jars until they are just fingertip-tight.

  5. Prepare the Pressure Canner: Place the jars in your pressure canner, ensuring they are not overcrowded. Add the recommended amount of water to the canner (usually 2 to 3 inches) and close the lid.

  6. Start the Pressure Canner: Set your pressure canner to the correct pressure for your altitude and the size of the jars. Typically, you’ll need to can potatoes at 10 to 11 pounds of pressure for quart-sized jars (adjust based on your altitude). Process the potatoes for 35 minutes if canning in pint jars, or 40 minutes for quart jars.

  7. Allow for Cooling: After processing, turn off the heat and allow the pressure to naturally release. Once the pressure has returned to normal, carefully open the canner lid and remove the jars using a jar lifter.

  8. Check the Seals: Once your jars have cooled for several hours, check the seals by pressing down on the center of each lid. If it doesn’t pop back, the jar is sealed properly.

Storing Your Canned Potatoes

After your potatoes are properly canned and sealed, it’s time to store them. Place your jars in a cool, dark place like a pantry or cupboard. Avoid direct sunlight and extreme temperatures. Properly sealed jars can last up to a year or more, though they are best used within six months to ensure the best quality.

How to Use Canned Potatoes

Canned potatoes can be used in a wide variety of dishes. Some of the most popular ways to use canned potatoes include:

  • Mashed Potatoes: Simply open the jar, drain, and mash them with butter, milk, and seasoning for an easy side dish.
  • Stews and Soups: Canned potatoes are a great addition to hearty stews and soups.
  • Potato Salad: Canned potatoes work well in potato salad since they hold their shape better than fresh potatoes that might break down during cooking.
  • Fried Potatoes: You can fry canned potatoes in a skillet for a quick and crispy snack or side dish.
  • Hash Browns: Drain and shred canned potatoes, then fry them for an easy breakfast treat.

Troubleshooting Tips

While canning potatoes is relatively straightforward, there are a few common issues that may arise:

  • Jar Seal Failures: If your jars fail to seal, check that you cleaned the jar rims properly, didn’t overtighten the lids, and processed at the correct pressure.
  • Cloudy Liquid: It’s common for the liquid in your jars to appear cloudy, especially with starchy potatoes like Russets. This does not affect the safety of the potatoes but may affect their appearance.
  • Soft Potatoes: Overprocessing can result in soft or mushy potatoes. Stick to the recommended processing times to avoid this issue.

Final Thoughts

Canning potatoes is a fantastic way to preserve this humble yet versatile vegetable for year-round use. Whether you want the convenience of having potatoes ready at a moment’s notice or you’re looking to stock up for the winter months, canned potatoes are a smart addition to your pantry. With the right equipment and preparation, you can enjoy fresh, home-preserved potatoes all year long. Happy canning!
