Exploring Soul Food: 7 Comforting Southern Recipes


Soul food, the quintessential cuisine of the American South, is a hearty and satisfying reflection of the region's warm hospitality and rich cultural heritage. Born out of the makeshift dishes prepared by enslaved Africans, soul food has evolved to become a beloved comfort food staple for many around the world. At its core, soul food is about community, tradition, and the joy of sharing a delicious meal with loved ones. In this post, we'll delve into the rich culinary traditions of the Southern United States, exploring 7 comforting recipes that showcase the region's most iconic soul food dishes.

  1. Classic Fried Chicken Crunchy on the outside, juicy on the inside, classic fried chicken is the undisputed king of Southern comfort food. This simple recipe requires just a few key ingredients: 2 lbs chicken pieces, 1 cup all-purpose flour, 2 tsp paprika, 1 tsp garlic powder, 1 tsp onion powder, 1 tsp salt, 1/2 tsp black pepper, and 1 cup buttermilk.

Season the chicken pieces with paprika, garlic powder, onion powder, salt, and black pepper. Dip the seasoned chicken in the buttermilk, then coat it in the flour mixture, shaking off excess. Heat about 1/2 inch of oil in a large skillet over medium-high heat, and fry the chicken in batches until golden brown, flipping halfway through. Drain the cooked chicken on paper towels and serve hot with your favorite sides.

  1. Hoppin' John with Smoked Sausage Hoppin' John is a traditional dish from the Gullah Geechee region of South Carolina, typically prepared to celebrate the New Year's Day. This rich, flavorful recipe combines the comforting tastes of black-eyed peas, smoked sausage, and a hint of spice.

Cook 1 cup dried black-eyed peas in a large pot of boiling salted water until tender. In a separate skillet, cook 1 cup sliced smoked sausage over medium-high heat until browned. Stir in 1 cup chopped onion, 2 cloves garlic, 1 tsp ground cumin, and 1 tsp smoked paprika into the skillet. Add the cooked black-eyed peas to the skillet and stir well to combine. Season with salt and pepper to taste and serve with crusty bread or over rice.

  1. Braised Greens with Ham Hocks Collard, mustard, or turnip greens – Southern Braised Greens are a hearty, wed-with flavors staple during the colder months. The addition of a ham hock gives this comforting dish an unmistakable depth of meaty flavor.

In a large pot, combine 1 quart braising liquid (such as chicken or beef broth with a splash of water), 2 tbsp brown sugar, 1 tsp salt, 1 tsp ground black pepper, and 2 tbsp chopped onion. Add in 2 cups chopped collard greens and 2 small ham hocks to the pot. Cover the pot and simmer until the greens are tender, about 30-40 minutes. Taste and adjust seasoning with additional sugar, salt, or pepper as needed.

  1. Sweet Potato Casserole The ultimate Southern dessert casserole, sweet potato casserole is a culmination of a simpler pleasure: the unmatched flavor of sweet potatoes baked with marshmallows and a crunchy pecan topping.

Combine 2 cups cooked and mashed sweet potatoes with 1 cup brown sugar, 1 tsp ground cinnamon, and 1/4 tsp ground nutmeg in a large bowl. Stir in 1/4 cup melted butter and 2 large eggs to the bowl. Press the sweet potato mixture into a 9x13 inch baking dish. Top the mixture with 1 cup mini marshmallows and a 1/2 cup chopped pecans. Bake at 350°F (180°C) until the marshmallows are golden brown and puffed.

  1. Creamy Grits with Scallions Grits are a staple Southern dish, traditionally prepared as a thick, rich pudding for breakfast. This creamy grits recipe with scallions is a delightful twist on the classic, adding a flavor boost from a pan-fried scallion and a sliver of butter.

In a large saucepan, bring 2 cups water to a boil over high heat. Stir in 1 cup stone-ground grits and a pinch of salt. Reduce the heat to low and cook for 5 minutes, stirring frequently. Stir in 2 tbsp unsalted butter, 1 scallion pan-fried in butter until softened, and a dash of salt to taste.

  1. Fried Catfish with Remoulade Sauce Southern fried catfish is an equal union of the peerless crunch and rich batter flavors aptly meets the tangy, creamy remoulade sauce.

In a shallow dish, mix together 1 cup all-purpose flour, 2 tsp paprika, 1 tsp garlic powder, 1 tsp onion powder, 1 tsp salt, and 1/2 tsp black pepper. Dip 1 lb catfish cutlets into the batter and fry in batches until golden brown in a skillet with 1/2 inch oil over medium-high heat. Meanwhile, combine 1 cup mayonnaise, 1/4 cup chopped fresh parsley, 2 tbsp freshly squeezed lemon juice, and 1 tsp Dijon mustard in a small bowl. Stir in 1 tsp grated onion, 1 tsp grated carrot, and 1 tsp chopped celery root if using.

  1. Baked Peach Cobbler A simple, stirring staple among Southern desserts, peach cobbler combines fresh peaches, cinnamon, nutmeg, and sugar-coated crisp biscuits for a comforting summertime dessert or snack that's synonymous with home-cooked goodness.

In a large bowl, combine 2 cups sliced peaches, 2 tbsp sugar, 1 tsp ground cinnamon, and 1/4 tsp ground nutmeg. Roll out 1 cup biscuit dough on a lightly floured surface, and cut into 6 pieces. Fill the baking dish with peaches and arrange biscuits atop peaches in this famous Southern dessert.

These comforting soul food recipes span generations, from great-aunts to social gatherings, evoking strong feelings of warmth, love, and nostalgia. Whether you're hosting a dinner party or cooking a simple meal for your loved ones, these classic Southern soul food made with love is sure is to find a spot in your long, long time, beloved recipes cookbook.
