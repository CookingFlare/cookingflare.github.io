How to Make Whole Wheat Sourdough Bread


Whole wheat sourdough bread is a hearty, flavorful bread that combines the rich nutty taste of whole wheat with the tangy, slightly sour flavor that only a traditional sourdough starter can provide. Whether you are a seasoned baker or a beginner, making whole wheat sourdough bread at home is a rewarding process that yields a loaf full of character and depth. In this guide, we’ll walk you through the steps to make whole wheat sourdough bread, from creating your own starter to baking a perfect loaf.

Understanding Whole Wheat Sourdough Bread

Whole wheat sourdough bread is different from traditional white sourdough due to the use of whole wheat flour, which includes all parts of the wheat grain – the bran, germ, and endosperm. This gives the bread more fiber, nutrients, and a heartier texture. The natural fermentation process, driven by wild yeast and bacteria in the sourdough starter, not only leavens the bread but also enhances its flavor.

The process of making sourdough bread from scratch may seem daunting at first, but with patience and practice, anyone can master the art. The following recipe covers everything you need to know to create your own whole wheat sourdough bread.

What You Will Need

Ingredients for the Sourdough Starter

  1. Whole Wheat Flour: This is the base of your starter and provides the necessary nutrients to foster the growth of wild yeast and bacteria.
  2. Water: Ideally filtered water, as chlorine and other chemicals in tap water can hinder yeast activity.
  3. A clean container: A glass or plastic jar will work best. Make sure it’s large enough to allow the starter to grow.

Ingredients for the Bread Dough

  1. Whole Wheat Flour: This will form the bulk of the bread dough.
  2. Water: To hydrate the flour and bring the dough together.
  3. Sourdough Starter: Your active starter will provide the leavening for the bread.
  4. Salt: Enhances flavor and strengthens the dough.
  5. Optional: Honey or Maple Syrup: Adds a touch of sweetness to balance the bread’s tang.

Step 1: Creating Your Sourdough Starter

Creating your own sourdough starter can take about 5 to 7 days, but don’t worry—it’s a fairly simple process that requires just two ingredients: whole wheat flour and water. Here’s how you can make it:

  1. Day 1: In a clean jar, mix 50g of whole wheat flour with 50g of water. Stir well to combine, making sure there are no dry patches of flour. Cover loosely with a lid or cloth and leave it in a warm spot, around 70-75°F (21-24°C), for 24 hours.
  2. Day 2: You may not see much activity yet, but it’s important to continue feeding your starter. Discard half of the mixture (about 50g) and add another 50g of flour and 50g of water. Stir well and cover loosely again. Leave it for another 24 hours.
  3. Days 3-5: Continue the process of discarding half the mixture and feeding it with equal parts flour and water. By day 3 or 4, you should start to notice some bubbling and a faint sour aroma.
  4. Day 6 and beyond: By now, your starter should be quite active, with visible bubbles and a strong, tangy aroma. When you feed it, the mixture should double in size within a few hours, indicating that it’s ready to use in baking.

Once your starter is ready, you can store it in the refrigerator between baking sessions, feeding it once a week to keep it healthy.

Step 2: Preparing the Dough

Now that you have your active sourdough starter, it’s time to prepare the dough for your whole wheat sourdough bread. You’ll need:

  • 500g Whole Wheat Flour: The base of your bread dough.
  • 350g Water: This will hydrate the flour and form the dough.
  • 100g Sourdough Starter: Your active starter that provides leavening.
  • 10g Salt: For flavor and structure.
  • Optional: 1 tablespoon of Honey or Maple Syrup: Adds a subtle sweetness.

Instructions:

  1. Autolyse: In a large mixing bowl, combine the whole wheat flour and water. Stir until there are no dry patches, then cover the bowl with a damp towel and let it sit for about 30 minutes. This process hydrates the flour and helps to develop the gluten.

  2. Add the Starter and Salt: After the autolyse period, add your sourdough starter and salt to the dough. Mix until everything is fully incorporated. The dough should be sticky but hold together.

  3. Kneading: Transfer the dough to a lightly floured surface. Begin kneading the dough by folding it over itself and pushing it away with the heels of your hands. Continue for about 8-10 minutes until the dough is smooth, elastic, and slightly tacky. If the dough is too sticky, you can add a little more flour, but be careful not to add too much as it can make the bread dense.

  4. Bulk Fermentation: Place the dough in a lightly oiled bowl and cover it with a damp cloth. Let the dough rise at room temperature for about 4-6 hours. During this time, the dough should roughly double in size. Every hour for the first three hours, perform a set of stretch and folds: gently pull one side of the dough, stretch it upwards, and fold it over the center. Turn the bowl and repeat this for all four sides. This helps develop the gluten and structure of the bread.

  5. Shaping the Dough: After the bulk fermentation is complete, the dough should be light and airy. Gently turn it out onto a lightly floured surface and shape it into a round or oval loaf, depending on your preference. If you are using a Dutch oven, shape the dough to fit the size of the pot. If using a bread pan, shape it to fit the pan.

  6. Final Proofing: Place the shaped dough in a proofing basket or bowl lined with a floured towel. Cover it and let it rest for 2-4 hours at room temperature, or for an overnight proof in the refrigerator. The dough should rise slightly but not double in size. This final proofing gives the bread a more complex flavor.

Step 3: Baking the Bread

  1. Preheat the Oven: While the dough is finishing its final proof, preheat your oven to 450°F (232°C). If you are using a Dutch oven, place it inside the oven to preheat as well. The hot pot will create a steamy environment, which helps the bread rise and develop a crispy crust.

  2. Scoring the Bread: Once the dough has finished proofing, it’s time to score it. Use a sharp knife or bread lame to make a few slashes on the top of the loaf. This allows the bread to expand during baking and gives it a professional, artisanal look.

  3. Baking: If using a Dutch oven, carefully transfer the dough into the hot pot and cover it with the lid. If baking on a baking sheet, place the dough directly into the oven. Bake for 20-25 minutes with the lid on (or with a steaming pan of water in the oven for moisture). After this, remove the lid (or steaming pan) and continue to bake for another 20-25 minutes until the loaf is golden brown and sounds hollow when tapped on the bottom.

  4. Cooling: Once the bread is baked, remove it from the oven and let it cool on a wire rack for at least an hour before slicing. This step is crucial because the bread continues to set as it cools, and slicing it too early can result in a doughy texture.

Tips for Perfect Whole Wheat Sourdough Bread

  • Use Fresh Flour: Freshly milled whole wheat flour yields the best flavor and texture. If you can, grind your own flour or buy from a local mill.
  • Temperature Control: Sourdough thrives in warm, stable temperatures. Keep the dough in a warm spot during fermentation, but not in direct heat or drafts.
  • Patience: Sourdough baking is a slow process, and the longer fermentation times result in better flavor and texture. Don’t rush it!
  • Adjust Hydration: Whole wheat flour absorbs more water than white flour, so feel free to adjust the amount of water if your dough feels too dry.

Troubleshooting Whole Wheat Sourdough Bread

  • Dense Bread: If your bread is too dense, it could be due to insufficient fermentation or too little water. Make sure you’re allowing enough time for the dough to rise, and consider adding a bit more water to the dough.
  • Underproofed Dough: If your dough doesn’t rise as much as you’d like, give it more time to ferment. Sourdough dough can be slow, and cooler temperatures can extend proofing times.
  • Crumb Texture Issues: If the crumb (the inside of the bread) is too tight or gummy, it may be because of too much flour or not enough hydration. Whole wheat flour needs more water than white flour, so adjust accordingly.

Conclusion

Making whole wheat sourdough bread is a satisfying and rewarding process that allows you to bake a delicious loaf from scratch. By following these steps and practicing patience, you’ll soon be able to enjoy homemade sourdough with a hearty, nutty flavor and a chewy, tangy crumb. Remember, the key to great sourdough is consistency—keep experimenting with your starter, dough hydration, and proofing times until you get the perfect loaf for you. Happy baking!
