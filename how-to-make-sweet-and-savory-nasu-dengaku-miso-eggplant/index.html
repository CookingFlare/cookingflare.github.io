How to Make Sweet and Savory Nasu Dengaku (Miso Eggplant)


When it comes to the world of Japanese cuisine, one dish that stands out for its unique blend of flavors and textures is Nasu Dengaku, also known as sweet and savory miso eggplant. This traditional dish is far more than just a vegetable; it’s an experience that encapsulates the very essence of Japanese culinary artistry. Whether served as an appetizer, side dish, or main course, Nasu Dengaku offers layers of umami that delight the senses. The combination of roasted eggplant topped with a rich, savory miso glaze creates an indulgent yet comforting dish that will have your guests asking for seconds. In this comprehensive guide, we will explore the origins, ingredients, preparation techniques, and serving suggestions for this classic dish. Origins of Nasu Dengaku The term 'Nasu Dengaku' derives from the Japanese word 'nasu,' meaning eggplant, and 'dengaku,' a reference to a traditional cooking style that dates back to the Edo period (1603-1868). Historically, Dengaku was associated with festival foods offered to the gods. The miso glaze was traditionally used on skewered vegetables, but it eventually made its way to eggplants, creating the dish we cherish today. The Allure of Miso The heart of Nasu Dengaku lies within its miso glaze. Miso, a fermented soybean paste, is rich in umami flavor, making it a staple in Japanese cooking. The balance of sweetness and savory notes in miso transforms simple ingredients into a culinary masterpiece. When making Nasu Dengaku, the choice of miso—be it red, white, or a blend—significantly influences the flavor profile of the dish.

Red Miso: Offers a robust, earthy flavor that adds depth. White Miso: Provides a milder, sweeter taste that balances well with the eggplant’s natural sweetness. Mixed Miso: A combination to give you the best of both worlds!

Ingredients For the Eggplant:

2 Japanese eggplants (or 1 large regular eggplant) 1 tablespoon of sesame oil (or vegetable oil) Salt, to taste

For the Miso Glaze:

3 tablespoons of miso (choose your preferred type: red, white, or mixed) 2 tablespoons of mirin (sweet rice wine) 1 tablespoon of sake (Japanese rice wine) 1 tablespoon of sugar (or honey for a natural sweetener) 1 teaspoon of soy sauce Optional: 1 tablespoon of sesame seeds for garnish Optional: Chopped green onions or chives for garnish

Preparation Steps Step 1: Preparing the Eggplants Begin by washing the eggplants thoroughly. Japanese eggplants are typically smaller and more slender, making them ideal for grilling or roasting. If you are using a larger globe eggplant, cut it in half lengthwise. The goal is to get the eggplant pieces to approximately 6 inches in length to allow for easy grilling. Next, score the eggplant flesh in a criss-cross pattern without cutting through the skin. This helps the eggplants cook evenly and allows the glaze to penetrate better. Step 2: Salting the Eggplants Sprinkle a generous amount of salt over the cut side of the eggplants. Let them sit for about 30 minutes. This step draws out excess moisture and bitterness from the eggplants. After 30 minutes, rinse the salt off and pat the eggplants dry with a paper towel. Step 3: Making the Miso Glaze In a small mixing bowl, combine the miso, mirin, sake, sugar, and soy sauce. Whisk until you achieve a smooth, cohesive paste. Adjust the sweetness according to your preference; add more sugar or honey if you like a sweeter glaze. Set aside and prepare for the next steps. Step 4: Cooking the Eggplants There are multiple methods to cook the eggplants for Nasu Dengaku, including grilling, broiling, or baking. Choose the method that suits your kitchen setup best. Grilling:

Preheat your grill or grill pan over medium-high heat. Brush the eggplants with sesame oil. Place the eggplants cut side down on the grill and cook for about 4-5 minutes until they develop a nice char. Flip the eggplants over and grill for another 4-5 minutes.

Broiling:

Preheat your broiler and set the oven rack about 6 inches from the heat source. Line a baking sheet with aluminum foil for easier cleanup. Place the eggplants cut side up on the baking sheet and brush with sesame oil. Broil for about 6-8 minutes, keeping an eye on them to prevent burning.

Baking:

Preheat your oven to 375°F (190°C). Place the eggplants in a baking dish cut side up and brush with sesame oil. Bake for about 20-25 minutes, until soft and tender.

Step 5: Applying the Miso Glaze Once your eggplants are cooked, it’s time to brush on the miso glaze. Use a pastry brush to evenly coat the cut side of each eggplant with the miso mixture. It’s ideal to apply the glaze while the eggplants are still hot, allowing the flavors to meld beautifully. Step 6: Final Cooking Steps

Grilling or Broiling Again: If you grilled the eggplants, return them to the grill for an additional 2-3 minutes until the glaze begins to caramelize. If you broiled them, return them to the broiler for 1-2 minutes, watching carefully. Baking Again: Place the glazed eggplants back into the oven for an additional 5-7 minutes. You want to achieve a slightly caramelized, bubbly top.

Step 7: Serving the Nasu Dengaku Once the glaze is caramelized to your liking, take the eggplants out of the heat. Transfer them to a serving platter, and garnish with toasted sesame seeds and chopped green onions or chives for a fresh touch. Serving Suggestions Nasu Dengaku can be served immediately while still warm. It pairs beautifully with a bowl of steaming white rice, or you can serve it as part of a larger Japanese-inspired meal that includes steamed vegetables, pickles, and grilled fish or chicken. Variations and Customizations While the traditional Nasu Dengaku recipe is delightful, you can experiment with various ingredients or cooking methods to add your personal touch. Here are a few variations to consider:

Spicy Miso Glaze: Add a teaspoon of chili paste or a dash of cayenne pepper to the glaze for a spicy kick. Topping Upgrade: Enhance the texture by topping your Nasu Dengaku with panko breadcrumbs before the final broiling for a crispy finish. Vegetarian Twist: Try using other vegetables, such as tofu, kabocha squash, or even zucchini, with the same miso glaze for alternative dishes.

Conclusion Nasu Dengaku is a wonderful embodiment of Japanese culinary traditions, showcasing the beautiful interplay of flavors, textures, and colors. The creamy richness of the eggplant, combined with the sweet and savory miso glaze, makes for a dish that is not only delicious but also visually appealing. From home cooking enthusiasts to seasoned chefs, everyone can appreciate the art of preparing Nasu Dengaku. With its simple ingredients and straightforward cooking techniques, you can create a dish that resonates with authenticity and comfort. So gather your ingredients, fire up your grill or oven, and immerse yourself in the delightful process of making Nasu Dengaku. The next time you have guests over, impress them with this beautiful Japanese delicacy that is sure to be a hit, leaving everyone wanting to savor every last bite. Enjoy your culinary adventure!
