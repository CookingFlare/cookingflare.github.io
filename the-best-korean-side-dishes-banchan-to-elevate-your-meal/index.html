The Best Korean Side Dishes (Banchan) to Elevate Your Meal


Korean cuisine is known for its balance of flavors, vibrant colors, and the way it harmonizes textures and tastes. One of the most distinct features of a traditional Korean meal is the array of banchan, which are small side dishes served alongside the main course. Banchan play a crucial role in Korean dining, offering variety, complementing main dishes, and enhancing the overall experience with their bold flavors. Whether you’re preparing a Korean-inspired dinner at home or dining at a Korean restaurant, understanding the importance of banchan and knowing which ones to include can take your meal to the next level.

In this post, we will explore some of the best Korean side dishes to elevate your meal, from classic favorites to lesser-known gems. These side dishes not only enrich the meal but also allow you to experience the depth of Korean culinary traditions.

What is Banchan?

Banchan are a collection of side dishes that accompany a main dish in Korean cuisine, typically served family-style. They can range from pickled vegetables to stir-fried dishes, fermented foods, and even stews. Traditionally, a Korean meal consists of a few different banchan dishes that complement the main course, often rice, soup, or stew. These small dishes are meant to be shared and enjoyed alongside the main dishes, creating a balanced and diverse flavor profile.

Banchan isn’t just about adding variety to the meal—it’s about enhancing the main dish and offering flavors that balance out the richness or heaviness of the main course. A simple bowl of kimchi can have a significant impact on elevating the meal by providing a tangy, spicy contrast, while a dish like sigeumchi namul (seasoned spinach) brings freshness and lightness.

Now, let’s dive into some of the best Korean side dishes that can truly elevate your meal.

1. Kimchi (김치)

No discussion of Korean side dishes would be complete without mentioning kimchi. This fermented dish, made primarily with napa cabbage and radish, is packed with bold flavors and is the quintessential side dish in Korean cuisine. Kimchi is often spicy, tangy, and umami-rich, thanks to the fermentation process that involves garlic, ginger, chili peppers, and fish sauce or salted shrimp.

Kimchi not only provides a burst of flavor but is also packed with beneficial probiotics, making it a healthy addition to any meal. You’ll find variations of kimchi across Korean cuisine, with different vegetables and spices depending on the region. Some of the most popular types include:

  • Baechu kimchi (napa cabbage kimchi)
  • Kkakdugi (cubed radish kimchi)
  • Oi sobagi (cucumber kimchi)

Kimchi can be served on its own, but it’s often paired with rice and other banchan for a well-rounded meal. Its piquant taste balances out the richness of other dishes and adds a kick to any Korean meal.

2. Sigeumchi Namul (시금치 나물)

Sigeumchi namul is a simple yet flavorful side dish made with spinach. Spinach is blanched and then seasoned with sesame oil, garlic, soy sauce, and a sprinkle of sesame seeds. This dish is light, refreshing, and healthy, making it a perfect complement to heartier, meat-based dishes.

The combination of earthy spinach with the nutty flavor of sesame oil creates a harmonious flavor profile, while the garlic adds depth. The texture is also a significant part of what makes this dish so appealing—blanched spinach is tender yet maintains a slight crunch when paired with the sesame seeds.

Sigeumchi namul is one of the most popular banchan dishes in Korean homes and restaurants. It pairs beautifully with rice and is often served with other vegetable-based banchan for a refreshing contrast to the richer dishes.

3. Japchae (잡채)

Japchae is a well-known Korean noodle dish that is often served as a banchan at Korean meals. Made from sweet potato starch noodles, known as dangmyeon, japchae is stir-fried with a variety of vegetables like carrots, mushrooms, onions, and spinach. It’s flavored with soy sauce, sesame oil, and a touch of sugar, creating a delightful balance of sweet, savory, and umami flavors.

The chewy texture of the sweet potato noodles, combined with the vibrant vegetables, makes japchae a satisfying side dish. It’s also a great dish for parties and gatherings due to its versatility and ability to be made in advance. Often served cold or at room temperature, japchae is a refreshing addition to any Korean meal.

Japchae can be served as a side dish or as a main dish itself, depending on the portion size. When included as a banchan, it pairs well with rice and other side dishes, providing a balance of textures and flavors.

4. Banchan Pickled Vegetables (장아찌)

Korean pickled vegetables, or jangajji, are an essential part of any Korean meal. These vegetables are typically marinated in soy sauce, vinegar, sugar, and other seasonings, allowing the flavors to develop over time. Popular pickled vegetables include pickled garlic, pickled radish, pickled cucumbers, and pickled peppers.

The tangy, slightly sweet flavor of pickled vegetables provides a refreshing contrast to the richer, savory dishes that often dominate Korean meals. Pickled vegetables also help cleanse the palate between bites, ensuring that the other flavors don’t overwhelm your senses. They are easy to prepare and can be stored for long periods, making them a convenient addition to any meal.

5. Kimchi Jeon (김치전)

Kimchi jeon is a savory pancake made from kimchi and flour. The kimchi is mixed with a simple batter and fried until crispy on the outside and soft on the inside. It’s a delicious way to use leftover kimchi, as the fermentation process enhances the flavor of the pancake, making it even more flavorful than fresh kimchi.

The crispy texture combined with the sour and spicy notes of kimchi creates an irresistible bite. Kimchi jeon is often served with a dipping sauce made from soy sauce, vinegar, and chili flakes, adding another layer of depth to the dish.

Kimchi jeon is especially popular during rainy days in Korea, as the crispy pancake pairs perfectly with a warm bowl of soju or makgeolli (Korean rice wine). It’s also a fantastic addition to any meal, adding a bit of indulgence and flavor.

6. Myeolchi Bokkeum (멸치볶음)

Myeolchi bokkeum is a savory dish made with dried anchovies that are stir-fried with soy sauce, garlic, sesame oil, and a touch of sugar. The result is a crispy, umami-packed side dish that can be eaten by itself or sprinkled on top of rice.

The small, dried anchovies add a savory depth to the dish, while the soy sauce and sesame oil provide a rich, earthy flavor. The slight sweetness from the sugar balances out the brininess of the anchovies, making this a delicious and satisfying banchan. It’s a popular dish among Korean families, and many home cooks prepare it in large batches to be enjoyed throughout the week.

7. Pajeon (파전)

Similar to kimchi jeon, pajeon is a savory pancake, but it’s made with green onions (scallions) instead of kimchi. The batter is mixed with chopped green onions and pan-fried to a golden brown. The resulting pancake is crispy on the edges and soft in the middle, with the subtle sweetness of green onions shining through.

Pajeon is often served as an appetizer or side dish, paired with a dipping sauce made of soy sauce, vinegar, and a bit of chili. It’s especially popular during the spring and summer months, when fresh green onions are in season.

While pajeon is often enjoyed as a snack or appetizer, it also makes an excellent side dish when paired with the main course, providing a comforting texture and a savory flavor profile.

8. Kongnamul Muchim (콩나물 무침)

Kongnamul muchim is a dish made from bean sprouts, which are blanched and seasoned with sesame oil, garlic, soy sauce, and a pinch of salt. This simple side dish has a crunchy texture and a slightly nutty flavor, making it a refreshing contrast to heavier dishes.

The bean sprouts are light and healthy, making this banchan an ideal choice for balancing out the richness of meats or stews. It’s often served at Korean barbecues and is known for its clean, simple taste that lets the natural flavor of the bean sprouts shine.

Conclusion

Korean banchan are the unsung heroes of a traditional Korean meal. These side dishes are an integral part of the dining experience, offering a wide variety of flavors, textures, and ingredients that enhance the main dishes. From the pungent and spicy flavors of kimchi to the light and refreshing taste of sigeumchi namul, banchan bring balance and complexity to every meal.

If you’re looking to elevate your next Korean meal, consider incorporating these banchan into your spread. Whether you’re cooking at home or dining out, these side dishes will not only add delicious variety to your meal but also provide a deeper understanding of the rich culinary heritage of Korea.
