Easter Recipes from Around the World: Global Flavors for Your Feast


Easter is one of the most widely celebrated holidays globally, and the traditions associated with it vary beautifully across cultures. While its religious significance might be universal, the ways people celebrate—and what they eat—reflect a stunning diversity that brings families and communities together. From sweet treats to savory mains, Easter recipes from around the world showcase how different regions honor the season while indulging in vibrant culinary traditions. This year, take your Easter feast to the next level by infusing it with global flavors that tell stories from distant places. Here’s a journey through some of the most iconic and delicious Easter recipes from around the world.

1. Italy: Colomba di Pasqua (Easter Dove Bread)

Italian Easter celebrations often center around family meals that are rich in tradition. One of the most treasured Easter treats is Colomba di Pasqua, a dove-shaped sweet bread that symbolizes peace and renewal. This light, fluffy bread is enriched with butter, eggs, and sugar, studded with candied orange peel, and crowned with a crunchy topping of almonds and pearl sugar.

How to Make It:

  1. Mix flour, sugar, and yeast before incorporating softened butter and eggs.
  2. Fold in candied orange peel for bursts of citrusy sweetness.
  3. Shape the dough into a dove form (or use a special mold) and let it rise.
  4. Top with a mix of sugar and ground almonds before baking until golden.

Not only is this bread deeply symbolic, but its tender crumb and delightful aroma guarantee a fresh start to your Easter morning.

2. Mexico: Capirotada (Mexican Bread Pudding)

A staple dessert during Lent and Easter in Mexico, Capirotada is a rich and spiced bread pudding that carries layers of meaning. The ingredients in this dish, like bread, cheese, raisins, and syrup, are said to represent the crucifixion. Some families have passed down their Capirotada recipe for generations, making it a culinary time capsule of sorts.

How to Make It:

  1. Start by slicing bolillo rolls (or baguettes) and toasting them until slightly crisp.
  2. Make a piloncillo syrup infused with cinnamon, cloves, and anise.
  3. Arrange layers of bread, cheese (often queso fresco), nuts, and dried fruits in a baking dish, drizzling each layer with syrup.
  4. Bake until the bread soaks up the syrup but remains distinct in texture.

The result is a sweet and savory masterpiece that combines comfort food vibes with spiritual reflection.

3. Greece: Magiritsa (Greek Easter Soup)

No traditional Greek Easter feast is complete without Magiritsa. This fragrant lamb soup is made on Holy Saturday and enjoyed after the midnight Easter service, serving as a way to break the Lenten fast. The dish is flavored with fresh herbs like dill, parsley, and scallions, along with a squeeze of lemon for brightness.

How to Make It:

  1. Sauté lamb liver and onions until browned, then add water or broth.
  2. Add rice to simmer and soften.
  3. Whisk eggs and lemon juice together to make a creamy avgolemono sauce, then stir it into the soup.
  4. Garnish with fresh herbs for a fresh, zesty finish.

This traditional dish delivers homey and comforting flavors perfect for ringing in Easter Sunday.

4. Poland: Babka Wielkanocna (Easter Babka)

In Poland, Babka Wielkanocna reigns supreme as a celebratory Easter dessert. This tall, round cake is airy yet rich, often flavored with hints of vanilla, citrus, or rum and finished with a sweet glaze.

How to Make It:

  1. Cream butter and sugar until light and fluffy, then mix in eggs, milk, and flour.
  2. Add a splash of citrus zest or vanilla for classic flavor.
  3. Bake in a fluted pan for the traditional shape.
  4. Drizzle with a simple powdered sugar glaze or sprinkle with confectioners’ sugar once cool.

Serve with tea or coffee, and enjoy every bite of this pillowy, festive cake.

5. Brazil: Bacalhau à Brás (Salt Cod with Eggs and Onions)

Easter in Brazil often highlights Bacalhau à Brás, a satisfying dish made with salt cod, eggs, olives, and onions. With roots in Portugal, this recipe reflects centuries of shared culture and culinary traditions.

How to Make It:

  1. Soak salted cod overnight to remove excess salt, then cook and flake it.
  2. Sauté onions and garlic until soft and add the cod.
  3. Toss in beaten eggs, stirring gently until cooked but creamy.
  4. Garnish with olives, parsley, and a drizzle of olive oil.

This hearty dish is perfect for Easter brunch or dinner—a celebration of bold flavors and textures.

6. Finland: Mämmi (Rye Pudding)

Finnish Easter tables often feature Mämmi, a traditional dessert made with rye flour, malt, and molasses. This dark, sticky pudding has an earthy sweetness that pairs beautifully with cream or vanilla sauce.

How to Make It:

  1. Mix rye flour, malt, and molasses with water to form a porridge-like consistency.
  2. Bake low and slow for several hours to develop deep flavors.
  3. Serve chilled, topped with sugar and cream.

Though it may be an acquired taste for some, Mämmi is a cherished tradition in Finland, offering a connection to the past.

7. United States: Deviled Eggs

Deviled eggs are a star of many American Easter gatherings. This dish transforms hard-boiled eggs into bite-sized appetizers filled with a tangy, creamy yolk mixture.

How to Make It:

  1. Halve hard-boiled eggs and remove the yolks.
  2. Mash the yolks and mix with mayonnaise, mustard, vinegar, salt, and pepper.
  3. Spoon or pipe the yolk mixture back into the egg whites.
  4. Sprinkle with paprika or chives for garnish.

These easy-to-make bites are a crowd-pleaser, perfect for grazing during Easter festivities.

8. South Africa: Pickled Fish

In South Africa, pickled fish is a must-have dish during Easter, particularly within the Cape Malay community. This dish consists of fried fish soaked in a tangy, aromatic pickling sauce made with curry spices, vinegar, and onions.

How to Make It:

  1. Fry firm white fish like hake or kingklip until golden.
  2. Make a sauce using vinegar, turmeric, curry powder, sugar, and onions.
  3. Layer the fish and pour the sauce over, allowing it to marinate overnight in the fridge.

The result? A bold, zesty dish bursting with aromatic spices and a uniquely South African flair.

9. Germany: Osterlamm (Easter Lamb Cake)

Symbolizing Christ as the “Lamb of God,” Osterlamm is a traditional German cake served during Easter celebrations. Shaped like a lamb, this simple recipe relies on classic ingredients: flour, eggs, sugar, and butter.

How to Make It:

  1. Beat butter and sugar until creamy, then add eggs and vanilla extract.
  2. Fold in flour, baking powder, and a pinch of salt.
  3. Pour the batter into a lamb-shaped mold and bake.
  4. Once cooled, dust with powdered sugar for an elegant finish.

This light and festive cake is as delightful to look at as it is to eat, making it a centerpiece and dessert rolled into one.

Bringing It All Together

Exploring Easter recipes from around the world adds variety and richness to your holiday meal. Whether you’re baking Italy’s tender Colomba di Pasqua or savoring South Africa’s tangy pickled fish, each dish brings its own story and tradition to the table. This year, try incorporating one or more of these recipes into your Easter celebration. Not only will you delight your family and friends with new flavors, but you’ll also embrace the beauty of a truly global feast.

Happy Easter, and may your celebrations be filled with love, laughter, and a spread as diverse and delicious as the cultures we honor today!
