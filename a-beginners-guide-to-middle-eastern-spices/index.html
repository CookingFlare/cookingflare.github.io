A Beginner's Guide to Middle Eastern Spices


Middle Eastern cuisine is a rich tapestry of flavors, colors, and textures, shaped by centuries of trade, migration, and cultural exchange. One of the most exciting aspects of Middle Eastern cooking is its vibrant use of spices. These spices not only bring depth and complexity to dishes but also tell a story of ancient trade routes and culinary traditions that have been passed down through generations.

If you’re new to Middle Eastern cooking, the wide array of spices might seem intimidating at first. But don’t worry! This guide will take you through the essential spices, how to use them, and how to create flavorful dishes in your own kitchen.

The Essence of Middle Eastern Spices

At the heart of Middle Eastern cooking is a balance of flavors. Spices are used not only for heat but to bring out the natural sweetness, sourness, and earthiness of the ingredients. While each region in the Middle East has its own distinctive spice blends and preferences, there are a few core spices that are common across the board.

Cumin: Earthy and Warm

Cumin is one of the most widely used spices in Middle Eastern cooking. With its earthy, nutty flavor and slightly citrusy aroma, cumin plays a crucial role in both savory and sweet dishes. It’s found in everything from stews and soups to rice and meat dishes. Ground cumin is often toasted to intensify its flavor, but whole cumin seeds can also be used for a more robust and aromatic experience.

How to use cumin:

  • Add it to meat rubs, particularly for lamb or beef.
  • Sprinkle it over roasted vegetables for a warm, aromatic touch.
  • Mix it into yogurt with garlic and lemon juice for a refreshing dip.
  • Use it in spice blends like baharat or shawarma.

Coriander: Citrusy and Light

Coriander, also known as cilantro when fresh, is another spice commonly found in Middle Eastern kitchens. The dried seeds of the coriander plant have a warm, citrusy flavor that adds brightness to dishes without overpowering them. Coriander complements cumin wonderfully, making it a staple in spice mixes like garam masala and harissa.

How to use coriander:

  • Incorporate it into marinades for chicken or lamb.
  • Pair it with cumin in stews and soups to create a balanced spice profile.
  • Use it in sweet dishes like baklava for an unexpected twist.
  • Add it to salads or sprinkle over hummus for extra flavor.

Sumac: Tart and Tangy

Sumac is a deep red-purple spice that adds a tangy, lemony flavor to dishes. It’s often used as a souring agent in Middle Eastern cooking, balancing out richer, more savory flavors. Sumac is the key ingredient in the famous spice mix za’atar, but it can also be used on its own to brighten up meats, vegetables, and even drinks.

How to use sumac:

  • Sprinkle over fattoush, a Middle Eastern salad, for a tangy finish.
  • Mix with olive oil and spread on flatbreads before baking for a quick snack.
  • Add it to grilled meats like kebabs for a citrusy punch.
  • Use it as a seasoning for roasted potatoes or fried eggplant.

Paprika: Sweet, Smoky, and Mildly Spicy

Paprika is one of the most versatile spices in Middle Eastern cuisine. While it is often associated with Hungarian cooking, it also plays an important role in the Middle Eastern spice cabinet. There are different varieties of paprika, ranging from sweet to smoky, and each adds a unique depth to dishes. Smoked paprika is particularly beloved for its ability to infuse a rich, smoky flavor into meats and vegetables.

How to use paprika:

  • Season meat dishes like kebabs, shawarma, and kofta.
  • Add a dash to soups or stews for a subtle smoky undertone.
  • Sprinkle on roasted or grilled vegetables for extra color and flavor.
  • Use it in combination with garlic and onion powder for a well-rounded rub.

Cinnamon: Sweet and Warm

Cinnamon is not only for desserts! In the Middle East, it’s often used in savory dishes, particularly in stews, rice, and meat dishes. Its sweet yet spicy warmth provides a complex layer of flavor, making it perfect for dishes that balance both savory and sweet elements. It is a key component in spice blends like baharat and is used in cooking both meat and vegetable-based dishes.

How to use cinnamon:

  • Add to braised lamb or chicken for a fragrant, warming flavor.
  • Sprinkle over rice pilafs, especially those with dried fruits and nuts.
  • Incorporate it into stews or soups with tomatoes and chickpeas.
  • Use it in desserts like baklava, rice pudding, or spiced cakes.

Allspice: Aromatic and Sweet

Despite its name, allspice is a single spice made from the dried berries of the Pimenta dioica plant. Allspice has a complex flavor profile, combining hints of cinnamon, nutmeg, and cloves. It’s a warm, aromatic spice that’s often used in both savory and sweet dishes in the Middle Eastern kitchen.

How to use allspice:

  • Add it to spice blends for meats like lamb or beef.
  • Use it in stews, particularly with tomatoes and onions.
  • Incorporate it into sweet dishes like cakes, pastries, and cookies.
  • Mix it with cinnamon and nutmeg for a warming, aromatic seasoning.

Turmeric: Earthy and Bitter

Turmeric is a vibrant yellow spice known for its earthy, slightly bitter flavor. It’s commonly used in Middle Eastern rice dishes, where it imparts a golden hue and adds depth to the flavor profile. Turmeric is also renowned for its anti-inflammatory properties, making it a healthy addition to your cooking routine.

How to use turmeric:

  • Stir it into rice or couscous dishes to create a beautiful golden color.
  • Use it in marinades for chicken or lamb, often paired with yogurt and garlic.
  • Add it to soups and stews for a subtle depth of flavor.
  • Combine with cumin and coriander in spice blends for a classic Middle Eastern taste.

Cardamom: Sweet and Floral

Cardamom is a fragrant spice with sweet, floral notes that can elevate both savory and sweet dishes. There are two main types of cardamom used in Middle Eastern cooking: green cardamom, which has a fresh, citrusy flavor, and black cardamom, which is smokier and more intense. In Middle Eastern cuisine, cardamom is frequently used in coffee and desserts, but it also works wonders in savory dishes.

How to use cardamom:

  • Use it to flavor Arabic coffee or tea, either whole or ground.
  • Add it to rice or pilaf for a subtle, aromatic flavor.
  • Incorporate it into pastries like baklava or cookies for an exotic twist.
  • Add a few pods to stews and soups for an aromatic touch.

Za’atar: The Quintessential Middle Eastern Blend

Za’atar is a quintessential spice blend in the Middle East, beloved for its versatility and bold flavors. The primary ingredients in za’atar are thyme, oregano, sesame seeds, sumac, and salt. Each region has its own variation, with some adding cumin, coriander, or fennel. Za’atar is used in everything from breads to meat dishes and even as a condiment for dips like hummus.

How to use za’atar:

  • Sprinkle over flatbreads or pita before baking, often with a drizzle of olive oil.
  • Mix into yogurt to create a quick dip for vegetables or pita.
  • Add to roasted vegetables like cauliflower, eggplant, or potatoes.
  • Rub onto chicken or lamb before grilling or roasting.

Cloves: Warm and Pungent

Cloves have a powerful, warm, and slightly sweet flavor that can enhance both savory and sweet dishes. In Middle Eastern cooking, cloves are used sparingly, as their flavor can be quite intense. They are often added to rice dishes, stews, and meat marinades, and they play a role in various spice mixes.

How to use cloves:

  • Add a few whole cloves to rice pilafs or stews to infuse them with warmth.
  • Use them in spice mixes for grilled meats, especially lamb.
  • Pair with cinnamon and cardamom in desserts like cakes and spiced puddings.
  • Incorporate them into hot beverages like mulled wine or chai.

Building a Spice Cabinet for Middle Eastern Cooking

Now that you’re familiar with some of the core spices used in Middle Eastern cuisine, it’s time to stock your spice cabinet. Here’s a basic list of spices and herbs to get you started:

  • Cumin
  • Coriander
  • Sumac
  • Paprika (smoked and sweet)
  • Cinnamon
  • Allspice
  • Turmeric
  • Cardamom (green and black)
  • Za’atar
  • Cloves

You don’t need to buy everything at once, but these spices will give you a solid foundation for making a wide variety of Middle Eastern dishes. As you gain more experience with these spices, you can experiment with creating your own spice blends and customizing flavors to suit your personal taste.

Tips for Using Middle Eastern Spices

  1. Use them in moderation: Middle Eastern spices are aromatic and potent, so a little goes a long way. Start with small amounts and adjust to taste.
  2. Toast whole spices: For maximum flavor, toast whole spices like cumin, coriander, and cardamom in a dry pan before grinding them. This brings out their natural oils and enhances their aroma.
  3. Experiment with spice blends: Spice blends like baharat, ras el hanout, and za’atar are integral to Middle Eastern cooking. Don’t be afraid to try out different combinations to suit your dishes.
  4. Store spices properly: Keep your spices in airtight containers, away from heat and light, to preserve their freshness and flavor.
  5. Balance flavors: In Middle Eastern cooking, balance is key. Spices are often paired with fresh herbs like parsley, mint, and cilantro, as well as ingredients like yogurt, lemon, and olive oil to create harmonious dishes.

Conclusion

Middle Eastern spices are the heart and soul of the cuisine, offering a treasure trove of flavors to explore. Whether you’re cooking a fragrant lamb stew, a vibrant salad, or a decadent dessert, these spices will transport your taste buds to the heart of the Middle East. With just a few key spices in your pantry, you can start creating delicious, aromatic dishes that showcase the best of this rich and diverse culinary tradition.

Don’t be afraid to experiment and discover new combinations that suit your taste. Cooking with Middle Eastern spices is not just about following recipes; it’s about discovering a new world of flavor and adding your own personal touch. Happy cooking!
