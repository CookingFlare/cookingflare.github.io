Fermentation for Beginners: The Best Foods to Start With


Fermentation is one of the oldest culinary techniques, with a history that spans thousands of years. It involves the transformation of food by microorganisms such as bacteria, yeast, or fungi, which break down sugars and starches into acids, gases, or alcohol. This process not only extends the shelf life of foods but also enhances their flavor, texture, and nutritional profile.

If you’re new to fermentation, it might seem a little intimidating at first. But fear not – starting with the right foods can make the process easy and rewarding. In this guide, we will introduce you to some of the best foods to begin fermenting at home. These beginner-friendly ferments are simple, delicious, and provide the perfect introduction to the world of fermentation.

1. Sauerkraut: A Classic Fermented Cabbage

What is it?
Sauerkraut is finely shredded cabbage that has been fermented by lactic acid bacteria. It’s one of the most accessible and popular fermented foods, offering a tangy, slightly sour taste.

Why start with sauerkraut?
Sauerkraut is one of the easiest ferments to make at home. The ingredients are simple—cabbage and salt—making it a great first project for fermentation beginners. The fermentation process is straightforward and doesn’t require any special equipment, though a fermentation vessel or jar can help.

How to make sauerkraut:

  1. Shred a cabbage finely.
  2. Massage the cabbage with salt until it releases its water.
  3. Pack the cabbage into a jar tightly, ensuring that it’s submerged under its own liquid.
  4. Leave the jar at room temperature for about 3 to 7 days, depending on the ambient temperature and how sour you like it. You can taste it throughout the process to monitor its flavor.
  5. Once it’s reached the desired sourness, transfer it to the refrigerator.

Tips for success:

  • Use non-iodized salt, like sea salt or kosher salt, as iodine can interfere with fermentation.
  • Ensure the cabbage is fully submerged in its brine to prevent exposure to air, which can lead to spoilage.

2. Kimchi: A Spicy Fermented Delight

What is it?
Kimchi is a traditional Korean dish made from fermented vegetables, primarily napa cabbage and radishes, seasoned with chili pepper, garlic, ginger, fish sauce, and other spices. It’s known for its bold, spicy, and umami-rich flavors.

Why start with kimchi?
Kimchi offers a more complex fermentation process compared to sauerkraut, but it’s still a relatively simple and fun project. The spiciness adds an exciting twist for those who enjoy a little heat in their foods, and the variety of ingredients means you can customize it to your taste.

How to make kimchi:

  1. Cut the napa cabbage into bite-sized pieces and sprinkle with salt. Let it sit for 2-3 hours to draw out water from the cabbage.
  2. Rinse the cabbage to remove excess salt and mix it with grated radish, garlic, ginger, fish sauce, and Korean chili flakes (gochugaru).
  3. Pack the mixture into a jar, pressing it down firmly.
  4. Leave the jar at room temperature for about 2 to 5 days, depending on your taste preferences.
  5. Store in the refrigerator once it has fermented to your liking.

Tips for success:

  • Kimchi’s flavor will continue to evolve after it’s made, so try it at different stages to find your ideal flavor.
  • For extra depth, some people add fermented fish sauce or a splash of fermented shrimp paste.

3. Yogurt: Creamy and Tangy Ferment

What is it?
Yogurt is a dairy product made by fermenting milk with specific bacterial cultures. The bacteria convert lactose (milk sugar) into lactic acid, thickening the milk and giving it its characteristic tangy flavor.

Why start with yogurt?
Yogurt is one of the simplest and most rewarding ferments. It requires very few ingredients (just milk and yogurt starter culture), and the process can be easily controlled with an inexpensive yogurt maker or even a simple jar in a warm place. Making your own yogurt allows you to experiment with different milk types and fermentation times to create a custom texture and flavor.

How to make yogurt:

  1. Heat milk to about 180°F (82°C) to denature the proteins, then cool it down to 110°F (43°C).
  2. Stir in a small amount of plain yogurt with live cultures as a starter.
  3. Pour the mixture into a clean jar or container and keep it at a warm, stable temperature (around 110°F) for 4-6 hours, or until it reaches your desired consistency and tartness.
  4. Refrigerate the yogurt once it’s set, and it will continue to thicken as it cools.

Tips for success:

  • Use whole milk for a creamier texture, or experiment with different types of milk (like almond or coconut milk) for non-dairy versions.
  • The longer you ferment the yogurt, the more tangy it will become. For milder yogurt, shorten the fermentation time.

4. Kefir: A Fermented Milk Drink

What is it?
Kefir is a fermented milk drink that originates from the Caucasus region. It’s similar to yogurt but has a thinner, drinkable consistency and contains a wider variety of beneficial microorganisms, including bacteria and yeasts.

Why start with kefir?
Kefir is incredibly versatile. It can be used as a drink on its own, poured over granola, or blended into smoothies. It’s also a fantastic introduction to fermenting with a symbiotic culture of bacteria and yeast (SCOBY), which is the starter culture for kefir.

How to make kefir:

  1. Add kefir grains (either purchased or from a previous batch) to fresh milk in a jar.
  2. Let the mixture ferment at room temperature for 12-24 hours.
  3. Strain out the grains, and store the kefir in the refrigerator.

Tips for success:

  • Make sure to use non-pasteurized milk, as pasteurization can destroy the live cultures.
  • You can reuse the kefir grains indefinitely, but be sure to refresh them with new milk regularly.

5. Pickles: Crunchy, Tangy, and Versatile

What is it?
Pickles are vegetables (often cucumbers) preserved through fermentation in a brine of water, salt, and sometimes spices. The process encourages the growth of beneficial lactic acid bacteria that give pickles their signature flavor.

Why start with pickles?
Pickles are among the easiest and most fun ferments to start with. The process is quick, and there are endless variations based on your choice of vegetables, spices, and the level of sourness you desire. Additionally, fermented pickles are often more healthful than their vinegary counterparts, as they contain beneficial probiotics.

How to make pickles:

  1. Choose your vegetables (cucumbers are the classic choice, but carrots, radishes, and green beans work well too).
  2. Prepare a brine by dissolving salt in water (use a ratio of about 1 tablespoon of salt per cup of water).
  3. Add your choice of spices, such as garlic, dill, mustard seeds, or peppercorns.
  4. Submerge the vegetables in the brine, ensuring they are fully covered.
  5. Let them ferment at room temperature for 3 to 7 days, tasting them daily until they reach the desired level of sourness.

Tips for success:

  • Be sure to keep your vegetables fully submerged in the brine to prevent exposure to air.
  • Experiment with different flavorings, such as bay leaves, peppercorns, or even chili flakes for some heat.

6. Sourdough Bread: A Wild Fermentation Journey

What is it?
Sourdough bread is made from a naturally fermented starter consisting of flour and water, which is colonized by wild yeasts and bacteria present in the environment. These microorganisms break down the sugars in the flour, creating carbon dioxide (which makes the dough rise) and lactic acid (which gives sourdough its signature tang).

Why start with sourdough?
Sourdough offers an introduction to wild fermentation. The process can take a bit longer than other ferments, but the results are incredibly rewarding. Baking sourdough from scratch allows you to develop a deep connection with your food and the fermentation process.

How to make sourdough starter:

  1. Mix equal parts water and flour in a jar. Leave it at room temperature for 24 hours.
  2. After 24 hours, discard half of the mixture and add more flour and water.
  3. Repeat the feeding process for about 5-7 days until the starter is bubbly and sour-smelling.
  4. Use this starter to make your sourdough bread by mixing it with more flour, water, and salt. Let the dough rise, shape it, and bake as usual.

Tips for success:

  • Make sure your starter is bubbly and active before using it to make bread.
  • Be patient with the fermentation process; sourdough can take time to develop the right texture and flavor.

Conclusion: Begin Your Fermentation Journey Today

Fermentation can seem like a complicated process at first, but with these beginner-friendly recipes, you can start your journey with confidence. The best part? Homemade ferments are not only delicious, but they also offer numerous health benefits, from supporting gut health with probiotics to increasing the nutritional value of your food.

Start with one or two of these foods, and as you gain confidence and experience, you can experiment with different flavors and techniques. Fermentation is a versatile and rewarding skill to develop, and with time, you’ll discover a whole new world of flavor possibilities. Happy fermenting!
