Simple and Delicious Fermented Foods You Can Make in a Jar


Fermented foods are a timeless tradition in various cultures around the world, cherished for their rich flavors, health benefits, and versatility. With the increasing interest in gut health and natural food preservation, many people are turning to fermentation as a way to create delicious, nutrient-dense dishes at home. One of the easiest and most accessible ways to ferment food is by using a simple jar, making it possible for anyone to enjoy the benefits of homemade fermented foods without needing specialized equipment.

Fermentation has been practiced for centuries and includes a wide variety of foods, from tangy pickles and kimchi to rich sourdough bread and yogurt. The process involves beneficial bacteria or yeast breaking down sugars and starches in food, producing lactic acid or alcohol, which preserves the food and gives it its distinctive flavor. Whether you’re a seasoned fermenter or just starting out, this guide will help you explore some of the most popular fermented foods that you can make easily in a jar, along with tips to ensure your fermentation journey is a success.

1. Sauerkraut – The Classic Fermented Cabbage

Sauerkraut is one of the most well-known fermented foods, originating in Eastern Europe. It is made by fermenting shredded cabbage with salt, and the result is a tangy, crunchy, and nutritious dish packed with probiotics. Sauerkraut is incredibly simple to make at home, requiring just two ingredients: cabbage and salt. Here’s a simple recipe to get you started:

Ingredients:

  • 1 medium head of green cabbage
  • 1-2 tablespoons of sea salt (non-iodized)

Instructions:

  1. Prepare the Cabbage: Remove the outer leaves of the cabbage and set them aside for later. Cut the cabbage into quarters and remove the core. Shred the cabbage finely using a knife or a mandolin.

  2. Salt the Cabbage: Place the shredded cabbage in a large bowl and sprinkle with salt. Use your hands to massage the cabbage and salt together. This will release the natural juices from the cabbage, which will help create the brine needed for fermentation.

  3. Pack the Jar: Once the cabbage has softened and released enough liquid, transfer it into a clean glass jar. Press the cabbage down firmly to ensure that it’s submerged in its own brine. You can use a fermentation weight or a small jar inside the larger jar to keep the cabbage submerged.

  4. Fermentation: Leave the jar at room temperature, away from direct sunlight, for about 5-7 days. Check it daily, and if necessary, top up the brine by adding a bit of salted water (1 teaspoon of salt per cup of water). Taste the sauerkraut after a few days to see if it’s fermented to your liking. Once it reaches the desired tanginess, transfer the jar to the refrigerator to slow down the fermentation process.

Tips:

  • Always use non-iodized salt, as iodized salt can inhibit the growth of beneficial bacteria.
  • If you prefer a spicier sauerkraut, you can add spices such as caraway seeds, garlic, or chili flakes before packing it into the jar.

2. Kimchi – A Flavorful Korean Delight

Kimchi, the iconic Korean side dish, is a spicy and flavorful fermented vegetable dish traditionally made with napa cabbage, radishes, and a mix of seasonings. It’s a must-try for anyone who loves bold flavors and a bit of heat. Making kimchi at home is surprisingly easy and requires only a few basic ingredients. Let’s dive into the recipe:

Ingredients:

  • 1 medium napa cabbage
  • 2 tablespoons sea salt
  • 3-4 cloves of garlic, minced
  • 1-inch piece of ginger, grated
  • 1-2 tablespoons of fish sauce (or tamari for a vegan version)
  • 1 tablespoon sugar
  • 2-3 tablespoons of gochugaru (Korean red pepper flakes)
  • 2 tablespoons of rice flour (optional, for thickening)

Instructions:

  1. Prepare the Cabbage: Cut the napa cabbage lengthwise into quarters, and then chop each quarter into smaller pieces. Sprinkle the cabbage with salt and massage it gently to ensure the salt penetrates the leaves. Let it sit for about 2 hours to wilt and release its water.

  2. Make the Paste: While the cabbage is resting, prepare the kimchi paste. In a small saucepan, add a bit of water and rice flour (if using), and bring it to a simmer. Stir constantly to prevent clumping until the mixture thickens to a paste-like consistency. Let it cool. Once cooled, add minced garlic, ginger, fish sauce, sugar, and gochugaru. Stir well to combine.

  3. Mix the Paste with the Cabbage: After the cabbage has rested and released excess water, rinse it well to remove the excess salt. Drain the cabbage thoroughly. In a large bowl, mix the cabbage with the kimchi paste. Use your hands (wear gloves to avoid irritation from the chili paste) to coat the cabbage evenly.

  4. Pack the Jar: Once the cabbage is coated in the paste, pack it tightly into a clean glass jar. Press down firmly to remove any air pockets and ensure the cabbage is submerged in the paste. Leave some space at the top of the jar to allow for expansion during fermentation.

  5. Ferment: Place the jar at room temperature for 2-4 days, depending on the temperature of your home and the level of fermentation you prefer. Check the jar daily and taste the kimchi to determine when it’s reached the level of tanginess and spice you desire. Once it’s ready, transfer the jar to the refrigerator to slow down the fermentation process.

Tips:

  • Kimchi can be customized with additional vegetables such as carrots, radishes, or green onions.
  • For a milder version, reduce the amount of gochugaru or use a mild chili powder.

3. Fermented Garlic Honey – A Sweet and Savory Treat

Fermented garlic honey is an easy-to-make, deliciously unique fermented food that offers a blend of savory, sweet, and tangy flavors. This potent elixir can be used as a natural remedy for colds or as a versatile addition to various dishes, including salad dressings, marinades, or simply drizzled over toast.

Ingredients:

  • 1 bulb of garlic, peeled and cloves separated
  • Raw honey (enough to cover the garlic)

Instructions:

  1. Prepare the Garlic: Peel the garlic cloves and place them into a clean glass jar. If you prefer, you can crush the garlic cloves to release more of their oils and active compounds.

  2. Add the Honey: Pour raw honey into the jar until the garlic cloves are fully submerged. Seal the jar with a lid.

  3. Ferment: Leave the jar at room temperature for about 1-2 weeks. During this time, the garlic will ferment in the honey, and the flavor will develop. You may notice bubbles in the jar, which is a sign that fermentation is taking place. Stir the mixture once a day if possible.

  4. Store: After the fermentation period, store the jar in the refrigerator. The garlic honey will continue to ferment slowly, and it can be used as needed.

Tips:

  • Always use raw, unfiltered honey for fermentation, as it contains beneficial enzymes that are vital for the process.
  • Garlic honey is a great remedy for sore throats and coughs, as both garlic and honey have natural antibacterial properties.

4. Pickled Vegetables – Simple and Versatile

Pickled vegetables are another fantastic fermented food that’s easy to make in a jar. You can pickle almost any vegetable you like, including cucumbers, carrots, cauliflower, and beets. Pickled veggies are a great way to preserve seasonal produce and enjoy a tangy snack or side dish throughout the year.

Ingredients:

  • Vegetables of your choice (e.g., cucumbers, carrots, cauliflower, etc.)
  • 2 cups water
  • 1 cup vinegar (white or apple cider vinegar)
  • 2 tablespoons sea salt
  • 1 tablespoon sugar
  • Optional: garlic, dill, mustard seeds, peppercorns, or any other seasonings you like

Instructions:

  1. Prepare the Vegetables: Clean and chop the vegetables into bite-sized pieces. If using cucumbers, you can slice them into rounds or spears. For carrots or cauliflower, cut them into appropriate-sized chunks.

  2. Make the Brine: In a saucepan, combine water, vinegar, salt, and sugar. Bring the mixture to a boil, stirring to dissolve the salt and sugar. Once the brine has come to a boil, remove it from heat and let it cool.

  3. Pack the Jar: Place the vegetables into a clean jar, packing them tightly but without crushing them. Add any additional seasonings, such as garlic, dill, or mustard seeds, to enhance the flavor.

  4. Pour the Brine: Once the brine has cooled, pour it over the vegetables, ensuring they are completely submerged. You can use a fermentation weight or a small jar to hold the vegetables down if needed.

  5. Ferment: Leave the jar at room temperature for 3-5 days. During this time, the vegetables will begin to ferment and develop their characteristic sour flavor. Once the pickles have reached your preferred level of tanginess, transfer them to the refrigerator to slow down fermentation.

Tips:

  • Feel free to experiment with different vegetables and seasonings to create your own custom pickled mixes.
  • For an extra boost of flavor, add a few sprigs of fresh herbs like rosemary or thyme to the jar.

Conclusion

Making fermented foods in a jar is an easy and rewarding way to incorporate more probiotic-rich foods into your diet. Whether you’re enjoying the tangy crunch of sauerkraut, the spicy kick of kimchi, or the sweet-savory goodness of garlic honey, fermented foods offer a delicious and healthful addition to your meals. These recipes require minimal ingredients, little preparation time, and the magic of patience as they ferment into something special. Experiment with these simple recipes and enjoy the many benefits of homemade fermented foods!
