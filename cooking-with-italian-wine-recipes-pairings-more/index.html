Cooking with Italian Wine: Recipes, Pairings & More

  1. A Symphony of Wine-Paired Italian Recipes

Now that you’re prepared to let Italian wine guide your cooking endeavors, let’s dive into a few enticing recipes that incorporate wine into the dish. Fair warning – your taste buds are about to experience a riot of emotions!

a) Risotto al Vino Rosso (Red Wine Risotto)

Ingredients:

  • 1 onion, finely chopped
  • 1 clove of garlic, minced
  • 300 ml good-quality red wine
  • 100 ml beef stock
  • 300 g Arborio rice
  • 1 liter boiling vegetable stock
  • 50 g Parmesan cheese, grated
  • Olive Oil
  • Salt and pepper to taste

Instructions:

  1. In a saucepan, heat the beef stock and keep it simmering.
  2. In another saucepan, heat the olive oil over medium heat, add the onion and garlic, and sauté until golden brown.
  3. Pour the red wine into the saucepan and bring it to a boil. Let it reduce by one-third.
  4. Add the Arborio rice to the wine mixture and toast it for one minute. Stir continuously to avoid sticking to the bottom.
  5. Begin adding the hot beef stock, one ladle at a time, constantly stirring the rice until it’s almost creamy and al dente. This should take around 15-20 minutes.
  6. Take the risotto off the heat, and stir in the Parmesan cheese. Season with salt and pepper. Let it rest for a few minutes before serving.

b) Ravioli with Prosciutto and Sage (Ravioli alla Boscaiola)

Ingredients:

  • 1 package of fresh ravioli or homemade dough
  • Olive Oil
  • 200 g cooked ham or prosciutto, chopped
  • 10 sage leaves
  • 200 ml white wine
  • 200 g peeled cherry tomatoes
  • Salt and pepper to taste
  • Grated Parmesan cheese for serving
  • Fresh basil leaves for garnish

Instructions:

  1. Bring a large pot of salted water to a boil and cook the ravioli according to the package instructions or until they are al dente.
  2. In a large pan, heat some olive oil over medium heat. Add the sage leaves and cook until crisp. Remove the sage leaves and set them aside.
  3. In the same pan, heat a little more oil and add the prosciutto pieces. Cook until browned.
  4. Deglaze the pan with white wine, stirring to ensure the pan’s flavors are evenly distributed. Let most of the liquid evaporate.
  5. Add the cherry tomatoes to the pan and cook until they start to break down.
  6. Drain the ravioli and add them to the sauce. Gently toss the ravioli to coat them with the sauce.
  7. Serve the ravioli with crispy sage leaves, grated Parmesan cheese, and fresh basil leaves on top.

c) Wine-Braised Pork Shoulder (Porchetta-style)

Ingredients:

  • 2 kg pork shoulder, bone in, skin on
  • Salt and pepper to taste
  • Olive Oil
  • 1 bulb of garlic, cloves separated and smashed
  • One 800 ml bottle of dry white or rosato wine
  • Fresh rosemary and thyme
  • 2 bay leaves
  • 1 large onion, sliced
  • 200 ml chicken stock

Instructions:

  1. Preheat your oven to 150°C (300°F).

  2. Prick the skin of the pork shoulder all over with a fork and season generously with salt and pepper.

  3. Heat some olive oil in a large, deep roasting pan over medium-high heat. Sear the pork skin-side down for 6-8 minutes. Flip and sear the meat on all sides, seasoned side down, for a minute each time.

  4. Add the garlic, rosemary, thyme, and bay leaves to the pan and stir for 1-2 minutes.

  5. Pour in the wine and bring it to a simmer, scraping any bits from the bottom of the pan.

  6. Add the sliced onion and chicken stock. Move the pork to the center of the pan, skin-side up, and baste with the wine mixture.

  7. Cover with aluminum foil and place the pan in the oven. Cook for about 2 hours, basting occasionally, until the meat is fork-tender.

  8. Remove the pork from the oven and let it rest for 15 minutes before carving. Serve with seasoned potatoes or a side of your choice.

  9. The Pleasures of Italian Wine Pairings

When you’ve decided on your Italian wine-infused delicacies, the next step is pairing them with the perfect wine. Some Italian wine varietals that suit a variety of dishes include:

  • Rosato (Rosé): Light and refreshing, Rosato pairs well with fish-based dishes, salads, or tomato-based pasta sauces.
  • Fresco Bianco (Dry White Wine): With its vibrant acidity, dry white wine complements vegetables, white fish, seafood, and fresh salads.
  • Rosso (Red Wine): This versatile option complements a variety of dishes, from robust meat-based pasta sauces to hearty red meats.

As you explore the pairing process, keep in mind that there are no strict rules. Taste, preference, and individual experiences are essential factors when deciding which wine to accompany your cooking. Don’t be afraid to experiment and trust your instincts – your palate will steer you in the right direction.
