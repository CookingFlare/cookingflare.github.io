Vegetarian Korean Dishes: 5 Recipes You Must Try


Bold flavors, vibrant textures, and fragrant aromas come together in Korean cuisine, a culinary tradition celebrated worldwide for its balance of sweet, spicy, salty, sour, and umami-rich elements. While some might associate Korean dishes with meat-centric staples like bulgogi and Korean barbecue, there’s an undeniable treasure trove of vegetarian-friendly options that are just as gratifying for the palate. For those looking to explore plant-based Korean recipes, there’s no need to sacrifice taste or variety. Here are five irresistible vegetarian Korean dishes you must try to embrace the flavors and authenticity of this beloved cuisine.

1. Bibimbap (Mixed Rice Bowl)

A quintessential Korean dish, bibimbap is as visually pleasing as it is satisfying. It’s essentially a customizable mixed rice bowl featuring a medley of fresh and cooked vegetables, along with delectable sauces.

Ingredients:

  • 2 cups cooked white or brown rice (short-grain works best)
  • 1 cup spinach, blanched
  • 1 cup bean sprouts, blanched and seasoned with sesame oil and salt
  • 1 carrot, julienned and lightly sautéed
  • 1 zucchini, julienned and lightly sautéed
  • 4-5 shiitake mushrooms, thinly sliced and sautéed in soy sauce
  • Gochujang (Korean red pepper paste)
  • Sesame oil for drizzling
  • 1 fried egg (optional for vegetarians who consume eggs)
  • Toasted sesame seeds
  • Optional garnish: green onions

Instructions:

  1. Start with a base of cooked rice and arrange your prepared vegetables in small clusters on top to create a colorful layout.
  2. If you enjoy the flavor contrast, add a sunny-side-up fried egg to the center of the bowl.
  3. Serve with a small dollop of gochujang on top, a light drizzle of sesame oil, and a sprinkle of sesame seeds.
  4. Once served, mix all the ingredients thoroughly before eating — that’s where bibimbap gets its name, which means “mixed rice.”

This dish is a great way to clear out your vegetable drawer, as you can use whatever you have on hand, from bell peppers to bok choy.

2. Kimchi Jeon (Kimchi Pancake)

Slightly spicy, tangy, and crispy, kimchi jeon is a savory pancake made primarily from chopped kimchi and a simple batter. It’s a crowd-pleasing comfort food that’s super easy to whip up as a snack, appetizer, or light meal.

Ingredients:

  • 1 cup aged kimchi, chopped
  • ½ cup kimchi juice (the liquid from the kimchi jar)
  • ½ cup all-purpose flour or rice flour (for a gluten-free version)
  • ½ cup water
  • 1 tbsp gochujang (optional, for more spice)
  • Vegetable oil, for frying
  • Optional dipping sauce: soy sauce mixed with a bit of sesame oil and rice vinegar

Instructions:

  1. In a mixing bowl, combine the chopped kimchi, kimchi juice, flour, and water to create a batter. Add gochujang for extra spice, if desired.
  2. Heat a non-stick skillet over medium heat with a thin layer of vegetable oil.
  3. Pour a portion of the batter into the pan and spread it evenly into a thin circular shape.
  4. Cook until the bottom is golden brown and crispy, about 2-3 minutes. Flip and cook the other side for an additional 2 minutes.
  5. Serve hot with the optional dipping sauce.

Kimchi jeon packs a punch of both flavor and crunch. Plus, it’s a fantastic way to use up that last bit of kimchi that’s been sitting in your fridge.

3. Doenjang Jjigae (Soybean Paste Stew)

This hearty, comforting stew is a cornerstone of Korean home cooking. The star of the show is doenjang, a fermented soybean paste, which lends the dish its distinct, savory depth. Traditionally made with anchovies in its base, this vegetarian version swaps them for umami-packed dried kelp or kombu.

Ingredients:

  • 4 cups water
  • 1 6-inch piece of dried kelp (kombu)
  • 2 tbsp doenjang (fermented soybean paste)
  • 1 zucchini, diced
  • ½ onion, sliced
  • 1 cup firm tofu, cubed
  • 1-2 green chilies, sliced (optional for heat)
  • 1 cup napa cabbage, roughly chopped
  • Green onions for garnish

Instructions:

  1. In a pot, bring water and dried kelp to a boil to create the base stock. Remove the kelp once it has softened, about 10 minutes.
  2. Stir in the doenjang until it’s fully dissolved.
  3. Add the zucchini, onion, tofu, green chilies, and cabbage to the pot. Simmer for 10-12 minutes or until the vegetables are tender.
  4. Garnish with green onions and serve hot with steamed rice on the side.

Doenjang jjigae is a soul-warming dish that’s perfect for chilly evenings, offering a delightful balance of flavors and textures.

4. Japchae (Stir-fried Glass Noodles)

Japchae is a wonderfully fragrant and colorful dish made from sweet potato starch noodles, stir-fried vegetables, and a slightly sweet soy-based sauce. It’s not only delicious but also gluten-free and perfect for entertaining.

Ingredients:

  • 6 oz glass noodles (dangmyeon)
  • 1 carrot, julienned
  • 1 bell pepper (any color), julienned
  • 1 cup spinach, blanched
  • 4 shiitake mushrooms, thinly sliced
  • 1/4 onion, thinly sliced
  • 2 tbsp soy sauce
  • 1 tbsp sugar
  • 1 tbsp sesame oil
  • 1 tbsp vegetable oil
  • Sesame seeds for garnish

Instructions:

  1. Cook glass noodles according to package instructions. Rinse under cold water, drain, and cut into shorter lengths with scissors.
  2. In a large skillet, heat vegetable oil and stir-fry each vegetable separately until just tender, seasoning lightly with salt. Set aside.
  3. Combine soy sauce, sugar, and sesame oil in a small bowl to make the sauce.
  4. Return all vegetables to the skillet, add the noodles, and drizzle the sauce evenly over the mixture. Toss everything gently but thoroughly over medium heat until well combined.
  5. Serve hot or at room temperature, garnished with sesame seeds.

Japchae’s flexibility in terms of ingredients makes it a go-to dish that can be tailored to your taste with minimal effort.

5. Baek Kimchi (White Kimchi)

For those who prefer a milder version of Korea’s famous kimchi, baek kimchi is the perfect alternative. Free of chili powder, this “white” kimchi is refreshing and lightly tangy, offering a unique yet familiar flavor profile. Unlike its fiery counterpart, it allows the natural sweetness of fermented napa cabbage to shine.

Ingredients:

  • 1 medium head napa cabbage
  • 1 carrot, julienned
  • 1 Korean pear, sliced thinly (or substitute with Asian pear)
  • 3 green onions, cut into 2-inch pieces
  • 4 cups water
  • 3 tbsp sea salt
  • 2 tbsp sugar
  • 5 garlic cloves, minced
  • 1-inch piece of ginger, grated

Instructions:

  1. Quarter the napa cabbage lengthwise and rinse thoroughly. Sprinkle sea salt between each leaf, ensuring the cabbage softens over 2-3 hours. Rinse to remove excess salt and drain.
  2. Prepare the brine by dissolving sugar and salt in water, then mix in garlic and ginger.
  3. Pack the cabbage tightly into a container, layering it with carrot, pear slices, and green onions. Pour the brine over the top, ensuring everything is submerged.
  4. Cover and ferment at room temperature for 1-2 days, then transfer to the refrigerator. Baek kimchi is best served chilled.

Baek kimchi’s lightness pairs beautifully with richer dishes like japchae or bibimbap, acting as a palate cleanser.

Exploring these vegetarian Korean dishes introduces a harmonious balance of flavors that are as complex as they are comforting. Whether you’re new to Korean cuisine or a seasoned enthusiast, these recipes are bound to become staples in your kitchen, offering both tradition and innovation on your plate.
