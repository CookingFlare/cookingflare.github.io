Comforting Mexican Pozole Recipe for Cold Nights


As the temperatures drop and the nights grow colder, there's nothing like a warm, satisfying bowl of Mexican pozole to lift your spirits and fill your belly. This traditional Mexican stew, also known as pozole rojo or pozole blanco, is more than just a comforting meal – it's an experience that transports you to the rich cultural heritage of Mexico. Gather around the table with family and friends and enjoy this hearty, slow-cooked dish that's perfect for a weeknight dinner or a special occasion. Our recipe serves 6-8 people, so feel free to adjust the ingredients as needed. What is Pozole? Pozole is a staple dish in Mexican cuisine, made with hominy (dried maize kernels that have been soaked in limewater to remove the hull) and pork or chicken, along with an array of spices and aromatics. There are two main types of pozole: pozole rojo (red pozole) made with pork and a spicy tomato-based broth, and pozole blanco (white pozole) made with chicken or pork and a clear broth. For this recipe, we'll be making a pozole rojo, but you can easily substitute the pork with chicken or turkey for a leaner alternative. The Ingredients:

2 lbs pork shoulder, cut into large pieces 2 medium onions, diced 4 cloves garlic, minced 2 dried ancho chilies, stemmed and seeded 2 dried guajillo chilies, stemmed and seeded 1 teaspoon ground cumin 1 teaspoon smoked paprika 1 teaspoon oregano 1/2 teaspoon cayenne pepper Salt and black pepper 2 cups hominy, rinsed and drained 2 cups chicken broth 1 can (14.5 oz) diced tomatoes 2 tablespoons lard or vegetable oil Fresh cilantro, chopped (optional) Lime wedges, for serving

The Process:

Prepare the Chilies: Ancho and guajillo chilies are the backbone of pozole's complex flavor profile, but you can substitute them with other dried red chilies if you prefer. Rehydrate the chilies by soaking them in hot water for about 20 minutes. Once soft, remove the stems and seeds, and chop the chilies into small pieces. Heat the Oil: Heat lard or oil in a large Dutch oven over medium heat. Add diced onions and cook until translucent, about 5 minutes. Brown the Pork: Add the pork pieces and cook until browned on all sides, about 5-7 minutes. Remove the pork from the pot and set it aside. Soften the Chilies: Add the rehydrated chilies to the pot and cook until fragrant, about 2-3 minutes. If using ancho chilies, be careful not to let the pot smoke. Add Aromatics: Add the minced garlic, cumin, smoked paprika, oregano, and cayenne pepper to the pot. Cook for 1-2 minutes, until the spices are fragrant. Deglaze the Pan: Pour in the chicken broth and diced tomatoes, scraping the bottom of the pot to release any browned bits. Bring the mixture to a boil, then reduce the heat to low and simmer for 10 minutes. Return the Pork: Return the pork to the pot and simmer for 2-3 hours, or until the meat is tender. Add the Hominy: Stir in the hominy and cook for an additional 30 minutes, or until the hominy is tender and the flavors have melded together.

Serve and Enjoy Serve the pozole in bowls, topped with chopped cilantro, a squeeze of fresh lime juice, and a dollop of sour cream or Mexican crema, if desired. Warm tortilla chips or crusty bread on the side are also a must-have for dipping into the rich broth. Tips and Variations:

For an added layer of flavor, roast the chilies in a 375°F oven for 20-30 minutes before rehydrating them. Substitute shredded chicken or turkey for the pork if you prefer. Add chopped avocado or diced scallions for extra creaminess and freshness. Experiment with different spices, such as ground cumin or coriander, to create a unique flavor profile.

As the temperatures drop and the nights grow colder, gather your loved ones and warm up with a hearty bowl of Mexican pozole. This comforting stew is more than just a meal – it's a celebration of culture, family, and tradition.
