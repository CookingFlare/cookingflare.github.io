How to Make Korean Kimchi Jjigae (Kimchi Stew)


Korean Kimchi Jjigae, also known as kimchi stew, is one of the most beloved dishes in Korean cuisine. This hearty, spicy, and savory dish is perfect for a cozy meal on a chilly day or as a satisfying side dish. Kimchi jjigae has a deep, complex flavor profile thanks to the fermented kimchi, which adds a perfect balance of sour, spicy, and umami tastes. Not only is it delicious, but it also offers various health benefits due to the probiotics found in the fermented kimchi.

In this blog post, we’ll walk you through the process of making traditional Korean kimchi jjigae from scratch, using easy-to-find ingredients. Whether you’re new to Korean cooking or a seasoned pro, this comforting stew will surely become a favorite in your kitchen.

Ingredients for Kimchi Jjigae

Before diving into the cooking process, let’s first gather all the ingredients you’ll need. The beauty of kimchi jjigae lies in its versatility, and you can always adjust the ingredients based on your taste preferences or what you have on hand.

Here’s a basic list of ingredients for a delicious kimchi jjigae:

Main Ingredients:

  • Kimchi: 1 ½ to 2 cups (preferably aged, sour kimchi)
  • Pork Belly or Pork Shoulder: 200g (can also use beef, tofu, or even seafood for a variation)
  • Tofu: ½ block (firm tofu is best)
  • Onion: 1 medium, sliced
  • Garlic: 3-4 cloves, minced
  • Green Onion: 1-2 stalks, chopped for garnish
  • Korean Red Pepper Flakes (Gochugaru): 1 tablespoon
  • Gochujang (Korean red pepper paste): 1 tablespoon
  • Sesame Oil: 1 tablespoon
  • Soy Sauce: 1 tablespoon
  • Sugar: 1 teaspoon (optional, for balance)
  • Salt and Pepper: to taste
  • Water or Broth: 4-5 cups (for a richer flavor, use chicken broth or beef broth)

Optional Ingredients:

  • Mushrooms: Shiitake or oyster mushrooms add a nice umami flavor.
  • Zucchini: Adds texture and a slight sweetness to the dish.
  • Kimchi Juice: If your kimchi has a lot of liquid, save it and add it to the stew for extra tanginess.

Now that you have everything prepared, let’s move on to the cooking process.

Step-by-Step Guide to Making Kimchi Jjigae

Making kimchi jjigae is fairly straightforward, but the key to bringing out its full flavor is to let the ingredients meld together over time. Here’s how to cook this classic Korean stew:

Step 1: Prepare the Ingredients

Before you begin cooking, it’s essential to prep all your ingredients. Cut the pork belly or shoulder into bite-sized pieces. Slice the onion and green onion, and mince the garlic. Cut the tofu into cubes (you can use firm or extra-firm tofu for better texture). Lastly, make sure your kimchi is well-fermented—old, sour kimchi is ideal for making jjigae.

If you’re adding vegetables like mushrooms or zucchini, chop them into pieces that will cook at the same rate as the pork. Having everything ready to go before you start cooking will make the process smooth and easy.

Step 2: Sauté the Pork and Aromatics

Start by heating the sesame oil in a large pot or Dutch oven over medium heat. Once the oil is hot, add the sliced pork and cook until it starts to brown. Pork belly works exceptionally well for this stew due to its fattiness, which gives the dish a rich and savory flavor.

After the pork is browned, add the minced garlic and sliced onion to the pot. Sauté them together for another 2-3 minutes until the onion becomes soft and fragrant. The key to a great kimchi jjigae is in the aromatics, so make sure they’re well-cooked before moving on.

Step 3: Add Kimchi and Seasonings

Once the pork and aromatics are nicely cooked, it’s time to add the star of the dish—kimchi. Add your kimchi to the pot, stirring it in with the pork and onions. If you like, you can also add some of the kimchi juice for extra tang and depth of flavor.

To enhance the stew’s spiciness and umami, add the gochugaru (Korean red pepper flakes) and gochujang (Korean red pepper paste). These ingredients will give the dish its signature heat and depth. Stir everything together, allowing the kimchi to fry slightly in the oil, which will release its juices and flavor.

Step 4: Add Liquid and Bring to a Boil

Now that the kimchi and seasonings have been incorporated, add the water or broth to the pot. If you prefer a richer taste, opt for chicken broth or beef broth. Bring the mixture to a boil, then reduce the heat to a simmer.

Let the stew simmer for about 20-30 minutes. During this time, the flavors will meld together, and the pork will become tender, while the kimchi will break down and infuse the broth with its distinct sour, spicy flavor. Taste the broth and adjust the seasoning with soy sauce, salt, and pepper if needed. A small pinch of sugar can help balance the acidity of the kimchi, but it’s entirely optional.

Step 5: Add Tofu and Optional Vegetables

After the stew has simmered and the flavors have developed, it’s time to add the tofu and any optional vegetables. Gently add the tofu cubes to the pot, being careful not to break them apart. Let the tofu simmer in the broth for another 10-15 minutes to absorb the flavors.

If you’re adding mushrooms or zucchini, now is the time to include them. These vegetables add extra texture and flavor, but they don’t need as much time to cook, so be sure to add them just before the tofu finishes cooking.

Step 6: Final Adjustments

Once the tofu and vegetables are cooked through, give the stew a final taste. If it’s too spicy, you can add a little more sugar to mellow the heat. If the broth needs more depth, a splash more soy sauce or a dash of fish sauce can intensify the umami flavors.

Once everything tastes perfect, remove the pot from the heat and garnish with chopped green onions. This adds a fresh and vibrant flavor to the stew, making it even more satisfying.

Step 7: Serve and Enjoy

Kimchi jjigae is best served hot, straight from the pot. Traditionally, it’s enjoyed with a bowl of steamed rice and a variety of banchan (Korean side dishes). The stew itself is flavorful enough to stand on its own, but the addition of rice helps to balance the heat and sourness of the dish.

To serve, ladle the kimchi jjigae into individual bowls, making sure to get some of the pork, tofu, and vegetables in each serving. The rich, spicy broth will warm you up from the inside out, and the combination of tender pork and soft tofu is simply irresistible.

Tips for the Best Kimchi Jjigae

  • Use Aged Kimchi: The best kimchi jjigae is made with kimchi that has been fermented for a while. Aged kimchi has a more complex and sour flavor, which is perfect for the stew.
  • Balance the Heat: Kimchi jjigae can be quite spicy, so if you prefer a milder version, reduce the amount of gochugaru and gochujang. On the other hand, if you love heat, don’t be afraid to add more.
  • Use Broth for Extra Flavor: While water works just fine, using chicken or beef broth will add a depth of flavor to the stew. You can even make your own broth for a more homemade touch.
  • Add Protein Variations: While pork is traditional, you can switch it up by using beef, tofu, or even seafood like clams or shrimp. Each protein brings its own unique taste to the stew.
  • Don’t Skip the Sesame Oil: Sesame oil adds a nutty richness that elevates the dish. Be sure to use it at the beginning to sauté the pork and aromatics.
  • Adjust for Sourness: If your kimchi jjigae turns out too sour for your taste, add a teaspoon of sugar to balance the flavor. Just a small amount can make a big difference.

Conclusion

Korean kimchi jjigae is a comforting, flavorful stew that’s packed with savory, spicy, and sour notes. With its easy preparation and customizable ingredients, it’s the perfect dish to warm you up on a cold day or to serve as part of a larger Korean meal. Whether you’re enjoying it for the first time or making it for the hundredth, this stew is sure to bring a satisfying depth of flavor to your table.

By following this simple recipe and adding your favorite variations, you can create your own perfect version of kimchi jjigae. So, gather your ingredients, start cooking, and enjoy a delicious homemade bowl of Korean comfort food!
