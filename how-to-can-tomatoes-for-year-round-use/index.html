How to Can Tomatoes for Year-Round Use


Canning tomatoes is one of the best ways to preserve their fresh, vibrant flavors for use long after the growing season ends. Whether you use them in soups, sauces, stews, or enjoy them straight out of the jar, canned tomatoes are a versatile staple that can elevate countless recipes. With just a little bit of know-how, you can build up your pantry with jars of garden-fresh tomatoes that will keep for months. This guide takes you through each step of safely and effectively canning tomatoes at home.

Benefits of Canning Tomatoes

Canning tomatoes not only stretches the bounty of your garden but also ensures that you’ll have access to nutrient-rich, flavorful alternatives to commercially canned goods. By preserving your own tomatoes, you can control the ingredients, eliminate additives, and maintain the quality you desire. Furthermore, it’s a sustainable way to reduce food waste by saving surplus fruits for future meals.

Equipment and Supplies

Before diving into the canning process, gather essential tools and supplies to ensure a safe and efficient process. Here’s what you’ll need:

  • Canning jars: Choose glass jars specifically designed for canning, such as mason jars with lids and rings.
  • Jar lifter: A jar lifter helps safely maneuver hot jars.
  • Large stockpot or canner: A boiling water canner or pressure canner is essential for processing.
  • Lid magnet or wand: To handle sanitized lids without touching them directly.
  • Wide-mouth funnel: This tool helps transfer tomatoes neatly into jars.
  • Knife and cutting board: For peeling and chopping tomatoes.
  • Large mixing bowl: Useful for holding peeled tomatoes.
  • Colander: For rinsing tomatoes efficiently.
  • Clean kitchen towels: For drying jars and reducing mess.
  • Basic kitchen utensils: Tongs, wooden spoons, and measuring cups.
  • Citric acid or lemon juice: Acidifies the tomatoes to ensure safe canning.

Ensuring all equipment is clean and ready to go before starting makes the process much easier and safer.

Choosing the Right Tomatoes

Not all tomato varieties are created equal when it comes to canning. Roma tomatoes, also known as plum tomatoes, are popular for their meatiness and low water content, making them ideal for sauces and canning. However, any variety can be preserved as long as they’re ripe, firm, and free of bruises or blemishes. Mix and match tomato types for more diverse flavor profiles.

Step-by-Step Guide to Canning Tomatoes

Step 1: Prepare the Tomatoes

Start by washing your tomatoes thoroughly under cold running water to remove dirt or debris. If necessary, remove stems and any damaged spots.

Next, you’ll need to peel the tomatoes, as skins can toughen during canning. Here’s an efficient method to peel them:

  1. Bring a large pot of water to a rolling boil.
  2. Prepare an ice bath in a mixing bowl.
  3. Score the bottom of each tomato with a small “X” using a knife.
  4. Drop the tomatoes into the boiling water for 30-60 seconds, until the skins start to loosen.
  5. Immediately transfer them to the ice bath to cool.
  6. Slip off the skins using your fingers or a small knife.

Once peeled, you can leave the tomatoes whole, halve them, or dice them, depending on your preference and intended use.

Step 2: Prepare the Jars and Lids

Proper sterilization of jars is critical for safe canning and prevention of contamination. To sterilize:

  1. Wash jars, lids, and rings in hot soapy water.
  2. Place the jars into a large pot of boiling water for 10 minutes.
  3. Warm the lids in simmering water (just below boiling) for 5 minutes.

Remove sterilized jars from the water with a jar lifter and allow them to air-dry.

Step 3: Acidify the Tomatoes

Tomatoes can sometimes have varying acidity levels, so it’s essential to acidify them to ensure food safety. For each pint jar, add:

  • 1 tablespoon of bottled lemon juice or ¼ teaspoon of citric acid. For quart jars, double this amount. This step protects against the growth of bacteria like Clostridium botulinum, which causes botulism.

Step 4: Fill the Jars

Using a wide-mouth funnel, fill each sterilized jar with prepared tomatoes, leaving about ½ inch of headspace. The headspace ensures there’s room for expansion during processing.

For whole or halved tomatoes, it’s helpful to pack them in tightly, squishing them gently with a spoon to release natural juices. If the tomatoes are dry, consider adding hot water or some tomato juice to fill the space.

Step 5: Remove Air Bubbles

Taking the time to remove air bubbles ensures your jars are properly sealed and helps protect the contents. Run a thin, non-metallic spatula or chopstick around the inside of the jar to release any trapped air pockets. Add more liquid if needed to maintain ½ inch of headspace.

Step 6: Seal the Jars

Carefully wipe the rim of each jar with a clean, damp cloth to remove residue or liquid that could interfere with sealing. Place a warm lid on each jar, then tighten the metal ring until it’s snug but not overly tight.

Step 7: Process the Jars

Place the filled jars into a boiling water canner (or pressure canner, depending on your recipe and altitude). The jars should be submerged in water that covers them by at least 1 inch.

For boiling water canning:

  • Pint jars need 35 minutes of processing time.
  • Quart jars need 45 minutes of processing time. Adjust for altitude: Higher elevations require longer processing times.

For pressure canning, consult specific pressure recommendations for your altitude and type of tomatoes.

Once processed, turn off the heat and allow jars to sit for 5 minutes before removing them from the canner.

Step 8: Cool and Check the Seals

Allow the jars to cool undisturbed for 12-24 hours on a countertop. After cooling, check the seals:

  1. Press down on the lid’s center; it shouldn’t spring back.
  2. Inspect for any leaks or gaps.

Unsealed jars should be refrigerated and used within one week or reprocessed immediately.

Step 9: Label and Store

Label your jars with the contents and date of canning using a permanent marker or stickers. Store them in a cool, dark place, like a pantry or basement. Properly canned tomatoes can last up to 18 months but are best enjoyed within 12 months for peak freshness.

Tips and Tricks for Canning Success

  • Use fresh, ripe tomatoes: The better the initial produce, the better the final result.
  • Organize your workspace: Clear counters make the process smoother.
  • Follow tested recipes: Improvising with ingredients can affect the safety of canned tomatoes.
  • Plan ahead: Make sure you have enough jars to accommodate your yield.

Creative Ways to Use Your Canned Tomatoes

Your hard work will pay off during dinner prep, as canned tomatoes are incredibly versatile. Use them as the base for marinara sauce, toss them into a hearty chili, blend them into creamy bisques, or bake them into casseroles. The possibilities are endless when you have a pantry stocked with homemade canned tomatoes.

Canning tomatoes at home is rewarding and practical, ensuring access to the fresh flavors of summer all year long. With careful preparation and attention to safety, you can master this timeless preservation method and enjoy a stocked pantry of vibrant tomatoes for months to come.
