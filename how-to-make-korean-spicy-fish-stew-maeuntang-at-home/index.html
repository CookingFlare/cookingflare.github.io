How to Make Korean Spicy Fish Stew (Maeuntang) at Home


No matter the time of year, there is something uniquely comforting about enjoying a homemade bowl of spicy, aromatic stew. One dish that encompasses bold flavors and nourishing ingredients is the beloved Korean spicy fish stew, known as Maeuntang. This dish has been enjoyed for generations, making it a staple in Korean cuisine. Its balance of spicy, savory, and umami flavors comes together in a broth steeped with red pepper paste, garlic, and fresh vegetables, offering a memorable dining experience that is surprisingly straightforward to recreate at home.

If you’ve ever wanted to bring authentic Korean flavors into your kitchen, look no further. In this post, we’ll guide you step by step on how to craft the perfect Maeuntang, creating a stew that honors the traditional recipe while allowing a bit of personalization to suit your taste preferences.

Ingredients Needed

Before getting started, you’ll want to gather the freshest ingredients possible. Maeuntang is all about letting the quality of the fish and vegetables shine, so ensure you source items in peak condition. Here’s what you’ll need:

Main Ingredients:

  • Fresh fish – 1 medium-sized whole fish (preferably white-fleshed fish, such as cod, snapper, or rockfish), cleaned and gutted. Alternatively, you can use fish fillets if preferred.
  • Seafood (Optional) – Shrimp, mussels, or clams can be added for extra depth of flavor.

For the Broth:

  • 6-7 cups of water
  • 2-3 dried anchovies or anchovy stock packets
  • 1 strip of dried kelp (kombu)
  • 1 small radish, peeled and sliced into thin half-moons (Korean radish works best, but daikon is a good substitute)
  • 1 small onion, halved

Seasoning Ingredients:

  • 2-3 tablespoons of Korean red pepper paste (gochujang)
  • 1-2 tablespoons of Korean red pepper flakes (gochugaru)
  • 2 tablespoons of soy sauce
  • 1 tablespoon of fish sauce for added umami
  • 4-5 garlic cloves, minced
  • 1 piece of ginger, thinly sliced (optional but adds aromatic depth)
  • 1 teaspoon of sugar (optional, for balance)

Vegetables and Garnish:

  • 1 handful of bean sprouts, washed and trimmed
  • 1 small zucchini, sliced into half-moons
  • 1/2 block of firm tofu, cut into bite-sized chunks
  • 1 bunch of enoki mushrooms or any mushroom variety of your choice
  • 2 green onions, chopped into 2-inch pieces
  • 1 red chili pepper and 1 green chili pepper, thinly sliced for garnish
  • Fresh cilantro or perilla leaves (optional)

Step-by-Step Guide

1. Prepare the Fish

The star of Maeuntang is the fish, so take care of its preparation. If you’re using a whole fish, make sure it’s properly gutted and cleaned—this is often done by fishmongers if you ask. Rinse the fish under cold water to remove any residual blood or scales. For fillets, simply pat them dry with a paper towel. Cut the fish into manageable pieces, about 2-inch chunks for easy serving.

2. Create the Base Broth

A flavor-packed broth is the backbone of this stew. Start by bringing 6-7 cups of water to a gentle boil in a large pot. Add the dried anchovies and kelp strip to the water. Simmer for about 10-15 minutes to infuse the broth with a deep seafood flavor. Once done, remove the anchovies and kelp and discard them. Add the sliced radish and onions to the broth and let them simmer for another 10 minutes until softened.

3. Mix the Seasoning Paste

In a small mixing bowl, combine the red pepper paste (gochujang), red pepper flakes (gochugaru), soy sauce, fish sauce, minced garlic, and optional ginger. Taste and adjust the seasoning by adding a little sugar if necessary for balance. Make it spicier by increasing the red pepper flakes or milder by slightly reducing them. Stir the mixture until smooth.

4. Add the Fish and Tofu

Now, it’s time to bring the stew to life. Carefully place the fish chunks and any additional seafood like shrimp or mussels into the simmering broth. Add the prepared seasoning paste, stirring gently so the paste dissolves evenly throughout the broth. Let the fish cook for 5-7 minutes until it turns opaque. Be careful not to overcook, as the fish can become tough. Add tofu cubes toward the end of this step so they absorb some of the flavor.

5. Layer in the Vegetables

Now it’s the turn of the vegetables. Add the zucchini, bean sprouts, and mushrooms to the pot. Let the stew cook for another 4-5 minutes, letting the vegetables soften without losing their texture. You’ll notice the entire pot becoming vibrant in color and brimming with aromas that are hard to resist.

6. Garnish and Serve

Once the vegetables are cooked to perfection, turn off the heat and sprinkle the dish with chopped green onions and sliced chili peppers. For an extra layer of freshness, consider adding cilantro or perilla leaves on top. Serve immediately, preferably alongside a bowl of steamed white rice to soak up the rich, spicy broth.

Tips for the Perfect Maeuntang

  • Fish Selection: Freshness is key. If you’re unsure which fish to use, ask your local fishmonger for suggestions.
  • Customizing Spice Levels: Adjust both the gochujang and gochugaru quantities according to your tolerance for heat. You can also add a squeeze of lemon or lime to balance out the spice.
  • Substitute for Anchovy Broth: If anchovies and kelp aren’t available, you can use store-bought seafood stock as a substitute, though it may slightly alter the flavor.
  • Experiment with Add-ins: Feel free to add other seafood such as squid or scallops, or even leafy greens like spinach or bok choy for added variety.
  • Make It Ahead: Like many stews, Maeuntang tastes even better the next day as the flavors meld together.

Why You Should Try Maeuntang

Maeuntang is more than just a dish; it’s an experience. Its complex layers of flavor are nourishing for the body and soul, perfect for warming up on a chilly evening or creating a hearty, satisfying meal for loved ones. Beyond its taste, this stew is packed with nutrient-rich ingredients like fresh fish, vegetables, and aromatics, making it as healthy as it is delicious.

Plus, making Maeuntang at home allows you to experiment with flavors and tailor the dish to your preferences. Whether you’re recreating a meal you’ve enjoyed at a Korean restaurant or exploring global cuisines from the comfort of your kitchen, this recipe is an excellent starting point.

So the next time you’re in the mood for something spicy, hearty, and utterly comforting, give this Korean spicy fish stew a try. Maeuntang isn’t just food—it’s a taste of Korea’s rich culinary heritage, brought right to your home.
