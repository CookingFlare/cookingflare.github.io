How to Make Authentic Kimchi: A Step-by-Step Guide


Kimchi, a staple in Korean cuisine, is a flavorful and fermented dish made from vegetables, most commonly napa cabbage, and seasoned with a variety of spices. It’s a beloved side dish in Korea, often served alongside meals or used in stews, soups, and even fried rice. Kimchi’s tangy, spicy, and umami flavors are the result of the fermentation process, which not only enhances its taste but also its health benefits.

Making authentic kimchi at home is easier than you might think. This step-by-step guide will walk you through the entire process, from selecting the ingredients to fermenting your kimchi to perfection.

Ingredients You Will Need

Before you begin, gather all the necessary ingredients. Traditional kimchi is made with napa cabbage, but other vegetables can be used, such as radish or cucumber. The seasoning paste typically includes garlic, ginger, Korean chili flakes, fish sauce, and salted shrimp. Here’s a detailed list of the ingredients for making authentic kimchi:

Main Ingredients:

  • 1 large napa cabbage
  • ½ cup sea salt (for salting cabbage)
  • 4 cups water

For the Kimchi Paste:

  • 1 tablespoon ginger (minced)
  • 1 tablespoon garlic (minced)
  • 2 tablespoons fish sauce
  • 2 tablespoons fermented salted shrimp (optional but adds depth of flavor)
  • ¼ cup gochugaru (Korean chili flakes)
  • 1 tablespoon sugar
  • 1 tablespoon rice flour
  • 2 cups water
  • 1 small carrot (julienned, optional)
  • 4 green onions (cut into 2-inch pieces)
  • 1 tablespoon sesame oil (optional)

For the Fermentation:

  • A large airtight container or glass jars
  • Gloves (optional, but helpful to avoid irritation from chili paste)

Preparing the Cabbage

The first step in making kimchi is preparing the napa cabbage. This crucial step helps break down the cabbage leaves, which are essential for the fermentation process.

  1. Cut the cabbage: Slice the napa cabbage in half lengthwise, and then cut each half into thirds. Each piece should be about 2-3 inches wide. This makes it easier to season and fit into jars later on.

  2. Salt the cabbage: Place the cabbage pieces into a large mixing bowl or basin and sprinkle sea salt evenly between the layers of cabbage. Salt helps to draw moisture out of the cabbage, softening it and allowing it to better absorb the kimchi paste. Make sure the cabbage is generously salted, especially at the center of each layer.

  3. Let it rest: Cover the cabbage with a cloth or lid and let it rest for 1-2 hours. During this time, the cabbage will release water, which helps to soften the leaves. After the resting period, rinse the cabbage thoroughly under cold water to remove excess salt.

  4. Drain the cabbage: Once rinsed, gently squeeze the cabbage pieces to remove any excess water. Leave them to drain in a colander while you prepare the kimchi paste.

Making the Kimchi Paste

The kimchi paste, or “gochujang” sauce, is what gives kimchi its signature flavor. The base of the paste consists of garlic, ginger, chili flakes, and fermented seafood, which adds a rich umami flavor to the kimchi. Here’s how to make the paste:

  1. Make the rice flour paste: In a small pot, combine 2 cups of water with 1 tablespoon of rice flour. Heat over medium heat, stirring constantly until the mixture thickens into a paste. This process is essential because the rice flour helps bind the chili flakes and other seasonings together.

  2. Add seasonings: Once the rice flour mixture has thickened, add 1 tablespoon of sugar, ¼ cup of gochugaru (Korean chili flakes), minced garlic, and minced ginger. Stir well to combine. The sugar helps balance the heat from the gochugaru.

  3. Add fermented seafood (optional): If you’re using fish sauce and fermented shrimp, now’s the time to add them to the paste. These ingredients are traditional in kimchi recipes, and they impart a deep umami flavor to the paste. You can skip this step if you prefer a vegetarian version.

  4. Finish the paste: Stir in the fish sauce (or soy sauce for a vegetarian alternative), and the optional sesame oil. Mix everything together until you have a thick, well-combined paste.

Preparing the Vegetables

While the cabbage is draining, prepare the other vegetables to add texture and flavor to your kimchi. These vegetables, such as julienned carrots and chopped green onions, will give your kimchi a delightful crunch and additional layers of flavor.

  1. Prepare carrots and green onions: Peel the carrot and cut it into thin julienne strips. Chop the green onions into 2-inch pieces. These vegetables are optional but highly recommended for added texture and taste.

Mixing the Cabbage and Paste

Once the cabbage has been thoroughly drained and the kimchi paste is ready, it’s time to mix everything together.

  1. Put on gloves (optional): It’s a good idea to wear disposable gloves when handling the kimchi paste. Gochugaru can stain your hands, and the chili paste can be irritating to your skin.

  2. Coat the cabbage with the paste: Working in batches, take a handful of cabbage pieces and gently rub the kimchi paste all over each piece. Make sure to coat each leaf thoroughly, ensuring every layer of cabbage is covered with the spicy paste.

  3. Add vegetables: As you coat the cabbage, also add the julienned carrots and chopped green onions to the mix. Toss everything together gently to make sure the vegetables are evenly distributed throughout.

Packing the Kimchi

Once all the cabbage and vegetables are coated in the kimchi paste, it’s time to pack everything into containers for fermentation.

  1. Choose containers: You can use a large airtight container or mason jars to store the kimchi. Glass containers are preferable because they don’t absorb odors, but plastic containers can also work if you don’t mind the occasional staining.

  2. Pack the kimchi: Gently pack the kimchi into the containers, pressing down as you go to remove air bubbles. Leave about an inch of space at the top of each container to allow room for the kimchi to expand as it ferments.

  3. Seal and store: Seal the containers with tight-fitting lids and leave them at room temperature for 1-2 days to begin the fermentation process. After 24-48 hours, the kimchi should start to develop a sour, tangy flavor as the fermentation progresses.

  4. Refrigerate: After the initial fermentation period, move the kimchi to the refrigerator. It will continue to ferment slowly in the fridge, developing more complex flavors over time. The kimchi can be eaten immediately after the initial fermentation, but it’s best to let it ferment for a week or longer for the best taste.

Tips for Perfect Kimchi

  • Adjust the spice level: If you prefer a milder kimchi, reduce the amount of gochugaru or omit the fermented shrimp. You can always taste the paste before applying it to the cabbage to see if it’s to your liking.

  • Use fresh vegetables: Fresh, high-quality napa cabbage and other vegetables make a big difference in the final flavor of your kimchi. The fresher the ingredients, the more vibrant the flavors will be.

  • Fermentation time: The longer you ferment the kimchi, the more sour and tangy it will become. If you’re new to kimchi-making, start by tasting it after a couple of days and decide if it’s to your liking. For milder kimchi, refrigerate it after just 1-2 days of fermentation at room temperature.

  • Storing kimchi: Kimchi can last for several weeks or even months in the fridge. It will continue to ferment, and the flavors will evolve over time, becoming more complex and intense.

Serving Your Kimchi

Kimchi is incredibly versatile. You can serve it as a side dish, toss it into soups, add it to fried rice, or use it in pancakes and savory dishes. It’s packed with probiotics, making it a healthy addition to any meal. Whether you enjoy it fresh, fermented, or in a variety of cooked dishes, homemade kimchi will elevate your meals with its unique, spicy, and tangy flavor.

A Few Ideas for Serving Kimchi:

  • Kimchi stew (Kimchi Jjigae): This comforting Korean dish is made by simmering kimchi with tofu, pork, and other vegetables.
  • Kimchi fried rice: Fry up some leftover rice with kimchi and a fried egg on top for a quick and satisfying meal.
  • Kimchi pancakes: Mix kimchi into a batter and pan-fry it for a crispy, savory snack.

Conclusion

Making authentic kimchi at home is a rewarding and enjoyable process. By following these steps and using fresh ingredients, you’ll be able to create your own homemade kimchi, packed with flavor and health benefits. Whether you’re a first-time maker or a seasoned kimchi enthusiast, this guide provides everything you need to make kimchi just like the Koreans do.

Enjoy your homemade kimchi, and don’t be afraid to experiment with different spices and vegetables to create your perfect recipe!
