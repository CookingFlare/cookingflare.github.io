Italian Cuisine throughout History: From Ancient Rome to Modern Times


Italian cuisine has evolved significantly over the centuries, reflecting the country’s rich history and diverse cultural influences. From the days of Ancient Rome to the present, Italian food has transformed to become the globally-loved cuisine that we know today. Here, we will journey through the various eras, from the origins of pasta to the rise of pizza and beyond.

1. Ancient Rome

Italian cuisine’s roots can be traced back to the days of Ancient Rome. Back then, the Romans were known for their love of bread, which was a staple food, and they also enjoyed a variety of grains and legumes. At this time, Italian ingredients such as tomatoes, potatoes, maize, bell peppers and chili peppers were not yet known. Nevertheless, the basic cooking techniques used today have developed in Ancient Rome, including the use of spices, herbs, and the art of salting and pickling.

While no one knows when Italians first began eating pasta, it is believed that the form we recognize today did not arise until the Middle Ages. The earliest forms of pasta were thin noodles that were made from flour and water.

2. The Middle Ages and the Renaissance

The Middle Ages saw major changes in the cuisine of Italy, primarily due to the introduction of a wider variety of ingredients. As tomatoes, potatoes, maize, and other ingredients arrived in Italy through trade with the New World, Italian cooks began experimenting with new combinations, creating a richer, more diverse cuisine.

During the Renaissance, Italy became a hub for innovation in the culinary arts. Notably, the city-state of Venice played a leading role in the evolution of Italian cuisine. Venetian traders introduced rare ingredients and cooking techniques to Italian cooks from around the world. Puttanesca, a pasta dish featuring tomatoes, olives, capers, and anchovies, is an example of this fusion cuisine.

3. Neapolitan Pizza: The Birth of a Legend

Pizza originated in Naples in the late 18th and early 19th centuries, during the time of the Bourbon kings when bread was scarce, and pizza became a popular street food. Originally, Neapolitan pizza was made flat and round, with a crust that was crisp rather than chewy. The base was topped with tomatoes, onions, garlic, and anchovies, while other ingredients such as basil, cheese, and oil were added at various points.

The popularity of pizza then spread beyond Naples, initially within Italy, and later throughout the world. It became a symbol of Italian identity and a universally beloved dish. In 2004, Neapolitan pizza became part of UNESCO’s intangible cultural heritage.

4. Pasta and Risotto: The Quintessence of Italian Cuisine

During the 19th and early 20th centuries, pasta and risotto became the staples of Italian cuisine. Pasta, which ranges from simple spaghetti to more elaborate stuffed forms such as cannelloni and ravioli, became a symbol of Italian identity. The different pasta shapes and sizes, combined with sauces—whether simple, rich, or complex—made pasta a versatile meal loved around the world.

Risotto made its way onto the tables of northern Italy, where arborio rice is grown. Experimenting with different broths and meats, Italians developed risotto alla milanese, with saffron and bone marrow, and risotto ai funghi, with wild mushrooms.

5. Emigration and fusion cuisine

As millions of Italians emigrated from the years following World War I until the 1970s, they took their rich culinary heritage with them, contributing to the development of fusion cuisines in various corners of the world.

Cuban pizza, a significant deviation from the traditional Neapolitan pizza, includes ingredients such as ham and Swiss cheese. Italian-American cuisine has also contributed to American comfort foods, leading to familiar dishes like spaghetti and meatballs and lasagna.

6. Modern Italian Cuisine

Modern Italian cuisine is an inspiration of tradition fused with contemporary influences. Chefs are constantly experimenting with new fusions and combinations inspired by world cuisine, all while staying true to the flavors and characteristics of the Italian regions.

From the slow food movement to the resurgence of traditional cheeses, cured meats, and bread, the contemporary evolution of Italian cuisine is still unfolding. Innovative chefs across the globe continue to preserve and refine the tradition while pushing the boundaries of taste and presentation.

Italian cuisine has continually transformed, reflecting the country’s diverse regional cultures, people, and history. From ancient times to the modern era, we continue to appreciate the richness of its culinary tradition, while also embracing the ever-evolving landscape of food and taste.
