Preserve the Harvest: Canning, Freezing, & Dehydrating for Busy Families


As the bounty of summer fades into the crispness of fall, preserving the excess harvest becomes more than just a tradition - it’s a necessity for busy families. With limited time and resources, it’s crucial to preserve the flavors, textures, and nutrients of fresh fruit and veggies for future use. In this post, we’ll explore canning, freezing, and dehydrating techniques, and how you can save time and energy with each method.

Canning

Canning is the process of preserving food in an airtight container to prevent spoilage. Select high-quality produce, preferably locally sourced, for the best-tasting canned goods.

Tools and Supplies:

  • Canning jars, lids, and bands
  • A water bath canner or pressure canner
  • Jar lifter, funnel, ladle, and magnetic wand

Steps:

  1. Prepare your food: Choose fruits, pickles, or tomatoes for water-bath canning. Meat, seafood, and vegetables require a pressure canner.
  2. Sterilize the jars and prepare the lids: Run the jars through a dishwasher or immerse them in boiling water for 10 minutes. Keep the lids in hot water until use.
  3. Prepare the food for canning: Pack the jars tightly, leaving ½-inch headspace. Some foods require an additional ingredient such as sugar, lemon juice, or vinegar to help the process along.
  4. Process the jars: Place the jars in the canner, ensuring they’re fully covered with water. Process the required time, then let them cool undisturbed for 12-24 hours.

Canning can be a daunting task for beginners, but mastering this preservation technique can save you money, time, and ensure that your family has access to nutrient-dense meals all year long.

Freezing

Freezing is an effortless way to freeze the harvest’s freshness, as it involves simply sealing your prepared food in airtight containers or freezer bags and storing them in the freezer.

Tips for Freezing Success:

  • Wash and prepare your produce, removing any stems and seeds.
  • Blanch vegetables (boil for a short time) to stop the enzymes that cause loss of flavor, color, and nutrients.
  • For optimal quality, use freezer-safe containers or double-bag your food in freezable plastic bags, and remove as much air as possible to prevent freezer burn.

For Meats & Seafood:

  • Cook the meats or seafood completely, then portion and freeze individually.
  • Use freezer-safe bags or containers for easy storage and reheat as you need.

Dehydrating

Dehydration removes moisture from fruits and vegetables, preserving them for extended periods, making it an ideal method for busy families wanting to save time and energy.

Dehydrator or Oven Drying:

  • Slice fruits and vegetables to an even thickness and remove seeds.
  • If using an oven, set it to the lowest temperature (usually 140°F or 60°C) and leave the door ajar for air circulation. For a dehydrator, follow the manufacturer’s instructions.
  • Dry the produce until they’re leather-like and brittle, then store in an airtight container or freezer bag.

Benefits of Dehydration:

  • Convenient, portable, on-the-go snacks
  • Easy to store, taking up less space
  • Retains nutrients better than canning, and is a naturally preservative-free method

In conclusion, preserving the harvest using canning, freezing, and dehydrating methods can provide busy families with a variety of nutritious and delicious meals throughout the year. These techniques can save time, improve food security, and cultivate valued family traditions. Experiment with different methods and staples - who knows, you may develop new favorites along the way!

Happy preserving!
