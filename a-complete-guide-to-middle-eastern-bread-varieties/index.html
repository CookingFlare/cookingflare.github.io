A Complete Guide to Middle Eastern Bread Varieties


Bread holds a central place in Middle Eastern cuisine, acting as both a staple food and an essential component of meals. The Middle East, a region known for its rich history and diverse cultures, offers a stunning array of bread varieties. From the soft and pillowy pita to the crispy and savory manakish, each type of bread has its unique preparation methods, flavor profiles, and cultural significance. Whether enjoyed alongside a hearty stew, used to scoop up dips, or savored with a simple drizzle of olive oil, these breads are more than just food; they are a symbol of hospitality and tradition.

In this complete guide, we will explore the different types of Middle Eastern bread, delve into their histories, and provide recipes to help you recreate these delicious varieties at home. Let’s break down the most popular and beloved Middle Eastern bread types.

1. Pita Bread

Overview

Pita bread is perhaps the most recognizable Middle Eastern bread variety. It is soft, round, and slightly puffed, with a signature pocket in the middle that makes it perfect for stuffing with various fillings, such as falafel, shawarma, or kebabs. The bread has a long history and is believed to date back to ancient civilizations in the Middle East. Pita has evolved over the centuries but remains a fundamental element of regional cuisine.

Ingredients

  • 3 cups of all-purpose flour
  • 1 tablespoon of sugar
  • 2 teaspoons of salt
  • 2 teaspoons of active dry yeast
  • 1 ¼ cups of warm water
  • 2 tablespoons of olive oil

Method

  1. In a large mixing bowl, combine the flour, sugar, salt, and yeast.
  2. Gradually add the warm water and olive oil, mixing the ingredients together until a dough forms.
  3. Knead the dough for about 10 minutes until it becomes smooth and elastic.
  4. Cover the dough with a damp cloth and let it rise in a warm place for about 1 hour or until it has doubled in size.
  5. Preheat the oven to 475°F (245°C) and place a baking stone or baking sheet inside to heat.
  6. Punch the dough down, then divide it into 8 equal portions and roll each portion into a ball.
  7. Using a rolling pin, flatten each dough ball into a disc about 6 inches in diameter.
  8. Place the discs onto the preheated baking stone or sheet and bake for about 5-7 minutes, or until the pitas puff up and turn lightly golden.
  9. Remove from the oven, allow to cool slightly, and serve.

Serving Suggestions

Pita bread is incredibly versatile. You can serve it with hummus, baba ganoush, or tzatziki as a starter. It’s also perfect for wrapping around shawarma, falafel, or grilled meats.

2. Manakish (Mana’eesh)

Overview

Manakish is a popular Levantine flatbread, particularly in Lebanon, Syria, Jordan, and Palestine. It is typically topped with a mixture of za’atar (a blend of herbs, sesame seeds, and sumac) and olive oil, then baked until golden. While za’atar is the most common topping, manakish can also be found with other toppings such as cheese, minced meat, or even vegetables.

Ingredients

  • 2 cups of all-purpose flour
  • 1 teaspoon of sugar
  • 1 teaspoon of salt
  • 1 teaspoon of active dry yeast
  • ¾ cup of warm water
  • 1 tablespoon of olive oil
  • 3 tablespoons of za’atar spice mix
  • 3 tablespoons of olive oil (for the topping)

Method

  1. In a large bowl, combine the flour, sugar, salt, and yeast.
  2. Slowly add the warm water and olive oil, stirring until a dough forms.
  3. Knead the dough for 8-10 minutes until it’s smooth and elastic.
  4. Cover the dough and allow it to rise for 1 hour in a warm place.
  5. Preheat the oven to 450°F (230°C) and place a baking sheet inside to heat.
  6. Once the dough has risen, divide it into small balls and roll each into a thin disc.
  7. Mix the za’atar with olive oil to create a paste, then spread it evenly over the dough discs.
  8. Place the discs on the preheated baking sheet and bake for 8-10 minutes, or until the edges are crisp and golden.
  9. Remove from the oven and serve warm.

Serving Suggestions

Manakish is often eaten for breakfast or as a snack. It pairs wonderfully with a cup of tea or as part of a larger spread with hummus, labneh, or a fresh salad.

3. Khubz (Arabic Flatbread)

Overview

Khubz, also known as Arabic flatbread, is another common bread found in many Middle Eastern countries. This bread is round, soft, and slightly thicker than pita, but it still remains light and airy. It is often used to scoop up dips or to wrap grilled meats and vegetables.

Ingredients

  • 3 cups of whole wheat flour
  • 1 cup of all-purpose flour
  • 1 tablespoon of salt
  • 2 teaspoons of yeast
  • 1 tablespoon of olive oil
  • 1 ½ cups of warm water

Method

  1. In a large bowl, combine the whole wheat flour, all-purpose flour, salt, and yeast.
  2. Gradually add the warm water and olive oil, mixing to form a dough.
  3. Knead the dough for 8-10 minutes until smooth and elastic.
  4. Cover the dough and let it rise for about 1 hour, or until doubled in size.
  5. Preheat a large skillet or griddle over medium heat.
  6. Divide the dough into 8-10 portions and roll each into a ball.
  7. Roll each ball into a flat disc about 7 inches in diameter.
  8. Place the discs on the preheated skillet and cook for 2-3 minutes per side, or until lightly browned and cooked through.
  9. Serve warm.

Serving Suggestions

Khubz is perfect for pairing with mezze, stews, or grilled meats. It can also be used as a wrap for shawarma or falafel.

4. Lavash

Overview

Lavash is a thin, soft flatbread that has been a staple of Armenian and Iranian cuisines for centuries. It is usually baked in a traditional clay oven called a tonir, giving it a unique texture. In modern times, lavash is often baked on a hot griddle or in a regular oven. It is incredibly versatile and can be eaten fresh, used as a wrap, or toasted for added crunch.

Ingredients

  • 2 cups of all-purpose flour
  • 1 teaspoon of salt
  • 1 teaspoon of sugar
  • 1 teaspoon of active dry yeast
  • 1 cup of warm water
  • 2 tablespoons of olive oil

Method

  1. In a mixing bowl, combine the flour, salt, sugar, and yeast.
  2. Gradually add the warm water and olive oil, mixing until a dough forms.
  3. Knead the dough for 8-10 minutes, then cover and let it rise for about 1 hour.
  4. Preheat a griddle or large skillet over medium heat.
  5. Once the dough has risen, divide it into small portions and roll each portion into a thin circle.
  6. Place the dough on the hot griddle and cook for 1-2 minutes per side, or until golden spots appear.
  7. Remove from the griddle and allow it to cool before serving.

Serving Suggestions

Lavash is often served with grilled meats, kebabs, or dips like hummus and baba ganoush. It is also used as a wrap for various fillings.

5. Samoon

Overview

Samoon is a traditional Iraqi bread known for its unique shape and soft, pillowy texture. It is slightly thicker than pita bread and often sprinkled with sesame seeds for added flavor. Samoon is frequently served alongside meat dishes and stews.

Ingredients

  • 3 cups of all-purpose flour
  • 1 teaspoon of sugar
  • 1 teaspoon of salt
  • 2 teaspoons of active dry yeast
  • 1 cup of warm water
  • 2 tablespoons of olive oil
  • Sesame seeds (optional)

Method

  1. In a large bowl, combine the flour, sugar, salt, and yeast.
  2. Gradually add the warm water and olive oil, mixing to form a dough.
  3. Knead the dough for about 10 minutes until smooth and elastic.
  4. Cover the dough and let it rise for 1 hour.
  5. Preheat the oven to 400°F (200°C).
  6. Punch the dough down and divide it into small balls.
  7. Shape each ball into a rounded oval and place them on a greased baking sheet.
  8. If desired, sprinkle sesame seeds on top.
  9. Bake for about 15 minutes, or until golden brown.
  10. Serve warm.

Serving Suggestions

Samoon is often served with stews, grilled meats, or as a side with hummus and pickles.

Conclusion

Middle Eastern breads offer a wide variety of textures, flavors, and uses. From the fluffy pita to the crispy lavash, each bread is a reflection of the region’s history, culture, and culinary traditions. These breads are not just food; they are an integral part of the social and cultural fabric of the Middle East, enjoyed by families and communities alike.

Whether you’re making pita bread to scoop up hummus, enjoying a manakish topped with za’atar for breakfast, or savoring a piece of lavash alongside a grilled lamb kebab, these breads provide a perfect foundation for a range of delicious Middle Eastern meals. So, roll up your sleeves, gather your ingredients, and dive into the art of making these timeless breads. Your taste buds will thank you for the journey!
