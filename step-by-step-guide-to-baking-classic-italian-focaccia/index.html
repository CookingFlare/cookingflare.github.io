Step-by-Step Guide to Baking Classic Italian Focaccia


Focaccia is one of the most beloved breads in Italy, known for its fluffy, airy texture, rich flavor, and simple yet satisfying taste. This versatile bread can be enjoyed as a snack, served alongside soups and salads, or even used to make sandwiches. The magic of focaccia lies in its simplicity – with just a few key ingredients, you can create a delicious homemade loaf that brings a taste of Italy to your kitchen.

In this guide, we’ll take you through a step-by-step process of making classic Italian focaccia from scratch. Whether you’re a beginner or an experienced baker, you’ll find that making focaccia is an incredibly rewarding experience. The recipe is flexible, and you can customize the toppings to suit your preferences. Let’s dive into the process!

Ingredients for Classic Italian Focaccia

To begin, gather the following ingredients:

For the dough:

  • 500g (about 4 cups) all-purpose flour
  • 10g (2 teaspoons) salt
  • 10g (1 tablespoon) sugar
  • 7g (1 packet) active dry yeast
  • 300ml (1 ¼ cups) warm water
  • 60ml (¼ cup) olive oil, plus extra for greasing
  • Optional: a tablespoon of honey (for a touch of sweetness)

For the topping:

  • 2 tablespoons fresh rosemary (or dried if fresh isn’t available)
  • Coarse sea salt, to taste
  • Extra virgin olive oil for drizzling
  • Optional: sliced onions, cherry tomatoes, olives, or any other toppings you enjoy

Step 1: Preparing the Dough

The key to a good focaccia is in the dough. To get started, dissolve the yeast and sugar into the warm water and let it sit for about 5-10 minutes. You’ll know it’s ready when the mixture becomes frothy and bubbly – this means the yeast is activated and ready to help your dough rise.

In a large bowl, combine the flour and salt, making a small well in the center. Once the yeast mixture is ready, pour it into the well along with the olive oil (and honey if you’re using it). Use a spoon or your hands to stir the ingredients together until a sticky dough forms.

Step 2: Kneading the Dough

At this point, it’s time to knead the dough. Transfer it to a lightly floured surface and begin kneading it for about 8-10 minutes. If the dough feels too sticky, you can sprinkle a bit more flour, but be careful not to add too much. The goal is to develop the gluten, which will give the focaccia its signature texture. The dough should be soft and smooth, but not overly sticky.

Alternatively, if you have a stand mixer, you can use the dough hook attachment to knead the dough on low speed for about 5-7 minutes. The dough is ready when it’s elastic and slightly tacky to the touch.

Step 3: First Rise

Once your dough is kneaded, it’s time for the first rise. Lightly oil a large bowl, place the dough inside, and cover it with a clean kitchen towel or plastic wrap. Let the dough rise in a warm, draft-free place for about 1 to 1 ½ hours, or until it has doubled in size. This process allows the yeast to work its magic and develop the airy, fluffy texture that focaccia is known for.

Pro Tip: If you’re in a hurry, you can place the dough in an oven that’s been preheated to the lowest setting for about 1-2 minutes, then turn the oven off. This creates a warm environment for the dough to rise quickly.

Step 4: Shaping the Dough

Once the dough has risen, it’s time to shape it. Lightly oil a baking sheet (preferably a rimmed sheet) or a large rectangular pan. Gently punch down the dough to release the air, and then transfer it to the prepared pan.

Using your fingers, gently spread the dough out to fit the shape of the pan. Be careful not to overwork it, as you want to maintain some of the air pockets in the dough. The dough should cover the bottom of the pan, but it doesn’t have to be perfectly smooth.

Once it’s in place, cover the dough again with a towel and let it rise for an additional 30 minutes. During this second rise, the dough will become even fluffier and lighter.

Step 5: Adding Toppings

While the dough is rising, prepare your toppings. The classic focaccia topping is simple: fresh rosemary, sea salt, and a generous drizzle of olive oil. However, you can get creative and add your favorite ingredients. Some popular variations include:

  • Sliced cherry tomatoes
  • Olives
  • Caramelized onions
  • Thinly sliced garlic
  • Fresh herbs like thyme or oregano

To add the toppings, press your fingertips gently into the dough, creating small dimples all over the surface. This is a characteristic feature of focaccia, and it helps to hold the oil and toppings in place.

After dimpling the dough, drizzle a generous amount of extra virgin olive oil over the top, ensuring that the surface is evenly coated. Sprinkle the fresh rosemary and coarse sea salt on top, pressing them gently into the dough with your fingers. If you’re adding any additional toppings like olives or tomatoes, now is the time to scatter them over the dough.

Step 6: Baking the Focaccia

Preheat your oven to 200°C (400°F) while the dough completes its second rise. Once the oven is hot, it’s time to bake the focaccia. Place the pan in the center of the oven and bake for 20-25 minutes, or until the top is golden brown and the edges are crispy. You can check for doneness by gently tapping the bottom of the focaccia – it should sound hollow when fully baked.

If you want an extra-crispy texture, you can bake it for a few more minutes, but be sure to keep an eye on it to prevent burning. The focaccia is best when it’s golden, crisp on the edges, and soft and airy on the inside.

Step 7: Cooling and Serving

Once your focaccia is done, remove it from the oven and let it cool in the pan for a few minutes. Then, transfer it to a wire rack to cool completely. While focaccia is best served fresh, it can also be stored in an airtight container for up to two days. If it starts to lose its softness, you can warm it up in the oven for a few minutes before serving.

Variations and Tips for Customizing Focaccia

Focaccia is highly versatile and can be customized with a wide range of toppings and flavors. Here are some variations you can try:

1. Cheese Focaccia:

Add shredded mozzarella or Parmesan cheese to the dough before baking for a cheesy twist. You can also top the focaccia with grated cheese during the last few minutes of baking for a delicious golden crust.

2. Garlic Focaccia:

For a more intense garlic flavor, you can finely mince garlic and mix it into the dough. Alternatively, drizzle the dough with garlic-infused olive oil before baking.

3. Sweet Focaccia:

If you’re craving something on the sweeter side, consider adding honey and fresh fruit like figs or grapes as toppings. A sprinkle of sugar and cinnamon can also turn the focaccia into a delightful sweet treat.

4. Focaccia with Balsamic Vinegar:

For a tangy contrast to the rich olive oil, drizzle some balsamic vinegar on top after baking. This adds a unique depth of flavor that complements the focaccia’s softness.

Troubleshooting Tips for Perfect Focaccia

Although making focaccia is relatively simple, here are some troubleshooting tips to ensure your loaf turns out perfectly:

  1. Dough Too Sticky? If the dough is too sticky to work with, add a little more flour, but do so gradually. Remember that focaccia dough should be a bit sticky and soft for the best texture.

  2. Dough Not Rising? If your dough isn’t rising as expected, ensure that the yeast is fresh and that the water you used was warm but not hot. If the water is too hot, it can kill the yeast, preventing the dough from rising properly.

  3. Focaccia Too Dense? If your focaccia turns out too dense, it may have been overworked during kneading. Be gentle when handling the dough to preserve its airy texture.

  4. Focaccia Too Dry? If your focaccia comes out dry, be sure to use enough olive oil both in the dough and on top before baking. The oil not only adds flavor but also helps keep the bread moist.

Conclusion

Baking classic Italian focaccia is a fun and rewarding experience. With just a few basic ingredients, you can create a delicious, aromatic loaf that is perfect for any occasion. Whether you’re enjoying it on its own, serving it as an appetizer, or making a sandwich, this homemade focaccia will transport you to Italy with every bite.

With the right technique and a little patience, you’ll be able to make focaccia that’s as good as the ones found in Italian bakeries. Don’t be afraid to experiment with different toppings, flavors, and variations – focaccia is incredibly adaptable and can be tailored to suit your tastes.

Happy baking, and enjoy your freshly baked focaccia!
