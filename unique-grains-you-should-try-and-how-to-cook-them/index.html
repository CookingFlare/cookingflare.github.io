Unique Grains You Should Try and How to Cook Them


Grains are an essential part of diets worldwide, providing an excellent source of fiber, vitamins, minerals, and energy. While many are familiar with common grains like rice, wheat, and oats, there is a world of unique and nutrient-dense grains that can elevate your meals. From ancient grains with rich histories to newer, lesser-known varieties, these grains can offer diverse flavors, textures, and health benefits.

If you’re looking to expand your culinary horizons, it’s time to explore some of these unique grains and learn how to cook them. This guide will introduce you to some interesting grains that should be part of your pantry, along with tips on how to cook them.

1. Farro: The Nutty Ancient Grain

Farro, often considered one of the oldest cultivated grains, hails from the Mediterranean region and has been a staple food for centuries. It’s a type of wheat with a chewy texture and a nutty, slightly sweet flavor. Farro is packed with fiber, protein, and essential minerals such as iron, magnesium, and zinc, making it an excellent choice for a nutritious, hearty meal.

How to Cook Farro

Cooking farro is relatively simple and requires only a few steps:

  1. Rinse: First, rinse the farro under cold water to remove any impurities.
  2. Boil: In a medium-sized pot, bring 3 cups of water or broth to a boil for every 1 cup of farro. Add a pinch of salt if desired.
  3. Simmer: Lower the heat to a simmer, cover, and cook the farro for about 25 to 30 minutes, or until tender. If you prefer a softer texture, cook it a little longer.
  4. Drain: Once cooked, drain any excess liquid and fluff the farro with a fork.

Farro works wonderfully in soups, salads, and as a side dish to pair with roasted vegetables or grilled meats.

2. Teff: Tiny But Mighty

Teff is an ancient grain originating from Ethiopia and Eritrea, known for its tiny size and immense nutritional profile. Although small in size, teff is loaded with protein, fiber, calcium, and iron. It’s also naturally gluten-free, making it a great option for those with dietary restrictions. Teff has a mildly sweet, earthy flavor, which makes it perfect for both savory and sweet dishes.

How to Cook Teff

Teff can be cooked in several ways depending on its use in the recipe. Here’s a simple method for cooking it as a grain:

  1. Rinse: Wash the teff under cold water to remove any dirt.
  2. Boil: For every 1 cup of teff, use 2 ½ cups of water or broth. Bring it to a boil.
  3. Simmer: Reduce the heat and let it simmer for 15 to 20 minutes. Stir occasionally.
  4. Let it Sit: After cooking, remove from the heat and let it sit, covered, for a few minutes. This will help it absorb any remaining liquid.
  5. Fluff: Fluff the teff with a fork and it’s ready to serve.

Teff is commonly used in Ethiopian dishes, especially in injera, a traditional flatbread. It can also be used as a hot cereal, added to baking recipes, or incorporated into savory pilafs.

3. Freekeh: A Supergrain with a Smoky Flavor

Freekeh is an ancient grain made from green durum wheat that is harvested while still young and then roasted. This gives freekeh its signature smoky flavor and chewy texture. Rich in fiber, protein, and antioxidants, freekeh is an excellent choice for maintaining a healthy digestive system and boosting your metabolism.

How to Cook Freekeh

To cook freekeh, follow these steps:

  1. Rinse: Begin by rinsing the freekeh in cold water to remove any debris.
  2. Boil: Use 2 ½ cups of water or broth for every 1 cup of freekeh. Bring the mixture to a boil.
  3. Simmer: Reduce the heat and simmer, covered, for 20 to 25 minutes. The freekeh should be tender but still slightly chewy.
  4. Drain: Drain any remaining liquid and let it rest for a few minutes.

Freekeh makes a wonderful addition to salads, soups, and grain bowls, and can also be used as a substitute for rice or couscous in a variety of dishes.

4. Black Rice: The Forbidden Rice

Black rice, also known as “forbidden rice,” has a rich history in Chinese culture, once reserved for royalty. Its deep, dark purple-black hue comes from anthocyanins, antioxidants that are also found in blueberries and other purple foods. Black rice is rich in fiber, protein, and essential nutrients, making it a nutrient-dense alternative to white rice.

How to Cook Black Rice

Cooking black rice is similar to cooking other types of rice. Here’s how:

  1. Rinse: Rinse the rice thoroughly under cold water.
  2. Boil: Combine 1 cup of black rice with 2 cups of water or broth in a pot. Bring to a boil.
  3. Simmer: Reduce the heat and let it simmer, covered, for about 30 to 35 minutes until the rice is tender.
  4. Rest: After cooking, remove the rice from heat and let it sit for 5 to 10 minutes before fluffing with a fork.

Black rice is perfect for both savory dishes, like stir-fries and grain bowls, and sweet dishes, such as puddings or paired with coconut milk and fruits.

5. Amaranth: A Gluten-Free Powerhouse

Amaranth is a tiny, round seed that was once a staple in the diet of the Aztecs and other ancient civilizations. It’s technically a pseudo-grain, meaning it is a seed that behaves like a grain. Amaranth is naturally gluten-free and has a slightly nutty flavor with a pleasant crunch. It’s packed with protein, iron, magnesium, and lysine, an essential amino acid that is usually absent in most grains.

How to Cook Amaranth

Amaranth can be cooked similarly to rice or quinoa:

  1. Rinse: Rinse the amaranth under cold water before cooking.
  2. Boil: Use a ratio of 2 cups of water or broth for every 1 cup of amaranth. Bring it to a boil.
  3. Simmer: Reduce the heat and cover the pot. Let it simmer for about 20 minutes, or until the liquid is absorbed.
  4. Rest: After the cooking time is up, let it rest for a few minutes before fluffing with a fork.

Amaranth can be enjoyed as a porridge for breakfast, added to salads, or used in baking. You can even pop amaranth seeds for a crunchy snack!

6. Quinoa: The High-Protein Grain

Quinoa is one of the most popular ancient grains, known for its high protein content and unique ability to be a complete protein, containing all nine essential amino acids. Native to the Andean region of South America, quinoa has a slightly earthy flavor and a fluffy texture once cooked. It’s also naturally gluten-free and packed with fiber, iron, magnesium, and B vitamins.

How to Cook Quinoa

Cooking quinoa is quick and easy:

  1. Rinse: Rinse the quinoa under cold water to remove its natural saponin coating, which can make it taste bitter.
  2. Boil: Combine 1 cup of quinoa with 2 cups of water or broth. Bring it to a boil.
  3. Simmer: Once boiling, reduce the heat and cover. Simmer for about 15 minutes, or until the liquid is absorbed and the quinoa is tender.
  4. Fluff: Let it sit for 5 minutes, then fluff with a fork.

Quinoa is a versatile grain that can be used in salads, grain bowls, soups, or as a base for vegetable stir-fries.

7. Spelt: The Versatile, Nutty Grain

Spelt, a type of ancient wheat, has been cultivated for thousands of years, particularly in Europe and the Middle East. It has a nutty, slightly sweet flavor and is a rich source of dietary fiber, protein, and important minerals like manganese, phosphorus, and magnesium. Spelt is more easily digestible than modern wheat, making it a great option for those with sensitive stomachs.

How to Cook Spelt

To cook spelt, follow these steps:

  1. Rinse: Wash the spelt thoroughly in cold water.
  2. Boil: For every 1 cup of spelt, use 3 cups of water or broth. Bring it to a boil.
  3. Simmer: Reduce the heat and cover the pot. Simmer for 45 to 60 minutes, or until tender.
  4. Drain: Drain any excess liquid and let it sit for a few minutes before serving.

Spelt can be used in a variety of dishes, including soups, stews, salads, and grain-based pilafs. It’s also an excellent substitute for rice or pasta.

Conclusion: Embrace the World of Unique Grains

The world of grains is vast, with so many varieties to explore. Each grain offers its own unique set of flavors, textures, and health benefits. Whether you’re cooking ancient grains like farro, quinoa, and teff, or experimenting with modern supergrains like freekeh and black rice, incorporating these unique grains into your meals will not only diversify your cooking but also provide additional nutrients that your body craves.

So why not broaden your culinary repertoire and try something new? With the right cooking techniques, these grains will elevate your meals and provide you with a delicious and wholesome dining experience.

Start with one of these grains today and discover the incredible flavors and nutritional benefits they bring to your table. Happy cooking!
