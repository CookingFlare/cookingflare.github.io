How to Grill a Whole Chicken: Tips for Crispy Skin


Grilling a whole chicken may sound like a challenge, but with the right technique and tips, you can master this deliciously juicy and crispy-skinned masterpiece. Whether you’re preparing for a family barbecue, a backyard party, or a cozy dinner, grilling a whole chicken is a great way to showcase your culinary skills and impress your guests.

In this guide, we’ll walk you through everything you need to know about grilling a whole chicken, from preparing it properly to achieving that perfect crispy skin everyone craves.

1. Choose the Right Chicken

The first step to grilling a perfect whole chicken is choosing the right bird. Go for a fresh, organic, or free-range chicken, as these typically have better flavor and texture. Whole chickens usually weigh between 3 and 5 pounds, which is the ideal size for grilling—large enough to feed a group but small enough to cook evenly.

Look for a chicken with an even color and firm skin. Check that the chicken hasn’t been previously frozen, as frozen poultry can be drier when cooked.

2. Prep Your Chicken for the Grill

Before you start grilling, proper preparation is key to ensuring your chicken is flavorful and cooks evenly. Here’s how to prepare your chicken:

Remove the Giblets

Most whole chickens come with giblets packed inside the cavity. Remove these and set them aside for making stock, or discard them if you prefer.

Pat Dry the Chicken

Use paper towels to thoroughly pat your chicken dry. Moisture on the surface will prevent the skin from becoming crispy, so ensure the chicken is as dry as possible.

Truss the Chicken

Trussing your chicken, or tying it up with kitchen twine, is important for even cooking. By securing the legs and wings to the body, you ensure the chicken maintains its shape while on the grill. This also helps the chicken cook more uniformly.

To truss a chicken:

  • Place the bird breast-side up.
  • Tie the legs together at the ankle joint with twine, pulling them toward the body.
  • Tuck the wing tips under the bird to prevent them from burning.

Season the Chicken

Now it’s time to season your chicken. A simple seasoning of kosher salt and black pepper is always a winning combination, but you can get creative with marinades, rubs, or a dry brine. If you’re looking to add more flavor, try the following:

  • Dry Rub: Combine garlic powder, onion powder, paprika, cayenne pepper, and thyme to create a flavorful dry rub.
  • Herb Butter: Mix softened butter with fresh herbs like rosemary, thyme, sage, and garlic. Rub this mixture under the skin of the chicken for added flavor.
  • Citrus Marinade: Marinate your chicken in a blend of olive oil, lemon juice, garlic, and herbs for a refreshing citrusy twist.

Once your chicken is seasoned, let it sit at room temperature for about 30 minutes before grilling. This allows the chicken to cook more evenly.

3. Set Up Your Grill

Grilling a whole chicken requires a two-zone cooking method: one side of the grill will be hot for searing, while the other side remains cooler for indirect cooking. This setup allows you to achieve crispy skin without burning the chicken and ensures the inside cooks evenly.

Charcoal Grill Setup:

  • Direct Heat Side: Pile the coals on one side of the grill to create a high-heat zone.
  • Indirect Heat Side: Leave the other side empty, allowing the chicken to cook slowly with indirect heat.

Gas Grill Setup:

  • Preheat the grill to medium-high heat (about 400°F).
  • Turn off one side of the burners to create the indirect cooking zone.

4. Grilling the Chicken

Now that your chicken is prepped and the grill is ready, it’s time to start grilling. The cooking process can take around 1 to 1.5 hours, depending on the size of your chicken, so be patient and keep an eye on the temperature.

Sear the Chicken

Start by placing the chicken on the direct heat side of the grill, breast-side down. Sear it for about 4-5 minutes, until the skin is golden brown and crispy. This step is crucial for getting that crispy skin, so don’t skip it!

Move to Indirect Heat

Once the chicken is seared, carefully flip it so it’s breast-side up and move it to the indirect heat side of the grill. Close the lid and cook the chicken slowly over medium heat.

Monitor the Temperature

Use a meat thermometer to check the internal temperature of the chicken. The chicken is done when the internal temperature reaches 165°F in the thickest part of the breast and 170°F in the thigh. It’s crucial not to overcook the chicken, as it can dry out the meat.

Flip Occasionally

During the grilling process, flip the chicken every 20-30 minutes to ensure even cooking and prevent the skin from burning. Be sure to check that the skin is becoming golden and crispy.

Rest the Chicken

Once the chicken reaches the proper internal temperature, remove it from the grill and let it rest for about 10-15 minutes. Resting allows the juices to redistribute throughout the meat, ensuring a juicy, tender chicken.

5. Tips for Extra Crispy Skin

Achieving perfectly crispy chicken skin is one of the biggest challenges when grilling a whole chicken. Here are some helpful tips to ensure your chicken skin turns out golden and crispy:

Dry the Skin Thoroughly

As mentioned earlier, moisture is the enemy of crispy skin. After removing any giblets, pat your chicken dry with paper towels. This step is essential for getting that golden, crispy exterior.

Use Oil or Butter

To promote crispiness, rub the skin with oil or softened butter before grilling. This will help the skin crisp up beautifully while adding flavor. You can even try using flavored oils like olive oil or avocado oil for extra taste.

Cook at the Right Temperature

While it’s tempting to turn the heat up to high to speed up cooking, doing so can lead to uneven cooking and burnt skin. Instead, aim for a medium heat level, around 375°F to 400°F. This allows the skin to cook evenly and turn crispy without burning.

Indirect Heat is Key

Although you start with direct heat to get that crispy sear, it’s the indirect heat that truly gives you the perfect balance between crispy skin and tender meat. By cooking your chicken slowly over indirect heat, you avoid drying out the skin or the meat.

Baste for Extra Flavor

Basting your chicken with oil, butter, or even a marinade every 30 minutes will not only help keep the meat moist but also contribute to the crispiness of the skin. Baste during the indirect cooking phase, but avoid over-basting, as this can make the skin soggy.

Finish with a Blast of Heat

Once the chicken is nearly done cooking, increase the heat for the final 10 minutes of grilling to crisp up the skin further. This final blast of heat will give the chicken that irresistible golden-brown exterior.

6. How to Serve Grilled Whole Chicken

Once your whole chicken is off the grill and resting, it’s time to carve and serve. Here are a few ideas for serving your grilled chicken:

  • Classic Sides: Serve your chicken with classic barbecue sides like potato salad, coleslaw, grilled vegetables, or corn on the cob.
  • Sauces: Pair your chicken with flavorful dipping sauces such as barbecue sauce, chimichurri, or a tangy yogurt-based sauce.
  • Sandwiches: For a fun twist, shred the grilled chicken and use it in sandwiches or wraps. Top with pickles, lettuce, and a drizzle of ranch dressing for a crowd-pleasing meal.

7. Troubleshooting Common Grilling Issues

Grilling a whole chicken isn’t always foolproof, and you may run into a few issues along the way. Here are some common problems and how to solve them:

Skin is Not Crispy

If your chicken skin isn’t getting crispy, it may be because there is too much moisture on the skin or the heat isn’t high enough. Be sure to dry the chicken thoroughly before grilling and cook it at a medium heat to achieve a crispy texture.

Chicken is Overcooked or Dry

Overcooking is a common mistake when grilling whole chicken. Always use a meat thermometer to check the internal temperature and remove the chicken from the grill when it reaches the correct temperature. If your chicken turns out dry, you may have cooked it for too long, or the grill temperature was too high.

Burnt Skin

If your chicken’s skin is burning, it could be because the grill was too hot or the chicken was too close to the direct heat. Always start by searing the chicken, then move it to indirect heat to finish cooking slowly.

Conclusion

Grilling a whole chicken can seem intimidating, but with the right preparation and grilling technique, you can achieve perfectly crispy skin and juicy, tender meat every time. By following these tips, you’ll impress your family and friends with your grilling skills, making your chicken the star of the meal.

Remember to start with a well-prepared bird, set up your grill with two heat zones, and cook slowly over indirect heat to ensure the best results. With a little patience and attention to detail, you’ll soon be grilling whole chickens like a pro!
