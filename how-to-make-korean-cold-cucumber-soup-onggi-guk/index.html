How to Make Korean Cold Cucumber Soup (Onggi Guk)


Korean cuisine is rich with dishes that bring balance to the table—delicate textures, vibrant colors, and bold flavors are celebrated in every bite. Among its treasures, Korean Cold Cucumber Soup, or “Onggi Guk” (오이 냉국), is a dish that embodies both simplicity and refreshing vibrancy. This chilled, tangy soup combines crisp cucumbers with a seasoned broth, making it a perfect side dish for hot summer days.

Onggi Guk offers a harmonious blend of savory, sweet, and sour flavors with a crunch that highlights the natural freshness of the cucumbers. If you love discovering new dishes and experimenting with traditional recipes, this cold soup is a must-add to your culinary repertoire. Whether you’re hosting a Korean-inspired dinner or want to cool off with a light meal, this soup will never fail to bring a sense of nostalgia and comfort.

Ingredients You’ll Need

One of the great joys of making Onggi Guk is its simplicity. The recipe requires basic ingredients, many of which you may already have in your pantry. Here’s what you’ll need:

Main Ingredients:

  • Cucumbers: 2 small or medium Korean cucumbers (or substitute with Persian cucumbers)
  • Salt: 1 teaspoon to gently season and draw out excess moisture from the cucumbers
  • Green onions: 1 stalk, finely chopped
  • Red chili pepper (optional): Thinly sliced for a mild heat and visual contrast

Broth Ingredients:

  • Cold water: About 3 cups
  • Soy sauce: 2 tablespoons for depth of flavor
  • Rice vinegar: 2 tablespoons for the signature tanginess
  • Sugar: 1 teaspoon to balance the acidity
  • Sesame seeds: 1 teaspoon, toasted and crushed, for a nutty finish
  • Fresh garlic: 1 small clove, minced
  • Ice cubes: A handful for extra chill

Preparing the Cucumbers

Crisp cucumbers are the essence of this soup, so handling them with care ensures the right texture and taste. Start by washing the cucumbers thoroughly under cold water, ensuring that all impurities or residues are removed. If you prefer cucumbers with a softer bite and less bitterness, peel them partially, leaving streaks of green for visual appeal.

Steps to Prepare Cucumbers:

  1. Slice Thinly: Use a sharp knife or a mandoline to slice the cucumbers into thin, uniform rounds. Thin slices maximize the surface area, allowing the broth to coat the cucumbers evenly.
  2. Season: Place the cucumber slices into a bowl and sprinkle 1 teaspoon of salt evenly. Let them sit for 10-15 minutes to draw out excess moisture. This step prevents the vegetables from turning watery while enhancing their natural flavor.
  3. Rinse: Once the cucumbers have released some water, rinse them under cold water and gently pat dry with a paper towel.

Making the Broth

The depth and balance of Onggi Guk come from its broth. Unlike heavier soups, the cold cucumber soup relies on a light, seasoned liquid that amplifies the natural taste of cucumbers. The key here is achieving the perfect ratio of savory, sour, and lightly sweet flavors.

Steps to Prepare the Broth:

  1. Combine the Base Ingredients: In a large mixing bowl, pour about 3 cups of cold water, then mix in 2 tablespoons of soy sauce, 2 tablespoons of rice vinegar, and 1 teaspoon of sugar. Stir well until the sugar dissolves completely.
  2. Add Aromatics: Stir in the minced garlic for a touch of pungency, along with toasted sesame seeds to enhance the nutty fragrance.
  3. Taste and Adjust: Adjust the flavor based on your preferences. Add more vinegar if you like a tangier broth or extra soy sauce if you prefer a stronger umami profile. Some people also add a drizzle of sesame oil for richness.

Assembling the Soup

Once the cucumbers are prepped and the broth is ready, it’s time to bring the dish together. Assembly is simple, and the result is stunningly refreshing.

Steps to Assemble:

  1. Combine Cucumbers and Broth: Add the salted and rinsed cucumber slices to the bowl of broth.
  2. Cool It Down: Add a handful of ice cubes to the soup. This not only keeps it chilled but also dilutes the broth just enough for balanced flavors.
  3. Add Optional Garnishes: Sprinkle thinly sliced green onions and red chili pepper on top for added texture and a burst of color.

Serving Korean Cold Cucumber Soup

Onggi Guk is typically served as a side dish in Korean meals. Its sharp, cooling notes act as a great palate cleanser alongside heartier main dishes such as bulgogi, bibimbap, or Korean BBQ. Serve it in small, individual bowls to showcase its simple elegance.

Tips for Serving:

  • Chill Before Serving: While the ice cubes quickly cool the soup, refrigerating the assembled soup for 20-30 minutes before serving enhances its flavors.
  • Pair With Rice: Enjoy Onggi Guk alongside warm steamed rice for an intriguing hot-cold contrast.
  • Make It Your Own: Feel free to adjust spice levels or even add thinly sliced carrots or radishes for variety.

Health Benefits of Onggi Guk

This cold cucumber soup isn’t just refreshing—it’s also packed with nutrients. Cucumbers, the star ingredient, are high in water content, which helps keep you hydrated and supports digestion. They’re low in calories yet rich in vitamins and minerals like Vitamin K and potassium. Rice vinegar, known for its probiotic benefits, aids gut health and complements the light nature of the dish.

The soup is also naturally low in fat and gluten-free, making it an excellent addition to a variety of diets.

Storage Tips

Have leftovers? Good news! Onggi Guk is easy to store and retains its flavor in the fridge. Keep the soup in an airtight container and refrigerate for up to 48 hours. However, consume the dish as fresh as possible for the crunchiest cucumbers and best flavor.

Do’s and Don’ts:

  • Do: Store the cucumbers immersed in the broth for optimal freshness.
  • Don’t: Store ice cubes in the soup if you plan to refrigerate it—add fresh ones when you’re ready to serve.

Final Thoughts

Learning how to make Korean Cold Cucumber Soup is a rewarding experience that offers insight into the delicate simplicity of Korean cuisine. Onggi Guk is more than just a dish—it’s a cooling ritual for summer that highlights the magic of fresh vegetables and balanced seasonings. Whether you’re making it for a holiday feast or as part of a weekday meal, this soup will undoubtedly become a new favorite.

Dust off your apron, gather your ingredients, and enjoy the refreshing allure of Onggi Guk. Delight your taste buds, impress your dinner guests, and embrace the irresistible charm of Korean flavors!
