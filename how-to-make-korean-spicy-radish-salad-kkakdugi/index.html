How to Make Korean Spicy Radish Salad (Kkakdugi)


Korean cuisine is celebrated for its bold flavors, vibrant colors, and intricate balance of spice, sweetness, and umami. Among its rich assortment of side dishes, known as banchan, there’s one that stands out with its crisp texture and tangy, spicy kick: Kkakdugi, or Korean Spicy Radish Salad. Perfectly fermented and packed with probiotic health benefits, this dish pairs wonderfully with rice, soups, and other traditional entrees. This step-by-step recipe will guide you through creating authentic Kkakdugi right in your own kitchen.

What is Kkakdugi?

Kkakdugi (깍두기), often referred to as cubed radish kimchi, is a type of fermented side dish made primarily from daikon radish, a mild-flavored root vegetable common in Korean cooking. Unlike napa cabbage kimchi, which is leafy and soft, Kkakdugi is known for its crunchy, juicy bite. The radish pieces are cut into small cubes, seasoned with a rich blend of spices, and left to ferment until they develop a tangy complexity. The key to great Kkakdugi lies in the balance of flavors and the freshness of ingredients.

Ingredients You’ll Need

To make Kkakdugi, you’ll need a mix of fresh produce, pantry staples, and traditional Korean seasonings. Make sure to gather the following:

Main Ingredients:

  • Korean radish (mu) or daikon radish – 4 pounds (approximately 2 medium-sized radishes)
  • Kosher salt – 1⁄4 cup (for salting and drawing out moisture)
  • Granulated sugar – 1 tablespoon (optional, for balancing flavors)

Seasoning Paste:

  • Korean red pepper flakes (gochugaru) – 1⁄3 cup (adjust based on your spice preference)
  • Fish sauce – 1⁄4 cup (alternatively, you can use salted shrimp paste for a deeper flavor)
  • Garlic cloves – 10 cloves, minced or finely grated
  • Fresh ginger – 1 tablespoon, grated
  • Korean rice syrup (or honey) – 2 tablespoons
  • Chives or green onions – 1 cup, chopped into 1-inch pieces

Optional Fermenting Enhancements:

  • Glutinous rice flour (sweet rice flour) – 2 tablespoons (for thickening the seasoning paste)
  • Water – 1⁄2 cup (to create a smooth paste)

Step-by-Step Instructions

1. Prep the Radish

Start by thoroughly washing the Korean radish or daikon radish to remove any dirt from the skin. There’s no need to peel the radish if the skin is clean and in good condition, as it adds a nice earthy flavor. However, feel free to peel it if you prefer a milder taste.

Cut the radish into evenly sized cubes, about 3/4 to 1 inch in size. Consistent sizing ensures even fermentation and a better texture. Place the cubed radishes into a large mixing bowl.

2. Salt the Radish

Sprinkle the kosher salt evenly over the radish cubes. Toss them gently to coat each piece. Salting the radish will help draw out moisture and allow the seasoning paste to adhere better.

Let the salted radish sit for about 30-40 minutes. As it rests, you’ll notice the radish releasing water. After the time is up, drain the excess liquid, but do not rinse the radish. Save a little of the brine in case you need it later to adjust the seasoning paste.

3. Make the Seasoning Paste

While the radish is resting, prepare the rich, spicy paste that will coat the cubes. If using glutinous rice flour for thickening, combine it with 1⁄2 cup of water in a small saucepan. Cook over medium heat, stirring constantly, until it thickens into a smooth, sticky paste. Let it cool to room temperature.

In a separate large mixing bowl, combine the cooled rice flour paste (if using), red pepper flakes (gochugaru), fish sauce, minced garlic, grated ginger, rice syrup (or honey), and sugar (if desired). Stir everything together until it forms a thick, vibrant red paste. Taste the paste and adjust as needed — if it’s too spicy, add more rice syrup or sugar to mellow it out, or add an extra splash of fish sauce for more umami.

4. Add Chives or Green Onions

Mix the chopped chives or green onions into the seasoning paste. These add a pop of freshness and an extra layer of flavor to the dish. Make sure the vegetables are well-coated in the paste before combining with the radish.

5. Coat the Radish Cubes

Once the radishes are finished salting and prepped, add them into the bowl with the seasoning paste. Using clean hands or a large spoon, mix everything together thoroughly. Be gentle but firm to ensure every radish cube is evenly coated with the paste. It’s helpful to wear food-safe gloves, as the red pepper flakes can stain your hands and nails.

6. Pack Into Containers

Transfer the seasoned radish cubes into a clean, airtight glass or plastic container, leaving a little room at the top for the mixture to expand as it ferments. Press the cubes down gently to remove any air pockets.

If you reserved any radish brine earlier, you can pour a small amount over the top to ensure the radishes are submerged in liquid, which prevents spoilage during fermentation.

7. Ferment and Store

Seal the container tightly and leave it at room temperature for 1-2 days to kickstart the fermentation process. The exact time will depend on the temperature of your kitchen; warmer environments will speed up fermentation, while cooler ones will slow it down.

After the initial fermentation, move the container to the refrigerator to slow the process and preserve the flavor. The radish will continue to ferment slowly and develop deeper, tangier notes over time.

8. Serve and Enjoy

Kkakdugi is ready to eat after the first day of fermentation, but it tastes best once the flavors have had a chance to meld together, usually after 5-7 days in the refrigerator. Serve cold as a crunchy, spicy side dish alongside bowls of steamed rice, warm soups like seolleongtang (ox bone soup), or hearty stews such as kimchi jjigae.

Tips and Tricks for Perfect Kkakdugi

  • Choose Fresh Radishes: Fresh, firm Korean radishes or daikon yield the best texture. Avoid radishes that are soft or have blemishes, as they can lead to mushy kkakdugi.
  • Control the Spice Level: Adjust the red pepper flakes to your heat tolerance. Start with a smaller amount if you’re unsure, and add more gradually.
  • Monitor Fermentation: Taste your kkakdugi as it ferments to find your preferred level of tanginess. Some people enjoy it lightly fermented, while others savor the intense flavors of a longer fermentation.
  • Experiment with Variations: While this recipe sticks to the traditional method, feel free to adapt it to your liking. Add shredded carrots for color, pear for sweetness, or even a touch of sesame oil for nuttiness.

Health Benefits of Kkakdugi

Kkakdugi isn’t just delicious—it’s also incredibly healthy. Thanks to the fermentation process, it’s rich in probiotics, which promote gut health and support digestion. Radishes are naturally low in calories and high in vitamins, especially vitamin C and potassium. The seasoning paste, made with garlic and ginger, further boosts the dish’s anti-inflammatory and immune-supporting properties.

Final Thoughts

Korean Spicy Radish Salad, or Kkakdugi, is a staple in traditional Korean cuisine and a versatile side dish that complements countless meals. By following this easy recipe, you can recreate the unique, bold flavors of Korean cooking right at home. Whether served fresh or fermented to perfection, Kkakdugi offers a delicious way to explore the art of Korean fermentation and elevate your dining experience. So grab your apron, gather your ingredients, and immerse yourself in the joy of making this iconic dish. Happy cooking!
