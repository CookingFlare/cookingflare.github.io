Canning Whole Tomatoes: A Step-by-Step Recipe Guide


Preserving fresh ingredients is a rewarding way to enjoy seasonal produce throughout the year. Among the many fruits and vegetables that beg to be preserved, tomatoes often top the list because of their versatility in countless recipes. From hearty soups and sauces to stews and casseroles, canned whole tomatoes are a pantry staple that adds flavor and vibrance to any dish. Let’s delve into a straightforward, step-by-step guide for canning whole tomatoes at home, so you can savor the goodness of summer even in the depths of winter.

Why Can Whole Tomatoes?

Canning whole tomatoes is an excellent way to capture the peak flavor and nutritional density of ripe tomatoes. Unlike store-bought cans, homemade preserved tomatoes allow you to control every aspect of their preparation—no additives, no overly processed flavors, just pure, quality produce sealed for longevity. Additionally, the canning process helps reduce food waste, saves money, and encourages sustainable living.

Ready to stock up your pantry? Follow this detailed guide, and soon you’ll have jars of whole tomatoes packed with the taste of summer.

Preparing to Can: Essential Tools and Ingredients

Before you begin the canning process, it’s crucial to gather the necessary tools and ingredients. Proper preparation will streamline the process and make for hassle-free results.

Tools You’ll Need:

  • Large Pot or Canner: Used for processing jars in boiling water.
  • Glass Mason Jars: Typically pint or quart-sized jars work best.
  • Lids and Rings: Ensure these are sterile and fit your jars properly.
  • Jar Lifter: Helps securely lift hot jars from boiling water.
  • Large Bowl: For blanching the tomatoes during peeling.
  • Sharp Knife: Necessary for scoring and coring the tomatoes.
  • Clean Towels: To wipe jars before sealing.
  • Funnel (optional): Helps transfer liquid or tomatoes into jars cleanly.

Ingredients You’ll Need:

  • Fresh Tomatoes: Opt for firm, ripe varieties like Roma, San Marzano, or Beefsteak.
  • Lemon Juice (or Citric Acid): Essential for maintaining acidity levels, which ensures food safety during storage.
  • Water: For blanching tomatoes and heating jars in the canner.
  • Optional Additions: Salt or herbs (though this is not required, it can enhance flavor).

Step-by-Step Guide to Canning Whole Tomatoes

Step 1: Select and Prepare Your Tomatoes

The first step in canning whole tomatoes starts with choosing the right produce. Select tomatoes that are ripe, firm, and free of blemishes or bruises. Wash them thoroughly under running water to remove dirt or debris.

Step 2: Sterilize Your Jars and Equipment

Proper sterilization is the cornerstone of successful canning. Place your mason jars, lids, and rings in a pot of boiling water or dishwasher at high heat. Allow them to dry completely on a clean towel, ensuring they remain sterile.

Step 3: Peel the Tomatoes

To peel your tomatoes, bring a large pot of water to a rolling boil. While waiting, fill a separate bowl with ice water. Using a sharp knife, score the bottom of each tomato with a small “X.” Dunk the tomatoes into boiling water for about 30 seconds, then quickly transfer them to the ice water. The skins should loosen easily—slip them off and discard them.

Step 4: Prep for Canning

Once peeled, you can decide whether to core your tomatoes. Coring isn’t necessary but can make them easier to use in cooking later. If you choose to core them, use a knife to remove the tough center.

Step 5: Add Lemon Juice or Citric Acid

Adding acidity is a vital step to prevent bacterial growth. Add two tablespoons of bottled lemon juice (or ½ teaspoon of citric acid) to each quart jar. For pint jars, use half these quantities—one tablespoon lemon juice or ¼ teaspoon citric acid.

Step 6: Pack the Tomatoes into Jars

Place the whole tomatoes into the jars, packing them snugly but without crushing. You can fill in gaps by adding tomato “chunks” or slices as needed. Pour boiling water over the tomatoes until jars are filled to within ½ inch of the rim. If desired, sprinkle a pinch of salt into each jar for flavor.

Pro Tip:

Make sure to remove all air bubbles from the jar before sealing. A non-metallic utensil, like a plastic spatula, is perfect for sliding alongside the tomatoes in the jar to release trapped air.

Step 7: Seal Jars and Process

Wipe the rims of the jars with a clean, damp cloth to remove residue. Place the sterilized lids and rings on each jar, twisting until fingertip-tight.

Carefully lower the sealed jars into a large pot or canner filled with boiling water. Ensure the jars are fully submerged, with at least one to two inches of water above the tops. Process pint jars for 40 minutes and quart jars for 45 minutes. Use a jar lifter to remove them once processing is complete.

Step 8: Cool and Check Seals

Transfer the hot jars onto a towel-lined counter, allowing them to cool undisturbed for 12–24 hours. As they cool, you should hear the characteristic “pop” of each lid sealing. Check the seals by pressing the center of each lid—if it doesn’t flex, your jar is sealed properly.

Step 9: Store Your Tomatoes

Label your jars with the date, and store them in a cool, dark place like a pantry or cellar. Properly processed and sealed jars can last up to a year.

Tips for Perfectly Canned Tomatoes

  • Use the Freshest Tomatoes: The quality of your canned tomatoes directly reflects the quality of your raw produce. Always opt for peak-season tomatoes for the best flavor.
  • Avoid Adding Too Many Extras: While herbs and spices are tempting additions, stick to pure tomatoes for versatile use. You can always add seasonings later when cooking.
  • Double-Check Safety Guidelines: Canning relies on correct acid levels to prevent spoilage. Follow acidity requirements strictly to ensure safety.

Creative Ways to Use Home-Canned Tomatoes

Now that you’ve successfully canned whole tomatoes, you can use them to elevate your recipes with a burst of vibrant flavor. Here are some ideas for incorporating your preserved tomatoes into dishes:

  1. Homemade Marinara Sauce: Simmer whole tomatoes with garlic, olive oil, and fresh basil for a simple yet robust pasta sauce.
  2. Rustic Tomato Soup: Puree the canned tomatoes with a splash of cream and serve alongside crusty bread for a comforting meal.
  3. Slow-Cooked Chili: Add your canned tomatoes to chili for a rich, tangy base that complements beans, spices, and ground meat.
  4. Shakshuka: Use your tomatoes as the base for a hearty dish of poached eggs, chili, and peppers.

Final Thoughts

Canning whole tomatoes is a timeless skill that brings both satisfaction and culinary convenience. By following this step-by-step guide, you can preserve your favorite tomatoes and enjoy their flavors long after the harvest season has ended. With jars of vibrant tomatoes perched neatly in your pantry, you’re only ever a few moments away from whipping up a delicious, tomato-infused dish.

Roll up your sleeves, gather your supplies, and make the most of tomatoes in their prime. Once you’ve mastered this process, you’ll wonder why you didn’t start canning sooner!
