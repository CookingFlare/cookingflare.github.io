How to Preserve Fall Apples Through Canning


When fall arrives, apple orchards are filled with ripe, juicy apples ready to be harvested. While you can enjoy these delicious fruits in their prime, preserving them through canning allows you to savor their flavor long after the season ends. Canning fall apples is a fantastic way to make apple preserves, sauces, and pie fillings that can brighten up any meal throughout the year. In this guide, we’ll walk you through the process of preserving apples through canning, offering helpful tips and tricks to ensure your apple preserves remain fresh and flavorful.

Why Preserve Apples Through Canning?

Canning is one of the most effective methods for preserving apples because it keeps their texture, flavor, and nutrients intact for months. By canning apples, you can enjoy homemade apple sauce, apple pie filling, apple butter, and other apple-based treats without worrying about spoilage. Canning also allows you to take advantage of seasonal apple prices, ensuring you can enjoy fresh apple products throughout the year, even when apples are out of season.

Choosing the Right Apples for Canning

The first step in any apple canning project is selecting the right variety of apples. Not all apples are ideal for preserving, so it’s important to choose apples that maintain their shape, texture, and flavor when canned. Here are some of the best apple varieties for canning:

1. Granny Smith Apples

Granny Smith apples are tart and firm, making them a great choice for apple pies, apple sauce, and preserves. They hold their shape well during the canning process and balance out sweetness when mixed with other varieties.

2. Honeycrisp Apples

Honeycrisp apples are sweet and juicy, providing a perfect balance of sweetness and tartness. These apples are great for making apple sauce, preserves, or jams, as they break down nicely while still retaining their flavor.

3. Jonathan Apples

Jonathans are known for their balance of sweet and tart flavors. These apples are versatile and are great for pie fillings, preserves, or canning whole, as they maintain their texture even after processing.

4. Fuji Apples

Fuji apples are sweet, crisp, and juicy, making them ideal for applesauce and jams. Their high sugar content also makes them great for creating sweeter preserves or apple butter.

5. Golden Delicious Apples

Golden Delicious apples are mild, sweet, and tender. They’re perfect for canning into sauces and jams, though they may not hold their shape as well as firmer varieties like Granny Smith or Jonathan.

While these are some of the best apples for canning, you can experiment with other varieties depending on the flavor profile and texture you’re looking for in your preserves.

Preparing Apples for Canning

Before you can begin the canning process, you’ll need to prepare the apples. Proper preparation ensures that your canned apples stay fresh and flavorful.

Step 1: Wash the Apples

Start by thoroughly washing the apples to remove any dirt, wax, or pesticides. If you’re using organic apples, be sure to clean them well to ensure no residues are left behind.

Step 2: Peel and Core the Apples

Peel the apples using a vegetable peeler or a paring knife. Removing the peel helps to create a smoother texture in your canned apple products. After peeling, core the apples to remove the seeds and the fibrous center.

If you plan to preserve the apples whole, leave the skins on, but remember to score a small X at the bottom of each apple to prevent them from bursting during the canning process.

Step 3: Cut the Apples into Desired Sizes

For apple pie filling or chunks, cut the apples into slices or wedges. For apple sauce or puree, chop the apples into smaller pieces to help them cook down more quickly. The size and shape of the apples will depend on the recipe you are following.

Step 4: Prevent Browning

To prevent your peeled and cut apples from browning, soak them in a mixture of water and lemon juice or a fruit preservative solution. This will help to preserve their vibrant color and fresh appearance throughout the canning process.

The Canning Process: Step-by-Step Guide

Once you’ve prepared your apples, it’s time to start the canning process. Here is a basic guide for preserving apples through canning.

What You’ll Need:

  • Jars: Use canning jars specifically designed for preserving foods. They come in various sizes, including pint and quart jars.
  • Lids and Bands: You will need new lids and bands for each jar you’re canning. Ensure the lids are sealed properly to avoid contamination.
  • A Canning Pot or Water Bath Canner: A large pot with a rack for processing the jars is essential for the water bath canning method.
  • A Jar Lifter: A jar lifter makes it easier and safer to handle hot jars.
  • A Funnel: A wide-mouth funnel helps prevent spills when transferring the apples into jars.
  • A Ladle: For pouring liquids into jars without making a mess.
  • A Canning Tool Kit: Optional, but helpful for measuring headspace and handling jars.

Step 1: Sterilize the Jars

Before beginning the canning process, sterilize your canning jars to prevent bacterial contamination. You can do this by placing them in a large pot of boiling water for 10 minutes. Alternatively, place the jars in the oven at 200°F (93°C) for 10-15 minutes to sterilize them.

Step 2: Cook the Apples

For apple sauce, pie filling, or preserves, cook the apples before placing them in the jars. You can use a large pot or slow cooker for this step. Add sugar, cinnamon, lemon juice, or other flavorings based on your recipe. Simmer the apples until they’re tender and have broken down to your desired consistency.

  • For apple sauce: Cook the apples until soft, then mash or blend them into a smooth texture. If you want chunky applesauce, leave some pieces intact.
  • For apple pie filling: Add spices like cinnamon, nutmeg, and allspice, along with sugar and a bit of cornstarch for thickening. Cook until the apples have softened but still maintain their shape.
  • For preserves or jam: Add sugar and pectin to help the fruit set. Follow your jam recipe for specific amounts of sugar and pectin.

Step 3: Pack the Apples into Jars

Once your apples are cooked, use a ladle and a funnel to pack the apples into your sterilized jars. Leave about ½ inch of headspace from the top of the jar to allow for expansion during the canning process.

Step 4: Seal the Jars

Wipe the rims of the jars with a clean, damp cloth to remove any food residue. This ensures that the lids seal properly. Place the lids on the jars and screw on the metal bands, tightening them just until they’re secure, but not too tight.

Step 5: Process the Jars in a Water Bath

Place the filled jars into your canning pot or water bath canner. Make sure the jars are covered with at least 1-2 inches of water. Bring the water to a boil and process the jars for the appropriate amount of time, typically 20-30 minutes for fruit products like apples. The exact processing time will depend on your altitude and the type of apples you’re canning.

Step 6: Remove and Cool the Jars

After processing, carefully use a jar lifter to remove the jars from the boiling water and place them on a towel or cooling rack. Allow the jars to cool completely. As they cool, you should hear a “pop” sound, indicating that the jars have sealed properly.

Step 7: Check the Seal

Once the jars have cooled, check the seals. Press down in the center of each lid—if it doesn’t pop back, the jar is sealed correctly. If any jars didn’t seal properly, refrigerate them and use them within a few days. Unsealed jars can be reprocessed or stored in the fridge and used immediately.

Storing Your Canned Apples

After your jars are sealed, label them with the date and contents. Store them in a cool, dark, and dry place, such as a pantry or cupboard. Properly canned apples can last for up to a year, though they may lose some quality over time. Always check for signs of spoilage, such as a broken seal, before using.

Tips for Successful Canning

  • Use fresh, ripe apples: Canning works best with fresh, undamaged apples that are in their prime.
  • Don’t overfill the jars: Leave enough headspace to allow the food to expand during processing.
  • Process at the right temperature: Ensure that the water bath is boiling before placing the jars in and maintain that temperature throughout the processing time.
  • Don’t rush the cooling process: Let the jars cool completely before storing them.

Conclusion

Canning apples is a great way to preserve the harvest and enjoy the flavors of fall all year long. Whether you’re making apple sauce, preserves, or pie filling, the canning process allows you to extend the shelf life of fresh apples while maintaining their flavor and texture. By following these simple steps, you’ll be able to create delicious, homemade apple products that bring a taste of autumn into your home, no matter the season.

Happy canning, and enjoy the fruits of your labor all year long!
