Food Photography Styling: How to Play with Shapes and Sizes


When it comes to food photography, the visual appeal of the dish plays a crucial role in capturing the audience’s attention. While colors, lighting, and props are essential, one often overlooked but powerful tool in food styling is the manipulation of shapes and sizes. Playing with the dimensions of your food and the overall composition can dramatically enhance the impact of your photographs. Whether you’re photographing a simple home-cooked meal or a gourmet creation, the way you use shapes and sizes in your shot can make all the difference in the final image.

In this post, we’ll dive deep into how you can creatively incorporate shapes and sizes in your food styling to elevate your food photography. From understanding how different shapes can influence the perception of your food to playing with sizes for balance and composition, we’ll explore how to master this element of food styling.

1. Understanding the Importance of Shapes in Food Photography

Shapes are one of the first things that viewers notice when they look at food photography. The human brain is hardwired to process shapes before colors or textures. The shapes you use in your photos can give a sense of order, symmetry, or even chaos, depending on how they are applied. In food photography, every shape tells a story. Let’s explore how different shapes affect the visual impact of a photograph:

Geometric Shapes for Structure

Straight lines and angles are a classic way to bring order to a photo. The clean lines of rectangular plates, square glasses, or triangular elements create a sense of structure and neatness. Geometric shapes like squares, triangles, and rectangles offer a modern and organized feel to the image, perfect for dishes that need to convey precision and sophistication.

For example, a minimalist dish served on a square plate creates sharp angles that visually guide the viewer’s eye. The rigid structure gives the meal a sense of refinement and allows every component of the dish to be appreciated individually.

Curves for Softness

In contrast to geometric shapes, curved lines evoke a sense of fluidity and softness. Round plates, bowls, and utensils introduce a gentler vibe to the image, which works wonderfully with organic or rustic dishes. Curves can be used to suggest comfort and warmth, which is why they’re often used in photographs of dishes like soups, salads, or desserts.

For example, the curves of a deep bowl filled with pasta can create an inviting visual flow, enhancing the perception of depth and warmth in the image. Similarly, a round dessert like a cupcake or a pie placed in a curving composition invites the viewer to experience the food as a comforting indulgence.

Abstract and Organic Shapes

Abstract and organic shapes are commonly used when you want to convey a sense of freeform beauty or whimsy in your food photography. These shapes often mimic nature’s forms, and they can be used to create an artistic and dynamic composition. Think of the way fruits, vegetables, or sauces spill and pour across a plate — the organic splatter of food can be captured in ways that are visually striking and fresh.

The natural shapes of ingredients like leafy greens, fruit wedges, or splashes of sauce lend a playful feel to your photos. For example, the curving lines of a leaf falling onto a dish or the abstract splatter of sauce on a plate can enhance the mood of the photo, offering a feeling of spontaneity and excitement.

2. Playing with Sizes for Dynamic Composition

Once you’ve chosen your shapes, it’s time to consider the role size plays in your food styling. The size of your food components and props can completely alter the visual balance and mood of a shot. Playing with proportions in your composition can either enhance the main dish or create an interesting focal point that adds intrigue to the photograph.

Large Food Elements to Create Focus

One of the most common techniques in food photography is to use larger elements to draw the eye to a central point of interest. Large elements—such as an oversized burger, a towering stack of pancakes, or a massive piece of cake—create an immediate focal point and help to tell the story of indulgence, abundance, and pleasure.

Large food items can also create a sense of drama and scale in your image. For instance, a close-up shot of a giant lobster tail or a bowl of pasta can emphasize the texture and detail of the food while making it the undeniable star of the photograph. This type of food styling works especially well for dishes that are meant to be hearty, bold, and unapologetically indulgent.

Small Food Elements for Delicate Appeal

On the other hand, small food elements can be used to evoke a sense of delicacy, finesse, and attention to detail. Miniature desserts, bite-sized appetizers, or delicate garnishes placed strategically on the plate can add an elegant touch to your food photography.

Incorporating small elements into your food styling can also help with storytelling. For example, a tiny macaroon placed delicately on the edge of a cup or a small portion of sauce placed in an elegant spoon can give the viewer the sense that they’re about to experience something rare or special. This can enhance the appeal of fine dining, upscale desserts, and anything that emphasizes careful craftsmanship.

Mixing Large and Small for Contrast

When you combine both large and small elements in the same photograph, you create a contrast that adds visual interest and tension. For example, a large, hearty bowl of soup might be accompanied by a few small slices of bread or small appetizers, bringing balance to the image. The eye moves between the two, and the varied sizes in the composition help break up the monotony of the shot.

The contrast between sizes is a great way to lead the viewer’s eye and create depth in the photograph. By using various sizes, you can guide the viewer to notice different aspects of the dish, from the larger main component to the smaller garnishes or sides.

3. Layering Shapes and Sizes to Build Depth

Layering is a key technique when it comes to playing with shapes and sizes. Layering adds dimension and depth to your food photos, transforming a flat scene into a more visually engaging one. There are several ways to layer shapes and sizes:

Vertical Layering

Vertical layering works exceptionally well in compositions where height is involved. A towering stack of pancakes, a stacked burger, or a multicolored salad layered in a tall glass can create an interesting focal point by introducing the third dimension. Vertical height adds grandeur and excitement to the image, making the dish appear more substantial.

Horizontal Layering

Horizontal layering involves stacking components side by side to create depth. This method is often used for dishes like platters, pizzas, or bento boxes. The use of horizontal layers can guide the viewer’s eye from one side of the image to the other, creating movement within the frame.

For example, a beautifully plated charcuterie board filled with various sizes of cheese wedges, cured meats, and fruits creates a sense of harmony through its horizontal arrangement. By balancing different shapes and sizes in a horizontal layer, you create a dynamic yet cohesive photograph.

Layering with Negative Space

Negative space, or the empty areas surrounding the subject, plays an important role in food styling. Layering shapes and sizes with a deliberate use of negative space can bring attention to the food itself while avoiding clutter. By allowing certain elements to “breathe” in the composition, you create a balance that doesn’t overwhelm the viewer.

For example, a single large element like a loaf of bread placed off-center with plenty of space around it allows the bread to stand out without distraction. Similarly, a simple, elegantly plated dish surrounded by empty space can highlight the meticulous nature of the food, giving it a refined, minimalist look.

4. Using Props to Enhance Shapes and Sizes

Props play a supporting role in food styling by helping to emphasize the shapes and sizes of the main food elements. Consider using plates, bowls, utensils, napkins, and even the surrounding environment to enhance the food’s appearance.

Matching Prop Shapes with Food Shapes

One of the most effective ways to complement your food styling is to match the shapes of the props with the food. For example, a round dessert served in a round bowl or a square piece of cake on a square plate can create a sense of unity. This subtle connection between the shapes of the food and the props helps bring a cohesive, well-thought-out look to the photograph.

Alternatively, you can mix contrasting shapes for a more dynamic effect. A round dish served on a square plate or an asymmetrical bowl placed next to a perfectly shaped food item can create a striking contrast that grabs attention.

Size and Scale of Props

The size of your props can also influence the overall feel of the image. Larger props like oversized plates or vintage teacups can make the food appear more delicate or intricate, while smaller props can bring a cozy, intimate feel to the scene. The choice of props depends on the type of dish you’re photographing and the mood you want to evoke.

Conclusion

Food photography is all about creativity and capturing the essence of a dish in a visually compelling way. By carefully playing with shapes and sizes, you can manipulate the composition to tell a more powerful story, evoke certain emotions, and highlight the unique features of your food. Whether you opt for geometric precision, soft curves, or a mix of both, remember that each shape and size in your composition has a purpose. By layering, contrasting, and using props thoughtfully, you can elevate your food photography to new heights.

The next time you photograph food, pay attention to how you can use shapes and sizes to create depth, balance, and intrigue. By experimenting with different proportions, you’ll quickly discover how powerful these elements can be in transforming your food photos from ordinary to extraordinary. Happy styling!
