Lost in China: Disappearing Chinese Culinary Delights and their Recipes That You Should Try!


China, a vast land with a rich and ancient culture, is home to a myriad of exquisite cuisines that date back thousands of years. Today, however, some age-old culinary gems are disappearing as the landscape of Chinese food rapidly evolves. To preserve these disappearing dishes and keep their spirit alive, let us explore the traditions behind them and recreate these delicious delicacies in our own kitchens.

#1: Beggar’s Chicken

This classic dish from Guangdong province is shrouded in mystery. Legend has it that many years ago, beggars would cook wild chickens and sell them to passersby. As their customers often inquired about the flavors, the beggars would concoct inventive stories. Realizing the power of storytelling, they began attaching small pieces of paper to the chickens, containing the fabricated tales. Beggar’s Chicken traditionally bakes in lotus leaves and clay, infusing the bird with a fragrant, aromatic flavor.

Recipe:

Ingredients:

  • 1 whole chicken (approximately 1.5-2 kg)
  • 4 slices ginger
  • 2 scallions, cut into lengths
  • 2 tablespoons Shaoxing wine
  • 1 teaspoon salt
  • 1 teaspoon sugar
  • 1/4 cup vegetable oil
  • 1 bunch flowering Chinese celery (optional)
  • 1 lotus leaf
  • Red clay
  • String

Instructions:

  1. Clean and pat dry the chicken. Stuff it with ginger, scallions, and flowers from flowering Chinese celery (if available). Pour Shaoxing wine, salt, and sugar inside the cavity.
  2. Truss the chicken and clean the cavity thoroughly to remove any traces of feathers or pin feathers.
  3. Wash and dry the lotus leaf.
  4. Rub the chicken’s skin all over with vegetable oil.
  5. Carefully wrap the chicken in lotus leaves, ensuring it’s tightly packed. Fold the edges and tie securely with string to prevent the filling from spilling out during cooking.
  6. Wrap the clay-lined, oil-coated lotus leaf package in a layer of red clay.
  7. Preheat the oven to 425°F (220°C). Place the clay-encased chicken in a roasting pan and bake for 1.5 to 2 hours until the clay turns brown and the chicken is fully cooked.
  8. Carefully unwrap the clay, lotus leaf, and remove the string from the chicken, then let it cool for 10-15 minutes before carving.
  9. Serve with a side of steamed rice.

#2: Twice-Cooked Pork

This dish, originating from the Sichuan province, is a testament to the ancient Chinese cooking method that combines stir-frying a protein with braising it to achieve a scrumptious, tender texture. Also known as “Guobaorou,” the name Twice-Cooked Pork cleverly describes the process it undergoes.

Recipe:

Ingredients:

  • 1 pound pork belly
  • 1 teaspoon salt
  • 1/4 teaspoon sugar
  • 2 teaspoons cornstarch
  • 2 tablespoons vegetable oil
  • 1 scallion, thinly sliced
  • 1 tablespoon Sichuan peppercorn (ground)
  • 2-3 red dried chilies
  • 1 teaspoon Chinkiang vinegar
  • 1 teaspoon Asian sesame oil
  • 1 tablespoon soy sauce

Instructions:

  1. Cut pork belly into thin strips. Mix the salt, sugar, and cornstarch in a bowl and coat the pork strips.
  2. Heat vegetable oil in a pan over medium heat, then place the pork strips in the pan, and shallow-fry until golden brown. Set the pork aside, and strain any excess oil.
  3. In the same pan, add scallion and Sichuan peppercorn, stirring until aromatic.
  4. Add the pre-fried pork and dried chilies to the pan, and braise over low heat for about 20 minutes, until the pork is tender.
  5. Add Chinkiang vinegar, sesame oil, and soy sauce, stirring well to combine.
  6. Serve hot with a side of rice or noodles.

#3: Yunnan Wild Mushroom Soup

The diverse terrain of Yunnan province is abundant in a wide range of wild mushrooms. However, due to the environmental concerns and increased risk of toxins, consuming wild mushrooms without proper identification is strongly discouraged. This Yunnan Wild Mushroom Soup recipe uses a combination of readily available dried mushrooms and fresh shiitake mushrooms.

Recipe:

Ingredients:

  • 2 cups water
  • 1/4 cup dried mushrooms (a mix of black, shiitake, oyster, or porcini)
  • 1 cup fresh shiitake mushrooms, stemmed and sliced
  • 1 tablespoon vegetable oil
  • 1 yellow onion, diced
  • 2 cloves garlic, minced
  • 2 cups vegetable or chicken broth
  • 1/2 cup heavy cream
  • 1/4 teaspoon white pepper
  • Salt, to taste
  • Chopped scallions and cilantro, for garnish
  • Croutons, for serving (optional)

Instructions:

  1. In a large saucepan, combine water and dried mushrooms. Bring the mixture to a boil and then lower the heat to a simmer. Let the mushrooms steep for 30 minutes, or until fully rehydrated.
  2. Remove the mushrooms and strain the liquid into the saucepan. Rinse the mushrooms and chop roughly before returning them to the saucepan.
  3. Add fresh shiitake mushrooms, vegetable oil, and onion to the saucepan, and cook until the onions are soft and translucent.
  4. Stir in the garlic and cook for 1 more minute, then add the broth, cream, and white pepper. Bring the soup to a boil, then reduce the heat to low and simmer for 15-20 minutes.
  5. Season the soup with salt and pepper before serving, garnished with chopped scallions and cilantro. For added crunch, sprinkle some croutons over the top.

As Chinese cuisine continues to evolve, it’s crucial to embrace and appreciate the illustrious culinary gems Chinese culture has to offer. By keeping these recipes alive, we ensure that these disappearing dishes continue to entice taste buds and spark curiosity about the fascinating world of Chinese cuisine.
