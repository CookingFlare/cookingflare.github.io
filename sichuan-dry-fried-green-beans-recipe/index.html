Sichuan Dry-Fried Green Beans Recipe


Sichuan cuisine, celebrated for its bold flavors, vibrant dishes, and liberal use of spices, brings us one of its most beloved vegetable creations—Dry-Fried Green Beans. This recipe is a delightful combination of crunch, heat, and savory notes, making it an irresistible side dish or even a main attraction for anyone who loves a touch of spice. With its rich umami flavor and satisfying texture, it’s no wonder that this dish has made its way to menus and dinner tables far beyond Sichuan province.

In today’s post, we’ll unlock the secrets behind making authentic Sichuan Dry-Fried Green Beans in your own kitchen. This step-by-step recipe is easy to follow, and you’ll be left with a dish bursting with caramelized flavors, aromatic spices, and that signature “wok hei” that elevates every bite.

What Makes Dry-Fried Green Beans Special?

Unlike other stir-fried vegetable dishes, dry-frying involves using a hot wok and minimal oil to blister and dehydrate the surface of the green beans. This cooking method not only intensifies their natural flavor but also creates a crisp, slightly charred texture that’s absolutely addictive. Once the beans are meticulously prepared, they’re tossed with fragrant ingredients like garlic, ginger, chili paste, and dried Sichuan peppercorns for that essential kick. The addition of ground pork or fermented pickles often rounds out the flavor profile, but this recipe is easily adaptable for vegetarians.

Ingredients You’ll Need

For Sichuan Dry-Fried Green Beans, quality ingredients and preparation make all the difference. Gather the following components for the recipe:

Green Beans & Main Ingredients:

  • Fresh green beans – 1 lb (trimmed and washed)
  • Neutral oil (such as vegetable or peanut oil) – 2–3 tablespoons
  • Ground pork (optional) – 2 oz (omit for vegetarian version)
  • Pickled mustard greens or preserved black beans – 2 tablespoons (fermented soybeans also work well)

Aromatics & Seasonings:

  • Fresh ginger – 1-inch piece, minced
  • Garlic – 3–4 cloves, minced
  • Dried red chilies – 3–4, sliced (adjust to taste)
  • Sichuan peppercorns – 1 teaspoon (adds a numbing heat)
  • Doubanjiang (Sichuan chili bean paste) – 1 tablespoon (key to authentic flavor)
  • Shaoxing wine – 1 tablespoon
  • Soy sauce – 1 tablespoon
  • Sugar – ½ teaspoon
  • Salt – to taste
  • Sesame oil – 1 teaspoon (optional, for fragrance)

Step-by-Step Cooking Instructions

Step 1: Prepare the Green Beans

The first step for perfect Sichuan Dry-Fried Green Beans is prepping the beans properly. Ensure they are washed and thoroughly dried—wet green beans will steam instead of blister. Trim off the ends if needed. Having dry green beans is a key factor in achieving that signature crispy finish.

Step 2: Heat Your Wok

Place your wok over high heat. Proper preheating is essential for “wok hei,” the smoky and aromatic quality achieved by cooking with intense heat. Once the wok is hot, add 2–3 tablespoons of neutral oil, swirling it around to coat the surface.

Step 3: Blister the Green Beans

Add the green beans to the hot wok, spreading them out to cover the full surface area. Stir frequently but allow moments of resting to let the beans char slightly. You’ll notice the skins starting to wrinkle and blister—this is precisely what you’re looking for. Cook for about 6–8 minutes until the beans are tender but maintain their crunch.

Remove the green beans and set them aside on a plate lined with paper towels to drain excess oil.

Step 4: Cook the Aromatics

Reduce the heat to medium, leaving a small amount of oil in the wok. Toss in the minced ginger, garlic, Sichuan peppercorns, and dried chilies. Stir-fry for 30–60 seconds until the aromatics are fragrant but not burnt. This foundation will build the flavor layers for your dish.

Step 5: Add Pork and Pickled Ingredients (Optional)

If using ground pork, add it to the wok and stir-fry thoroughly until browned and fully cooked. Break up any large chunks with your spatula. For extra umami, toss in the preserved pickled mustard greens or black beans, stirring for another minute to allow their flavors to meld.

Step 6: Incorporate Doubanjiang and Seasonings

To tie everything together, push the cooked aromatics and pork to the side of the wok, creating space for the chili bean paste. Stir-fry the paste for a few seconds to awaken its flavors before combining it with the rest of the ingredients.

Pour in the Shaoxing wine, soy sauce, and a sprinkle of sugar. This combination balances out the dish, adding depth and a mix of salty, spicy, and slightly sweet notes.

Step 7: Toss the Green Beans

Return the blistered green beans to the wok, mixing them with the aromatic base. Allow the sauce to coat the beans evenly, stirring for 2–3 minutes.

Taste and adjust the seasoning as needed. A pinch of salt or a dash of soy sauce can enhance the flavor if necessary.

Pro Tips for the Perfect Dry-Fried Green Beans

  1. Work in Small Batches: If you’re making a larger portion, blister the green beans in batches to ensure even cooking and avoid steaming.

  2. Doubanjiang is Essential: Authentic Sichuan Dry-Fried Green Beans depend greatly on the unique flavor of doubanjiang. If you don’t have it, consider substituting with a mix of chili paste and fermented soy sauce, but the result won’t be quite the same.

  3. Vegetarian Variations: Skip the ground pork and add mushrooms like finely chopped shiitakes or cremini for an earthy twist. Tofu crumbles or tempeh can also be great protein replacements.

  4. Choose Fresh Ingredients: Fresh green beans and quality spices will make your dish stand out—and don’t forget to check the freshness of your Sichuan peppercorns for maximum flavor impact.

Serving Suggestions

Sichuan Dry-Fried Green Beans are a versatile dish that pairs beautifully with rice, noodles, or an assortment of other traditional Chinese dishes. Serve it as a side dish alongside Mapo Tofu, Gong Bao Chicken, or stir-fried noodles for a complete Sichuan-inspired feast. You can also enjoy it solo as a vegetarian main course topped with steamed jasmine rice.

Storing and Reheating

This dish is best enjoyed fresh when the blistered beans retain their crunch, but leftovers can be stored in an airtight container in the refrigerator for up to 2 days. To reheat, use a hot wok or skillet to revive the flavors instead of microwaving, which can make the beans soggy.

A Culinary Adventure Worth Savoring

Mastering Sichuan Dry-Fried Green Beans is not only an exciting kitchen adventure but also an opportunity to explore one of China’s most celebrated regional cuisines. With its irresistible combination of spicy, savory, and aromatic flavors, this recipe is sure to impress your family and friends. Whether you’re a seasoned fan of Sichuan cuisine or someone looking to venture into new culinary territory, this dish is a must-try for anyone who appreciates bold, unforgettable flavors.

Give this recipe a shot, and let the authentic flavors of Sichuan transform your dining table into a spice-filled culinary journey! Now, grab your wok and get cooking—you’ll never look at green beans the same way again.
