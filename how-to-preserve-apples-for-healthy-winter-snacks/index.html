How to Preserve Apples for Healthy Winter Snacks


As the crisp fall air settles in and the apple harvest season peaks, it’s the perfect time to think about how to preserve apples for the coming months. Whether you’re an apple enthusiast or simply looking to make the most of this seasonal fruit, preserving apples for winter is a great way to enjoy their sweetness and nutrition long after the harvest ends. From dehydrating and canning to freezing and fermenting, there are numerous ways to store apples for healthy snacks throughout the winter months.

In this guide, we’ll explore various methods of apple preservation, step-by-step instructions, tips for selecting the best apples, and creative ways to incorporate your preserved apples into winter snacks. Let’s dive into these methods and ensure you have a stash of delicious apples ready to enjoy all winter long!

Choosing the Best Apples for Preservation

When it comes to preserving apples, choosing the right variety is crucial. Different apple varieties have distinct flavors, textures, and levels of sweetness or tartness. Here are some of the best apples for preservation:

1. Fuji Apples

  • Flavor: Sweet and crisp
  • Best for: Drying, freezing, and making applesauce
  • Why: Fuji apples hold their flavor well and maintain their texture even when preserved.

2. Granny Smith Apples

  • Flavor: Tart and firm
  • Best for: Canning, making apple pie filling, or drying
  • Why: Granny Smith apples retain their firmness when cooked and are perfect for preserving.

3. Honeycrisp Apples

  • Flavor: Sweet and juicy
  • Best for: Freezing, dehydrating, or making apple butter
  • Why: Honeycrisp apples have a balanced flavor and maintain their texture when preserved.

4. Gala Apples

  • Flavor: Sweet and mild
  • Best for: Canning, making applesauce, or dehydrating
  • Why: Their thin skin makes them great for making applesauce and drying.

5. Jonathan Apples

  • Flavor: Sweet-tart and slightly spicy
  • Best for: Making apple jelly, cider, or drying
  • Why: Jonathan apples have a unique flavor profile that adds a zesty kick to preserved goods.

Once you’ve selected your apples, it’s important to inspect them for any blemishes or signs of rot, as these imperfections can affect the quality of your preserved apples.

Method 1: Freezing Apples

Freezing apples is a simple and efficient way to preserve them for later use in smoothies, baking, or as a healthy snack straight from the freezer. Here’s how to freeze apples properly:

Step-by-Step Instructions for Freezing Apples:

  1. Prepare the Apples: Wash your apples thoroughly. Peel them if desired, though leaving the skins on adds fiber and nutrients. Core the apples and cut them into slices or wedges, depending on your preference.
  2. Prevent Browning: To keep your apples from turning brown during freezing, you’ll need to blanch them. Prepare a bowl of water with a few teaspoons of lemon juice or ascorbic acid, and dip the apple slices into this solution for about 3-5 minutes.
  3. Dry the Apples: After blanching, lay the apple slices on a clean towel and pat them dry to remove excess moisture.
  4. Freeze the Apples: Spread the apple slices in a single layer on a baking sheet, ensuring they don’t touch each other. Place the tray in the freezer for a few hours until the apple slices are fully frozen.
  5. Store the Apples: Once the apple slices are frozen, transfer them to a freezer-safe bag or container. Label the bag with the date to ensure you use them within 6-12 months for optimal flavor.

Frozen apples can be used in baking (like pies or crisps), blended into smoothies, or enjoyed as a refreshing snack right out of the freezer.

Method 2: Dehydrating Apples

Dehydrating apples is an excellent way to preserve them for snacking during the winter. Dried apples retain most of their flavor and nutrients and make for a healthy, on-the-go snack. Here’s how to dehydrate apples using a dehydrator or an oven.

Step-by-Step Instructions for Dehydrating Apples:

  1. Prepare the Apples: Wash, peel (optional), core, and slice the apples into thin, uniform slices. Thinner slices will dehydrate more evenly and quickly.
  2. Prevent Browning: Just like freezing, you’ll want to soak the apple slices in a solution to prevent them from browning. Soak them in a mixture of lemon juice and water, or use ascorbic acid.
  3. Dehydrate the Apples:
    • Using a Dehydrator: Arrange the apple slices in a single layer on the dehydrator trays. Set the temperature to 135°F (57°C) and allow the apples to dehydrate for 6-12 hours, depending on the thickness of the slices and the type of dehydrator.
    • Using an Oven: Preheat the oven to 150°F (65°C). Place the apple slices in a single layer on a baking sheet lined with parchment paper. Leave the oven door slightly ajar to allow moisture to escape. Dehydrate the apples for 4-6 hours, flipping them halfway through.
  4. Store the Dried Apples: Once the apples are fully dehydrated (they should be leathery and pliable, not brittle), store them in airtight containers or vacuum-sealed bags. For the best shelf life, store them in a cool, dark place. Properly dried apples can last for up to a year.

Dried apples are perfect for snacking, adding to oatmeal, or using in baked goods like muffins or cakes. You can also rehydrate them by soaking them in water before using them in recipes.

Method 3: Canning Apples

Canning apples allows you to preserve them in jars, making them a convenient option for long-term storage. You can can apples in their natural juice, make apple pie filling, or create spiced apple slices for a delightful winter treat.

Step-by-Step Instructions for Canning Apples:

  1. Prepare the Apples: Wash, peel, and core the apples, then cut them into slices or chunks. You can use a mixture of sweet and tart apples to balance flavor.
  2. Prepare the Syrup: Depending on your preference, you can create a simple syrup using sugar and water. For a lighter option, use honey or agave syrup. Alternatively, you can can the apples without syrup, just in their natural juices.
  3. Pack the Jars: Sterilize your canning jars and lids by boiling them for 10 minutes. Pack the apple slices into the jars, leaving about 1/2 inch of headspace at the top. Pour the syrup or juice over the apples, making sure the slices are fully submerged.
  4. Process the Jars: Seal the jars with the lids and process them in a boiling water bath for about 20-25 minutes. If you’re canning apple pie filling or spiced apples, make sure to follow specific instructions for each recipe to ensure the right processing time.
  5. Cool and Store: After processing, allow the jars to cool completely. Once they’ve sealed (you’ll hear a “pop” sound as the lids seal), store them in a cool, dark place. Properly canned apples can last up to a year.

Canned apples are versatile and can be used in pies, crisps, sauces, or eaten straight from the jar.

Method 4: Making Applesauce

Applesauce is a delicious and nutritious snack that’s easy to make and store for the winter. It’s a great way to use up apples and can be enjoyed on its own or used as a topping for oatmeal, pancakes, or yogurt.

Step-by-Step Instructions for Making Applesauce:

  1. Prepare the Apples: Wash, peel, core, and chop the apples into chunks.
  2. Cook the Apples: In a large pot, add the apple chunks and a little water (about 1/2 cup per 4 apples). Cook over medium heat, stirring occasionally, until the apples soften (about 15-20 minutes).
  3. Mash or Blend: Once the apples are soft, mash them with a potato masher for a chunky applesauce or blend them for a smoother consistency.
  4. Add Sweetener and Flavor: If desired, add sweeteners like sugar, honey, or maple syrup. You can also add spices like cinnamon, nutmeg, or cloves for extra flavor.
  5. Store the Applesauce: Pour the applesauce into sterilized jars, leaving about 1/2 inch of headspace. Process the jars in a water bath for 20 minutes to seal them properly. Alternatively, you can freeze the applesauce in freezer-safe containers.

Homemade applesauce can be enjoyed as a healthy snack or used in baking recipes like cakes, muffins, or bread.

Method 5: Fermenting Apples for Apple Cider

Fermenting apples to make apple cider is an ancient method of preservation that not only provides a tangy beverage but also offers the benefits of probiotics. If you enjoy fermented foods, this is a fun way to preserve apples and make a naturally sweet, effervescent drink.

Step-by-Step Instructions for Fermenting Apples:

  1. Prepare the Apples: Wash the apples thoroughly and chop them into chunks, including the cores and peels. The more apples you use, the more cider you’ll get.
  2. Crush the Apples: Using a food processor or a manual press, crush the apples to release their juice. You can also mash them by hand.
  3. Ferment the Cider: Place the crushed apples in a fermentation vessel (such as a glass jar or a fermentation crock). Add water, and sweeten with honey or sugar to help the fermentation process.
  4. Seal and Ferment: Cover the vessel with a clean cloth and let the apples ferment for 1-3 weeks, stirring daily. Once fermentation slows down and the cider has developed the desired flavor, strain out the solids.
  5. Store the Cider: Pour the finished cider into sterilized bottles and seal. If you want a bubbly, carbonated drink, you can carbonate it by bottling it in sealed bottles and leaving it for a few more days.

Fermented apple cider is a tangy, probiotic-rich drink that makes a healthy winter treat.

Final Thoughts on Preserving Apples for Healthy Winter Snacks

There are many ways to preserve apples to ensure you have access to their fresh taste and health benefits during the winter months. Whether you choose freezing, dehydrating, canning, making applesauce, or fermenting apples into cider, each method allows you to enjoy this nutrient-packed fruit long after the apple harvest season ends.

Not only do preserved apples make great winter snacks, but they also add a healthy twist to a wide variety of recipes. So, gather up your apples, choose your preferred preservation method, and get ready to enjoy the taste of autumn all through the colder months.
