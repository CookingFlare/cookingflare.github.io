Japanese Ramen Recipes: Broths That Shine


Ramen, a beloved dish that has taken the world by storm, is more than just noodles in a bowl. At its heart, ramen is about the broth – the rich, savory foundation that elevates this humble meal into something extraordinary. If you’ve ever tasted a bowl of ramen in Japan, you know that the broth is what truly makes the dish. The secret lies in the way each broth is prepared, simmered, and seasoned to perfection, creating a symphony of flavors.

In this blog post, we will dive deep into the world of ramen broths, exploring different types, their origins, and how you can recreate them in your own kitchen. From the comforting, milky tonkotsu to the delicate shoyu, each ramen broth has its own unique character, giving you the opportunity to craft a dish tailored to your taste. Whether you’re a ramen enthusiast or a beginner looking to start your culinary journey, this guide to ramen broths will provide you with the knowledge and techniques to create delicious bowls of ramen that shine.

1. The Basics of Ramen Broths

Before diving into the different types of ramen broths, it’s important to understand what goes into a ramen broth. Traditionally, ramen broth is made by simmering a combination of meat, bones, vegetables, and seasonings over an extended period of time. This slow cooking process allows the flavors to meld together, creating a deep and complex base.

The two main components of a ramen broth are:

  • Stock (Dashi): The stock forms the base of the ramen broth. It’s typically made from meat, bones, and sometimes fish or seaweed, which give the broth its umami flavor.
  • Seasoning (Tare): The tare is the seasoning that adds depth and complexity to the broth. Common tare ingredients include soy sauce, miso, salt, or even a combination of these.

The balance between the stock and tare is what makes a great ramen broth. If the stock is too light, it can be bland; too heavy, and it can overwhelm the noodles. The tare should complement the stock without overpowering its natural flavors.

2. Types of Ramen Broths

Japanese ramen broths can be classified into four main types, each with its distinct flavor profile. Let’s explore them one by one.

2.1 Shoyu Ramen: The Soy Sauce Broth

Shoyu ramen is one of the oldest and most iconic types of ramen in Japan. It features a clear, brown broth that is seasoned with soy sauce, giving it a deep, savory flavor with just a touch of sweetness. The broth can be made from chicken, pork, or even seafood, and the addition of soy sauce imparts a slightly tangy, umami-rich flavor that enhances the natural taste of the stock.

Key Characteristics:

  • Flavor Profile: Salty, savory, and slightly tangy with a light body.
  • Common Stock: Typically made with chicken, pork, or a combination of both. Sometimes, it can also include fish or dried bonito flakes.
  • Tare: Soy sauce-based seasoning, sometimes with a dash of mirin (a sweet rice wine) or sake for depth.

How to Make Shoyu Ramen Broth:

  1. Start by making the stock with chicken bones, pork bones, or a mix of both. Simmer the bones for several hours to extract all the flavors.
  2. Prepare the tare by combining soy sauce, sake, mirin, and a pinch of sugar. You can also add garlic, ginger, and scallions for extra aromatics.
  3. Once the stock is ready, strain it and combine it with the tare. Let it simmer for a few more minutes to allow the flavors to meld together.
  4. Serve the broth with freshly cooked ramen noodles, sliced pork (chashu), a soft-boiled egg, and green onions for garnish.

2.2 Shio Ramen: The Salt-Based Broth

Shio ramen is the simplest of the ramen broths, but its simplicity is what makes it so elegant. The broth is light and clear, made by seasoning the stock with salt (shio). It has a delicate, clean flavor that lets the natural taste of the stock shine through. Shio ramen is often paired with lighter proteins, such as chicken or seafood, to complement its refreshing taste.

Key Characteristics:

  • Flavor Profile: Clean, salty, and light with a subtle umami undertone.
  • Common Stock: Typically made with chicken, seafood, or sometimes a combination of both. It is usually a clear broth.
  • Tare: A simple salt-based seasoning, sometimes enhanced with a touch of fish sauce or kelp for added umami.

How to Make Shio Ramen Broth:

  1. Begin by making a clear chicken stock or a seafood-based stock by simmering the ingredients for several hours.
  2. For the tare, mix salt with a bit of fish sauce and kombu (kelp) to create a well-balanced seasoning.
  3. Combine the stock and tare, then simmer for another 10-15 minutes to ensure the flavors are well integrated.
  4. Serve with a light protein like grilled chicken or fish, fresh ramen noodles, and a garnish of pickled ginger, seaweed, and green onions.

2.3 Miso Ramen: The Rich, Fermented Flavor

Miso ramen is known for its hearty, rich flavor. The key ingredient in this broth is miso, a fermented soybean paste that adds depth, earthiness, and a touch of sweetness. Miso ramen has a thicker, more robust broth compared to shoyu or shio ramen, making it perfect for colder weather. The combination of miso, garlic, and pork creates a deeply satisfying and umami-packed broth.

Key Characteristics:

  • Flavor Profile: Rich, savory, slightly sweet, and full of umami with a thicker texture.
  • Common Stock: Often made with pork, chicken, or a mix of both.
  • Tare: Miso paste is the primary seasoning, which may be combined with garlic, ginger, and other spices.

How to Make Miso Ramen Broth:

  1. Start by making a rich pork or chicken stock, simmering the bones for hours to extract all the flavors.
  2. Prepare the miso tare by combining white or red miso paste with garlic, ginger, and a little bit of sesame oil.
  3. Once the stock is ready, mix in the miso tare and allow it to simmer for 15-20 minutes to meld the flavors together.
  4. Serve with hearty toppings like corn, butter, bean sprouts, and a soft-boiled egg. Miso ramen pairs wonderfully with thick, curly noodles that can hold up to the rich broth.

2.4 Tonkotsu Ramen: The Milky, Pork Bone Broth

Tonkotsu ramen is a fan favorite, especially for those who love a rich and indulgent bowl of ramen. The hallmark of tonkotsu ramen is its creamy, milky broth, made by simmering pork bones for hours, extracting collagen and marrow, which gives the broth its luscious texture. The result is a thick, hearty broth with a deep umami flavor and a smooth, velvety finish.

Key Characteristics:

  • Flavor Profile: Rich, creamy, and full-bodied with a deep umami flavor.
  • Common Stock: Made from pork bones, often including the marrow and cartilage to achieve the signature creamy texture.
  • Tare: Typically flavored with soy sauce or salt, though some variations include garlic, sesame, or ginger.

How to Make Tonkotsu Ramen Broth:

  1. Begin by boiling pork bones, including neck bones, trotters, and sometimes even pig’s feet, for several hours to break down the collagen.
  2. Skim the scum from the surface as it simmers to ensure the broth stays clear.
  3. After about 8 hours, the bones will break down, and the broth will turn white and creamy.
  4. Season the broth with tare, which can be a mixture of soy sauce, salt, or a touch of miso for added flavor.
  5. Serve with thin, straight noodles, slices of chashu pork, bamboo shoots, and a soft-boiled egg.

3. Enhancing the Ramen Broth with Toppings

The toppings you choose for your ramen can make or break the dish. Here are some popular ramen toppings that not only add flavor but also texture and visual appeal to your bowl:

  • Chashu Pork: Braised pork belly that is tender and flavorful. This is a classic topping for many ramen types.
  • Soft-Boiled Egg: A perfectly cooked egg with a runny yolk adds richness to the broth.
  • Nori (Seaweed): Seaweed sheets add a salty, umami touch to ramen and balance out the richness of the broth.
  • Bamboo Shoots: Pickled bamboo shoots provide a crunchy contrast to the tender noodles.
  • Green Onions: Freshly chopped green onions add a crisp, slightly spicy flavor.
  • Corn and Butter: A popular addition to miso ramen, corn adds sweetness, while butter contributes richness.

4. Conclusion

Ramen is a dish that has evolved over time, and while the broths may vary from region to region, they all share one common goal: to provide a deep, flavorful base for the noodles. Whether you prefer the light and clean taste of shio ramen, the rich and hearty miso ramen, the savory and umami-packed tonkotsu ramen, or the classic soy sauce-infused shoyu ramen, there’s a broth out there for everyone.

By mastering the art of ramen broths, you’ll be able to create a bowl that not only satisfies your cravings but also showcases the depth and complexity of Japanese cooking. So, grab your ingredients, roll up your sleeves, and start experimenting with these ramen broths that shine. Each bowl you create will bring you one step closer to the perfect ramen experience right in your own home. Happy cooking!
