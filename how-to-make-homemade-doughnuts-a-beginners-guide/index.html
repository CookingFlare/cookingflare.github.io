How to Make Homemade Doughnuts: A Beginner's Guide


There’s something undeniably magical about a perfectly soft, fluffy doughnut fresh from the fryer—warm, pillowy, and coated with just the right amount of sugary goodness. While professional doughnuts from bakeries can be tempting, making homemade doughnuts is a rewarding and surprisingly approachable experience, even for beginners. The scent alone, drifting through your kitchen as the dough fries to golden perfection, is reward enough. Plus, the creative freedom is unlimited once you get the basics down.

If you’ve ever wondered how to make homemade doughnuts that rival those from your favorite shop, this guide has you covered. From mixing the dough to frying, glazing, and decorating, we’ll break down every step in detail. Let’s dive right in.

Understanding Doughnuts: The Basics

Before you start, it helps to know the types of doughnuts out there. The two most common varieties are:

  • Yeast-raised doughnuts: These doughnuts use yeast to make the dough rise, resulting in a light, airy, and fluffy texture.
  • Cake doughnuts: Made with a chemical leavening agent like baking powder, these have a denser, crumbly texture similar to cake.

This beginner’s guide focuses on yeast-raised doughnuts, which produce that classic doughnut texture everyone loves. They take a bit longer to make because of the rising times, but the results are well worth it.

Ingredients You’ll Need

Before you begin, assemble the following ingredients. Most are pantry staples:

  • All-purpose flour: Provides the structure for your doughnuts.
  • Active dry yeast: The rising agent for fluffy doughnuts.
  • Granulated sugar: Adds sweetness and helps yeast fermentation.
  • Milk: For moistening the dough and enhancing flavor.
  • Butter: Adds richness and tenderness.
  • Eggs: Bind the dough and add moisture.
  • Salt: Balances sweetness and strengthens gluten.
  • Vegetable oil (or any neutral oil): For frying.

Optional toppings and glazes include powdered sugar, cinnamon sugar, chocolate glaze, or vanilla glaze.

Tools Needed

Here’s what will make the process smoother:

  • Large mixing bowl
  • Small saucepan for warming milk
  • Wooden spoon or dough hook attachment for a stand mixer
  • Rolling pin
  • Doughnut cutter or two round cutters of different sizes
  • Deep frying pan or deep fryer
  • Slotted spoon or spider strainer
  • Cooling rack
  • Thermometer (to monitor oil temperature)

Step 1: Preparing the Dough

  1. Activate Yeast: Start by warming 1/2 cup of milk (not too hot—ideal is about 110°F/43°C). Pour it into a small bowl, add 2 1/4 teaspoons of active dry yeast (one packet), and 1 tablespoon of sugar. Stir lightly and let it sit for 5-10 minutes until foamy.

  2. Mix Dry Ingredients: In a large bowl, sift together 3 1/2 cups of all-purpose flour, 1/4 cup of sugar, and 1 teaspoon of salt.

  3. Combine Wet Ingredients: In another bowl, beat 2 large eggs. Add 1/4 cup melted butter and the activated yeast mixture to the eggs.

  4. Form the Dough: Make a well in the dry ingredients and pour in the egg mixture. Mix with a wooden spoon or the dough hook of a mixer until it starts to form a sticky dough.

  5. Knead: Transfer the dough to a floured surface and knead for about 8-10 minutes until it becomes smooth and elastic. If using a stand mixer, knead with the dough hook attachment on medium speed for about 7 minutes.

  6. First Rise: Shape the dough into a ball, place it in a lightly greased bowl, cover with a clean towel or plastic wrap, and let it rise in a warm spot for 1 to 1.5 hours or until it doubles in size.

Step 2: Shaping the Doughnuts

After the dough has doubled, it’s time to shape the doughnuts.

  1. Roll Out the Dough: Punch down the dough to release air. Lightly flour a clean surface and roll the dough out to about 1/2-inch thickness.

  2. Cut the Doughnuts: Use a doughnut cutter if available, or two round cutters—a larger one (about 3 inches in diameter) for the outer edge, and a smaller one (about 1 inch) for the hole.

  3. Place on a Tray: Transfer the cut doughnuts and holes onto parchment paper-lined baking trays, leaving space between each to allow for a second rise.

  4. Second Rise: Cover them again and allow to rise for another 30-45 minutes until puffy and almost doubled.

Step 3: Frying the Doughnuts

Frying can be intimidating, but with a little care, it’s easy to master.

  1. Heat the Oil: Pour vegetable oil into a deep pan or fryer, about 2-3 inches deep, and heat to 350°F (175°C). Use a thermometer to keep the temperature steady.

  2. Test the Oil: Drop a small piece of dough into the oil. It should bubble and start to brown within a minute, but not burn.

  3. Fry Doughnuts: Carefully place 2-3 doughnuts at a time into the hot oil. Don’t overcrowd the pan, or the temperature will drop too much.

  4. Flip When Golden: Fry the first side for about 1-2 minutes until golden brown, then flip and fry the other side for another 1-2 minutes.

  5. Drain: Use a slotted spoon to remove the doughnuts and place them on a cooling rack over a baking sheet or on paper towels to drain excess oil.

  6. Fry Doughnut Holes: Repeat with the holes, but these will cook faster—about 30 seconds on each side.

Step 4: Glazing and Decorating

This is the fun part—making your doughnuts look and taste incredible.

Basic Glaze Recipe:

  • 2 cups powdered sugar
  • 1/4 cup milk
  • 1 teaspoon vanilla extract

Mix all ingredients until smooth. Dip the warm doughnuts into the glaze, allowing the excess to drip off. Place back on the rack for the glaze to set.

Other Topping Ideas:

  • Cinnamon Sugar: Toss warm doughnuts in a mixture of cinnamon and granulated sugar.
  • Chocolate Glaze: Melt semi-sweet chocolate with a little butter or cream; dip the doughnuts for a rich coating.
  • Sprinkles: Add colorful sprinkles over glazed doughnuts before the glaze hardens.
  • Filled Doughnuts: Inject fruit jams, custards, or chocolate cream after frying using a piping bag.

Tips for Perfect Doughnuts Every Time

  • Consistent Oil Temperature: Keeping the oil at 350°F ensures the doughnuts cook evenly and soak up less oil.
  • Don’t Overwork the Dough: Knead until just smooth and elastic; over-kneading can make doughnuts tough.
  • Use Fresh Yeast: Check the expiration on your yeast to make sure it’s active.
  • Room Temperature Ingredients: Helps the yeast activate properly and dough to rise well.
  • Drain Properly: Let doughnuts rest on a wire rack to avoid sogginess.
  • Enjoy Warm: Doughnuts at their best are warm and freshly glazed.

Troubleshooting Common Issues

  • Doughnuts are Dense: This could be due to under-proofing (not enough rising time) or over-kneading. Let the dough rest longer and be gentle when kneading.
  • Doughnuts Absorb Too Much Oil: Oil temperature is too low; it should be hot enough to cook quickly, around 350°F.
  • Doughnuts Burn Quickly: Oil is too hot or doughnuts are too thin.
  • Yeast Doesn’t Foam: Yeast may be dead or milk/water used was too hot or cold.

Wrapping Up

Making homemade doughnuts fills your kitchen with the smells of nostalgia and indulgence. With just a handful of ingredients and some patience, you can enjoy these delightful treats anytime you want. Whether you prefer a classic glazed doughnut or love experimenting with flavors and toppings, mastering this recipe will set you on a delicious path.

Next time your sweet tooth calls, skip the store-bought aisle and whip up your own batch of fluffy homemade doughnuts. The joy of frying and decorating, paired with the taste of fresh doughnuts, is an experience in itself. Happy cooking!

Feel free to share your homemade doughnut stories and any creative topping ideas you discover. After all, every great doughnut starts with a little mix, rise, and fry!
