Making and Canning Homemade Hot Pepper Jelly


Hot pepper jelly is a zesty and versatile treat that can spice up a variety of dishes, whether you’re using it as a condiment, glaze, or even a dip. The balance of sweet and heat, along with its vibrant color, makes hot pepper jelly a favorite for many. Not only is it a fantastic way to preserve the harvest from your pepper garden, but it’s also an easy and fun project to take on. If you’ve ever wondered how to make your own homemade hot pepper jelly, you’re in the right place. This guide will walk you through every step of the process, from selecting the right peppers to the canning method, ensuring your jelly will be shelf-stable and ready to enjoy for months.

Choosing the Right Peppers for Hot Pepper Jelly

The first step in making hot pepper jelly is choosing the right peppers. The flavor and level of heat in your jelly will depend largely on the type of peppers you use. Hot pepper jelly can be made with a variety of peppers, but the most common choices include:

Jalapeño Peppers

Jalapeños are a popular choice because they offer a moderate level of heat without overwhelming the jelly. They also have a crisp, fresh flavor that pairs well with the sweetness of the jelly. If you prefer a milder jelly, remove the seeds and membranes of the peppers, as these parts contain the most heat.

Habanero Peppers

For those who like their jelly to pack a punch, habanero peppers are an excellent option. These peppers are significantly hotter than jalapeños, and they bring a fruity, citrusy flavor that complements the sweetness of the jelly. Be sure to use gloves when handling habaneros, as their oils can irritate the skin and eyes.

Red Bell Peppers

If you’re looking to balance the heat with sweetness, red bell peppers can be added to your recipe. While bell peppers aren’t spicy, their sweetness adds depth and color to the jelly, making them an ideal complement to hotter peppers like jalapeños or habaneros.

Other Pepper Varieties

Feel free to experiment with other types of hot peppers, such as serranos, Fresno, or Thai bird’s eye chilies. Each pepper will bring its unique heat profile and flavor to your jelly.

For the best results, use a mix of peppers that suit your preferred spice level and flavor.

Ingredients You’ll Need

Making homemade hot pepper jelly is quite simple, and you probably already have most of the ingredients in your pantry. Here’s a list of what you’ll need:

  • Fresh hot peppers (about 10 to 12 medium-sized peppers for a small batch)
  • Sweet bell pepper (1 large red bell pepper or 2 smaller ones for balance)
  • Apple cider vinegar (1 cup)
  • Granulated sugar (4 cups)
  • Pectin (1 package, typically 1.75 oz, for jelly-making)
  • Salt (1/4 teaspoon)
  • Canning jars (4 to 5 half-pint jars, or 2 to 3 pint jars depending on the batch size)
  • Water (1/2 cup)
  • Optional: Lemon juice (2 tablespoons, for added acidity and a slight tartness)

Equipment Needed:

  • Large saucepan or stockpot
  • Knife and cutting board
  • Food processor or blender
  • Measuring cups and spoons
  • Canning tongs or jar lifter
  • Canning funnel
  • Ladle
  • Canning jars with lids and rings
  • Hot water bath canner or large pot for processing

Preparing the Peppers

Before you begin cooking your hot pepper jelly, it’s important to properly prepare the peppers.

  1. Wash the peppers: Rinse all the peppers thoroughly under cool water. This will remove any dirt or residue from the skins.
  2. De-stem the peppers: Cut off the stems of your peppers and discard them.
  3. Remove the seeds (optional): If you prefer a milder jelly, remove the seeds and membranes from your hot peppers. This step is optional but highly recommended for those who are sensitive to spice.
  4. Chop the peppers: Finely chop the peppers. You can use a food processor or blender to speed up this process, but be cautious not to make the peppers too finely pureed. You want small pieces of pepper in your jelly for texture and color.
  5. Chop the bell pepper: Similarly, chop the red bell pepper into small pieces. This pepper will add sweetness and color to balance out the heat from the hot peppers.

Safety Tip:

When handling hot peppers, always wear gloves to avoid irritation to your skin. After working with peppers, be sure to wash your hands thoroughly. Avoid touching your face, especially your eyes, as the oils from the peppers can cause discomfort.

Cooking the Jelly

Once your peppers are prepped, it’s time to cook the jelly.

Step 1: Combine the Ingredients

In a large saucepan, add the chopped hot peppers, bell pepper, apple cider vinegar, water, and sugar. Stir everything together over medium heat.

Step 2: Bring to a Boil

Allow the mixture to come to a full boil, stirring frequently to ensure the sugar dissolves. Once it begins to boil, continue to stir occasionally to prevent the mixture from burning or sticking to the bottom of the pan.

Step 3: Add the Pectin

After the mixture has boiled for about 5 minutes, it’s time to add the pectin. Sprinkle the pectin over the mixture, stirring it in slowly. Allow the jelly to come back to a boil for another 1-2 minutes.

Step 4: Test the Jelly’s Consistency

To check if your jelly is ready, perform the “cold plate test.” Drop a spoonful of jelly onto a cold plate and let it sit for a minute. Then, run your finger through the jelly. If it wrinkles and holds its shape, it’s ready to be canned. If it’s still too runny, continue boiling for a few more minutes and repeat the test until the desired consistency is achieved.

Step 5: Add Lemon Juice (Optional)

If you’d like a slight tartness to balance the sweetness and heat, add lemon juice at this stage and stir it in. The acidity from the lemon juice will also help the jelly set more firmly.

Canning Your Hot Pepper Jelly

Canning is the final step that ensures your homemade hot pepper jelly is shelf-stable and safe to store for long periods. Follow these steps carefully:

Step 1: Sterilize the Jars

Before you begin canning, sterilize your jars and lids. Place the jars in a large pot of boiling water and allow them to boil for about 10 minutes. This will remove any bacteria and prepare them for the jelly. Place the lids in a separate pot of hot (but not boiling) water to soften the seals.

Step 2: Fill the Jars

Once the jars are sterilized, use a ladle to pour the hot pepper jelly into the jars. Leave about 1/4 inch of headspace at the top of each jar to allow for expansion during processing. Be careful not to overfill.

Step 3: Remove Air Bubbles

Use a small spatula or a butter knife to run along the edges of the jar to release any air bubbles. This helps the jelly set properly.

Step 4: Wipe the Jar Rims

Before sealing the jars, use a clean, damp cloth to wipe any jelly from the rims of the jars. This ensures a proper seal.

Step 5: Seal the Jars

Place the sterilized lids onto the jars and screw the metal rings on tightly. Be sure not to overtighten, as the lids need some space to expand during the canning process.

Step 6: Process the Jars

Place the filled jars into a hot water bath canner or a large pot of boiling water. The water should cover the jars by at least an inch. Boil the jars for about 5 to 10 minutes to ensure that they are properly sealed and pasteurized.

Step 7: Cool and Store

Once the jars have been processed, carefully remove them from the water using canning tongs or a jar lifter. Place them on a towel to cool for 12 to 24 hours. During this time, you should hear a “pop” as the jars seal. After they’ve cooled, check the seals by pressing down on the center of each lid. If it doesn’t pop back, the jar is sealed properly. Any jars that haven’t sealed can be refrigerated and used within a few weeks.

Once sealed, store your hot pepper jelly in a cool, dark place for up to a year. Once opened, refrigerate and consume within a few months.

How to Use Hot Pepper Jelly

Hot pepper jelly isn’t just a condiment for cheese platters. There are so many creative ways to use it, and it can enhance a wide range of dishes.

  • As a glaze: Brush it over grilled meats such as chicken, pork, or shrimp to add a sweet and spicy finish.
  • With cheese: Pair it with cream cheese or brie for an easy appetizer.
  • In sandwiches: Spread it on a turkey or ham sandwich to give a kick of flavor.
  • As a dipping sauce: Serve it as a dip with crackers, chips, or fresh bread.
  • In stir-fries or marinades: Add a spoonful of pepper jelly to stir-fries for a unique twist.

Final Thoughts

Making homemade hot pepper jelly is a fun and rewarding process that allows you to create a unique, flavorful product with your own hands. By selecting your favorite peppers and following the simple canning steps, you can preserve your pepper harvest and enjoy a jar of spicy sweetness whenever you want. Whether you’re using it as a glaze, dip, or gift, homemade hot pepper jelly is a fantastic addition to any kitchen.
