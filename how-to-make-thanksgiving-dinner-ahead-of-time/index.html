How to Make Thanksgiving Dinner Ahead of Time


Thanksgiving is a time for family, food, and fun, but it can also be a lot of work. The last thing you want to be doing on the big day is scrambling to prepare everything from scratch while your guests are arriving. Fortunately, you can make most of your Thanksgiving dinner ahead of time. With a little planning, you can enjoy a stress-free holiday and still serve a delicious meal. Here’s your ultimate guide to preparing Thanksgiving dinner in advance.

Plan Ahead: The Key to Success

The secret to making Thanksgiving dinner ahead of time is preparation. Plan your menu and make a detailed timeline of what needs to be done and when. The earlier you begin, the more time you have to prepare dishes and organize everything.

Start by deciding on your menu. Classic Thanksgiving dishes like turkey, stuffing, mashed potatoes, gravy, and pies are typically the staples, but feel free to add your personal touches. Once you have your menu set, break down each dish into components and figure out which items can be prepped in advance. Some dishes can be made up to a week ahead, while others may need just a few hours before serving.

Week Before Thanksgiving: The Prepping Phase

1. Prepare the Turkey

If you’re making a turkey, it’s best to prepare it a day or two before Thanksgiving. Brining the turkey in advance can enhance its flavor and moisture. Here’s how to do it:

  • Brining the Turkey: If you plan to brine your turkey, do this at least 24 hours before roasting. Mix water, salt, sugar, and any herbs or spices you want to use, and submerge the turkey in the brine. If you have a large turkey, you might need a cooler or large container to fit it. Make sure the brine is cold before placing the turkey inside, and let it soak in the fridge for 12–24 hours. This step can also be done up to 48 hours in advance. Just make sure to remove the turkey from the brine and dry it thoroughly before roasting.

  • Stuffing: Make your stuffing ahead of time and store it in an airtight container in the fridge. The night before Thanksgiving, you can stuff the turkey or bake the stuffing separately in a casserole dish. If you decide to bake it separately, be sure to follow the reheating instructions to ensure it’s nice and hot for serving.

2. Prepare Cranberry Sauce

Cranberry sauce can be made up to a week in advance and stored in the fridge. Fresh cranberry sauce, made with sugar, orange juice, and cranberries, often tastes better after a few days of sitting in the fridge, allowing the flavors to meld together. Simply cook the cranberries with the sugar and orange juice, and let it cool before storing. The sauce can be served cold or at room temperature on Thanksgiving day.

3. Mashed Potatoes

Mashed potatoes can be made ahead of time and stored in the refrigerator for up to two days. Prepare the potatoes by peeling and boiling them, then mashing them with butter, milk, salt, and pepper. Once mashed, let the potatoes cool before storing them in an airtight container. On Thanksgiving day, simply reheat them in the oven, covered with foil, and add more butter or milk to adjust the texture. You can also make mashed potatoes the night before and reheat them in a slow cooker on Thanksgiving day.

4. Gravy

Gravy is another component of your meal that can be prepped ahead of time. If you make your gravy from scratch, use turkey drippings or stock, and cook it a few days before Thanksgiving. Allow it to cool and store it in the fridge. On the big day, simply reheat the gravy, adding a bit more stock if needed to reach the right consistency. If you’re preparing a vegetarian or vegan version of the gravy, use vegetable broth and plant-based butter.

A Few Days Before Thanksgiving

5. Make Pies and Desserts

Pies and other desserts are an essential part of the Thanksgiving meal, and the great news is they can be made days in advance. Whether you’re making a classic pumpkin pie, pecan pie, or apple crisp, they all store well in the fridge or freezer.

  • Pumpkin Pie: Pumpkin pies can be baked 2–3 days in advance. After baking, let the pie cool completely, and then cover it tightly with plastic wrap. Refrigerate it until you’re ready to serve. When reheating, cover the pie with foil to prevent the crust from getting too dark.

  • Pecan Pie: Pecan pie is another dessert that holds up well in advance. It can be baked up to 2–3 days before serving, and like pumpkin pie, it should be refrigerated once cooled.

  • Apple Crisp: Apple crisp is a dessert that actually benefits from being made ahead of time. The flavors have time to develop, and when it’s reheated, the topping will get nice and crisp. You can make it a day or two before Thanksgiving, then bake it on the day of.

If you prefer, you can also prepare some dessert components, such as pie crusts or cake batters, ahead of time and freeze them. This saves time on the day of the celebration and ensures that everything is ready when needed.

6. Prep Vegetables and Side Dishes

Prepare vegetables for your side dishes a day or two before Thanksgiving. Peel and chop carrots, sweet potatoes, Brussels sprouts, or other vegetables you plan to serve. Store them in airtight containers in the fridge. You can even roast or blanch the vegetables ahead of time and store them in the fridge. On Thanksgiving day, simply reheat or finish cooking them.

The Day Before Thanksgiving

7. Assemble Casseroles

Many Thanksgiving casseroles, such as green bean casserole, sweet potato casserole, and macaroni and cheese, can be assembled a day before the big feast. Prepare the casserole fully, then cover it and refrigerate it overnight. On Thanksgiving day, simply pop it in the oven to bake while you prepare other components.

8. Set the Table and Organize Serving Dishes

The day before Thanksgiving, set the table and organize all of your serving dishes. This way, you don’t have to worry about scrambling around for plates, bowls, or utensils when it’s time to serve the food. Consider arranging the table with centerpieces, linens, and decorations, so everything looks festive and ready to go.

9. Make the Final Preparations for the Turkey

If you’re cooking a whole turkey, take it out of the fridge about 30–60 minutes before roasting to allow it to come to room temperature. If you’re roasting the turkey in the oven, you can prepare the seasoning, butter, or marinade and apply it the night before. If you prefer, you can also cook the turkey the day before and reheat it on Thanksgiving day.

Thanksgiving Day: The Final Stretch

10. Reheat Everything

On Thanksgiving day, the goal is to reheat as much of the meal as possible without overcooking or drying out the dishes. Start by reheating dishes that need the most time, like mashed potatoes, stuffing, and casseroles. Use your oven, stovetop, or slow cooker to warm everything up. Don’t forget to add extra seasoning or liquid, such as stock or butter, if needed to restore the texture and flavor.

11. Cook the Turkey

If you haven’t already cooked the turkey, now is the time. While the turkey roasts, you can focus on finishing up other dishes. Be sure to use a meat thermometer to ensure the turkey reaches an internal temperature of 165°F (75°C) in the thickest part of the thigh.

12. Prepare Gravy and Final Touches

Once the turkey is done, use the pan drippings to make the gravy. This should be the final step, as the gravy should be served fresh and hot. Warm up your pre-made side dishes and desserts, and you’re all set for a fantastic meal.

Tips for a Stress-Free Thanksgiving Dinner

  • Delegate: If possible, ask your family members to help with the meal prep. Assign tasks like setting the table, making appetizers, or slicing bread to spread out the workload.

  • Don’t Overcrowd the Oven: Make sure you plan your oven time carefully. If you have multiple dishes that need to be baked, try to schedule their cooking times so they don’t all require the oven at the same time. You can reheat side dishes in a slow cooker, microwave, or on the stovetop to save space.

  • Use Slow Cookers and Instant Pots: These appliances are your best friends on Thanksgiving. Use them for mashed potatoes, green beans, or even dessert to save valuable oven space and reduce your cooking time.

  • Keep It Simple: While it’s tempting to try new recipes and be overly ambitious, Thanksgiving is about spending time with loved ones. Keep the menu simple and focus on your favorites.

Conclusion

Making Thanksgiving dinner ahead of time isn’t just a time-saver; it can also reduce stress and allow you to focus on what matters most—enjoying the company of family and friends. With careful planning and preparation, you can have a delicious, homemade meal without the last-minute rush. By preparing dishes a few days in advance, you’ll have more time to relax, enjoy the festivities, and savor the flavors of the season. Happy Thanksgiving!
