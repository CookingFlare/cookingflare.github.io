Breaking Bread: How Fermentation Can Help Reduce the Divide


Fermentation, a process that has been in existence for thousands of years, is often linked to the creation of exquisite, savory foods such as bread, beer, cheese, and pickles. However, this traditional practice goes beyond culinary satisfaction. It can significantly transform society by breaking bread, fostering connections and required us to unite for the greater good.

Fermentation is the process that involves the conversion of carbohydrates into various chemical products by microorganisms, mainly yeasts and bacteria. Ancient civilizations quickly discovered that keeping food preserved through fermentation helped sustain communities and encourage unity.

In today’s rapidly advancing technology-driven world, we find ourselves more disconnected than ever. Fraudulence, disharmony, clickbait-materialistic content is rife, with the importance of human relationships hiding single linkages among the bevy of infinite information. We can learn from our ancestors that use fermentation to break bread and build stronger communities.

The art of fermentation not only provides a delicious, healthy, and sustainable way to store food but also can create a sustainable income stream that can empower local economies and communities unnoticed.

In the legendary process, communities gather around tables breaking bread - both literally and figuratively. Breaking bread means coming together, sharing stories, sparking conversations, bridging gaps, and prioritizing togetherness, oneness, and harmony. This shared experience of fermentation goes beyond simple gastronomy, creating a different environment where we can learn, share, and build stable relationships.

Consider a simple Serbian dish called “p aj Sendžoš ljivi student." Translated, it means “Emancipated student.” It is a fermented mix of grapes and herbs served during communal events such as weddings, celebrations, and other gatherings. Recipes have been passed down through generations, each adding their unique twist to keep the tradition flourishing.

This sharing of fermentation recipes and tradition revives the sense of community and harmony that has dwindled due to today’s fast-paced lifestyle. Practices such as pickling vegetables together in a group, fermenting milk at the village co-op, or baking bread communally help reinforce local economies, making the community stronger and the people more resilient.

Moreover, fermentation processes have been proven to increase the structure and taste of certain foods dramatically, making them healthier in the process. Fermented foods are sourced from marginal areas, and their study shows several potential health benefits.

For a more globally approachable fermentation food try Kimchi - a beloved Korean dish featuring the clear evidence of fermentation. The combination of fermented vegetables like cabbage and radish with red pepper powder, garlic, ginger, and fish sauce adds a unique tang to the side dish. Not only is Kimchi full of flavor and nutrition, but sharing it fosters a healthy, almost ritualistic meal to bring a table together.

Teaming up to ferment food sources provides an ideal opportunity to combine innovative techniques and flavors to create a diverse, equitable, and connected society. As we move away from a singular food source diet, fermentation techniques can make a meal more enjoyable and accessible simultaneously.

Undoubtedly, fermenting food is making a comeback, and for good reason. The practices of fermentation enrich our daily food intake and have the potential to create stronger ties, a much-needed solution in our current hyper-connected digital world. From shared knowledge about flora and fauna to the science-based remedy to strengthen their community ties and remain connected to their roots, fermentation is truly a way that links the past with the future.

As societies grapple with how to reduce the divide and establish Amity as the ultimate goal, techniques like fermentation and breaking bread could become the primary suspect for achieving global peace. So take this knowledge and add it to your grocery list. If you haven’t already, start fermenting!
