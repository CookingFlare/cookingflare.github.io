A Generation's Recipe for Success: A Millennial's Perspective on Fermented Foods


Fermented foods have been enjoying a meteoric rise in popularity over the past few years. As more and more people discover the incredible health benefits and unique flavors, it’s no wonder they’re becoming a staple in home kitchens and restaurants alike. As a generation born in the midst of this culinary revolution, millennials have found fresh inspiration in the world of fermentation.

For those unfamiliar with this process, fermentation is the natural breakdown of carbohydrates by way of microorganisms, which create enzymes that aid in digestion and produce beneficial bacteria for the gut. This ancient practice, traced back to as far as 6000 BC, is now experiencing a resurgence in modern cooking. Whether through sourdough bread, kimchi, or probiotic-rich kombucha, fermented foods provide an explosion of flavor and a boost to gut health.

As a millennial, I’ve tasted many of these creative fermentation combinations and have honed several recipes for success. My journey began with the universal sourdough bread starter, which I use to whip up dutch cocoa pancakes or pizza crusts. From there, I’ve explored the bubbly world of kombucha and ventured into the zesty realm of kimchi.

One favorite recipe of mine is my fermented cucumber tangy base. Not only is it a refreshing addition to most dishes, but it also serves as a versatile ingredient to create unique, zesty flavors. Here is my step-by-step guide for this delight:

Fermented Cucumber Tangy Base Recipe

Ingredients:

  • 2-3 pounds of pickling cucumbers
  • 2 tablespoons of sea salt
  • 2 cloves of garlic
  • 1 tablespoon of grated fresh ginger
  • 1 cup of water
  • Jar and lid

Instructions:

  1. Begin by slicing the cucumbers and layering them in a large non-reactive container (glass or food-grade plastic). They should fit snugly to create a tight stack.
  2. Add the sea salt gently over the cucumber slices. It’s important not to over-salt, as it can negatively affect the fermentation process.
  3. Prepare the tangy base by boiling water with some garlic cloves and grated ginger. Allow it to cool to room temperature.
  4. Pour the tangy liquid base over the cucumbers, ensuring they are completely covered.
  5. Cover the cucumbers with a lid or wrap tightly in plastic wrap, and allow to ferment at room temperature. Depending on the temperature, fermentation time can vary from 3-7 days.
  6. Keep an eye on your fermentation progress to ensure the cucumbers maintain their crunch and the liquid doesn’t overflow. Taste daily to determine when your cucumbers reach the desired tanginess.
  7. Once satisfied, transfer the cucumbers and liquid into a clean jar, leaving the salt behind. Fill the jar with a fresh weight (like a smaller jar filled with water and sealed) to pushing down the cucumbers further under the brine.
  8. Cover the jar with a tight seal, and refrigerate for up to a month. The cucumbers can be eaten once they’ve reached your preferred tanginess.

This fermented cucumber tangy base is a game-changer in your culinary adventures. Try them out on sandwiches, mixed into tuna or chicken salad, or as a topping for pizzas and burgers.

My generation’s enthusiasm for fermentation has brought new and unique takes to age-old recipes, and the result is accessible and flavorful dishes that our generation can be proud of. And as fermentation’s popularity continues to grow, we will surely see more creative ways to incorporate these gut-friendly delicacies into our daily meals.
