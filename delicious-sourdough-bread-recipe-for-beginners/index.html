Delicious Sourdough Bread Recipe for Beginners


Sourdough bread has been a beloved staple for centuries, known for its tangy flavor, chewy texture, and satisfying crust. But despite its reputation as a complex and intimidating bread to make, sourdough can be surprisingly easy and incredibly rewarding, even for beginners. Whether you’re a first-time baker or looking to refine your sourdough skills, this simple and delicious sourdough bread recipe will guide you step-by-step to creating the perfect loaf.

In this post, we’ll take you through the entire process: from preparing the starter to baking the bread. We’ll provide tips, tricks, and helpful insights along the way to ensure that your homemade sourdough turns out amazing every time.

What Makes Sourdough So Special?

Sourdough is different from other types of bread because it uses wild yeast and bacteria naturally present in the environment for leavening, rather than relying on store-bought yeast. The result is a bread with a unique flavor profile and texture. The tanginess of sourdough comes from the lactic acid produced by the bacteria, while the wild yeast gives the bread its characteristic rise.

Making sourdough requires patience, but the process is rewarding. It’s a slow fermentation that enhances the flavor and texture of the bread, making it more complex and delicious than anything you can buy at the store.

The Essential Tools You’ll Need

Before diving into the recipe, let’s make sure you have the tools you’ll need to succeed. While you don’t need a lot of fancy equipment, there are a few things that will make the process easier and more efficient:

  • A large mixing bowl: For mixing the dough.
  • A kitchen scale: Baking by weight is far more accurate than using volume measurements.
  • A Dutch oven or baking stone: These will help create the perfect crispy crust by trapping steam.
  • A bench scraper or dough scraper: To help with handling the dough, especially when shaping.
  • A thermometer: To check the internal temperature of the bread, ensuring it’s fully baked.
  • A lame or sharp knife: To score the dough before baking, which helps it expand properly in the oven.
  • A proofing basket (optional): Also known as a banneton, this helps shape your dough and adds a nice texture to the finished loaf.

Making the Starter

The first step in the sourdough process is to create a starter, which is essentially a mixture of flour, water, and wild yeast. You’ll need a few days to develop the starter, but it’s well worth the time and effort. Don’t be intimidated—making your own sourdough starter is simple and natural.

Ingredients for the Starter:

  • 100g (about 1/2 cup) of all-purpose flour
  • 100g (about 1/2 cup) of water (preferably filtered)

Directions for Making the Starter:

  1. Day 1: In a clean jar or bowl, combine 100g of flour and 100g of water. Stir the mixture until all the flour is hydrated, then cover loosely with a lid or cloth. Leave it in a warm, draft-free spot for 24 hours.

  2. Day 2: After 24 hours, you might not see much change in the starter. That’s okay! Discard half of the mixture and add another 100g of flour and 100g of water. Stir well, cover, and let it sit for another 24 hours.

  3. Day 3-5: Repeat the process of discarding half and feeding with fresh flour and water each day. By day 3 or 4, you should start seeing bubbles forming in the mixture, and the starter should begin to have a slight, sour smell.

  4. Day 5: Your starter should now be bubbly, active, and have a tangy, yeasty smell. If it has doubled in size within 4-6 hours of feeding, it’s ready to use. If not, continue to feed for a few more days until it becomes active.

How to Know Your Starter Is Ready:

Your starter is ready when it has doubled in size within about 4-6 hours of feeding and produces bubbles throughout. It should have a tangy, slightly sour aroma and a thick, but scoopable texture.

Preparing the Dough

Now that your sourdough starter is active and ready, it’s time to mix the dough! Here’s where you’ll combine your starter with flour, water, and salt to create the base for your loaf of sourdough.

Ingredients for the Dough:

  • 100g sourdough starter (at peak activity)
  • 375g water (room temperature)
  • 500g bread flour (or a combination of bread and all-purpose flour)
  • 10g salt

Directions for Making the Dough:

  1. Autolyse: In a large mixing bowl, combine the water and flour (reserve the salt for later). Stir the mixture until everything is hydrated, and there are no dry patches of flour. Cover the bowl with a damp cloth and let it rest for 30 minutes. This rest period helps the flour fully hydrate and improves the dough’s extensibility.

  2. Add the Starter and Salt: After the autolyse period, add your active sourdough starter and salt to the dough. Mix everything together until well incorporated. You can use your hands or a dough scraper to fold the dough and ensure all ingredients are evenly distributed.

  3. Knead the Dough: Knead the dough for about 8-10 minutes, until it becomes smooth and elastic. If you prefer, you can also use the stretch-and-fold technique instead of traditional kneading. To do this, gently stretch one side of the dough, fold it over itself, and repeat for about 4-6 folds on all sides.

  4. Bulk Fermentation: Transfer the dough to a lightly oiled bowl and cover it with a damp cloth or plastic wrap. Let the dough rise at room temperature for 4-6 hours. During this time, the dough should roughly double in size. Every 30-60 minutes, perform a set of stretch-and-folds to help develop the dough’s structure.

  5. Final Shaping: Once the dough has risen and has a nice, airy texture, it’s time to shape it. Turn the dough onto a lightly floured surface and gently press it into a rectangle. Fold the edges toward the center, then roll it up into a tight loaf shape. Use a bench scraper to help with shaping, ensuring you create surface tension on the dough.

Proofing the Dough

After shaping, the dough needs to undergo the proofing stage. This is when the dough rests and ferments further, allowing the flavors to develop and the dough to rise.

Cold Proofing (Overnight):

For the best flavor and texture, it’s ideal to proof the dough overnight in the refrigerator. This slow fermentation allows the dough to develop deeper flavor and complexity.

  1. Place your shaped dough into a well-floured proofing basket (or bowl). Cover the dough loosely with a damp cloth or plastic wrap and refrigerate it for 12-16 hours.

  2. If you’re short on time, you can opt for a shorter proofing period of 3-4 hours at room temperature, but the flavor and texture won’t be as developed as with an overnight proof.

Baking the Sourdough

The final step in creating your sourdough masterpiece is baking. The key to a perfect sourdough loaf is high heat and steam, which will help form a beautiful crust while keeping the interior soft and airy.

Directions for Baking:

  1. Preheat the Oven: Place your Dutch oven or baking stone in the oven and preheat it to 475°F (245°C). Allow it to heat for at least 30 minutes before baking.

  2. Score the Dough: When your dough has finished proofing, carefully turn it out onto a piece of parchment paper. Use a sharp knife or a lame to score the top of the loaf. Scoring helps the bread expand properly while baking and adds an artistic touch.

  3. Bake the Bread: Transfer the dough into the preheated Dutch oven (or onto the baking stone if using one). Cover with the lid (if using a Dutch oven) and bake for 20 minutes. After 20 minutes, remove the lid and reduce the temperature to 450°F (230°C). Bake for an additional 25-30 minutes, or until the crust is golden brown and the loaf sounds hollow when tapped on the bottom.

  4. Cool the Bread: Once your sourdough is baked to perfection, remove it from the oven and let it cool on a wire rack. It’s important to allow the bread to cool completely before slicing, as the texture continues to set as it cools.

Tips for Perfect Sourdough Bread

  • Hydration: A higher hydration dough (meaning more water) will result in a more open crumb and a lighter texture, but it can be trickier to handle. If you’re new to sourdough, you might want to start with a lower hydration dough until you’re comfortable with the process.

  • Temperature: The ambient temperature can affect how quickly your dough rises. Warmer temperatures speed up fermentation, while cooler temperatures slow it down. If you’re baking in a colder climate, consider placing your dough in a warm spot, like an oven with the light on, during the fermentation process.

  • Patience Is Key: Sourdough is a slow bread, and it requires time. From making the starter to proofing the dough, the entire process can take up to 3 days. Embrace the process and allow time for the flavors to develop.

Conclusion

Making sourdough bread from scratch can seem daunting at first, but with a little practice and patience, you’ll soon be enjoying your own homemade loaves with a deliciously tangy flavor and perfect texture. By following this beginner-friendly recipe and tips, you’ll create sourdough bread that rivals any artisan bakery, all from the comfort of your own kitchen. Happy baking!
