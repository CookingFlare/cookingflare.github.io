The History of Pies: From Ancient Times to Today


Pies have been a beloved dish across cultures and centuries, evolving from simple sustenance to a symbol of comfort and celebration. This culinary delight has a rich history that reflects the changing tastes, technologies, and social customs of societies throughout time. In this blog post, we will explore the fascinating journey of pies, tracing their origins from ancient civilizations to their modern-day variations.

Ancient Beginnings: The Birth of the Pie

The history of pies can be traced back to ancient civilizations, where the concept of encasing food in a crust was born out of necessity. The earliest known pies date back to around 2000 BC in ancient Egypt. These early pies were made with a simple mixture of flour and water, creating a rudimentary crust that was used to encase various fillings, often made from fruits, nuts, or meats. The Egyptians baked these pies in clay ovens, and they were often offered to the gods as part of religious ceremonies.

The Greeks and Romans further developed the concept of pies. The Greeks created a dish called “artocreas,” which consisted of a pastry shell filled with meat and spices. The Romans, known for their culinary innovations, embraced pies as well, using a variety of fillings, including seafood and game. They even had a saying, “Pies are the food of the gods,” highlighting the significance of this dish in their culture.

The Medieval Era: Pies Take Center Stage

As we move into the medieval era, pies became more prevalent in Europe, particularly in England. The term “pie” is believed to have originated from the Latin word “pica,” which means magpie, a bird known for collecting various objects. This reflects the pie’s nature of combining different ingredients into one dish.

During the Middle Ages, pies were often referred to as “coffins,” a term that described the crust used to encase the filling. These coffins were typically made from a thick, sturdy pastry that could withstand long cooking times. The fillings varied widely, from savory meats to sweet fruits, and pies were often served at feasts and celebrations.

One of the most notable developments during this time was the introduction of the “raised pie.” This type of pie featured a crust that was shaped into a deep dish, allowing for more substantial fillings. Raised pies were often used to preserve meats, as the thick crust acted as a barrier against spoilage. This method of preservation was particularly important in an era without refrigeration.

The Renaissance: A Culinary Revolution

The Renaissance marked a significant turning point in the history of pies. With the advent of new ingredients and cooking techniques, pies became more refined and diverse. The introduction of sugar and spices from the East transformed sweet pies into a delicacy enjoyed by the upper classes. Fruit pies, such as apple and cherry, became popular, and recipes began to appear in cookbooks.

One of the most famous pie recipes from this era is the “mince pie,” which originated in England. Initially, mince pies were filled with a mixture of meat, fruits, and spices, reflecting the opulence of the time. However, over the years, the recipe evolved, and the meat was gradually replaced with sweet ingredients, leading to the modern version we know today.

The Renaissance also saw the rise of pie-making as an art form. Skilled bakers began to experiment with intricate designs and decorative crusts, elevating pies to a status of culinary artistry. This period laid the groundwork for the diverse pie traditions that would emerge in the centuries to come.

The 18th and 19th Centuries: Pies in America

As European settlers made their way to the New World, they brought their culinary traditions with them, including the beloved pie. The 18th and 19th centuries saw the establishment of pie as a staple in American cuisine. Early American settlers adapted traditional recipes to incorporate local ingredients, leading to the creation of iconic American pies.

One of the most famous American pies is the apple pie, which has become synonymous with American culture. The phrase “as American as apple pie” reflects the deep-rooted connection between this dessert and the nation’s identity. Apple pie recipes varied by region, with different varieties of apples and spices being used, showcasing the diversity of American agriculture.

During this time, pies also became a symbol of home and comfort. They were often baked for special occasions, family gatherings, and holidays. The tradition of baking pies for Thanksgiving, for example, has its roots in early American history, where pies were a way to celebrate the harvest and give thanks.

The 20th Century: Industrialization and Innovation

The 20th century brought significant changes to the way pies were made and consumed. With the rise of industrialization, mass production of pie crusts and fillings became possible, making it easier for people to enjoy pies without the labor-intensive process of homemade baking. Frozen pies and pre-made crusts became popular, allowing for convenience without sacrificing flavor.

However, the homemade pie remained a cherished tradition for many families.
