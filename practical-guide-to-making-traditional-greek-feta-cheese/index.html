Practical Guide to Making Traditional Greek Feta Cheese


Feta cheese is a staple of Greek cuisine, known for its crumbly texture and sharp, tangy flavor. Whether you’re using it to top a Greek salad, sprinkle over a savory pie, or enjoy it on its own with olives and bread, Feta brings a unique and authentic touch to countless dishes. Making traditional Greek Feta cheese at home is an incredibly rewarding experience, and in this guide, we’ll walk you through the entire process, from gathering ingredients to storing your finished product.

What Is Feta Cheese?

Feta cheese is a brined curd cheese traditionally made from sheep’s milk, or a combination of sheep and goat milk. It has been made in Greece for centuries, with records dating back to the 8th century BCE. The unique tangy and creamy texture is achieved through the aging process and the specific bacteria that are used in fermentation. The salty brine in which it is stored also contributes to its distinct flavor profile.

While there are variations of Feta cheese made in different countries, the Greek version is recognized as a protected designation of origin (PDO) product by the European Union. This means that authentic Greek Feta can only be produced in specific regions of Greece, under strict guidelines to maintain its traditional characteristics.

Why Make Your Own Feta Cheese?

Making your own Feta cheese has numerous benefits. First and foremost, homemade Feta tends to have a fresher, richer flavor compared to store-bought versions, which can often be too salty or lack the creaminess of authentic Feta. By making it yourself, you have complete control over the ingredients and the fermentation process, ensuring the cheese turns out just the way you like it.

Additionally, homemade Feta cheese allows you to experiment with variations in the recipe, such as adding herbs or spices for unique twists. It also offers the satisfaction of creating something from scratch, connecting you to the ancient traditions of Greek cheesemaking.

Ingredients for Traditional Greek Feta Cheese

To make traditional Greek Feta at home, you’ll need a few essential ingredients. Here’s a list of what you’ll need:

  1. Sheep’s Milk or Sheep and Goat Milk Blend – The best Feta is made from high-quality sheep’s milk. If you’re unable to find pure sheep’s milk, you can use a blend of sheep and goat milk, with the goat milk making up no more than 30% of the total. This blend ensures the Feta retains its authentic taste and texture.

  2. Mesophilic Starter Culture – Mesophilic cultures are specific bacteria that help the milk ferment and coagulate into curds. You can buy these cultures online or at cheesemaking supply stores. Look for a culture that is specifically designed for making Feta cheese.

  3. Rennet – Rennet is an enzyme that helps coagulate the milk, turning it from liquid into curds. It’s available in both liquid and tablet form and can be found at many grocery stores or online.

  4. Non-Iodized Salt – Salt is critical in both the flavor and preservation of Feta cheese. Use non-iodized salt, such as kosher salt or sea salt, as iodine can interfere with the fermentation process.

  5. Water – You’ll need some water for diluting the rennet and for brining the cheese once it’s finished.

  6. Brine Solution – Feta cheese is traditionally stored in a brine solution, which helps preserve it and imparts flavor. The brine is simply a mix of water and salt, and the cheese will be stored in this solution for several weeks or even months.

Equipment You’ll Need

To make Feta cheese at home, the following equipment will be helpful:

  • Large pot (for heating milk)
  • Cheese thermometer (to monitor milk temperature)
  • Cheesecloth (to strain curds)
  • Colander (for draining curds)
  • Long knife or curd cutter (for cutting curds)
  • Cheese mold or container (for shaping the Feta)
  • Cheese press (optional but recommended for uniform pressing)
  • Cheese aging container (for storing in brine)

Step-by-Step Guide to Making Traditional Greek Feta Cheese

Making Feta cheese is a multi-step process that requires patience, but with the right ingredients and careful attention to detail, you can produce cheese that rivals anything you’d find in a Greek market.

Step 1: Prepare the Milk

Start by heating your sheep’s milk (or sheep and goat milk blend) in a large pot. Pour the milk into the pot and slowly heat it over medium-low heat to around 86°F (30°C). Use your cheese thermometer to carefully monitor the temperature. Stir occasionally to prevent the milk from scorching.

Once the milk reaches the target temperature, remove it from the heat.

Step 2: Add the Mesophilic Starter Culture

Sprinkle the mesophilic starter culture evenly over the surface of the milk. Let it sit for 1-2 minutes to rehydrate, then gently stir it into the milk using an up-and-down motion. Be sure to stir gently so as not to create any bubbles or foam.

Let the milk sit at 86°F (30°C) for about 30 minutes to allow the culture to fully develop.

Step 3: Add Rennet to Coagulate the Milk

After 30 minutes, dilute your rennet in about 1/4 cup of cool, non-chlorinated water. Stir the rennet solution into the milk gently for 1-2 minutes, ensuring it is evenly distributed. Once you’ve added the rennet, cover the pot and let the milk sit undisturbed for 1-2 hours at 86°F (30°C). During this time, the milk will coagulate and form curds.

After 1-2 hours, check the curds by gently inserting your finger or a knife into the milk. If it comes out clean and the curd has set properly, it’s ready for the next step.

Step 4: Cut the Curds

Once the curds have formed, it’s time to cut them. Using a long knife or curd cutter, cut the curds into small, uniform cubes about 1/2 inch (1.3 cm) in size. Be gentle while cutting to avoid breaking the curds too much. Allow the curds to rest for about 10-15 minutes to firm up further.

Step 5: Cook the Curds

After the curds have rested, it’s time to cook them. Slowly raise the temperature of the curds to 105°F (40°C) over the course of 30-45 minutes, stirring occasionally to prevent the curds from sticking together. The goal is to make the curds firm and release more whey, which will help the cheese retain its characteristic texture. Once the curds have reached the desired consistency, remove them from the heat.

Step 6: Drain the Curds

Next, transfer the curds into a colander lined with cheesecloth. Let the curds drain for about 30 minutes to remove excess whey. You can also lightly press the curds to speed up the drainage process.

Step 7: Shape the Feta

Once the curds have drained, transfer them into a cheese mold or container. Press the curds gently to form a block. You can use a cheese press if you want a firmer texture, or press manually for a softer result. Let the cheese rest for 4-6 hours to set into its shape.

Step 8: Brine the Feta

After the cheese has been pressed, it’s time to transfer it into a brine solution. To make the brine, dissolve 1/4 cup of salt in 1 quart (4 cups) of water. Place the cheese in a container and pour the brine over it, ensuring the cheese is fully submerged. You can store the Feta in the brine for several weeks to allow the flavors to develop and mature.

Step 9: Age and Enjoy

The Feta cheese will be ready to eat after about 2 weeks in the brine, but for a more intense flavor, you can age it for several months. Over time, the brine will infuse the cheese with a saltiness that is characteristic of traditional Greek Feta.

Once your Feta has aged to your liking, it can be enjoyed in a variety of ways. Serve it crumbled over salads, as a topping for savory pastries, or simply paired with fresh bread and olives.

Storing Homemade Feta Cheese

Homemade Feta cheese should be stored in the brine and kept in the refrigerator. It’s important to ensure the cheese remains submerged in the brine to prevent spoilage. If you find that the brine becomes too salty over time, you can dilute it with a little water.

Tips for Success

  • Use high-quality milk – The flavor and texture of the cheese will depend heavily on the quality of the milk you use. Fresh, locally-sourced milk is ideal for making authentic Feta.
  • Control the temperature carefully – Cheesemaking is a delicate process, and maintaining the right temperature throughout each step is crucial. A reliable thermometer is essential to avoid overheating or underheating the milk and curds.
  • Be patient – Cheesemaking takes time, but the end result is well worth the wait. Allow the curds to rest, cook slowly, and age in brine for the best flavor and texture.

Conclusion

Making traditional Greek Feta cheese at home is a fulfilling and educational experience. Not only will you be able to enjoy the authentic taste of Feta, but you’ll also connect with the ancient art of Greek cheesemaking. Whether you’re a seasoned cheese maker or just starting out, the steps outlined here will guide you through the process of crafting your own delicious, homemade Feta.

By following the steps in this guide, you can bring a taste of Greece into your kitchen and enjoy the flavors of this iconic cheese in your own dishes. Happy cheesemaking!
