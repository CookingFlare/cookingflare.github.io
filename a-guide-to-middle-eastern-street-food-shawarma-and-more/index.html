A Guide to Middle Eastern Street Food: Shawarma and More


Middle Eastern street food is a vibrant and tantalizing world of flavors that offer an unforgettable culinary experience. From the sizzling grills to the mouthwatering aromas that fill the air, the streets of cities like Beirut, Istanbul, Cairo, and Dubai are a true celebration of food. Among the most iconic and beloved dishes is shawarma, a savory meat-based treat that’s become a global favorite. However, the Middle Eastern street food scene is far more diverse than just shawarma, with countless other dishes that showcase the rich flavors and traditions of the region. In this guide, we will take you through the most popular street foods of the Middle East, from shawarma to falafel, kebabs, and beyond.

What is Shawarma?

Shawarma is perhaps the most famous Middle Eastern street food worldwide. Originating from the Levant region, specifically Lebanon, shawarma consists of marinated meat (often lamb, chicken, or beef) that is slow-cooked on a vertical rotisserie. The process involves stacking layers of thinly sliced meat, which are then gradually cooked by heat from the side. This method not only ensures the meat remains juicy but also gives it a signature crispy outer layer, adding texture and flavor.

Traditionally, shawarma is served in pita bread or laffa (a type of flatbread), accompanied by a variety of condiments such as tahini, garlic sauce, pickles, and vegetables. It’s a complete meal in a handheld form, making it the ideal street food.

Shawarma Marinade

To achieve the perfect shawarma, the key is in the marinade. The meat is typically marinated for several hours or overnight to allow the flavors to fully develop. Some common ingredients used in shawarma marinades include:

  • Yogurt
  • Lemon juice
  • Olive oil
  • Garlic
  • Cumin
  • Paprika
  • Cinnamon
  • Cardamom
  • Cloves

The combination of these spices gives shawarma its aromatic, warm flavor profile. The yogurt helps tenderize the meat while also providing a subtle tang.

The Evolution of Shawarma

While shawarma is a street food staple in countries like Lebanon, Turkey, Syria, and Jordan, it has evolved over time and become a beloved dish worldwide. In fact, its influence can be seen in many cultures, with variations such as the Turkish döner kebab, the Greek gyro, and the Israeli shawarma. While these dishes have similar preparation methods, the flavoring, sauces, and garnishes may vary slightly based on the region.

In the Middle East, shawarma has become so ingrained in daily life that it’s often eaten as a late-night snack after a long day of work or partying. It’s also a popular choice for gatherings and casual celebrations, adding to its status as a beloved national dish in many countries.

Other Middle Eastern Street Foods to Try

While shawarma often steals the spotlight, there are plenty of other delicious Middle Eastern street foods that deserve your attention. Let’s dive into some other beloved dishes you can find on the streets of the Middle East.

1. Falafel

Falafel is a classic vegetarian dish that’s popular across the Middle East, particularly in countries like Lebanon, Egypt, and Palestine. These deep-fried balls of spiced chickpeas (or fava beans) are crispy on the outside and soft on the inside, making them the perfect snack.

Falafel is typically served in pita bread or laffa, and topped with tahini sauce, pickles, fresh vegetables, and sometimes even a dollop of hummus. It’s often eaten on its own or as part of a larger mezze spread, where it’s served alongside other appetizers like hummus, baba ghanoush, and tabbouleh.

2. Kebab

Kebab is another iconic street food in the Middle East. While it’s often associated with grilled meat, kebab can come in many forms depending on the country and region. The meat is typically marinated in a mixture of olive oil, lemon juice, and a variety of spices before being skewered and grilled over an open flame.

Some popular types of kebabs include:

  • Shish Kebab: Skewered chunks of marinated meat, often lamb or chicken, grilled until perfectly charred.
  • Kofta Kebab: Ground meat (usually beef or lamb) mixed with herbs and spices, formed into sausages, and grilled on skewers.
  • Adana Kebab: Originating from Turkey, this kebab features minced lamb mixed with peppers and spices, cooked on a flat skewer.

Kebabs are usually served with flatbread, rice, or salad, and are often complemented with fresh dips like hummus or garlic sauce.

3. Manakish

Manakish is a type of Lebanese flatbread that is often enjoyed for breakfast or as a snack. The dough is topped with a variety of toppings, with the most popular being za’atar (a mix of thyme, sesame seeds, and sumac) and olive oil. The dough is baked until golden and crispy, creating a delicious savory snack.

For a heartier version, manakish can also be topped with cheese, ground meat, or even vegetables. It’s commonly served with a side of yogurt or fresh salad, making it a satisfying and flavorful dish.

4. Sfiha

Sfiha, sometimes referred to as “Arabic pizza,” is a savory pastry that originated in the Levant region. The dough is topped with a mixture of ground meat, onions, tomatoes, and spices, then baked until crispy. While it’s similar to a pizza, sfiha is typically smaller and more compact, making it the perfect street food snack.

Sfiha can be made with a variety of meats, including beef, lamb, or chicken, and is often served with a squeeze of fresh lemon or a dollop of yogurt.

5. Fattoush

Fattoush is a popular salad in the Middle East, made with fresh vegetables like tomatoes, cucumbers, and radishes, along with crispy pieces of fried pita bread. The salad is dressed in a tangy mixture of olive oil, lemon juice, and sumac, which gives it a bright, zesty flavor.

Fattoush is often served as part of a mezze platter or as a side dish to grilled meats and kebabs. It’s a refreshing and healthy option that balances the rich, savory flavors of the other street foods.

6. Bourek

Bourek is a savory pastry filled with a variety of ingredients, such as cheese, spinach, or ground meat. It’s commonly found in countries like Lebanon, Syria, and Turkey, where it’s served as a snack or appetizer. The pastry is usually made with phyllo dough or a similar thin, flaky dough, which crisps up beautifully when fried or baked.

Bourek can be served hot or cold, and it’s often accompanied by yogurt or a side salad. Its crispy, golden exterior contrasts beautifully with the flavorful fillings inside, making it a popular choice for street food lovers.

7. Koshari

Koshari is a hearty Egyptian street food made from a mix of rice, lentils, and pasta, topped with fried onions and a tangy tomato sauce. It’s a filling, budget-friendly dish that’s commonly eaten for lunch or dinner. Koshari’s combination of textures and flavors makes it a beloved comfort food in Egypt and throughout the Middle East.

8. Hummus and Pita

No guide to Middle Eastern street food would be complete without mentioning hummus, the famous chickpea dip that’s become a global sensation. Hummus is made by blending cooked chickpeas with tahini, lemon juice, garlic, and olive oil until smooth. It’s typically served with warm pita bread and garnished with olive oil, paprika, and fresh herbs.

Hummus is often eaten as a starter or part of a mezze platter, where it’s paired with other dips and appetizers. Its creamy, nutty flavor and rich texture make it the perfect complement to any Middle Eastern meal.

9. Shawarma Fries

A fun variation on the classic shawarma, shawarma fries are crispy fries topped with shawarma meat, garlic sauce, and tahini. This dish is a favorite in many Middle Eastern countries, often eaten as a snack or side dish to accompany kebabs and other street foods. The combination of crunchy fries and tender, flavorful shawarma meat is simply irresistible.

10. Arabic Ice Cream (Booza)

For dessert, Arabic ice cream, or boza, is a delicious treat that’s unlike any ice cream you’ve ever had. Made with milk, sugar, and a natural gum called mastic, boza has a chewy, elastic texture that sets it apart from traditional ice cream. It’s often served with pistachios or other nuts on top, and it comes in flavors like rose water, saffron, and pistachio.

Why Middle Eastern Street Food Is So Special

Middle Eastern street food has a unique ability to bring people together. Whether it’s enjoying a steaming hot plate of falafel with friends or savoring the tender layers of shawarma after a long day, these dishes are more than just food—they’re an experience. The communal nature of these meals, the vibrant mix of spices, and the focus on fresh ingredients make Middle Eastern street food a reflection of the region’s rich cultural heritage.

The emphasis on grilling, slow-cooking, and using natural spices ensures that each dish is packed with flavor. Moreover, many of these dishes have been passed down through generations, ensuring that their preparation methods and tastes stay true to the traditions of the region.

Conclusion

From the sizzling shawarma stands in Cairo to the bustling markets of Beirut, Middle Eastern street food offers a delicious journey through one of the world’s most vibrant culinary traditions. Shawarma, falafel, kebabs, and the many other dishes that make up this diverse and flavorful street food scene provide a perfect introduction to the region’s rich culture and history.

Whether you’re savoring a piece of manakish for breakfast, sharing a plate of fattoush with friends, or indulging in a late-night shawarma, the experience of Middle Eastern street food is one that’s both satisfying and memorable. So, next time you’re in the mood for something new, take a culinary journey to the Middle East, and discover the incredible flavors that await you on the streets.
